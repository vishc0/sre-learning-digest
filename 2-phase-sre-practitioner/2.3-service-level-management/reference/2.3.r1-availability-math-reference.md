## Key Numbers to Know Cold (VP Interview Prep)

| Commitment | Monthly Allowance | Commonly Called |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|
| 99.9% | **43.8 minutes/month** | "Three nines" |
| 99.99% | **4.4 minutes/month** | "Four nines" |
| 99.999% | **26 seconds/month** | "Five nines" |

Five nines is the target for mission-critical telecom services (emergency systems, financial settlement). Four nines is achievable with active-active architecture and automated failover. Three nines is achievable with standard cloud infrastructure and disciplined operations.

## Downtime Budget Calculation

### Formula

```
Allowable Downtime (minutes) = (1 − SLA%) × Period_Minutes

Period_Minutes:
  Annual:  525,960 minutes (365.25 days × 24 hours × 60 minutes)
  Monthly: 43,200 minutes (30-day month)
           44,640 minutes (31-day month)
           40,320 minutes (28-day month — February)
  Weekly:  10,080 minutes
  Daily:   1,440 minutes
```

### Examples

**99.9% SLA, 30-day month**:
```
Allowable = (1 − 0.999) × 43,200 = 0.001 × 43,200 = 43.2 minutes
```

**99.95% SLA, 31-day month**:
```
Allowable = (1 − 0.9995) × 44,640 = 0.0005 × 44,640 = 22.32 minutes
```

**99.99% SLA, 30-day month**:
```
Allowable = (1 − 0.9999) × 43,200 = 0.0001 × 43,200 = 4.32 minutes
```

## Actual Availability Calculation

### Formula

```
Availability % = 
    (Total Minutes in Period − Unavailable Minutes) 
    ÷ Total Minutes in Period × 100
```

Where:
- **Total Minutes in Period** = Calendar month minutes MINUS excluded period minutes (scheduled maintenance, force majeure)
- **Unavailable Minutes** = Minutes where the SLI measurement indicates failure conditions, occurring within included (non-excluded) time

### Example: Calendar Month Calculation

**Scenario**: October 2026 (31 days = 44,640 minutes)
- Scheduled maintenance (excluded): 2 hours = 120 minutes
- Incident INC-001: 45 minutes (during non-excluded time)
- Incident INC-002: 12 minutes (during non-excluded time)

```
Total Period Minutes = 44,640 − 120 = 44,520 minutes (after exclusions)
Unavailable Minutes = 45 + 12 = 57 minutes
Availability = (44,520 − 57) / 44,520 × 100 = 44,463 / 44,520 × 100 = 99.872%
SLA Commitment = 99.9%
Delta = 99.872% − 99.9% = −0.028% → BREACH
```

## Compound Availability: The Dependency Chain Problem

### Single Dependency

If Service A depends on Service B:
```
Compound Availability = Availability(A) × Availability(B)
```

Example: A = 99.95%, B = 99.97%
```
Compound = 0.9995 × 0.9997 = 0.9992 = 99.92%
```

### Multiple Dependencies

For a chain of N services:
```
Compound Availability = ∏ Availability(i) for i = 1 to N
```

Example: Four-service chain, each at 99.95%:
```
Compound = 0.9995⁴ = 0.9980 = 99.80%
```

Even though each individual service meets its SLO (99.95%), the compound SLA exposure is 99.80% — 10 minutes/month below the 99.9% SLA commitment.

### Compound Availability Table (Common SLO Combinations)

| Dependency Count | Each at 99.99% | Each at 99.95% | Each at 99.9% | Each at 99.5% |
|-----------------|---------------|---------------|--------------|--------------|
| 2 services | 99.98% | 99.90% | 99.80% | 99.00% |
| 3 services | 99.97% | 99.85% | 99.70% | 98.51% |
| 4 services | 99.96% | 99.80% | 99.60% | 98.02% |
| 5 services | 99.95% | 99.75% | 99.50% | 97.53% |
| 6 services | 99.94% | 99.70% | 99.40% | 97.04% |

**VP Implication**: A five-service chain where each service maintains 99.9% individually will compound to a 99.5% effective availability — which may be below the SLA commitment if the SLA was written assuming the chain delivers 99.9%.

### Parallel Redundancy (For Reference)

When two independent systems run in parallel and either can handle traffic (active-active):
```
Compound Availability = 1 − ((1 − Availability(A)) × (1 − Availability(B)))
```

Example: Two systems each at 99.9% in active-active:
```
Compound = 1 − (0.001 × 0.001) = 1 − 0.000001 = 99.9999%
```

Active-active redundancy dramatically improves compound availability — this is why Gold tier services require multi-AZ or multi-region architectures.

## The Distribution Problem: Why Total Downtime Is Not Enough

Total monthly availability does not tell the full story. Distribution of downtime within the month matters commercially.

### Scenario Comparison

Both scenarios produce 99.9% availability (43.8 minutes of downtime in a 30-day month):

**Scenario A**: One 43.8-minute outage at 3:00 AM Sunday
- Customer impact: Low (most enterprise users inactive)
- Customer perception: Single manageable event; SLA barely met

**Scenario B**: 43 separate 1-minute interruptions spread throughout weekday business hours
- Customer impact: High (constant random failures during peak hours)
- Customer perception: Unreliable service; confidence destroyed despite same availability number

The SLA says both are equivalent. The customer does not agree.

### Addressing Distribution in SLA Design

Consider adding supplemental commitments for SLAs covering business-critical services:
- **Maximum contiguous outage** (e.g., "No single contiguous outage exceeding 15 minutes during business hours 08:00–22:00 customer local time")
- **Business-hours weighted availability** (different availability calculation during defined business hours)
- **Monthly incident count cap** (e.g., "No more than [X] availability-impacting events per month regardless of aggregate duration")

These are harder to measure and operationalize, but they better reflect what enterprise customers actually care about.

## SLO → SLA Gap Guidelines

| SLA Tier | SLA Commitment | Recommended Minimum SLO | Minimum Gap |
|----------|---------------|------------------------|-------------|
| Gold | 99.99% | 99.995% | 0.005% (2.2 min/month) |
| Silver | 99.9% | 99.95% | 0.05% (21.6 min/month) |
| Bronze | 99.5% | 99.7% | 0.2% (86.4 min/month) |

**Rule**: The SLO-SLA gap must be large enough to absorb normal operational variance without triggering a breach. If actual performance frequently dips within 20% of the error budget, the gap is insufficient.
