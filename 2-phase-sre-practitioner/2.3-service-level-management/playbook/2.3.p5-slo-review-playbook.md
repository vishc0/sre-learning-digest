# SLO Review Playbook

## Purpose and Scope

The quarterly SLO review is the mechanism by which the SLM program ensures every SLO in the service catalog reflects operational reality and supports SLA commitments. It is not a performance review — it is a calibration exercise. SLOs that are too conservative waste engineering capacity. SLOs that are too aggressive hide breach risk.

**Owner**: SLM Manager (facilitates); VP of Operations (approves SLO changes)
**Attendees**: Service Owner, SRE Lead assigned to the service, Engineering Manager
**VP Attendance**: Required for Gold and Silver tier service reviews; recommended for any service with active SLA backing
**Frequency**: Quarterly; also triggered by major incidents or architectural changes

---

## Pre-Meeting Preparation (T-minus 1 week)

### SLM Manager Prepares the Review Package

For each service under review, compile:

**Performance Data (trailing 90 days)**:
- [ ] Daily availability chart (actual vs. SLO target)
- [ ] Error budget consumption: start of quarter, end of quarter, deepest burn point
- [ ] Error budget burn rate by week (identify periods of high burn)
- [ ] Incident attribution: which incidents drove budget consumption
- [ ] Maintenance window impact (did planned maintenance consume significant budget?)

**SLO Alignment Check**:
- [ ] Compare current SLO against trailing actual performance
- [ ] Calculate the average gap between actual and SLO target
- [ ] Check whether compound SLO (including dependencies) is still above the SLA commitment
- [ ] Flag any dependency SLOs that changed since the last review

**Proposed Changes (if any)**:
- Any proposed SLO changes submitted by the Service Owner since the last review
- Assessment of whether the proposed change is supported by performance data
- Commercial impact: does the proposed change affect SLA alignment?

### Service Owner Prepares the Self-Assessment

Service Owner submits a brief written self-assessment 3 business days before the review:
- Is the SLO achievable at current deployment frequency and on-call load?
- Have architectural changes occurred that make the SLO more or less achievable?
- Is the SLI measurement methodology still appropriate for how the service is used?
- Are there dependency changes that affect compound SLO?
- Proposed SLO change (if any) with justification

## Review Meeting Agenda (90 minutes for Gold tier; 60 minutes for Silver; 45 minutes for Bronze)

### Section 1: Performance Review (20–30 minutes)

**Walk the data**:
1. Present the daily availability chart. Identify periods of high burn.
2. Review the incident attribution. Which incidents drove budget consumption?
3. Assess the pattern: is the burn driven by incidents, deployments, or maintenance?

**Key questions the VP asks**:
- "Where is the performance concentrated — is this one bad month in three, or consistent underperformance?"
- "Are the incidents that drove burn in this quarter representative of normal operation, or were they anomalies?"
- "If we project this quarter's burn rate forward, where does the service land relative to the SLA threshold?"

**SLO Alignment Assessment**:
- Is actual performance consistently above the SLO? By how much?
- Is actual performance approaching the SLO threshold? How often did it get within 20% of the error budget?
- Is the SLO cushion between SLO and SLA still adequate?

### Section 2: SLO Calibration Decision (20–30 minutes)

Based on the performance data, the team evaluates whether the SLO should remain, be raised, or be lowered.

**Decision Framework**:

| Scenario | Data Signal | SLO Recommendation |
|----------|------------|-------------------|
| Consistently outperforming | Actual 0.5%+ above SLO for 3+ quarters | Consider raising SLO (tighten the target) |
| Performing at target | Actual within ±0.1% of SLO, some variation | Maintain SLO |
| Approaching target frequently | Actual within 10% of error budget multiple months | Review: is SLO too aggressive or is engineering investment needed? |
| Consistently failing SLO | Actual below SLO multiple months | SLO may be aspirational (lower it) OR service needs investment (do not lower SLO without engineering commitment) |

**The key distinction**: There is a difference between an SLO that is too aggressive (the team cannot achieve it with current architecture and investment) and an SLO that the team is failing to achieve because engineering investment has been directed elsewhere. The VP must distinguish between these before approving any SLO lowering. Lowering an SLO to hide underperformance is a governance failure.

### Section 3: SLO Change Review (10–15 minutes)

If a SLO change is proposed:

**For SLO raises (tightening)**:
- [ ] Confirm trailing data supports the new target (actual performance must be at or above proposed SLO for 2+ consecutive quarters)
- [ ] Check SLA alignment: does the raised SLO still provide adequate buffer above the SLA commitment?
- [ ] Check compound SLO: does the raised SLO create any dependency chain problems?
- [ ] Confirm the SLI measurement methodology can accurately measure at the new target level

**For SLO lowerings (relaxing)**:
- [ ] Confirm the lowering does not eliminate the SLA buffer (SLO must remain above SLA commitment)
- [ ] Require explicit written justification: is this an architectural reality or a performance management issue?
- [ ] Require the Service Owner to confirm with Engineering Manager that the lowered target is achievable without further engineering investment
- [ ] VP approves all SLO lowerings for Gold and Silver tier services

**SLO Change Approval Gate**:
- Bronze tier: Engineering Manager approval
- Silver tier: VP of Operations approval
- Gold tier: VP of Operations + VP of Engineering approval

### Section 4: Dependency Review (10 minutes)

For any service with dependencies on other services:
- [ ] Check whether dependency SLOs have changed since the last review
- [ ] Recalculate compound SLO: `SLO(A) × SLO(B) × SLO(C)...`
- [ ] If compound SLO is within 0.05% of the SLA commitment: flag as SLA exposure risk
- [ ] If compound SLO is below the SLA commitment: escalate immediately — this is a current SLA coverage gap

### Section 5: Action Items and Next Review (5 minutes)

For each agreed action, document:
- [ ] Action description
- [ ] Owner (named individual, not a team)
- [ ] Due date
- [ ] How the VP will be notified of completion

Schedule the next review date before closing.

## SLO Adjustment Decision Criteria

### Criteria to Raise an SLO

All criteria must be met:
1. Trailing actual performance has been ≥ 0.05% above the current SLO for 2+ consecutive quarters
2. No significant architectural changes are planned that would introduce reliability risk
3. Raising the SLO still maintains a ≥ 0.05% buffer above the SLA commitment
4. The SLI measurement methodology is accurate enough to measure at the new threshold

### Criteria to Maintain an SLO

Any of these conditions:
- Trailing performance is within ±0.05% of the SLO target (system is performing to design)
- Performance has been above the SLO but an architectural change is planned that may reduce reliability
- The SLO was recently set and insufficient data exists to justify a change (< 2 quarters of data)

### Criteria to Lower an SLO

All criteria must be met:
1. Trailing performance has been consistently below the current SLO for 2+ consecutive quarters
2. Engineering analysis confirms the SLO is not achievable at current architecture and investment levels
3. The proposed new SLO still maintains a ≥ 0.05% buffer above the SLA commitment
4. The Service Owner and Engineering Manager jointly confirm the new SLO is achievable
5. VP approval (required for Gold and Silver)

### Hard Rule: SLO Cannot Be Below (SLA + 0.02%)

Under no circumstances should the SLO be set within 0.02% of the SLA commitment. This absolute minimum buffer exists because:
- SLI measurement has inherent precision limits
- The error budget calculation is based on a 30-day window; the SLA may be on a calendar month
- Any measurement variance below this threshold creates SLA breach risk from measurement error alone

## Post-Review Documentation

Within 5 business days of the review:

**SLM Manager**:
- [ ] Update the service catalog with: revised SLO (if changed), last review date, next review date
- [ ] If SLO was changed: create an SLO change record with prior value, new value, effective date, approving VP
- [ ] Distribute meeting notes to all attendees and to the VP if not present

**Service Owner**:
- [ ] If SLO was raised: update the SLO definition document and error budget calculations
- [ ] If SLO was changed: notify the on-call team and update runbooks that reference SLO thresholds
- [ ] Complete any action items assigned during the review by the documented due date

**VP (if SLO was changed)**:
- [ ] If SLO lowering affects SLA alignment: notify Legal and Account Management
- [ ] If SLO change affects customer-facing service reports or portal data: confirm the reporting system is updated before the next report is generated

## Triggered Reviews (Outside Quarterly Cadence)

These events trigger an immediate SLO review:

**Major incident triggering > 25% error budget burn in a single event**:
- Review within 10 business days of incident resolution
- Focus: does the failure mode revealed by the incident require SLO recalibration?

**Significant dependency change**:
- Review within 30 days of the change going to production
- Focus: compound SLO recalculation with the new dependency configuration

**SLA change (new customer, SLA renewal with different terms)**:
- Review before the SLA is signed
- Focus: confirm existing SLO provides adequate buffer for the new SLA commitment

**Engineering team escalation**:
- Triggered when the SRE lead or Service Owner reports that the SLO is no longer achievable
- Focus: is this a measurement problem, an engineering investment problem, or an SLO calibration problem?
