# QBR Preparation Playbook

## Purpose and Scope

The Quarterly Business Review (QBR) is the highest-stakes recurring SLM event. It is a formal meeting between the organization's operational and account leadership and the customer's executive team. Unlike a monthly service report (which delivers data), the QBR builds the relationship, addresses strategic concerns, and positions the partnership for renewal or expansion.

This playbook covers the 3-week preparation process, the meeting structure, and the follow-through actions.

**Owner**: VP of Operations (runs the QBR)
**Supporting roles**: SLM Manager (data preparation), Account Manager (customer relationship intelligence), Director of SRE (technical depth if needed)
**Frequency**: Quarterly, aligned with the customer's fiscal quarter or as specified in the SLA

---

## T-Minus 3 Weeks: Data Gathering and Validation

### Step 1: Pull Quarterly Availability Data

For each service covered by this customer's SLA:
- [ ] Monthly availability (actual vs. SLA commitment) for each month of the quarter
- [ ] Quarterly aggregate availability
- [ ] Total downtime in minutes (by month and quarter total)
- [ ] Breach status for each month (breach / no breach)
- [ ] Credits issued (if any) and confirmation of invoice application
- [ ] Error budget consumption: start of quarter, end of quarter, worst point in quarter

Source: ServiceNow SLM module (availability) + Datadog (SLO data) + Finance (credits)

### Step 2: Compile the Incident Log

For each incident that affected this customer's service during the quarter:
- [ ] Incident date and time
- [ ] Duration (customer-visible impact window, not internal incident clock)
- [ ] Severity
- [ ] Root cause (confirmed — do not present preliminary findings in a QBR)
- [ ] Resolution action
- [ ] Remediation commitment from post-incident follow-up (and current completion status)

### Step 3: Validate the Data

Before building any narrative on the data:
- [ ] Cross-check availability data against customer's own monitoring if they track it (ask Account Manager if customer has independent tracking)
- [ ] Confirm all incident records are complete and root causes are finalized
- [ ] Confirm all credit records are reconciled with Finance
- [ ] Confirm all remediation commitments from prior QBR are tracked and statusable

**Rule**: Do not walk into a QBR with data the customer will dispute. If the customer has independent availability tracking and a different number, resolve the discrepancy before the meeting — not during it.

### Step 4: Gather Customer Intelligence from Account Manager

The Account Manager knows things about the customer's current state that the data does not show:
- [ ] Is the customer currently satisfied, neutral, or dissatisfied?
- [ ] Is the customer evaluating alternatives or actively at risk of churn?
- [ ] Are there pending contract changes, expansions, or reductions?
- [ ] What has the customer been saying informally about the service quality?
- [ ] Who will attend the QBR from the customer side, and what are their individual priorities?
- [ ] Are there any known sensitivities (executive change at customer, internal customer complaints, etc.)?

This intelligence shapes the narrative. A satisfied customer's QBR emphasizes growth and innovation. An at-risk customer's QBR emphasizes reliability evidence, remediation proof, and personal commitment.

## T-Minus 2 Weeks: Narrative Construction

### Step 5: Build the Quarterly Narrative

The QBR narrative has four acts (see ch06):

**Act 1: Where We Were**
Draft the quarterly performance summary. Lead with the number the customer cares most about: was the SLA met?
- If SLA was met every month: lead with this, then show the availability trend
- If SLA was missed one month: lead with acknowledgment ("We had a challenging month in [month] — here's what happened and what we've done about it")
- If SLA was missed multiple months: this QBR requires VP-level preparation; see "At-Risk QBR" section below

**Act 2: What We Learned**
What did incidents in the quarter reveal about the service? What systemic improvements were made?
- Identify the 1–3 most significant reliability learnings of the quarter
- For each: what was the finding, what was the engineering response, what is the evidence the response worked
- Do not include minor incidents unless the customer specifically experienced them

**Act 3: Where We Are Going**
What is changing in the next quarter that the customer should know about?
- Planned architectural improvements
- Known high-risk changes (major releases, infrastructure migrations)
- SLO review results — did any SLOs change?
- New service additions or deprecations

**Act 4: What We Need from the Customer**
This act is often omitted — don't. It signals a genuine partnership:
- Are there customer-side changes that will affect usage patterns (volume, peak times)?
- Are there integration upgrades the customer needs to plan for?
- Are there support preferences the customer wants to adjust?

### Step 6: Identify the Two or Three Strategic Discussion Points

Not every QBR needs a strategic discussion — some are operational reviews. But for major accounts, the VP should prepare 2–3 discussion questions that elevate the conversation beyond the data:
- "As your notification volume grows 30% next year, how can we plan the capacity investment together?"
- "We've been discussing adding [new capability] — are you at a point where that's worth scoping?"
- "We're investing in [reliability improvement] — what would need to be true for you to consider upgrading from Silver to Gold tier?"

### Step 7: Prepare for Difficult Questions

Anticipate the questions that could derail the QBR if unprepared. Standard difficult questions:

| Question | Preparation Required |
|----------|---------------------|
| "Why did you have two breaches in Q2?" | Complete root cause for each; evidence that fixes are in place; trending data showing improvement |
| "Your competitor offers 99.99%. Why can't you?" | Know the trailing actual performance number; know the architectural investment required for 99.99%; know the pricing implication |
| "We're thinking about moving some volume to [competitor]" | Have the risk/benefit analysis ready; know what would retain the business; escalate to Account Executive if revenue material |
| "When will [specific open action item] be complete?" | Know the status of every open commitment made to this customer |
| "The credit we received was less than our lost revenue" | "Service credits are our contractual commitment per the agreed credit schedule. I understand the business impact was larger, and I'd like to understand your experience better. What I can commit to is [forward reliability improvement]." |

## T-Minus 1 Week: Presentation Preparation

### Step 8: Build the Deck

The QBR deck is 10–15 slides. Structure:

1. Title slide (service name, customer name, quarter, attendees)
2. Quarter at a Glance (availability by month, SLA met/missed, credits if any)
3. Availability Trend (12-month availability chart — shows context)
4. Incident Summary (count, severity distribution, brief table)
5. Significant Incidents — Root Cause and Resolution (1 slide per significant incident)
6. Remediation Commitments: Status (table of all open commitments with RAG status)
7. Error Budget Summary (start, end, worst point, what drove consumption)
8. Q[Next] Forward Look: Planned Changes
9. Q[Next] Forward Look: Reliability Investments
10. Discussion: [Strategic topic 1]
11. Discussion: [Strategic topic 2]
12. Action Items from This QBR (blank until the meeting)
13. Appendix: Monthly Availability Detail, Credit Calculation

### Step 9: Practice the Executive Summary

The VP should be able to deliver the entire QBR narrative in 5 minutes without slides. This is the version that gets used if:
- The customer CEO drops in unexpectedly and has 10 minutes
- The meeting goes longer than planned and slides are dropped
- A customer executive asks for the "headline version" before diving into detail

Practice: "In Q[X], we delivered [X]% average availability against our [X]% commitment — [met/missed by X] in [month]. Significant incidents included [1-2 sentence summary]. We've made [X] engineering changes to address the root causes. Looking ahead, [key change in next quarter] will be our focus."

### Step 10: Confirm Logistics with Account Manager

- [ ] Confirm attendees from customer side (names, titles) — VP should know who will be in the room before walking in
- [ ] Confirm location/platform (on-site, video call)
- [ ] Confirm time zone and calendar invites are set
- [ ] Confirm whether customer wants the deck in advance (many do — send 48 hours before)

## The QBR Meeting: Running It

### Opening (5 minutes)

Introductions (if not all known). State the agenda and time allocation. Set the tone: "We're here to review Q[X] together, look at what we've learned, and make sure we're aligned on what's coming. And I want to make sure there's time for your questions and any concerns your team has."

### Act 1: Quarterly Performance (10–15 minutes)

Present the availability data. Direct, factual. If there were breaches, acknowledge them early — do not bury them at the end of the data section. Customers who see breach data at the end of a long data section feel like it was being minimized.

For each breach month: present the data, then immediately go to "here's what happened and what we did about it." Do not present breach data and then move to a different slide without addressing it.

### Act 2: Incidents and Remediation (15–20 minutes)

Walk through significant incidents. For each:
- What the customer experienced (from the customer's perspective, not internal engineering perspective)
- Root cause (brief, non-technical)
- What was changed
- Evidence the change worked (post-incident reliability data)

Review the open remediation commitments table. Be honest about RAG status. If a commitment is red (late), acknowledge it before the customer asks.

### Act 3: Forward Look (10 minutes)

Planned changes and investments. Frame this as "what you should know about our engineering roadmap and how it affects your service." This act demonstrates that the VP is thinking ahead, not just managing the past.

### Act 4: Discussion (15–20 minutes)

Present the 2–3 strategic discussion topics. Then stop talking. The purpose of this act is to listen. The VP has been delivering for 40 minutes — the customer's perspective in the remaining time is what determines whether the QBR was successful.

### Action Item Capture

Before closing: "Let me make sure we've captured all the action items from today's conversation." Review each commitment made (by both parties) during the meeting. Assign owner and due date. Read back to confirm.

### Close

"Thank you for the time and for the direct feedback. I'll send a written summary of today's meeting and action items by [date — typically within 48 hours]."

---

## Post-QBR Follow-Through

### Within 48 Hours
- [ ] Send written meeting summary (attendees, key points, all action items with owners and dates)
- [ ] Update any breach records or remediation commitment records with new commitments made during the QBR
- [ ] Brief Account Manager on customer's stated priorities and any escalation signals observed

### Within 2 Weeks
- [ ] Confirm any action items due within 30 days are assigned and in progress
- [ ] If contract renewal was discussed: hand off to Account Executive with notes from the QBR

### At Next QBR
- [ ] Review all action items from this QBR (first slide after the title: "Actions from Last QBR — Status")

## At-Risk QBR: When the Relationship Is Fragile

If the Account Manager has flagged the customer as at-risk (evaluating alternatives, expressed dissatisfaction, second breach in the quarter), the QBR preparation requires additional steps:

1. **Pre-QBR executive call**: VP calls the customer VP before the QBR to acknowledge the issues directly, not for the first time in the meeting
2. **Legal briefing**: Understand the contract exit rights and any triggers that have been approached
3. **Retention offer**: If Account Management has approval, have a retention offer ready (SLA upgrade, discount, additional support commitment) — but do not lead with it; lead with accountability and improvement evidence
4. **Escalated attendees**: Consider inviting VP of Engineering or CTO to demonstrate organizational commitment
5. **Shorter, denser deck**: An at-risk customer does not need 15 slides. They need 5 slides and a genuine conversation.
