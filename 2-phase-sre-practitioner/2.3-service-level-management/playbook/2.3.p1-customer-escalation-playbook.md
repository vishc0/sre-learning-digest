# Customer Escalation Playbook

## Purpose and Scope

This playbook covers VP-level customer escalations — situations where the standard account management and technical support channels are insufficient and executive engagement is required to manage the customer relationship. Escalations are not just about SLA breaches; they cover any situation where the customer relationship is at operational or commercial risk.

**Owner**: VP of Operations
**Triggers**: See Phase 1 below
**Preparation time**: 24–48 hours (unless emergency)

---

## Phase 1: Recognizing Escalation Triggers

### Automatic Escalation Triggers (VP Must Act)

These situations automatically require VP engagement — the Account Manager cannot resolve them:

| Trigger | Response Required |
|---------|-----------------|
| Second SLA breach in a 90-day period | VP-to-VP call within 24 hours of second breach declaration |
| Customer invokes contractual escalation rights | VP + Legal within 24 hours |
| Customer signals vendor evaluation or RFP | VP call within 24 hours; brief CRO or CEO |
| Customer's CIO or COO contacts the organization directly | VP responds within 4 business hours |
| Written complaint escalated above Account Manager level | VP response within 24 hours |
| Press inquiry regarding service quality | VP + Communications immediately; do not respond to press without Communications |
| Regulatory inquiry related to service performance | VP + Legal + Regulatory Affairs immediately |

### Account Manager-Flagged Triggers (VP Judgment Required)

These require VP to evaluate whether to escalate:
- Customer satisfaction survey response below threshold (typically < 6/10)
- Account Manager reports customer expressing dissatisfaction in business reviews
- Customer requesting SLA revision or credit above standard terms
- New executive at customer who has stated concerns about vendor relationships
- Customer reducing usage or delaying renewal discussions

---

## Phase 2: Preparation (24–48 Hours)

### Step 2.1: Intelligence Gathering

Before making any contact with the customer at VP level, the VP must know:

**Account History**:
- [ ] All SLA breaches in the last 12 months: dates, duration, credit amounts, root causes
- [ ] All open remediation commitments: status, overdue items
- [ ] QBR history: when was the last QBR, what were the key themes, what commitments were made
- [ ] Contract terms: term length, renewal date, credit clauses, exit rights
- [ ] Revenue: annual contract value, strategic classification

**Current Situation**:
- [ ] Precise nature of the issue triggering the escalation (specific incidents, specific impact the customer has experienced)
- [ ] Customer's stated complaint (from Account Manager briefing)
- [ ] Customer's unstated concern (read between the lines — what is the real risk?)
- [ ] Any public statements by the customer or their competitors that provide context

**Internal Readiness**:
- [ ] Status of any engineering fixes relevant to the customer's complaints
- [ ] Whether the fix is complete, in-progress, or not yet started
- [ ] What specific commitments the VP is authorized to make
- [ ] Whether a remediation offer (SLA upgrade, service credit beyond standard terms, dedicated support) has been approved

### Step 2.2: Determine the Objective

VP escalation calls have one of four objectives. Know which one before calling:

| Objective | Situation | Approach |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Damage control** | Active crisis, customer distressed | Lead with acknowledgment; demonstrate personal accountability; commit to specific near-term actions |
| **Relationship restoration** | Post-incident, trust damaged | Focus on listening; understand the full extent of their experience; build a joint recovery plan |
| **Commercial protection** | Customer evaluating alternatives | Acknowledge the legitimacy of their evaluation; make the case for staying with specifics; know when to escalate to retention offer |
| **Proactive investment** | No crisis, but VP judges relationship needs executive investment | QBR format; strategic discussion; forward-looking partnership |

### Step 2.3: Brief Internal Stakeholders

Before the call:
- [ ] Account Manager briefed (they should be aware a VP call is being made; they may be invited to listen)
- [ ] Legal briefed (for any call involving breach disputes, contract rights, or regulatory matters)
- [ ] Account Executive briefed (any call where commercial terms may be discussed)

## Phase 3: The Escalation Call

### Opening the Call (2 minutes)

Do not start with a lengthy preamble. The customer knows why you are calling.

"[Customer name], I'm calling you directly because [I wanted to ensure you heard from me personally about what happened / I understand your team has had a frustrating experience and I wanted to address that directly / I've been tracking your account and I think we're overdue for a direct conversation]."

State the purpose in one sentence. Then transition to the situation.

### Situation Summary (3 minutes)

VP delivers a factual summary of what has happened:
- What service events occurred (dates, duration, impact)
- Current status
- What the engineering team has done or is doing

Keep this to 3 minutes. The objective is to demonstrate command of the facts — not to give a detailed briefing.

### Accountability Statement (1 minute)

"This is not the level of service we committed to you, and I take personal responsibility for the gap. I'm not here to defend it — I'm here to make it right."

The accountability statement:
- Does not use the word "apologize" more than once (repetitive apology sounds defensive)
- Does not include caveats ("but given the circumstances..." removes accountability)
- Does not blame dependencies or other parties, even if they contributed

### Forward Commitment (3–5 minutes)

Only commit to things the organization can deliver. Specific commitments:
- Root cause: "We've confirmed the root cause is [X], and the engineering fix is [Y], scheduled to be complete by [date]."
- Evidence: "You'll see this reflected in the next monthly service report, and I'll personally follow up with you after we've had 30 days of post-fix data."
- Credit: "You're entitled to a credit of $[X] on your next invoice, and if you haven't received confirmation of that, I'll have our SLM Manager send you the calculation today."
- Escalation path: "If anything further goes wrong before this fix is in place, I want you to call me directly at [number]. Not the support line."

### Customer's Turn (Remaining Time)

"Now I want to hear from you. I've been doing the talking — can you tell me about the impact this has had on your team?"

Listen. Do not interrupt to defend or explain. The VP's job for the remainder of the call is to understand the customer's experience, not to manage it.

Ask follow-up questions:
- "Can you tell me more about [specific impact they mentioned]?"
- "How has this affected your team's ability to [relevant workflow]?"
- "What would need to be true for you to feel confident in our service going forward?"

The last question is the most important. The customer's answer tells the VP exactly what the remediation needs to accomplish.

### Close (2 minutes)

"Here's what I'd like to do. [Restate the specific commitments made during the call.] I'll have [SLM Manager name] send you a written summary of everything we've discussed today, including the action items on our side, within 24 hours. And I'd like to schedule a follow-up call in [30 days] to review how things are progressing. Does that work for you?"

## Phase 4: Post-Call Actions

### Within 24 Hours
- [ ] Send the written call summary (from VP or SLM Manager on VP's behalf):
  - Attendees and date
  - Key issues discussed
  - All commitments made (owner, due date)
  - Next call date
- [ ] Brief Account Manager with full call summary and customer's stated concerns
- [ ] Create or update action items in ServiceNow for all commitments made
- [ ] If contract/commercial matters were discussed: brief Legal and Account Executive

### Within 48 Hours
- [ ] Confirm all engineering commitments made on the call are known to Engineering Manager and are tracked
- [ ] If credit discussion occurred: confirm with Finance that credit is being processed
- [ ] If SLA upgrade or special terms were discussed: route to Legal for contract review

### At Follow-Up Call (30 Days)
- [ ] Review each commitment made on the original call: status for each
- [ ] Show evidence: if a fix was committed, show post-fix availability data
- [ ] Assess relationship status: has trust been restored? Is there residual risk?
- [ ] If all commitments have been met and customer expresses satisfaction: schedule return to normal account management cadence (no longer VP-level weekly monitoring needed)

## Escalation Talking Points by Scenario

### Scenario: Customer Demands Credit Beyond Contract Terms

**Customer says**: "The credit doesn't cover our losses. We want [larger amount]."

**VP response**: "I understand the business impact you experienced was greater than what the contractual credit covers. Our service agreement defines the credit structure, and I want to honor that fully. What I can do beyond the credit is [specific offer: dedicated support resource, SLA tier upgrade, priority placement in engineering queue for fix]. I want to make sure we're addressing the impact to your business, not just the contract minimum."

Do not agree to out-of-contract payments without Legal approval. Do offer alternative forms of value that do not create financial precedent.

### Scenario: Customer Threatens Public Disclosure

**Customer says**: "We're going to tell our industry partners about these issues."

**VP response**: "I hear that your experience has been frustrating enough that you're considering that, and I take that seriously. I'd rather earn your trust back than have you take that step. Here's what I can commit to in the next [30 days]: [specific commitments]. If after that you feel we haven't delivered, I would understand your perspective. But I'd like the chance to demonstrate we can do better."

Then escalate internally: notify Communications and Legal that the customer has made a public disclosure threat. Do not wait for it to happen.

### Scenario: Customer Is Evaluating Alternatives

**Customer says**: "We're talking to [competitor] about some of our volume."

**VP response**: "I appreciate you telling me directly — that's a sign you'd rather work this out than just move the business. Can I ask: what specifically would need to be different for you to feel confident keeping this with us? I want to understand whether what you need is within our ability to deliver."

Listen to the answer. If the customer's requirements are achievable, commit specifically. If they are not achievable (e.g., they want 99.999% which the architecture cannot support), be honest: "I don't want to make a commitment I can't keep. Let me get you a realistic picture of what we can deliver and when, and let's decide together whether that works for you."

### Scenario: Customer Invokes Termination Rights

**Customer says**: "We're invoking the termination clause per section [X] of our agreement."

**VP response**: Do not negotiate contract exit rights on the phone. "I understand, and I want to make sure I understand the full scope of what you're telling me. Let me loop in our legal team to ensure we handle this correctly for both of us. Can I have someone from Legal and Account Management reach out to you today to begin that process?"

Then: brief Legal immediately. Do not confirm or dispute the termination trigger without Legal review.

## Escalation Anti-Patterns

**Sending the Account Manager instead**: The customer expected the VP. Sending a surrogate signals that the VP does not consider this a priority.

**Calling without preparation**: The VP who cannot answer "what's the current status of the fix" or "what credit amount has been issued" loses credibility in the first 5 minutes.

**Over-promising on the call**: Committing to engineering changes that have not been resourced, or to SLA terms that have not been approved, creates a second escalation when the commitment is missed.

**Not following up in writing**: A verbal commitment that is not confirmed in writing will be remembered differently by the two parties within a week.

**Making the call a reporting session**: If the VP talks for 80% of the call and the customer talks for 20%, it was not an escalation call — it was a status update. Escalation calls are about listening.
