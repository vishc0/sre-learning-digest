# Chapter 8: SLM Program Governance

## Governance Is What Makes SLM a Program, Not a Practice

Individual SLOs, individual SLAs, individual service reports — these are SLM artifacts. A program is what governs the creation, maintenance, review, and evolution of those artifacts. Without governance, the artifacts degrade: SLOs become stale, SLAs are never renewed, service reports are produced late or not at all, and the service catalog drifts away from operational reality.

The SLM program governance model answers three questions: who is responsible for which SLM function, how do SLM decisions get made and documented, and how does the organization know if the program is working?

---

## The Service Level Manager Role

The Service Level Manager (SLM Manager) is a dedicated role — or a clearly assigned responsibility for an existing role — responsible for the day-to-day operation of the SLM program. This is distinct from the VP of Operations, who owns SLM governance at the executive level.

### Service Level Manager Responsibilities

**Operational**:
- Maintain the service catalog: currency, accuracy, quarterly audits
- Coordinate SLO reviews: schedule, agenda preparation, documentation
- Manage the SLA library: version control, renewal tracking, active vs. historical
- Produce and distribute service reports: monthly on schedule, QBR preparation support
- Track open breach records: notification status, credit issuance, remediation commitment status
- Maintain the error budget tracking dashboard

**Advisory**:
- Advise service owners on SLO definition and measurement methodology
- Review SLA drafts for measurement consistency before Legal and VP review
- Flag SLA renewal timelines to the VP 90 days before expiration
- Identify SLO performance trends that require VP attention

**Reporting line**: The Service Level Manager reports to the VP of Operations (or to a Director of Operations who reports to the VP). This is not an IT Service Management role that reports to an ITSM function independently of engineering operations — the SLM Manager must be connected to the engineering decision-making chain.

**Why a dedicated role matters**: In organizations without a dedicated SLM Manager, SLO reviews slip, service reports are produced inconsistently, the SLA library is unmanaged, and the VP is alerted to breach risk only when a breach has already occurred. The SLM Manager is the operational nervous system of the program.

## The SLM Review Board

The SLM Review Board is the quarterly cross-functional governance body that reviews SLM program performance, makes decisions on SLA and SLO changes, and ensures cross-functional alignment.

### Membership

| Role | Attendance | Voting Authority |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| VP of Operations | Chair | Yes |
| VP of Engineering | Required | Yes |
| Director(s) of SRE | Required | Advisory |
| Service Level Manager | Required (facilitates) | No |
| Product Management VP or Director | Required | Advisory |
| Head of Account Management / Customer Success | Required | Advisory |
| Legal Counsel (SLA matters) | As needed | Advisory |

### Agenda (Standard Quarterly)

**30 minutes — Portfolio Performance Review**
- Quarterly availability summary: Gold, Silver, Bronze tiers
- Error budget consumption: which services are near exhaustion, which are healthy
- Breach summary: any breaches in the quarter, credits issued, remediation status
- Problem Management interface: which open Problems are affecting SLM performance

**30 minutes — SLO Review Decisions**
- Proposed SLO changes from service owners (flagged by SLM Manager pre-meeting)
- Compound SLO assessment for SLA-backed service chains
- SLO changes requiring VP approval

**20 minutes — SLA Library Review**
- SLAs approaching renewal (next 90 days)
- SLA change requests from customers
- New SLA commitments requiring board visibility

**20 minutes — Program Governance**
- Service catalog audit results
- SLM tool performance (gaps, issues)
- Service Level Manager operational report
- SLM maturity assessment update

### Decision Authority

The SLM Review Board has binding authority on:
- SLO changes for Gold and Silver tier services
- Service tier reclassification (upgrade or downgrade)
- SLM policy changes (error budget policy thresholds, breach notification windows)
- Service catalog governance standards

The board does not have authority to approve SLAs — that authority belongs to the VP of Operations (for approval) and Legal (for contract review). The board advises on SLA commitments that are being proposed or renewed.

## SLA Library Management

The SLA library is the authoritative repository of all service level agreements the organization has signed, is currently maintaining, and has historically retired.

### Library Structure

**Active SLAs**: Currently in force, customers currently under contract
- Indexed by customer name, service, contract term, and next renewal date
- Each record: SLA document (latest signed version), amendment history, breach record history, credit record history

**Historical SLAs**: Expired or terminated contracts, retained for reference and legal compliance
- Retained for the longer of: 7 years or the applicable contract dispute statute of limitations

**SLA Templates**: Standard templates by tier (Gold/Silver/Bronze) maintained by Legal
- Versioned: each time a template is revised, prior version is retained
- New SLAs use the current template unless a customer-specific addendum is required

### Renewal Tracking

The SLM Manager maintains a renewal calendar:
- **90 days before expiration**: VP and Account Manager alerted; renewal preparation begins
- **60 days before expiration**: SLA performance summary prepared (trailing 12 months) for renewal negotiation support
- **30 days before expiration**: Draft renewal terms prepared by Legal, reviewed by VP
- **Expiration date**: Signed renewal or documented extension in place. An SLA that expires without renewal or extension creates a commercial liability: the customer may be operating under terms that the organization has not agreed to continue.

### Version Control

Every SLA document has a version number. When a customer requests a change (during the term or at renewal), the change is documented as an amendment with:
- Amendment number and date
- Prior SLA version reference
- Specific changes made
- Signatures of both parties

No verbal SLA modifications. No "we agreed on the call." Written, signed, versioned.

## SLM Tooling

The SLM program requires tooling support across three categories: SLO tracking and error budget management, SLA and service report management, and customer-facing status communication.

### ServiceNow SLM Module

**Best for**: SLA lifecycle management, breach records, credit calculation workflow, service report generation, reporting to internal stakeholders

**Capabilities**:
- SLA definition records (availability target, measurement methodology, credit schedule)
- Automated SLA availability tracking against incident records
- Breach notification workflow (automated alerts when availability drops below threshold)
- Credit calculation and invoice integration
- Service report generation from SLA data

**Gaps**:
- Does not provide real-time SLI measurement (must integrate with monitoring tool)
- Customer-facing portal functionality is limited without customization
- SLO tracking (vs. SLA tracking) requires additional configuration

### Datadog SLO Tracking

**Best for**: Real-time SLO monitoring, error budget burn rate tracking, SLI data collection, engineering-facing dashboards

**Capabilities**:
- SLO definition from existing monitors (availability, latency, custom metrics)
- Error budget burn rate tracking with alerting (7-day and 30-day burn windows)
- SLO status history
- SLO dashboards for service owners and VP-level roll-ups

**Gaps**:
- Does not manage SLA documents, credit calculations, or breach records
- Customer-facing reporting requires additional tooling
- Cross-service dependency tracking is limited

### Grafana SLO Panels

**Best for**: Custom SLO dashboards built from Prometheus or InfluxDB metrics, VP-level portfolio views

**Capabilities**:
- Flexible SLO visualization panels
- Multi-service roll-up dashboards
- Error budget burn rate visualization
- Integration with multiple data sources

**Gaps**:
- Requires engineering effort to build and maintain
- No built-in SLA or breach management workflow
- Customer-facing access requires additional security configuration

### Atlassian Statuspage

**Best for**: Customer-facing service status communication, incident notification to customers, public availability history

**Capabilities**:
- Per-component status (operational / degraded / partial outage / major outage)
- Scheduled maintenance announcements
- Incident update posting with notification to subscribers
- Historical uptime display (optional, configurable)
- Customer notification via email, SMS, webhook

**Gaps**:
- Not a measurement tool — status is manually updated (or via monitoring integration)
- Does not calculate SLA availability from historical data
- No credit management or breach record integration

### The Integrated Stack

No single tool covers the full SLM requirement. The production-grade SLM stack is:

```
[Datadog / Grafana]  →  SLI measurement, SLO tracking, error budget
        ↓
[ServiceNow SLM]     →  SLA records, breach management, credit calculation, reporting workflow
        ↓
[Statuspage]         →  Customer-facing status and incident communication
```

Integration between layers (particularly Datadog SLO data feeding ServiceNow SLA tracking) is often custom-built and requires ongoing maintenance. The SLM Manager owns the tooling integration.

## SLM Maturity Model

The organization's SLM capability exists on a maturity spectrum. Knowing where the organization currently sits determines what the next investments should be.

### Level 1: Ad-Hoc Commitments

**Indicators**: SLAs exist but are inconsistently measured. Some services have SLOs; many do not. Breach is discovered when the customer complains, not when monitoring alerts. Service reports are produced manually and inconsistently.

**VP Action**: Mandate SLO definition for all SLA-backed services within 90 days. Assign a dedicated SLM Manager. Implement basic SLA tracking in ServiceNow.

### Level 2: Defined SLAs

**Indicators**: All SLA-backed services have SLOs. SLAs are documented and version-controlled. Monthly service reports are produced consistently. Breach notification exists but is reactive.

**VP Action**: Implement error budget policy. Stand up the SLM Review Board. Begin quarterly SLO reviews. Build the VP executive dashboard.

### Level 3: Governed SLOs

**Indicators**: SLO review cadence is operational. Error budget policy is active and enforced. SLM Review Board meets quarterly. Service catalog is current. Breach notification is proactive (VP is alerted before the customer). Leading indicators are monitored.

**VP Action**: Invest in customer-facing reporting portal. Develop tiered SLA design. Begin SLM maturity assessment as a quarterly metric.

### Level 4: Predictive SLM

**Indicators**: The organization predicts breach risk before it materializes. Error budget burn rate alerts give the VP 7+ days of warning before a breach. Dependency SLO modeling identifies compound SLO risks before they cause customer impact. SLA renewal is a strategic conversation, not a contract exercise.

**VP Action**: Invest in machine learning-based burn rate prediction (if data maturity supports it). Begin publishing SLO performance data proactively to customers (not just monthly reports). Participate in industry SLM benchmarking.

### Maturity as a VP Metric

The VP should assess SLM maturity annually and include it in the SLM Review Board agenda. The target progression:
- Year 1: Level 1 → Level 2
- Year 2: Level 2 → Level 3
- Year 3+: Level 3 → Level 4 (ongoing)

## Regulatory Angle: SLM in Regulated Industries

### Telecom (T-Mobile Context)

The FCC requires carriers to report major outages via the Network Outage Reporting System (NORS). The threshold for mandatory reporting: any outage affecting ≥ 900,000 user minutes (approximately 10,000 users × 90 minutes, or 900,000 users × 1 minute).

For enterprise SLA purposes, the VP must know whether an SLA breach that affected enterprise customers also crossed a regulatory reporting threshold. These are separate obligations:
- The SLA breach triggers a commercial response (credit, customer notification)
- The NORS threshold triggers a regulatory response (FCC filing within specific timeframes)

The SLM program must integrate with Legal and Regulatory Affairs to ensure:
- Breach records are assessed for NORS threshold compliance
- FCC filing timelines are tracked separately from commercial breach remediation timelines
- Customer communications about breached SLAs do not contradict regulatory filings

### Finance (If Applicable)

Financial services regulators (OCC, Federal Reserve, FFIEC) have operational resilience requirements that effectively mandate SLM for critical banking services. DORA (EU Digital Operational Resilience Act) requires financial entities to have defined service level agreements with critical third-party providers and documented governance around their management.

For T-Mobile SRE teams supporting banking or financial clients: the SLA commitments to those clients may be subject to regulatory audit. The SLM documentation (service catalog, SLO records, breach history) must be audit-ready.

### What Auditors Look For

When a regulated industry auditor reviews SLM:
- Does the organization have documented SLAs for all critical services?
- Are SLAs reviewed and renewed on a defined cadence?
- Is there evidence of SLO monitoring (not just SLA tracking)?
- Are breach records complete and retained?
- Is the remediation process documented and followed?
- Are there defined escalation paths for breach scenarios?

The SLM program documentation this framework produces — service catalog, SLO definitions, breach records, remediation letters, service reports — is the audit evidence. It must be organized, version-controlled, and accessible.

## SLM Integration with Change and Problem Management

### SLM + Change Management

The error budget is the SLM control on the change management process. [Cross-reference: change-release-management/]

The key integration point: before a high-risk change is approved for an SLA-backed service, the change management process must assess the current error budget status. If the budget is at 75% consumed or above, the change management process should require VP approval (not just engineering manager approval) before the change proceeds.

The change freeze that the error budget policy triggers is implemented through the change management process — not as a separate mechanism. When the error budget hits 90%, the change advisory board (CAB) for that service automatically requires VP sign-off for any change.

### SLM + Problem Management

Problem Management identifies the root causes of recurring incidents that erode SLO compliance. [Cross-reference: problem-management/]

The key integration point: a breach that results from a repeat failure mode must create a Problem record if one does not already exist, and must update an existing Problem record if one does. The VP monitors open Problem records for SLA-backed services — these records are the SLM program's visibility into systemic reliability debt.

The Problem Management process should flag SLA-backed service problems with elevated priority: not just because the technical severity warrants it, but because every day the Problem is open, the organization accumulates commercial risk.

## Summary: What the VP Owns in SLM Governance

> **What the VP Owns**: The VP owns the SLM program. Not the execution of individual SLOs (that's the service owner), not the day-to-day SLM operations (that's the SLM Manager), but the program itself: its structure, its governance model, its tooling investment, its maturity trajectory, and its regulatory compliance. The VP chairs the SLM Review Board. The VP approves the error budget policy. The VP signs every external SLA. The VP is the face of the SLM program to the customer and to the board. If the SLM program is failing — SLOs aren't reviewed, SLAs aren't tracked, breaches are surprises — the VP is accountable.
