# Chapter 5: Breach Management and Remediation

## What a Breach Actually Is

An SLA breach is not the same as an incident. An incident is an operational event. A breach is a commercial consequence: the moment when accumulated operational failures have exceeded the contractual commitment the organization made to a customer.

A breach can happen two ways:
1. **Single event breach**: A single incident causes enough downtime to exceed the monthly SLA threshold in one event (e.g., a 90-minute outage on a 99.9% SLA where only 43.8 minutes were allowed)
2. **Accumulated breach**: Multiple smaller incidents across the month accumulate to push total downtime beyond the SLA threshold (e.g., five 10-minute incidents totaling 50 minutes against a 43.8-minute limit)

Both types have identical commercial consequences — the customer is owed a credit. But they have different root causes, different remediation strategies, and different customer communication challenges. The accumulated breach is particularly difficult because the customer may not be tracking the cumulative exposure in real time, and the conversation at month-end can be a surprise to both parties.

## SLA Breach Declaration

### When Breach Is Declared

An SLA breach is declared when the VP of Operations confirms (or is briefed that) actual availability for the measurement period has fallen, or is mathematically certain to fall, below the SLA commitment before the measurement period closes.

There are two practical scenarios:

**Confirmed breach (retrospective)**: The measurement period has closed (month-end) and the data shows actual availability below the SLA threshold. Breach is certain.

**Imminent breach (prospective)**: The measurement period is still open, but accumulated downtime has already exceeded or is mathematically guaranteed to exceed the SLA threshold given remaining time. Example: It is day 20 of the month, the SLA allows 43.8 minutes of downtime, and 50 minutes of downtime have already accumulated. Breach is mathematically confirmed even if the service runs perfectly for the remaining 10 days.

### Who Declares the Breach

The VP of Operations declares the breach. Not the on-call engineer. Not the service owner. Not the SRE lead. The VP.

Why: A breach declaration initiates a commercial and legal process — customer notification, credit calculation, escalation. This authority belongs at the executive level. The SRE team identifies the situation; the VP declares it and initiates the commercial response.

### The Declaration Record

When a breach is declared, a formal breach record is created in the SLM system (ServiceNow or equivalent):

- **Service**: which service breached
- **SLA reference**: which specific SLA agreement was breached (contract ID, version)
- **Commitment**: what availability was promised
- **Actual**: what availability was delivered during the measurement period
- **Period**: which measurement period (calendar month) the breach covers
- **Contributing incidents**: which incident records contributed to the accumulated downtime
- **Declaration date**: when the VP declared the breach
- **Declaring officer**: VP name, title, and contact information
- **Notification deadline**: when customer notification is due (based on SLA notification clause)

## Breach Notification Requirements

### The Notification Window

Most enterprise SLAs specify that the vendor must notify the customer of a breach within a defined window — typically 2–4 business hours for a confirmed breach from an active incident, and 5 business days for a month-end retrospective breach.

The VP must ensure the notification process can execute within this window. At 3am, this means:
- The on-call chain escalates to the VP within 30 minutes of breach confirmation
- The VP or designee approves and sends the initial notification
- The formal follow-up (with credit calculation and remediation commitment) follows within 24 hours

### What Goes in the Notification

The breach notification to the customer contains (see template [breach-notification-template.md]):

1. **Statement of breach**: "We are writing to inform you that [Service Name] experienced [X minutes] of unavailability during [measurement period], resulting in actual availability of [Y]% against our committed [Z]%."
2. **Factual summary of what happened**: Non-technical, customer-appropriate description of the incident(s)
3. **Business impact acknowledgment**: Acknowledge the customer's experience without accepting liability beyond the SLA terms
4. **Credit commitment**: "You are entitled to a service credit of [X]% of your [month] service fee, which will appear on your [next month] invoice."
5. **Remediation commitment**: What the organization is doing to prevent recurrence — specific, time-bound, verifiable
6. **Follow-up schedule**: "Our [VP name] will contact you by [date] to discuss the root cause analysis and our improvement plan."

### What Does Not Go in the Notification

- Technical root cause detail (save this for the follow-up call)
- Speculation about contributing causes not yet confirmed
- Apologies that go beyond what the SLA terms require (these can be interpreted as admitting liability beyond the credit)
- Language that disputes the customer's experience
- Vague commitments ("we are looking into it")

The notification is a commercial document. It is reviewed by Legal before it goes out for any breach involving a major enterprise customer.

## SLA Credit Calculation

### Standard Credit Formula

Credit Amount = Monthly Service Fee × Credit Percentage (from the credit schedule)

The applicable credit percentage is determined by which tier of the credit schedule the actual availability falls into.

Example:
- Monthly service fee: $50,000
- SLA commitment: 99.9% availability
- Actual availability: 98.7%
- Credit schedule: < 99.9% but ≥ 99.0% → 10%; < 99.0% but ≥ 95.0% → 25%
- Applicable tier: 98.7% falls in "< 99.0% but ≥ 95.0%"
- Credit: $50,000 × 25% = $12,500

The credit appears on the next month's invoice as a deduction. It is not paid as cash.

See [sla-credit-calculation-guide.md] for the complete formula guide with worked examples for multiple scenarios.

### Escalating Credits for Repeat Breaches

Some SLAs include escalating credit provisions for repeat breaches:

| Breach Frequency | Credit Multiplier |
|-----------------|------------------|
| First breach in 12-month period | Standard credit |
| Second breach in 12-month period | 1.5× standard credit |
| Third or subsequent breach | 2× standard credit |

If the organization's SLAs include escalating provisions, the VP must track breach history by contract and ensure credit calculations reflect the correct multiplier.

### Credit Cap

The credit cap prevents a single catastrophic month from generating unlimited credit obligations. Standard: total credits in any calendar month cannot exceed 50% of the monthly service fee, regardless of how many or how severe the breaches.

If a customer contests the cap ("we were down 80% of the month, you owe us more than 50%"), the VP's response: "The credit cap is a defined term in our service agreement, and the credit reflects our contractual obligation. We understand this has been a severe service disruption, and I want to discuss what additional support we can provide beyond the credit to help your team through the recovery." This keeps the conversation out of a legal dispute while showing genuine engagement.

## Breach Communication: What to Say and What Not to Say

### Communication Authority

Only the VP of Operations or their designated representative (typically Director-level or above) communicates directly with the customer about an SLA breach. The on-call engineer does not contact the customer. The SRE lead does not contact the customer. Account management is copied, but does not lead the conversation.

Why: Breach communication is a commercial conversation. The person leading it must have authority to commit to credits, remediation timelines, and escalation paths. That authority belongs at VP level.

### The Tone Principles

**Fact-first**: Lead with what happened, not with apologies. Excessive apology language signals uncertainty about the facts or liability exposure.

**Acknowledge the impact**: "We understand this disruption affected [specific customer function]." Acknowledge without overstating or accepting liability for consequential damages.

**Commit to specifics**: Vague remediation commitments ("we'll do better") are worse than no commitment. Every commitment must be specific (what), time-bound (when), and verifiable (how the customer will know it was done).

**Do not speculate**: "We believe the root cause was X but we are still investigating." Until root cause is confirmed, do not speculate in writing.

**Do not minimize**: "This only affected a small percentage of requests" may be technically true and commercially tone-deaf. If the customer's critical workflow was disrupted, the percentage is irrelevant to them.

## Customer Escalation Path During Breach

The escalation path during an active breach (concurrent with an ongoing incident) is distinct from the post-breach commercial escalation.

### During an Active Incident That Is Causing a Breach

The incident escalation chain handles the operational response [cross-reference: incident-management/]. Separately, the VP must activate the SLM escalation path:

1. **Account Manager notified** by VP within 30 minutes of breach declaration
2. **Account Manager contacts Customer Success Manager** at the customer organization
3. **VP or Director sends initial customer communication** (operational level — situation acknowledged, team on it)
4. **VP stands by for executive escalation** — if the customer's executive requests a call, the VP takes it
5. **VP provides regular updates** to the customer (frequency based on incident severity and customer relationship)

The VP cannot delegate the executive-level customer call. If the customer's CIO calls, the VP picks up.

### After a Confirmed Breach (Post-Incident)

1. **Breach notification sent** by VP (or reviewed and approved by VP before sending)
2. **Credit calculation completed** and communicated within 5 business days
3. **Root cause analysis shared** (with customer-appropriate level of detail) within 10 business days
4. **Remediation commitment letter** — formal, VP-signed — within 15 business days [cross-reference: ch07 — customer communication]
5. **Follow-up call** — VP-to-VP or VP-to-executive sponsor — within 30 days to confirm remediation is on track

## Post-Breach Remediation

### The Credit Is Not Remediation

Issuing the credit is the contractual minimum. It is not a remediation. A customer who received a 10% credit and sees the same type of failure recur the following month is a customer who is evaluating whether to stay.

Real remediation is:
- Identifying the root cause of the availability failure that caused the breach
- Demonstrating that the engineering fix addresses the root cause
- Committing to a specific timeline for the fix and delivering it
- Reporting back to the customer that the fix is in place and showing subsequent reliability improvement

### The Service Improvement Commitment Letter

For any breach that involves a major enterprise customer (top 10 revenue, strategic relationship, or contractual requirement), the VP sends a Service Improvement Commitment Letter within 15 business days of breach declaration.

The letter (VP-signed, Legal-reviewed) contains:
- Acknowledgment of the breach and the credit being issued
- Summary of the root cause analysis findings
- Specific engineering improvements committed (not vague — specific changes, specific dates)
- Reporting commitment (the organization will report on improvement metrics in the next monthly service report)
- Escalation path if the customer has concerns about progress

This letter serves three purposes: it demonstrates executive-level accountability, it creates a documented remediation commitment (which can be tracked), and it protects the commercial relationship by showing the organization is taking the breach seriously beyond the minimum contractual response.

### When Breach Triggers a Problem Record

Any breach caused by a repeat failure mode (same root cause as a previous incident), or any breach that reveals a systemic weakness in the architecture, must trigger a formal Problem record [cross-reference: problem-management/].

The Problem record tracks:
- Root cause of the breach
- Engineering investment required for permanent fix
- Timeline for fix delivery
- SLO impact until fix is in place

The VP monitors open Problem records for SLA-backed services monthly. A Problem record that is aging without resolution is a future breach risk and must be escalated to engineering leadership.

## Repeat Breach Consequences

A single breach is an operational failure with commercial consequences. A repeat breach — the same service failing to meet the same SLA commitment in consecutive or near-consecutive months — is a signal of systemic failure and carries escalating commercial and relational risk.

### The Risk Progression

| Breach Frequency | Customer Risk Level | Likely Customer Action |
|-----------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| First breach | Medium | Expects credit, watches for recurrence |
| Second breach (within 6 months) | High | Formal complaint, requests executive meeting, evaluates alternatives |
| Third breach (within 12 months) | Critical | Invokes contract exit rights or demands SLA renegotiation, may escalate to public communications |
| Chronic breach (> 3 per 12 months) | Emergency | Contract termination risk, regulatory complaint (in regulated industries), churn |

### The VP's Response to Repeat Breach Risk

When a second breach is imminent or confirmed for the same service:

1. **Do not wait for the customer to call** — proactive executive contact is far better than reactive damage control
2. **Escalate engineering investment** — a second breach on the same root cause means the first remediation commitment was not executed or did not work. This is an engineering accountability failure that requires VP-level attention, not delegation.
3. **Reassess the SLA commitment** — if the service cannot reliably deliver the committed SLA, the VP must either fix the service or renegotiate the SLA. Continuing to issue credits for ongoing failures is not a sustainable commercial position.
4. **Engage Legal** — repeat breach puts the organization in a weaker negotiating position if the customer invokes exit rights. Legal should review the contract before the third breach conversation.

### Regulatory Exposure in Telecom

At T-Mobile scale, SLA breaches for enterprise customers — particularly in communication services — may have regulatory dimensions. The FCC has record-keeping and reporting requirements for certain service disruption events. The VP must know whether any breach triggers a regulatory notification obligation, and if so, who owns that notification.

In regulated industries (finance, healthcare, telecom), an SLA breach on a critical service is not just a commercial event. It may be a compliance event that requires documentation, incident reporting to regulators, and external audit of the remediation.

---

## Summary: What the VP Owns in Breach Management

> **What the VP Owns**: The VP declares the breach, approves the notification, commits to the credit, leads the customer conversation, signs the remediation letter, and owns the customer relationship until the trust is restored. Every step of the breach process is initiated or approved by the VP. Breach management cannot be delegated — the customer signed an SLA with the organization's executive, and that executive must be present when the commitment is missed.
