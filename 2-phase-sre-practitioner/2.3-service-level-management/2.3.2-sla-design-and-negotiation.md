# Chapter 2: SLA Design and Negotiation

## The VP's Frame: An SLA Is a Commercial Instrument, Not a Technical Document

SRE engineers think about SLAs as reliability targets. That is correct as far as it goes. But the VP must think about SLAs as commercial instruments: they define what the organization has promised, what it owes when it fails, and under what conditions the customer can exit. Getting the SLA design wrong means signing a contract the engineering team cannot meet, or signing one so conservative the organization loses the deal.

This chapter covers what goes into an SLA, how to calculate and communicate availability commitments, the traps that create commercial exposure, and how to negotiate with enterprise customers who know what they want.

---

## What Goes Into an SLA: The Full Anatomy

A well-constructed SLA has eight components. Missing any of them creates either commercial exposure or operational ambiguity.

### Component 1: Service Scope

**What it is**: The precise definition of what is covered by the availability commitment.

**Why it matters**: If the scope is "the notification platform," does that include the management console? The reporting API? The third-party delivery layer? Ambiguous scope means every breach becomes a scope dispute before it becomes a credit discussion.

**Best practice**: Define scope by API endpoint, service tier, or specific customer-facing feature set. Attach an exhibit to the SLA that lists exactly which services are in scope. Update the exhibit when new services are added.

**VP trap**: Sales teams under pressure to close deals expand scope verbally ("of course your management console is covered") without updating the written SLA. Every verbal scope expansion creates a potential breach exposure that engineering has not planned for.

### Component 2: Availability Commitment

**What it is**: The percentage uptime or success rate the organization commits to deliver, over a defined measurement period.

**Standard language**: "Vendor will maintain Service Availability of [X]% during each calendar month."

**The measurement period choice matters**:
- **Calendar month**: Resets on the 1st. A bad week in the middle of the month has maximum impact. Customers prefer this because it aligns with billing cycles.
- **Rolling 30-day window**: Smooths out short degradation periods. Better reflects actual service health. Engineers prefer this.
- **Annual**: Rarely used in enterprise SLAs — too much can hide in a year.

Most enterprise SLAs use calendar month. Accept this, and make sure the SLO uses a rolling window so engineering has visibility into approaching calendar month risk.

### Component 3: Availability Definition

**What it is**: Precisely how "availability" is calculated for this specific service.

This is where most SLAs have fatal ambiguity. "Availability" without definition is not a commitment — it is a debate trigger.

**Required elements**:
- Measurement method (synthetic probe? Real user monitoring? Server-side success rate?)
- Measurement vantage point (from the customer's location? From the vendor's infrastructure edge?)
- What constitutes a "failure" (HTTP 5xx? Any response > 3 seconds? Message not delivered within SLA window?)
- How partial failures are treated (is 50% of requests failing a 50% availability event, or a 0% availability event?)

**Example of a well-defined availability clause**:
> "Service Availability is calculated as: (Total Minutes in Calendar Month − Unavailable Minutes) / Total Minutes in Calendar Month × 100. Unavailable Minutes are those during which > 5% of synthetic monitoring probes to the primary API endpoint return HTTP 5xx responses or timeout after 30 seconds. Probes are executed from [monitoring vendor] at 1-minute intervals."

**VP checkpoint**: Before signing, verify that the availability definition in the SLA matches what the SLI actually measures. If they don't match, the SLA is measuring something the engineering team cannot see.

### Component 4: Exclusion Windows

**What it is**: Categories of time or events that are explicitly excluded from availability calculations. During excluded periods, downtime does not count against the SLA commitment.

**Standard exclusions**:
- Scheduled maintenance windows (typically communicated 72+ hours in advance)
- Force majeure events (natural disasters, acts of government, large-scale internet infrastructure failures)
- Customer-caused failures (customer misconfigured their integration, customer exceeded agreed rate limits)
- Third-party failures outside vendor control (if customer chose a specific CDN or DNS provider that failed)
- Beta or preview feature failures (explicitly listed features not yet GA)

**Negotiation principle**: Customers will push to minimize exclusions. The VP's position: "We can commit to 99.9% availability for everything within our operational control. We cannot commit to availability of internet infrastructure we do not own, or to maintain SLA compliance during windows when we are performing agreed maintenance." This is reasonable and defensible.

**VP trap**: Agreeing to "no scheduled maintenance windows" to close a deal. This means every maintenance window is potential SLA exposure. Instead, negotiate a defined monthly maintenance allowance (e.g., "up to 4 hours per month, communicated 72 hours in advance, excluded from availability calculation").

### Component 5: Credit Schedule

**What it is**: The compensation structure the organization pays when availability falls below the committed threshold.

**Standard tiered credit structure**:

| Availability Achieved | Credit as % of Monthly Service Fee |
|-----------------------|-------------------------------------|
| < 99.9% but ≥ 99.0% | 10% |
| < 99.0% but ≥ 95.0% | 25% |
| < 95.0% | 50% |

**Key terms to define**:
- Credit is applied to the next invoice, not paid as cash
- Credit is the customer's sole remedy (limits legal exposure — without this, customers can claim consequential damages)
- Credit cap: total credits in any month cannot exceed 100% (or 50%) of the monthly service fee
- Credit request window: customer must request credit within X days of month close (prevents retroactive claims for months-old data)

**What the VP negotiates**:
- Cap the credit as sole remedy — this is the most important protection
- Set the bottom tier threshold conservatively (99.0% not 99.5%) so normal operational variance doesn't trigger the first tier
- Negotiate a credit request window — customers who don't track their SLA compliance shouldn't be able to claim credits a year later

### Component 6: Escalation Rights

**What it is**: The contractual rights the customer gains when a breach occurs or when a persistent reliability problem is not remediated.

**Standard escalation rights**:
- Right to receive a remediation plan within X business days of a breach
- Right to executive escalation (VP-to-VP call) if breach recurs within 90 days
- Right to audit vendor's reliability data if breach is disputed

**What to avoid**:
- Right to terminate for convenience without cause triggered by a single minor breach
- Uncapped SLA credit liability for extended outages
- Right to demand architectural changes or specific technology choices as remediation

### Component 7: Reporting Commitment

**What it is**: The vendor's commitment to provide regular availability data to the customer.

**Standard reporting commitment**: "Vendor will provide a monthly Service Report by the 10th business day of the following month, including actual availability, incident count, breach status, and credit calculation if applicable."

**Portal access**: Many enterprise SLAs now include a commitment to customer-accessible dashboards showing real-time availability data. If you commit to this, the engineering infrastructure must support it before the SLA is signed.

### Component 8: Breach Notification

**What it is**: The maximum time between a breach event occurring and the vendor notifying the customer.

**Standard**: 2–4 hours from breach declaration for Tier 1 services; 24 hours for Tier 2.

**The 4-hour window reality**: If a breach is declared at 2am, the VP-level notification to the customer may go out at 6am when the executive escalation path can be activated. The initial notification can be operational-level. The VP-level follow-up must happen within business hours. Both must be documented.

## Availability Math: Translating Percentages Into Business Language

Enterprise customers do not think in percentages. They think in hours, minutes, and business impact. The VP must be fluent in both.

### The Nines Table

| Availability | Downtime/Year | Downtime/Month | Downtime/Week |
|-------------|--------------|----------------|---------------|
| 99.0% | 87.6 hours | 7.3 hours | 1.68 hours |
| 99.5% | 43.8 hours | 3.65 hours | 50.4 minutes |
| 99.9% | 8.76 hours | 43.8 minutes | 10.1 minutes |
| 99.95% | 4.38 hours | 21.9 minutes | 5.04 minutes |
| 99.99% | 52.6 minutes | 4.38 minutes | 1.01 minutes |
| 99.999% | 5.26 minutes | 26.3 seconds | 6.05 seconds |

See [availability-math-reference.md] for the complete compound availability table and calculation methodology.

### The Distribution Problem (More Important Than the Total)

A customer who experiences 43 minutes of downtime spread as 43 separate 1-minute interruptions has a very different experience than a customer who experiences a single 43-minute outage. The total availability calculation is identical — 99.9% — but the customer impact is completely different.

**The VP must address distribution in the SLA negotiation**:
- Define a "contiguous outage" threshold (e.g., any contiguous period > 15 minutes during business hours triggers escalation notification regardless of monthly availability percentage)
- Consider business-hours weighting: 43 minutes of downtime at 3am is different from 43 minutes at 10am during the customer's peak transaction window

**The shift from "total downtime" to "longest contiguous outage" is a more honest SLA metric** and one that sophisticated enterprise customers are beginning to demand. T-Mobile enterprise customers with critical notification dependencies care about whether they had 10 uninterrupted minutes of delivery failure, not whether it averaged out to 99.9% for the month.

## Common SLA Traps

These traps create commercial exposure without improving service quality. The VP must recognize and avoid them.

### Trap 1: Unmeasurable Commitments

"The platform will be highly available" is not a commitment — it is a marketing statement. SLA language that cannot be measured from existing telemetry is either unmeasurable (the organization can't defend it) or measured by the customer (the organization can't control the measurement).

**Rule**: Never commit to a metric you cannot measure from your own systems in real time.

### Trap 2: Undefined "Availability"

"99.9% availability" without defining what "availability" means is a breach waiting to happen. Customer definition: "I couldn't log in." Vendor definition: "The API returned 200 OK." Both are correct. Neither is useful.

### Trap 3: Missing Exclusion Windows

Signing an SLA with no scheduled maintenance exclusion creates a situation where every planned maintenance activity carries SLA risk. The team must either take no maintenance or absorb the credit risk. Neither is operationally sustainable.

### Trap 4: Credit as the Only Remedy (Without the Cap)

Including "credits are the customer's sole remedy" protects the organization from consequential damage claims. But if you include this language without a credit cap, a prolonged outage (30% of the month down) could result in 100% credit month after month. Cap credits at 30–50% of monthly service fee and specify that a single month's credits cannot exceed that cap.

### Trap 5: Committing to Future Architecture

"The service will be architected with N+1 redundancy across three availability zones" is an architectural commitment embedded in a legal document. When the architecture changes (and it will), the organization may be in technical breach of contract. Keep SLAs outcome-focused (availability percentage, latency target) and architecture-agnostic.

### Trap 6: Perpetual SLAs Without Review Triggers

An SLA signed in 2021 covering a service that has been completely re-architected, moved to a new cloud region, and had its dependency graph doubled is a liability. Build mid-term review triggers into every SLA: "Either party may request an SLA review if the service architecture changes materially or if actual availability consistently differs from the committed threshold by more than 0.5% for three consecutive months."

## Tiered SLA Design: Gold, Silver, Bronze

Not all customers require the same commitment level, and not all commitments require the same engineering investment. Tiered SLA design allows the organization to offer differentiated service levels at differentiated price points.

### Typical Tier Structure

| Tier | Availability Commitment | Support Response | Reporting | Price Premium |
|------|------------------------|-----------------|-----------|---------------|
| **Gold** | 99.99% | 15-min P1 response, 24/7 | Real-time portal + monthly report | Highest |
| **Silver** | 99.9% | 1-hour P1 response, 24/7 | Monthly report | Standard |
| **Bronze** | 99.5% | 4-hour P1 response, business hours | Quarterly summary | Discounted |

### VP Considerations for Tiered Design

**Engineering cost of each tier**: The difference between 99.9% and 99.99% is not a small engineering increment. 99.99% requires active-active multi-region architecture, zero-downtime deployments, and automated failover tested quarterly. The VP must know whether the organization can operationally sustain Gold tier before offering it.

**Tier assignment governance**: Who decides which customers are on which tier? If Sales can move customers to Gold to close a deal, the organization may be committing to engineering capabilities that don't exist. The VP must own tier assignment approval.

**Tier reviews**: Customers on a lower tier who experience better service than their SLA commits may request upgrade. Customers on a higher tier who experience chronic issues may demand credit and downgrade. Build annual tier review into the SLA lifecycle.

## Internal vs. External SLAs

### External SLAs (with Paying Customers)

Covered above. Legally binding, commercially consequential, VP-signed.

### Internal SLAs (with Business Units)

Many organizations have internal "customers" — business units that consume shared platform services. IT Operations commits to 99.9% availability of the internal HR system to the People organization. Finance commits to data pipeline freshness SLAs to the CFO organization.

**Critical difference from external SLAs**: Internal SLAs have no credit mechanism and no exit rights. The "consequence" of breach is internal escalation, resource reallocation, or leadership attention — not a credit invoice.

**VP consideration**: Internal SLAs are valuable for creating internal accountability. But they can create false confidence if the internal SLA commitment exceeds what the team can actually deliver. Internal SLA governance must be as rigorous as external — with the same SLO buffer, the same measurement discipline, and the same breach review process.

## SLA Review Cadence

### Annual Renewal

Every SLA should have a defined term (typically 12 months, aligned with the commercial contract) and a renewal process. The renewal is an opportunity to:
- Adjust the availability commitment based on trailing performance and new architecture
- Update the exclusion list to reflect new maintenance patterns
- Revise the credit schedule if the original terms have become commercially unworkable
- Add new services that have come online since the last signing

The VP owns the renewal process. Legal owns the documentation. Sales owns the commercial negotiation. Engineering provides the performance data that anchors the discussion.

### Mid-Term Adjustment Triggers

Do not wait for annual renewal when:
- Actual availability has consistently exceeded the SLA commitment by > 1% for 6 consecutive months — there is pricing upside in offering a tighter SLA at a premium
- A significant incident caused a breach and the customer is requesting SLA revision as part of remediation
- A service has been migrated to new infrastructure and the old SLA commitment is no longer valid
- A regulatory change creates new reporting or performance requirements

## Negotiating SLAs with Enterprise Customers

### Anchoring

The vendor's first offer is the anchor. Anchor high on commitments (99.95%, not 99.9%) if the trailing data supports it — this gives room to negotiate down to 99.9% while appearing responsive. Anchor low on credits (10% for first tier) and negotiate up to 15% if needed. Never anchor where you would be uncomfortable landing.

### The Three-Concession Rule

Prepare three concessions in advance and know their relative value:
1. **Low value**: Increase reporting frequency (monthly → bi-weekly) — costs the team time but no commercial liability
2. **Medium value**: Add a business-hours weighting clause — limits exposure to off-hours incidents
3. **High value**: Increase credit percentage by 5% — accept this only in exchange for a higher contract value or extended term

Trade concessions sequentially, starting with low value. The customer who gets a concession on every ask has no idea what the vendor actually values. Selective concession reveals priority.

### Carve-Outs and Protections

The VP's non-negotiables:
- Credit cap (total credits ≤ 50% of monthly fee)
- Credits as sole remedy
- Exclusion for customer-caused failures
- Exclusion for force majeure
- Vendor's right to terminate if customer's usage materially exceeds contracted volume

Everything else is negotiable. Know which terms the sales team can trade and which are reserved for the VP.

---

## Summary: What the VP Owns

> **What the VP Owns**: The VP approves every SLA before it is signed. This means reviewing the availability commitment against trailing SLO performance, verifying the credit schedule doesn't create unlimited liability, confirming the exclusion list is operationally realistic, and signing the document with full understanding of the breach consequences. No SLA is signed by anyone below VP without VP review and approval — because no one below VP will be in the room when the breach conversation happens.
