# Chapter 1: The Three-Layer Model — SLI, SLO, SLA, OLA, and UC

## The Architecture in One Sentence

SLM is a three-layer stack: the SLI is the measurement instrument, the SLO is the internal engineering contract, and the SLA is the external business promise — each layer exists to serve a different stakeholder and carry a different consequence when violated.

---

## The Analogy Before the Theory

Think of a T-Mobile cell tower serving enterprise customers on a guaranteed-uptime contract.

The **SLI** is the signal strength measurement at the antenna — raw, continuous, technical. It is a number. It does not care about business impact.

The **SLO** is the internal engineering target: the RF engineering team commits to maintaining signal strength above a threshold 99.5% of the time. This is their professional standard. It is not in any customer contract. It is the engineering team's self-imposed discipline.

The **SLA** is the enterprise customer contract: "Your LTE service will be available 99.9% of each calendar month. If we miss that, you receive a credit equivalent to one day's service fee." That sentence is legally binding. A lawyer reviewed it. The customer signed it. A VP signed it.

The gap between SLO (99.5%) and SLA (99.9%) is intentional. It exists because the RF engineering team cannot commit to a number they've never seen the system achieve, and the business cannot commit to a number that no engineering team has validated. The gap is the negotiation space — and the VP's job is to govern it.

## Layer 1: The SLI — What the Signal Is

### Definition

A Service Level Indicator is a quantitative measurement of a specific service behavior. It is always expressed as a ratio, rate, or latency number derived from actual service telemetry.

### Properties of a Valid SLI

An SLI is not useful unless it meets four criteria:

**1. Measurable continuously**: The signal must be available in real time, not reconstructed from logs after the fact. If you can only calculate it after the month closes, it cannot drive operational decisions.

**2. Attributable**: The measurement must be clearly tied to one service or component. An SLI that blends multiple services obscures accountability.

**3. Customer-correlated**: The signal must reflect something the customer actually experiences. CPU utilization is not an SLI — it is a resource metric. Request success rate is an SLI. Latency at the 95th percentile is an SLI.

**4. Consistent measurement**: The SLI must be measured the same way every time, from the same measurement point, against the same denominator. If measurement methodology shifts, SLO trend data becomes meaningless.

### Common SLI Types

| SLI Type | Example | Measurement Point |
|----------|---------|------------------|
| Availability | % of HTTP requests returning 2xx or 3xx | Load balancer / API gateway |
| Latency | % of requests completing in < 200ms | Application telemetry |
| Throughput | Messages processed per second vs. capacity | Queue consumer metrics |
| Error rate | % of transactions resulting in application errors | Application logs |
| Freshness | Age of most recent data record (for data pipelines) | Pipeline output timestamp |
| Durability | % of write operations successfully persisted | Storage system metrics |

### The VP's Relationship to SLIs

The VP does not define individual SLIs — that is the service owner's job, with SRE guidance. The VP asks two questions about SLIs:

1. "Is every SLA-committed service being measured by an SLI that genuinely reflects customer experience?" If not, the SLA commitment is unmeasurable and legally precarious.
2. "Who is responsible for maintaining the SLI measurement infrastructure?" If the measurement system fails, the SLA can neither be enforced nor defended.

## Layer 2: The SLO — The Engineering Target

### Definition

A Service Level Objective is an internal engineering target expressed as a threshold on an SLI over a defined time window. It is not customer-facing. It carries no commercial consequences. It is the professional standard the engineering team sets for itself — and commits to maintaining.

### SLO Structure

A complete SLO has four components:

```
[SLI type] [comparison] [threshold] over [time window]
```

Example: "HTTP request success rate ≥ 99.5% over a rolling 30-day window"

- **SLI type**: what is being measured (request success rate)
- **Comparison**: the direction of the commitment (≥, ≤)
- **Threshold**: the target value (99.5%)
- **Time window**: the period over which compliance is evaluated (rolling 30-day)

### Why the Time Window Matters

A 30-day rolling window and a calendar month window produce different outcomes from the same data. Rolling windows are smoother — a bad week at the end of the month does not catastrophically reset the calculation. Calendar month windows create sharp resets on the first of each month and concentrate commercial risk in specific windows.

Most SLOs use rolling windows because they better reflect actual service behavior. Most SLAs use calendar month windows because customers and finance teams think in billing cycles. The VP must understand both and manage the gap between them.

### SLO Is Internal — and That Is the Point

SLOs are internal because:

- They must be set conservatively enough that the engineering team can actually achieve them
- They must have room to be improved without renegotiating customer contracts
- They must allow engineering to experiment without every feature flag being a commercial liability
- They create the buffer that protects the SLA from normal operational variance

When a VP hears "our SLO is 99.5% but our SLA is 99.9%," the correct response is not "good, we have margin." The correct response is: "What is our actual trailing performance? Are we consistently at 99.7-99.8%, or are we hovering at 99.5% and one bad week away from an SLA breach?"

### What the VP Owns in SLO Governance

- **Approval authority**: SLO changes for Tier 1 (SLA-backed) services require VP sign-off. Teams cannot unilaterally raise or lower an SLO for a service backed by a customer SLA.
- **Review cadence**: VP chairs or participates in quarterly SLO reviews [cross-reference: slo-review-playbook.md]
- **Escalation path**: When a team's engineering decisions threaten SLO compliance, the escalation path ends at the VP, not the engineering manager
- **Accountability for gaps**: When a service has no SLO, the VP is accountable — not for writing the SLO, but for ensuring one exists before the service enters production

## Layer 3: The SLA — The Business Commitment

### Definition

A Service Level Agreement is a contractual commitment made to a customer specifying the performance standard the vendor agrees to maintain, the method by which compliance will be measured, and the consequences if the commitment is not met. It is customer-facing, legally binding, and carries commercial consequences.

### What Distinguishes an SLA from an SLO

| Dimension | SLO | SLA |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| Audience | Engineering team | Customer / Legal |
| Binding | No | Yes — contract |
| Consequences | Engineering accountability | Credits, contract rights |
| Review cadence | Quarterly (operational) | Annual (contract renewal) |
| Language | Technical (%, ms, events) | Business + legal |
| Who sets it | SRE + Service Owner | VP + Legal + Sales |
| Who signs it | Nobody — it's internal | VP (or above) + Customer |

### SLA Components

A complete SLA contains:

1. **Service scope**: Exactly what service, feature set, or API is covered. Vague service scope is the most common source of breach disputes.
2. **Availability commitment**: The percentage target and the time window (e.g., "99.9% availability per calendar month").
3. **Measurement methodology**: How availability is measured, from which vantage point, using which tool or system of record.
4. **Exclusion windows**: Scheduled maintenance, force majeure events, customer-caused failures. Exclusions are your legal protection — never negotiate them away entirely.
5. **Credit schedule**: What the customer receives if the commitment is missed (typically a percentage of monthly service fee).
6. **Escalation rights**: What the customer can demand if breach is not remediated (e.g., right to demand a remediation plan, right to terminate with notice).
7. **Reporting commitment**: How and when the vendor will report actual availability to the customer (monthly service report, portal access, etc.).
8. **Breach notification**: The maximum time between a breach event and customer notification (typically 4–24 hours).

### The VP's Signature

The VP signs the SLA. This is not a formality — it is a commercial commitment of the organization's resources and reputation. Before signing:

- Confirm the SLO buffer: the SLA commitment must be achievable given current SLO performance and trend
- Confirm the measurement system: if the SLI infrastructure fails, the SLA cannot be measured or defended
- Confirm legal review of credit schedule and termination rights
- Confirm the breach playbook exists and the team knows how to execute it [cross-reference: sla-breach-playbook.md]

> **What the VP Owns**: The VP is the only person in the organization who can commit the organization to an SLA. This authority carries matching accountability: when the SLA is breached, the VP owns the customer conversation, the credit decision, and the remediation commitment.

## The Fourth Layer: OLA — Internal Accountability

### Definition

An Operational Level Agreement is an internal agreement between two teams or departments within the organization that supports the delivery of an externally committed SLA. It is not a customer-facing document. It is the engineering accountability chain inside the organization.

### OLA Example

If the application team has an external SLA of 99.9% availability, they need the infrastructure team, the database team, and the networking team to each maintain their components reliably enough to support that target. The OLA with the database team might read: "The database team commits to 99.95% availability of the production database cluster, measured monthly." This OLA is tighter than the application SLA to provide headroom.

### OLA Hierarchy

```
[Customer SLA: 99.9%]
        ↓
[Application Team SLO: 99.95%]
        ↓  (supported by)
[Database OLA: 99.97%]  [Network OLA: 99.99%]  [Infrastructure OLA: 99.98%]
```

### Why OLAs Matter to the VP

When an SLA is breached because the database team was unavailable, the OLA is the document that defines whether the breach was caused by an internal accountability failure and who carries the remediation responsibility. Without OLAs, internal breach attribution becomes political. With OLAs, it is mechanical: compare actual performance against the OLA commitment and determine accountability.

The VP sets the OLA framework and ensures that every external SLA is backed by a documented chain of internal OLAs. This is not optional — it is the organizational architecture that makes accountability work.

## The Fifth Element: UC — Vendor Commitments That Underpin Yours

### Definition

An Underpinning Contract is a commercial agreement with an external supplier or vendor whose service availability is a dependency for the organization's own SLA commitments. The UC defines what the vendor has committed to provide, and that commitment is what the organization's SLA is built on.

### UC Examples in Telecom/Cloud Context

| Vendor | UC Commitment | Underpins |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| AWS (S3) | 99.99% availability | Object storage SLA |
| AWS (EKS) | 99.95% API server uptime | Kubernetes-hosted application SLA |
| Twilio | 99.95% SMS delivery | Notification service SLA |
| Equinix | 99.9999% power uptime | Co-location SLA |

### The UC Gap Problem

If AWS commits to 99.95% EKS availability and the organization commits to customers at 99.9%, the organization appears safe — the vendor commitment is higher than the customer commitment. But if the application layer adds its own failure modes, the compound availability can fall below 99.9% even when AWS is fully available.

**Compound availability formula**: If Service A depends on Services B and C:
```
Compound Availability = Availability(B) × Availability(C)
```

Example: Database at 99.95% × Application at 99.97% = 99.92% compound — above the 99.9% SLA target. Add a third dependency at 99.95%: 99.92% × 99.95% = 99.87% — below the SLA target.

The VP must know the compound availability of every SLA-backed service. See [availability-math-reference.md] for the compound calculation table.

### The VP's Job on UCs

- Ensure every UC is reviewed annually alongside the SLA it underpins
- Verify that UC commitments exceed (not just match) the downstream SLA commitment
- When a vendor drops their UC commitment at renewal, immediately assess which customer SLAs are now exposed
- Include UC failure as an explicit exclusion or carve-out in the customer SLA (if the failure is entirely the vendor's fault and outside the organization's control)

## The Gap Between SLO and SLA: The Engineering Risk Budget

### Why the Gap Is Intentional

The SLO (internal target) is always tighter than the SLA (external commitment) — or it should be. The gap between them is the engineering risk budget: the space the team has to absorb normal operational variance, scheduled maintenance, and deployment risk without triggering a customer credit.

If SLO = 99.9% and SLA = 99.9%, there is no buffer. Every incident that moves actual availability below 99.9% simultaneously violates both the internal standard and the customer contract. The engineering team has no room to recover.

A healthy gap looks like this:

```
SLA Commitment:   99.9%   ← what the customer was promised
                    ↑
                  0.05%    ← gap = engineering risk buffer
                    ↑
SLO Target:       99.95%  ← internal engineering standard
                    ↑
                  0.04%    ← additional buffer to SLO
                    ↑
Actual (trailing):  99.99% ← where the system actually runs
```

### The VP's Job: Govern the Gap

The gap is not a static artifact — it must be managed. Specifically:

**Widen the gap when**:
- Actual performance is consistently at or near the SLO target (dangerous — no headroom)
- A major architectural change is planned that will increase deployment risk
- On-call burn is high and team capacity to respond to incidents is reduced

**Narrow the gap when**:
- The customer is demanding a higher SLA commitment and the trailing performance justifies it
- The business is offering a premium SLA tier that requires tighter commitments
- Actual performance has been consistently excellent and the gap is creating commercial opportunity

**Redefine both when**:
- The service architecture has fundamentally changed and historical performance is not predictive
- A significant dependency has changed (new vendor, new cloud region, new database technology)
- Post a major incident that demonstrated the SLO was not achievable in adverse conditions

## The Complete Accountability Map

| Layer | Document | Audience | Consequences of Violation | Signed By |
|-------|----------|----------|--------------------------|-----------|
| SLI | Telemetry dashboard | SRE / Operations | Operational alert | N/A |
| SLO | SLO definition doc | Service Owner / VP | Engineering accountability, error budget burn | Service Owner |
| OLA | Internal agreement | Teams / Dept heads | Internal accountability, escalation | Team leads / Directors |
| UC | Vendor contract | Legal / VP | Vendor SLA credit, sourcing risk | VP / Legal |
| SLA | Customer contract | Customer / Legal | Commercial credit, contract rights | VP + Customer |

---

## Summary: What the VP Owns in This Model

The VP's role in the three-layer model is architectural and commercial:

1. **Set the gap policy**: Define how much buffer must exist between SLO and SLA for each service tier. Enforce it.
2. **Approve SLA language**: No external SLA is signed without VP review of the availability commitment, exclusions, and credit schedule.
3. **Own breach accountability**: When the SLA is breached, the VP is the organization's face to the customer.
4. **Govern the OLA chain**: Ensure every external SLA has a documented chain of internal OLAs that allocates accountability across teams.
5. **Track UC alignment**: Review UC commitments annually and flag when vendor degradation creates downstream SLA exposure.

The engineer asks: "Is the service meeting its SLO?" The VP asks: "Is the SLO meeting its SLA, is the SLA defensible, and do I know what happens commercially if it isn't?"
