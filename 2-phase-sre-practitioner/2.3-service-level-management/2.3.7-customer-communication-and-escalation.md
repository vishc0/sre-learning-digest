# Chapter 7: Customer Communication and Escalation

## The Communication Discipline

Every word written or spoken to a customer during or after a service disruption is either building trust or eroding it. There is no neutral communication. A delayed notification erodes trust — the customer assumes the organization was trying to hide the problem. An overly technical explanation erodes trust — the customer feels talked at rather than informed. Vague commitments erode trust — the customer learns to assume "we'll fix it" means nothing will change.

The VP's communication discipline is built on three principles:

**Principle 1: Proactive beats reactive by a factor of 10.** A customer who receives an unprompted breach notification from the VP trusts the organization. A customer who discovers a breach from their own data analysis and has to call to complain is evaluating whether to exit the contract.

**Principle 2: Accurate beats reassuring.** If the situation is bad, saying it is bad — with a plan — is more credible than minimizing. Customers are sophisticated enough to know when they are being managed rather than informed.

**Principle 3: Every communication is a documented record.** In regulated industries and enterprise contracts, every written customer communication can become an exhibit in a contract dispute. Write accordingly.

## The Communication Hierarchy

Not every service event requires the same type or level of customer communication. The hierarchy has three tiers:

### Tier 1: Technical Update

**Audience**: Customer technical team / operations contacts
**Channel**: Email or customer portal
**Timing**: During or immediately following an incident
**Tone**: Operational, factual, specific

Content:
- What is happening or what happened
- Approximate duration / current status
- What the engineering team is doing
- Next update expected at [specific time]

**Rule**: Technical updates are sent before the customer asks. If the customer has to call to find out what's happening, the first update was already late.

### Tier 2: Operational Update

**Audience**: Customer operations leadership (Director level at the customer)
**Channel**: Email from VP of Operations or Director
**Timing**: Within 2 hours of incident declaration for SLA-impacting events; within 24 hours for post-resolution wrap-up
**Tone**: Professional, accountable, action-oriented

Content:
- Executive summary of what happened
- Customer impact (factual, not minimized)
- Current status and timeline to resolution/confirmed resolution
- Credit and remediation commitment if applicable
- Named point of contact for questions

**Rule**: Operational updates go from the vendor's Director/VP to the customer's equivalent. Sending an operational update to a customer executive from a technical level employee signals that the vendor is not taking the situation seriously at the right level.

### Tier 3: Executive Briefing

**Audience**: Customer C-suite (CIO, COO, VP of Operations)
**Channel**: Phone call (VP to VP or VP to C-suite), followed by written summary
**Timing**: For SEV1 events, upon resolution; for SLA breaches, within 48 hours of breach declaration
**Tone**: Strategic, relationship-first, accountability without defensiveness

Content:
- Personal acknowledgment of the impact on the customer's business
- Brief summary of what happened and why
- What has changed to prevent recurrence
- Personal commitment to follow through on remediation
- Invitation for the customer executive to share their concerns directly

**Rule**: The executive briefing is a relationship conversation, not a report delivery. The VP should speak for 30% of the time and listen for 70%. The objective is to understand the customer's experience and concerns, not to deliver information.

## Status Page Governance

The status page is the organization's public-facing real-time communication of service health. For enterprise SLAs, the status page is often referenced in the agreement as the official channel for incident communication.

### Who Updates the Status Page

Status page updates must be governed, not ad-hoc. The governance model:

- **On-call engineer**: triggers the initial incident status (operational, within 5 minutes of detection)
- **Incident Commander**: approves and initiates the first customer-visible update (within 15 minutes for P1)
- **VP or Director delegate**: approves updates during SLA-impacting events
- **VP**: reviews and approves the final "Resolved" update for any event that caused SLA-level impact

Why VP approval for the resolution update? Because the resolution update is the de facto start of the breach notification and credit calculation window. The timestamp of the resolution update is used to calculate total downtime. The VP should confirm that the resolution is genuine and that the credit calculation team has been activated before the resolution update is posted.

### Status Page Language Standards

| Stage | Language Standard | Example |
|-------|------------------|---------|
| Detecting | Vague is acceptable — do not speculate | "We are investigating reports of degraded performance affecting [Service]." |
| Identified | Factual, non-technical | "We have identified an issue affecting [Service] causing [customer-observable symptom]. Our team is working to resolve." |
| Monitoring | Signals improving, not resolved | "We have applied a fix and are monitoring the service for stability. Customer impact should be [improving/resolved]. We will confirm full resolution." |
| Resolved | Precise timestamp and brief cause | "[Service] has fully restored as of [timestamp]. This issue was caused by [brief, non-technical cause]. We will publish a full incident summary by [date]." |

**Never write**:
- "We apologize for any inconvenience" — generic, signals template-speak, not genuine engagement
- "Minor degradation" — if it was minor, the customer would not be checking the status page
- "We are aware of the issue" — this is what you write when you have nothing else to say; it communicates nothing useful
- Technical jargon that customers cannot interpret ("database failover triggered by a pod eviction in the eu-west-1 EKS cluster")

### Update Frequency Commitments

If the SLA specifies update frequency commitments ("vendor will provide status updates every 30 minutes during active incidents"), the status page governance must enforce it. A missed update window during a breach is a secondary breach of the SLA's communication commitment.

Standard update cadence for SLA-impacting events:
- First update: within 15 minutes of detection
- Subsequent updates: every 30 minutes until "Monitoring" or "Resolved"
- Final resolution update: within 30 minutes of confirmed resolution

## Customer-Facing vs. Internal Communication

The same incident generates two communication streams that must be managed separately and must not contradict each other.

### The Content Divergence

| Dimension | Internal Communication | Customer-Facing Communication |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Root cause | Full technical detail, hypothesis chain | Brief, non-technical summary after confirmation |
| Impact scope | Specific affected systems, blast radius | Affected customer features and workflows |
| Timeline | Minute-by-minute action log | Key milestones only |
| Speculation | Acceptable in incident channel | Never — only confirmed facts |
| Escalation | IC, engineering management chain | Customer Success, Account Manager, VP |
| Tone | Technical, action-oriented | Professional, accountable, customer-perspective |
| Cadence | Continuous (every action logged) | Scheduled (every 30 minutes) |

### The Consistency Requirement

The technical facts must be consistent. If the internal timeline says the incident started at 14:35 and lasted 47 minutes, the customer communication must reflect the same start time and duration. Discrepancies between internal timelines and customer-facing statements are discovered — by customers who receive both, by regulators in audits, or by legal during contract disputes — and they destroy credibility far more than the original incident did.

Before any written communication goes to the customer, the VP confirms consistency with the internal incident record.

## Proactive vs. Reactive Communication

### The Proactive Window

Reactive communication happens after the customer notices the problem. Proactive communication happens before — or at the moment of — impact.

The proactive window for a customer communication is:
- The moment a P1 incident is declared that affects an SLA-backed service
- The moment error budget burn reaches 90% for an SLA-backed service (breach is imminent — customer should know)
- The moment a planned maintenance window is confirmed that will affect the customer's service
- The moment the engineering team discovers a vulnerability or upcoming reliability risk that will require customer preparation

Most organizations communicate reactively. They wait until the customer calls. At T-Mobile enterprise scale, this is commercially dangerous. An enterprise customer with their customer notifications dependent on the T-Mobile notification platform who discovers they were having a degraded experience because they checked their metrics — rather than receiving a proactive notification from the vendor — is a customer writing a contract exit plan.

The standard: **communicate before the customer discovers it from their own monitoring**.

### Managing Customer Perception During Prolonged Degradation

Prolonged degradation — a service that is "mostly working" but clearly impaired — is often worse for the customer relationship than a complete outage. During a complete outage, the customer knows the service is down and can plan around it. During prolonged degradation:
- They cannot determine whether to wait or route around the problem
- Their users are experiencing random failures without explanation
- The customer has no clear "when will this be fixed" answer to give internally
- Every passing hour without resolution increases frustration

Communication strategy for prolonged degradation:
1. **Acknowledge immediately and specifically**: "We are experiencing intermittent failures affecting [X]% of [notification deliveries]. The impact is not uniform — [specific symptom customers are seeing]."
2. **Set a realistic resolution timeline and revise it if wrong**: If you said "resolved by 3pm" and it is 3pm and you are not resolved, say so immediately and give a new estimate.
3. **Provide a workaround if one exists**: Even a partial workaround demonstrates that the organization is thinking about the customer's needs, not just the technical fix.
4. **Escalate the communication tier as duration extends**: A 15-minute degradation can be handled by technical updates. A 4-hour degradation requires VP-level communication.

## Executive-to-Executive Escalation

### When the VP Makes the Call

The VP initiates an executive-to-executive call (VP to the customer's VP or CIO) when:
- An SLA breach has occurred and the customer has not acknowledged the breach notification within 24 hours
- The customer's leadership has expressed dissatisfaction through the account manager
- A second SLA breach has occurred within 90 days
- The customer has invoked contract escalation rights
- The account manager indicates the customer is evaluating alternative vendors

This call is not optional. The VP does not delegate it. The customer signed an SLA with the organization's executive and expects to hear from that executive when things go wrong.

### Preparing for the Executive Escalation Call

**Know before calling**:
- Full incident and breach history for this customer's account (last 12 months)
- Current open remediation commitments and status of each
- Contract renewal timeline and any active renewal negotiations
- Account revenue and strategic significance
- The customer executive's known priorities and pain points

**The call structure**:
1. Personal acknowledgment (30 seconds): "I wanted to call you directly because I know how important this service is to your team."
2. Status update (2 minutes): What happened, where things stand now, what is being done
3. Accountability (1 minute): Own the failure without excessive apology — "This did not meet the standard we committed to and you deserve better"
4. Forward commitment (2 minutes): Specific actions with dates, how the customer will be informed of progress
5. Customer's turn (as long as needed): "I want to make sure I understand how this has affected your team. Can you tell me what you've been experiencing?"
6. Close with clear next steps and your direct contact information

**What not to do**:
- Do not start with excuses or technical context before the acknowledgment
- Do not promise outcomes you cannot guarantee ("this will never happen again")
- Do not minimize the customer's experience ("it was only 47 minutes")
- Do not pass the call to a subordinate mid-call without warning

### Agenda Documentation

After every executive escalation call, a call summary is sent to the customer within 24 hours:
- Date and participants
- Issues discussed
- Commitments made by the vendor (with owner name and due date)
- Commitments made by the customer (if any)
- Next scheduled contact

This summary protects both parties: it creates a record of what was agreed, prevents "I thought you said..." disputes, and demonstrates follow-through when the VP delivers on the commitments.

## The 5-Business-Day Post-Incident Customer Communication

For any P1 incident affecting an SLA-backed service, the organization sends a formal post-incident communication to the customer within 5 business days of resolution. This is distinct from:
- The initial breach notification (sent within hours)
- The monthly service report (sent at month-end)
- The executive briefing call (done verbally)

The 5-day communication is the written follow-up that confirms:
- Full timeline of the incident (when it was detected, when the fix was applied, when the service was confirmed restored)
- Root cause finding (confirmed, not preliminary)
- Actions taken during the incident
- Remediation steps and their expected completion dates
- Credit amount and how it will appear on the invoice

This document is VP-reviewed and often VP-signed for major enterprise accounts. It serves as the organization's official post-incident record for this customer's account.

---

## Summary: What the VP Owns in Customer Communication

> **What the VP Owns**: The VP owns the voice of the organization to enterprise customers during service disruptions. This means: approving all written breach notifications before they go out, personally leading executive escalation calls, reviewing the post-incident customer communication before it is sent, and ensuring the status page language is accurate and appropriate during SLA-impacting events. Customer communication cannot be fully delegated — it is the organization's most visible expression of accountability, and it is what customers remember long after the technical details of the incident have faded.
