# Service Level Management: The VP/Director Framework

## What This Is

Service Level Management (SLM) is the discipline that translates engineering reliability signals into business commitments, commercial contracts, and customer trust. At the engineer level, SLM is about measuring SLIs and staying within SLO thresholds. At the VP level, SLM is about signing agreements with customers, defending those commitments operationally, and managing the commercial consequences when the system fails to deliver.

This framework is written for the VP or Director of Operations who must:
- Sign service level agreements with enterprise customers and explain breach consequences to legal
- Use error budgets as a management tool to govern the velocity of feature delivery
- Prepare quarterly business reviews that translate uptime percentages into business narrative
- Negotiate SLA renewals with customers who just experienced a breach
- Govern the service catalog and ensure every service has an accountable owner, a defined SLO, and a tested runbook

This is not a theory document. It is an operating framework for the executive who sits at the intersection of engineering reliability and commercial accountability.

## What SLM Is at the VP Level (vs. Engineer Level)

Most SRE engineers think of SLM as: define SLIs, set SLO thresholds, track error budget burn, alert when burn is too fast. That is correct — and it is about 30% of the discipline.

The other 70% is a VP problem:

**The commercial layer**: SLAs are legal contracts. When you breach one, the consequences are credits, contract renegotiation, customer churn risk, and in regulated industries, potential regulatory exposure. The VP signs the SLA. The VP owns the breach conversation.

**The governance layer**: SLOs do not set themselves. Someone must decide what "acceptable reliability" means for each service tier, who reviews those decisions, and what happens when a team's engineering velocity threatens the SLO. That someone is the VP.

**The communication layer**: Enterprise customers do not speak SLO. They speak "was my service available when my employees needed it." The VP translates engineering reality into business language — accurately, without minimizing, and with a credible remediation story.

**The accountability layer**: When a service has no SLO, no defined owner, and no runbook — that accountability gap exists because no one at the VP level demanded otherwise. SLM governance is an executive function.

Think of it this way: the SRE engineer is the pilot monitoring the instruments. The VP is the airline executive who sold the ticket, defined the safety minimums, and will face the regulatory inquiry if the plane is late too often.

## The Three Failure Modes of SLM Programs

Most organizations fail at service level management in one of three patterns:

**Failure Mode 1: SLOs without SLAs**
Engineering has defined SLOs. The SRE team tracks error budgets. But the commercial agreements with customers — the actual SLAs — were written by a legal team five years ago, bear no relationship to what the systems actually measure, and have never been updated to reflect the current architecture. When a breach happens, nobody knows if it technically triggered the SLA credit clause because the SLA commitment is undefined or unmeasurable. The result: legal exposure, customer mistrust, and a frantic post-breach negotiation.

**Failure Mode 2: SLAs without governance**
The organization has well-written SLAs with clear availability commitments and credit clauses. But there is no SLO review cadence, no service catalog, no error budget policy, and no one accountable for the gap between what was promised and what is being delivered. SLA compliance is measured retroactively — the team discovers a breach after the customer does. No one has the authority to freeze deployments when reliability deteriorates. The SLA is a liability document, not a management tool.

**Failure Mode 3: Governance without culture**
The SLM program has all the artifacts: service catalog, SLO review cadence, error budget policy, breach playbook. But engineering teams treat SLOs as paperwork and ignore error budget burn signals until a breach is imminent. Postmortems identify root causes but SLO improvements are never funded. The SLM review board meets quarterly and produces no binding decisions. Governance theater: the program exists on paper, the culture hasn't changed, and customers eventually notice.

This framework addresses all three. Chapters 1–4 address Failure Mode 1. Chapters 3, 5, 8 address Failure Mode 2. Chapters 4, 8 address Failure Mode 3.

## Key Definitions (Used Consistently Across All Files)

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **SLI** (Service Level Indicator) | A quantitative measurement of a service behavior — the raw signal. Examples: request success rate, latency at 99th percentile, data freshness age |
| **SLO** (Service Level Objective) | An internal engineering target expressed against an SLI. Not customer-facing. Example: "success rate ≥ 99.5% over a 30-day rolling window" |
| **SLA** (Service Level Agreement) | A contractual commitment made to a customer, with defined consequences for breach. Customer-facing. Legally binding. Example: "availability ≥ 99.9% per calendar month; breach triggers 10% service credit" |
| **Error Budget** | The allowable margin between the SLO target and 100%. If SLO is 99.5%, the error budget is 0.5% of requests — the "spend" allowed on failures, experiments, and deployments |
| **OLA** (Operational Level Agreement) | An internal agreement between teams or departments that underpins an SLA commitment. Example: the DBA team agrees to 99.8% database availability so the application team can commit to 99.5% application availability |
| **UC** (Underpinning Contract) | A vendor or supplier contract that the organization relies on to meet its own SLA. Example: AWS guarantees 99.99% S3 availability; this underpins your storage SLA commitment |
| **Breach** | A period during which actual availability or performance fell below the SLA-committed threshold, triggering contractual consequences |
| **Service Credit** | Compensation paid (as account credit, not cash) to a customer following an SLA breach, per the contract's credit schedule |
| **Service Catalog** | The authoritative registry of services the organization operates, including tier, SLO, owner, SLI measurement method, and dependencies |
| **Service Report** | A periodic (monthly or quarterly) document delivered to customers summarizing actual availability, incidents, breach status, and trend |
| **QBR** (Quarterly Business Review) | A formal meeting with the customer executive team to review service performance, strategic alignment, and forward commitments |

## Relationship to Incident Management and Problem Management

SLM does not operate in isolation. It is downstream of [Incident Management] and upstream of commercial risk.

**SLM ↔ Incident Management [cross-reference: incident-management/]**
Every incident that triggers SLO burn is a potential SLA breach contributor. The incident management framework determines how quickly the event is declared, who communicates to customers, and when it is resolved. SLM is what happens after resolution: was the SLO breached? Did the accumulation of incidents this month breach the SLA? What credit is owed?

The VP of Operations owns both: the incident command structure (tactical) and the SLM consequence management (commercial). When a SEV1 incident happens, the IC manages the incident; the VP is simultaneously calculating SLA exposure and preparing the customer communication.

**SLM ↔ Problem Management [cross-reference: problem-management/]**
SLA breaches frequently trigger formal Problem records. Problem Management identifies the root cause of repeat incidents that are eroding SLO compliance. When the same dependency causes three SLA-threatening incidents in a quarter, the VP uses Problem Management's root cause output to drive a "service improvement commitment" in the post-breach remediation letter.

The connection is bidirectional: SLM tells Problem Management which issues are commercially critical (not all SLO violations are equal — the ones that threaten the SLA are the ones that must be addressed). Problem Management tells SLM what the systematic fixes are and when the customer can expect reliability to improve.

## Why SLM Is the VP's Primary Interface to the Business

Every other discipline an SRE VP oversees — incident management, change management, capacity planning, observability — is internally focused. The organization's internal stakeholders care about these processes. External customers do not.

SLM is the exception. It is the discipline through which:
- The organization's reliability commitments become legally binding
- Engineering decisions become commercial risk
- Downtime becomes dollars (credits, churn risk, regulatory fines)
- The VP becomes personally accountable to the customer, not just to internal engineering leadership

At T-Mobile scale — millions of customers, enterprise agreements with Fortune 500 companies, FCC reporting obligations — SLM failures are not just operational embarrassments. They are contract breaches, potential regulatory events, and material business risks. The VP of Operations is the executive who owns that risk.

When the CFO asks "what is our SLA exposure this quarter?" — that is an SLM question. When the enterprise account manager says "the customer is threatening to invoke the exit clause" — that is an SLM escalation. When the board asks "are we operationally ready to take on the new enterprise contract?" — the answer is SLM data, translated into business language.

This framework gives you the tools to be that executive.
