# Chapter 3: Service Catalog and SLO Governance

## Why the Catalog Exists

Imagine walking into a hospital and asking "which doctor is responsible for this patient?" If no one has been assigned, the answer is "everyone is responsible, which means no one is." The service catalog solves the same problem for engineering organizations: it is the authoritative record of what the organization operates, who is accountable for each service, and what standard that service must meet.

Without a service catalog, the SLM program rests on informal knowledge. Someone knows which team owns the authentication service. Someone else knows the SLO is 99.9%. A third person knows the on-call rotation. When that person leaves, or during an incident at 3am, that informal knowledge fails. The catalog makes the implicit explicit.

---

## The Service Catalog: Definition and Scope

The service catalog is a structured registry — maintained as living operational data, not a static document — that contains an authoritative record for every service the organization operates. "Operates" means: customer traffic flows through it, other services depend on it, or an SLA commitment covers it.

### What Goes In the Catalog

Every service record contains:

| Field | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Service Name** | Canonical, unambiguous name | `notification-dispatch-api` |
| **Service Owner** | Named individual (not a team) accountable for SLO | Jane Doe, Staff Engineer |
| **Business Owner** | Named executive accountable for business impact | VP of Product — Messaging |
| **Service Tier** | Gold / Silver / Bronze — drives SLO and support commitment | Silver |
| **SLO** | Availability target, latency target, error rate target | 99.95% availability, 30-day rolling |
| **SLI Method** | How availability is measured, from which vantage point | HTTP probe, API gateway success rate, 1-min cadence |
| **Error Budget** | Total allowable failure margin per period | 0.05% = 21.9 min/month |
| **Dependencies** | Services this service depends on | `auth-service`, `user-profile-db`, `kafka-cluster-east` |
| **Depended On By** | Services that depend on this service | `mobile-push-gateway`, `sms-gateway`, `email-relay` |
| **On-Call Rotation** | PagerDuty or equivalent rotation name | `sre-notification-primary` |
| **Runbook Location** | Link to primary runbook | `wiki/ops/notification-dispatch-runbook` |
| **SLA Backed** | Whether this service is covered by a customer SLA | Yes — Enterprise Tier SLA v3.2 |
| **Last SLO Review** | Date of last formal SLO review | 2026-04-15 |
| **Next SLO Review** | Scheduled next review | 2026-07-15 |
| **Status** | Active / Deprecated / Sunset | Active |

### What Does Not Go in the Catalog

- Implementation details (language, framework) — those belong in architecture documentation
- Deployment procedures — those belong in runbooks
- Cost allocation — that belongs in FinOps tooling (though tier assignment feeds cost modeling)

The catalog is not a wiki. It is an operational record. Every field must be current, every owner field must be a named person, and the record must be the single source of truth that incident responders consult at 3am.

## Service Tier Definitions

Tier assignment is the VP's most consequential catalog governance decision. It determines:
- What SLO the service must maintain
- What support response the on-call team provides
- Whether an SLA exists for this service
- How much engineering investment is required for reliability

### Standard Tier Definitions

**Gold Tier**:
- Customer-facing, SLA-backed, directly revenue-generating
- SLO: ≥ 99.99% availability
- Support: 24/7, P1 response in 15 minutes
- Characteristics: Active-active architecture, zero-downtime deployment required, quarterly chaos testing
- Examples: Payment processing API, enterprise notification core, authentication service

**Silver Tier**:
- Customer-facing or business-critical, SLA-backed or SLA-adjacent
- SLO: ≥ 99.9% availability
- Support: 24/7, P1 response in 60 minutes
- Characteristics: Active-passive architecture, maintenance window allowed, monthly load testing
- Examples: Notification management console, reporting API, batch processing

**Bronze Tier**:
- Internal, non-customer-facing, or explicitly SLA-excluded
- SLO: ≥ 99.5% availability
- Support: Business hours, P1 response in 4 hours
- Characteristics: Single-region, maintenance allowed, no active HA required
- Examples: Internal dashboards, development tools, non-production environments

### Tier Assignment Authority

The VP owns tier assignment for Gold and Silver services. Engineering managers own Bronze tier assignment, subject to quarterly VP review.

Tier downgrade (Gold → Silver, Silver → Bronze) requires VP approval — because downgrade may change or eliminate an SLA commitment to a customer.

Tier upgrade requires VP approval — because upgrade commits engineering resources to higher reliability standards.

## SLO Governance at the VP Level

SLO governance answers three questions: who has the authority to set SLOs, who reviews whether SLOs are being met, and what happens when a team's engineering decisions threaten SLO compliance.

### Who Sets SLOs

**The principle**: SLOs are set by the people who will be held accountable for meeting them, subject to review by the people who made commitments based on them.

In practice:
- **Service Owner** drafts the SLO, based on trailing performance data and architectural knowledge
- **SRE team** reviews the SLO for measurement methodology, error budget policy, and consistency with related services
- **VP** approves the SLO for any Gold or Silver tier service, and for any service backed by an external SLA

The VP does not draft SLOs. But the VP's approval is required before any Gold/Silver SLO is published, and before any existing SLO is changed.

### Why Individual Teams Cannot Set SLOs Without Coordination

Three reasons:

**1. Customer SLA anchoring**: If Team A's service is covered by an SLA commitment of 99.9%, Team A's SLO must be set above 99.9% to provide the buffer. If Team A unilaterally sets their SLO at 99.9% (matching the SLA), the buffer disappears and any incident immediately creates SLA exposure. The VP must see SLO changes for SLA-backed services because the VP owns the SLA consequence.

**2. Dependency cascading**: If Service A depends on Service B, and Service B lowers its SLO from 99.97% to 99.9%, the compound availability of Service A is now lower — potentially below the SLA commitment — even if Service A's own engineering hasn't changed. SLO changes in dependency services affect downstream SLA exposure in ways that individual teams cannot see.

**3. Organizational consistency**: If each team sets its own SLO methodology (different measurement windows, different success criteria definitions, different error counting), SLO data across the portfolio becomes incomparable. The SLM review board cannot assess portfolio health. The VP cannot have an honest conversation with a customer about relative service reliability.

### SLO Changes: The Approval Process

Any SLO change for a Gold or Silver tier service follows this process:

1. **Service Owner** proposes change with justification (trailing performance data, architectural rationale)
2. **SRE lead** reviews measurement methodology and error budget impact
3. **VP** reviews SLA alignment and commercial impact
4. **Change is recorded** in the service catalog with effective date, previous value, and justification
5. **If the SLO is being lowered** (reduced commitment): Legal reviews whether the change requires customer notification or SLA revision

Unilateral SLO changes by teams without this process are a governance failure. The VP must create the culture where teams understand that SLO changes for SLA-backed services are not engineering decisions — they are commercial decisions that happen to require engineering input.

## The Dependency Problem: Compound SLOs

The most underappreciated challenge in SLO governance is dependency management. A service's actual reliability is not determined solely by its own engineering — it is the product of its own reliability and the reliability of every service it depends on.

### The Cascade Model

Consider a simplified notification path:

```
Customer Request
      ↓
[Auth Service]          SLO: 99.99%
      ↓
[Notification Dispatch]  SLO: 99.95%
      ↓
[Message Queue]          SLO: 99.97%
      ↓
[Delivery Gateway]       SLO: 99.95%
      ↓
Customer Notification Delivered
```

Compound SLO = 99.99% × 99.95% × 99.97% × 99.95% = **99.86%**

If the SLA commitment is 99.9%, the service chain is already exposing the organization to breach risk — even if every individual component meets its own SLO.

### The VP's Response to the Dependency Problem

**Map the dependency chains**: For every SLA-backed service, maintain a visual dependency map in the service catalog. This is not optional — it is the document that determines whether the SLO portfolio is coherent.

**Set compound SLO targets**: The VP should set a compound SLO floor for each SLA tier. For a Gold service with 99.99% SLA commitment, the compound SLO of the dependency chain must achieve at least 99.995%.

**Drive SLO elevation in bottleneck dependencies**: When compound calculation reveals a weak link, the VP must either:
- Drive the engineering investment to raise the dependency's SLO, or
- Restructure the architecture to eliminate the dependency, or
- Accept a lower SLA commitment to the customer (rarely acceptable once the SLA is signed)

**Trigger SLO review when dependencies change**: Any change to a Gold or Silver service's dependency graph triggers a compound SLO recalculation. This must be a mandatory step in the change management process for SLA-backed services.

## SLO Review Cadence

SLOs are not permanent. They must be reviewed regularly against actual performance, adjusted when architecture changes, and recalibrated after major incidents.

### Quarterly Review (Standard)

Every SLO in the catalog is reviewed quarterly. The review answers:
- Is actual availability above, at, or below the SLO target?
- Is the error budget burn rate sustainable?
- Has the dependency graph changed in ways that affect compound SLO?
- Has the service architecture changed in ways that make the SLO too conservative or too aggressive?
- Is the SLO still aligned with the SLA commitment?

The quarterly review is led by the Service Owner and attended by the VP or VP delegate for Gold and Silver services. [Cross-reference: slo-review-playbook.md]

### Incident-Triggered Review

Any incident that burns > 25% of the monthly error budget in a single event triggers an SLO review within 10 business days of resolution. The review is focused on:
- Was the SLO realistic given the failure mode observed?
- Does the SLO need to be recalibrated?
- Does the incident reveal a dependency vulnerability that affects compound SLO?

### Architecture-Triggered Review

When a service undergoes a major architectural change (new cloud provider, new database technology, significant dependency changes), an SLO review is triggered before the change goes to production. Existing SLOs were validated against existing architecture — a new architecture requires new validation.

## What a VP Does When a Service Has No SLO

A service with no SLO is an accountability gap. If that service fails, there is no standard to compare actual performance against, no error budget to measure burn, and no basis for any SLA commitment. It is operationally invisible.

When the VP discovers a service without an SLO:

**Step 1: Determine criticality** — Is this service in the customer critical path? Does it underpin any SLA-backed service? If yes, it is immediately treated as a governance priority.

**Step 2: Assign an interim tier** — Based on the service's role in the architecture, assign an interim tier (Gold/Silver/Bronze). This determines the urgency of the SLO definition process.

**Step 3: Require SLO definition within 30 days** — For Gold and Silver interim tier services, the Service Owner must submit a draft SLO within 30 days. This is not a suggestion.

**Step 4: Prohibit SLA coverage until SLO is defined** — No service without an SLO can be covered by a customer SLA. If a customer SLA currently covers such a service, this is a commercial liability that must be resolved — either by defining the SLO or by removing the service from the SLA scope.

**Step 5: Track as a governance metric** — The VP should track "% of production services with defined SLOs" as a quarterly metric. The target is 100% for Gold and Silver. Bronze services should reach 100% within 12 months of the SLM program launch.

> **What the VP Owns**: When a production service lacks an SLO, the VP owns the remediation timeline. Not the SRE. Not the team lead. The VP is the one who made (or allowed someone else to make) a customer commitment without an engineering foundation. The VP fixes it.

## Service Catalog Governance: Operational Maintenance

The catalog is only valuable if it is current. A stale catalog is worse than no catalog — it creates false confidence and leads incident responders to wrong owners and wrong runbooks.

### Mandatory Update Triggers

The service catalog must be updated when:
- A new service enters production (new entry required before go-live)
- A service owner changes (within 5 business days of the change)
- A dependency is added or removed (as part of the change management process)
- An SLO is changed (triggered by the approval process)
- A service is decommissioned or deprecated (status update required)
- An SLA is created, renewed, or terminated (SLA Backed field update)

### Quarterly Audit

The SLM program conducts a quarterly catalog audit:
- Verify all Service Owner fields are current named individuals (not departed employees)
- Verify all runbook links are accessible and current
- Verify all SLO values match the approved SLO documentation
- Verify all dependency fields are current given recent architectural changes
- Verify Next SLO Review dates are scheduled in the calendar

The audit report goes to the VP. Unresolved discrepancies from the previous quarter are escalated.

### Catalog Tooling

The service catalog should be maintained in a tool that supports:
- Structured fields (not a wiki page or spreadsheet)
- Version history (every change tracked with who changed it and when)
- API access (so monitoring tools, incident management platforms, and dashboards can pull catalog data)
- Dependency visualization (ability to render the dependency graph)

ServiceNow CMDB, Backstage, or purpose-built catalog tools (Port, OpsLevel) are appropriate. A spreadsheet is not — it cannot be the operational source of truth at scale.

---

## The VP's Catalog Discipline: Six Non-Negotiables

1. **No Gold or Silver service enters production without a catalog entry and an approved SLO**
2. **No service is covered by an SLA without an SLO that provides a meaningful buffer**
3. **Every Service Owner field is a named individual — team names are not accountable**
4. **Dependency graphs for all SLA-backed services are documented and current**
5. **The catalog is audited quarterly; unresolved discrepancies are escalated to VP**
6. **Service decommissions update the catalog before the service is taken offline — not after**
