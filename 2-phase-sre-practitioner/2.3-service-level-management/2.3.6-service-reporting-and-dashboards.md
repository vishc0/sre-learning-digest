# Chapter 6: Service Reporting and Dashboards

## Why Reporting Is a VP Function

Service reporting is not data delivery — it is narrative construction. The same availability number (99.87%) can be framed as "we met our SLO" (it exceeded the 99.85% SLO target), "we missed our SLA" (it fell below the 99.9% SLA commitment), or "we are trending in the right direction" (it is an improvement over last month's 99.74%). All three are true simultaneously.

The VP's job in service reporting is to ensure the narrative is accurate, the right audience receives the right view of the data, and the organization is never in a position where the customer discovers a breach from their own data analysis before hearing it from the VP.

This chapter covers what belongs in each report type, who receives it, how to read trend data, and how to prepare for the highest-stakes reporting event: the Quarterly Business Review.

---

## Service Report Structure

A service report is a structured document that presents actual service performance for a defined period against the SLA commitment and SLO target. It is not a dashboard — it is a formal document that can be referenced in contract disputes and customer escalations.

### The Seven Sections of a Complete Service Report

**Section 1: Executive Summary**
- Service name and reporting period
- SLA commitment (what was promised)
- Actual availability (what was delivered)
- Breach status (breach / no breach)
- Credit issued if applicable
- One-sentence trend statement: improving / stable / degrading

**Section 2: Availability Detail**
- Daily availability breakdown (table or chart placeholder)
- Worst single-day availability and date
- Total downtime in minutes
- Downtime distribution (single event vs. accumulated)
- Comparison to prior period (month over month)
- Comparison to 3-month and 12-month trailing average

**Section 3: Incident Log**
- All incidents contributing to SLO/SLA impact during the period
- For each incident: date, duration, severity, root cause (brief), resolution
- Total incidents in period
- Comparison to prior period (trend: increasing / stable / decreasing)

**Section 4: Breach Status**
- Whether SLA threshold was breached
- If breached: credit calculation methodology and credit amount
- If not breached: remaining error budget at month-end

**Section 5: Error Budget Status**
- Error budget allocated for period
- Error budget consumed (by incident, by planned maintenance, by deployments)
- Remaining error budget at period end
- Burn rate trend

**Section 6: Action Items and Commitments**
- Open action items from prior service reports
- Status of each (complete / in progress / at risk / not started)
- New commitments from this period's incidents

**Section 7: Forward Look**
- Known planned maintenance in the next period
- High-risk changes planned (migration, major releases)
- Reliability investments underway and expected completion dates

## Reporting Cadence and Audience

Different audiences need different views of service performance data at different frequencies.

### Weekly (Internal Operations)

**Audience**: VP of Operations, SRE leads, Service Owners
**Format**: Dashboard + brief written summary (not a full report)
**Content**:
- Current month availability by service (actual vs. SLO target)
- Error budget burn rate (current week vs. projected monthly)
- Open P1 incidents
- High-risk changes scheduled for the upcoming week

**Purpose**: Operational decision-making. Is anything approaching SLA exposure? Are deployment plans safe given current budget? Is there anything the VP needs to address this week?

This is the report the VP reviews every Monday morning. If the VP cannot read the weekly summary in under 5 minutes and know whether anything requires immediate attention, the format needs to be redesigned.

### Monthly (To Business Units / Internal Customers)

**Audience**: Internal business unit leaders, shared services customers
**Format**: Full service report (all seven sections)
**Delivery**: By the 10th business day of the following month
**Content**: Everything in the report structure above

For internal service reports, the credit mechanism does not apply. The breach status section is replaced by a "service commitment status" section that documents whether the team met its OLA commitments to the business unit.

### Monthly (To External Enterprise Customers)

**Audience**: Customer technical team, customer operations leadership
**Format**: Abbreviated service report (sections 1–4 only; section 7 for major accounts)
**Delivery**: By the 10th business day of the following month, per SLA reporting commitment

The customer-facing report should be clean and direct. Do not include operational detail that creates confusion or reveals internal engineering decisions. Do include:
- Actual availability number (they have the right to know)
- Breach status (do not wait for them to calculate it themselves)
- Credit amount if applicable (confirm the number before they ask)
- Incidents that affected them (brief, factual)

If there was a breach, the service report is not the primary breach communication — the breach notification should have already gone out. The service report confirms the numbers and the credit.

### Quarterly (To Customers — QBR Prep)

**Audience**: Customer executive team
**Format**: Presentation (not a document) backed by data
**Purpose**: Strategic conversation, not operational report delivery

The quarterly service report rolls up three monthly reports into a trend picture. But the QBR is not about the data — it is about the relationship, the forward plan, and whether the customer sees the organization as a strategic partner. The data is the foundation; the conversation is the point.

## The VP's Executive Dashboard

The VP needs a single view — visible every morning, updated in near-real time — that shows whether the SLM portfolio is healthy. This is not the same as the SRE team's monitoring dashboard. It is a business-facing view built on top of the operational data.

### What the VP Sees Every Morning

**Section 1: Availability — Current Month to Date**

| Service | SLA Commitment | MTD Actual | Remaining Budget | Breach Risk |
|---------|---------------|------------|-----------------|-------------|
| notification-dispatch | 99.9% | 99.94% | 28 min | Low |
| auth-service | 99.99% | 99.96% | 1.2 min | High |
| sms-gateway | 99.9% | 99.99% | 41 min | None |

Color coding: Green (> 50% budget remaining), Yellow (25–50% remaining), Red (< 25% remaining or breach imminent).

**Section 2: Error Budget Burn Rate**

The burn rate view shows whether each service is consuming its error budget at a sustainable pace. A service with 80% of the month remaining but 60% of the budget consumed is on a trajectory to breach.

**Section 3: Open P1 Problems**

Problem records (not incidents) that are open and affecting SLA-backed services. The VP monitors these because an unresolved Problem is a future breach risk. Each Problem shows: title, root cause status, estimated fix date, SLO impact.

**Section 4: Upcoming High-Risk Changes**

Any change classified as high-risk (major version upgrade, database migration, architecture change) that is scheduled in the next 14 days for a Gold or Silver service. The VP uses this to review error budget status before the change window and decide whether to approve, defer, or require additional safeguards.

### Dashboard Tooling

The VP dashboard is built on top of the SLI data that already exists in the monitoring infrastructure:
- Datadog SLO dashboards (if using Datadog) provide the availability and error budget data
- ServiceNow SLM module provides the breach records, credit calculations, and reporting workflow
- Grafana SLO panels (if using Grafana) provide the trend visualization
- Atlassian Statuspage provides the customer-facing availability view

The VP dashboard is not another tool — it is a configured view within the existing tooling that surfaces executive-relevant data. If the tooling cannot produce this view, the SLM program needs a tooling investment. [Cross-reference: slm-tooling-reference.md]

## Customer-Facing Reporting Portal

Enterprise customers increasingly expect real-time access to their service performance data. A customer-facing reporting portal — whether a dedicated dashboard, a Statuspage page scoped to their account, or an API feed — provides:
- Current availability status (live)
- Historical availability for the current and prior periods
- Incident history (customer-relevant incidents only)
- Current month availability vs. SLA commitment

### What to Expose, What to Hold Back

**Expose**:
- Availability percentage (actual vs. committed)
- Incident timestamps and duration for incidents that affected their service
- Current month status (meeting commitment / approaching threshold / breach)
- Credit balance if applicable

**Hold back**:
- Internal SLO data (the gap between SLO and SLA is internal information)
- Error budget details (the customer's commercial relationship is with the SLA, not the SLO)
- Root cause analysis in progress (preliminary root causes can be disputed and create liability)
- Other customers' availability data or incidents

### Portal Commitment in the SLA

If the SLA includes a commitment to portal access (many modern enterprise SLAs do), the VP must ensure the portal is operational before the SLA is signed. A portal commitment that is not delivered creates a secondary breach type — and customers notice immediately when the promised visibility tool does not exist.

## SLO Trend Analysis

The service report presents monthly snapshots. The VP reads trends. A single month's data is context; three months is a trend; twelve months is evidence.

### The Three Trends That Require VP Action

**Improving trend** (availability increasing month over month):
- Confirm the improvement is real (not measurement methodology change)
- If improving significantly, consider whether the SLO should be raised
- If the gap between actual and SLA commitment has grown, explore whether a tighter SLA could create commercial opportunity
- Report the improvement to the customer proactively — good news shared before the customer asks builds trust

**Stable trend** (availability consistent within a narrow band):
- Stable and above SLO target: healthy state, maintain operational discipline
- Stable and below SLO target: chronic underperformance — this is an engineering investment problem, not an operational one
- Stable and near SLA threshold: dangerous — any variance will cause a breach. Requires VP attention to either raise performance or adjust engineering risk appetite (fewer deployments, more resilience investment)

**Degrading trend** (availability declining month over month):
- This is the most urgent signal: the direction of travel is toward breach
- VP must understand the cause: is this a dependency issue? Increasing deployment frequency? Growing system complexity? Technical debt accumulation?
- VP must set a clear intervention: by what date must the trend reverse? What specific engineering investment will reverse it?
- VP must communicate with the customer proactively if degradation trend is visible in their monthly reports — better to surface it and show a plan than to let them discover the trajectory from three monthly reports in a row

### Leading vs. Lagging Indicators in SLM

The monthly availability number is a lagging indicator — it tells you what already happened. To govern proactively, the VP needs leading indicators:

| Leading Indicator | What It Signals | Response Threshold |
|------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Error budget burn rate (weekly) | Whether current month will end in breach | > 2× normal burn rate for 72+ hours |
| Deployment failure rate | Whether deployments are adding reliability risk | > 10% of deployments require rollback |
| Dependency SLO degradation | Whether a dependency is approaching failure | Dependency actual availability declining for 3+ weeks |
| Alert fatigue index | Whether the team is missing signals | > 20% of alerts are acknowledged-and-ignored |
| On-call incident rate | Whether the system is becoming less stable | > 15% increase in P2/P3 incidents month over month |

The VP reviews leading indicators weekly (not monthly). Leading indicators give time to intervene before the lagging indicator (availability) moves.

## Quarterly Business Review Preparation

The QBR is the highest-stakes reporting event in the SLM calendar. It is not a report delivery session — it is a strategic conversation with the customer's executive team, backed by service performance data.

### Preparation Timeline

**T-minus 3 weeks**: Pull and validate all availability data for Q. Reconcile with customer data if they track their own.

**T-minus 2 weeks**: Draft the QBR narrative. Identify the two or three points that require VP-level discussion. Confirm no open breach records or outstanding credit disputes. Confirm account manager alignment on customer relationship status.

**T-minus 1 week**: Prepare the presentation deck. Practice the 5-minute executive summary version (because the customer CIO has 30 minutes, not 90). Prepare for the difficult questions (see below). Brief the account manager.

**Day of**: Review the deck one more time. Know the numbers cold — the customer expects the VP to know the availability number without looking at a slide.

### The QBR Narrative Structure

The QBR is presented in four acts:

**Act 1: Where We Were (5 minutes)**
Quarterly availability, incident count, breach status (if any). Direct, factual. No minimizing, no over-explaining.

**Act 2: What We Learned (10 minutes)**
Root causes of significant incidents. What systemic improvements were implemented. Evidence that the improvements are working.

**Act 3: Where We Are Going (10 minutes)**
Roadmap of reliability investments. Planned architectural improvements. SLO review results and any adjustments. Changes that will affect the customer in the next quarter.

**Act 4: What We Need from the Customer (5 minutes)**
Any customer-side dependencies that affect reliability (customer-side rate limits, integration upgrade requirements, planned volume increases). Upcoming changes the customer should plan for.

### Preparing for Difficult QBR Questions

**"You breached the SLA twice this quarter. Why should we renew?"**
Response structure: Acknowledge. Explain (root cause). Demonstrate action (what changed). Show evidence (post-fix reliability data). Commit (forward reliability target). Do not defend, minimize, or blame dependencies.

**"Your competitor offers a 99.99% SLA. Why are you only at 99.9%?"**
Response structure: "Our 99.9% SLA has been backed by actual performance of [X]% this quarter. A 99.99% SLA from a competitor must be matched by their architectural capability to deliver it. Here's what we've invested to make our commitment real." Know the trailing performance number cold.

**"Can you give us a better SLA at the current price?"**
This is a negotiation trigger. Do not commit in the QBR. "Let me take that back and model what the engineering investment would require to deliver [higher target] within the current contract structure. I'll have a response within two weeks." Then have it, and have Legal review it.

## Summary: What the VP Owns in Service Reporting

> **What the VP Owns**: The VP owns the service report narrative — not the data assembly (that's the service owner's job) but the framing, the trend interpretation, and the decisions that the data should be driving. The VP reviews the weekly dashboard personally. The VP approves the customer-facing monthly report before it goes out (for enterprise accounts). The VP leads the QBR. If the reporting is accurate but the narrative is absent, the customer fills in the narrative themselves — usually with the least charitable interpretation of the data.
