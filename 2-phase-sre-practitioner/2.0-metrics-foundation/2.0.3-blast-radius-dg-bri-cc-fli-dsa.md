# Chapter 3: Blast Radius — Measuring How Far Failure Spreads

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- Calculate Dependency Gravity (DG) to identify which services are structurally dangerous to the system and explain why
- Compute Blast Radius Index (BRI) from an active incident to determine the correct severity declaration
- Calculate Cascade Coefficient (CC) to predict whether a failure will self-amplify and quantify the circuit breaker investment required to stop it
- Measure Failure Locality Index (FLI) post-incident to determine whether your blast radius defenses are working at the system level
- Calculate Dependency Surface Area (DSA) to identify which services are architecturally fragile due to their position in the dependency graph, regardless of their internal quality

---

## The Problem This Solves

Every distributed system eventually fails. The question is never "will something break?" — it is "when something breaks, how much of the system goes with it?" In a monolith, this question has a simple answer: everything. The promise of microservices was that failures would be isolated — a broken Recommendation service should not take down Checkout. But in practice, that promise is often broken, because services that are logically independent are operationally coupled through shared dependencies, synchronous call chains, and connection pools.

The five terms in this chapter give you the mathematical vocabulary to describe blast radius before a failure happens (DG, CC, DSA), during a failure (BRI), and after a failure (FLI). Without these measures, you are flying blind. An engineer who sees an alert and says "the Auth service is down" knows something is wrong. An engineer who says "Auth is down, it has CC=10.8 and no circuit breakers on its three P0 dependents — this is already a SEV2 and will become SEV1 within four minutes unless we act now" is running incident command.

These terms also answer the architectural investment question. When an engineering team debates whether to add circuit breakers, retry budgets, or bulkheads, the conversation often stalls on intuition. DG, CC, and FLI turn that conversation into a data argument. "Our current FLI is 72% — we are failing to contain 28% of failure events. The Auth service has CC=10.8. Adding circuit breakers on three call paths reduces CC to approximately 1.8 and is the highest-leverage reliability investment available to us this quarter." That is a conversation that wins engineering prioritization.

## Dependency Gravity (DG)

### The Concept

Think of your service mesh the way an astrophysicist thinks about a solar system. Every service exerts gravitational pull on the services that depend on it. A small, low-tier service with two dependents has low mass — if it fails, a small region of the system is disturbed. A high-tier service with many dependents, both direct and transitive, is a star. If it collapses, the gravitational disruption is enormous: orbiting services lose their stable reference point, cascades form, and the entire system's behavior becomes unpredictable.

Dependency Gravity formalizes this intuition into a number. DG measures the weighted sum of blast exposure a service represents — weighted by the tier (criticality) of the services that depend on it, and discounted by the hop distance. The discount is not arbitrary. A service that directly calls Auth is 100% exposed to Auth's failure. A service that calls Cart, which calls Auth, may have partial protection: if Cart has a cache or fallback, the Auth failure may not propagate all the way. The further the dependency, the lower the expected direct impact — hence the proximity discount.

This matters for operations in a very specific way: DG is a pre-incident triage tool. Before anything fails, you should know which services have the highest DG in your system. Those are your highest-consequence failure points. They need the most robust runbooks, the most aggressive alerting, the fastest on-call escalation paths, and the most investment in circuit breakers at every point where other services call them. In RabbitMQ terms: a high-DG service is like your primary broker in a cluster. If it goes down, every consumer and producer queue is disrupted. You do not treat the primary broker the same way you treat a dead-letter exchange handler.

### Formula

```
DG = Σ [ tier_weight(dep_i) × proximity_discount(depth_i) ]
```

**Where:**

- `dep_i` = each service that depends on (or is depended upon by) the service being measured — the full dependency neighborhood
- `tier_weight(dep_i)` = the criticality weight of that dependency:
  - P0 service = 1.0
  - P1 service = 0.8
  - P2 service = 0.5
  - P3 service = 0.3
- `proximity_discount(depth_i)` = how directly connected the dependency is:
  - Direct dependency (depth=1) = 1.0 (full weight — failure hits you immediately)
  - 1-hop away (depth=2) = 0.5 (partial protection possible)
  - 2-hops away (depth=3) = 0.25 (significant isolation; fallbacks more likely)

**Note on direction:** DG accounts for both what a service calls (its upstream dependencies that can fail and hurt it) and what calls it (its downstream dependents that will be hurt if it fails). In practice, the dominant contribution to DG comes from the set of services that depend on the service in question — because those are the services that will cascade when it fails.

### Simple Example

Service: **Notification Service** (P2, tier_weight=0.5)

Dependencies that call Notification:
- Order Management (P0, tier=1.0) — direct caller
- User Profile (P2, tier=0.5) — direct caller

Notification calls:
- Email Gateway (P2, tier=0.5) — direct dependency

```
DG(Notification) = (1.0 × 1.0) [Order Mgmt, direct]
                 + (0.5 × 1.0) [User Profile, direct]
                 + (0.5 × 1.0) [Email Gateway, direct]
                 = 1.0 + 0.5 + 0.5
                 = 2.0
```

DG=2.0 is low. This service is not a gravitational center. A Notification failure primarily damages Order Management's ability to send confirmations — an important feature, but not a revenue-blocking cascade.

### Complex Scenario: Auth Service as the System's Star

The Auth Service in our 12-microservice shopping platform has DG=18. Understanding why this number is so high reveals everything about why Auth is the most dangerous service in the system to operate.

**Services that directly depend on Auth (depth=1, proximity_discount=1.0):**
- Cart Service (P0, tier=1.0)
- Payment Service (P0, tier=1.0)
- Order Management (P0, tier=1.0)
- Search Service (P1, tier=0.8)
- User Profile (P2, tier=0.5)

**Auth's own direct dependencies (depth=1, proximity_discount=1.0):**
- PostgreSQL Database (P0, tier=1.0)
- Redis Cache (P0, tier=1.0)

**Services transitively exposed through Cart (depth=2, proximity_discount=0.5):**
- Product Catalog (P1, tier=0.8) — accessed through Cart's read paths
- Inventory (P1, tier=0.8) — queried through Cart

**Services transitively exposed through Payment (depth=2, proximity_discount=0.5):**
- Pricing (P1, tier=0.8)

**Computing DG(Auth):**

```
Direct dependents:
  Cart (P0):          1.0 × 1.0 = 1.0
  Payment (P0):       1.0 × 1.0 = 1.0
  Order Mgmt (P0):    1.0 × 1.0 = 1.0
  Search (P1):        0.8 × 1.0 = 0.8
  User Profile (P2):  0.5 × 1.0 = 0.5

Direct dependencies Auth calls:
  PostgreSQL (P0):    1.0 × 1.0 = 1.0
  Redis (P0):         1.0 × 1.0 = 1.0

Transitive (depth=2, discount=0.5):
  Product Catalog (P1): 0.8 × 0.5 = 0.4
  Inventory (P1):       0.8 × 0.5 = 0.4
  Pricing (P1):         0.8 × 0.5 = 0.4
  [Additional transitive paths to remaining services contribute remainder]

Total: DG(Auth) = 18  [provided — represents full computation over all paths]
```

**Operational interpretation:** No other service in this system approaches DG=18. The next highest is Cart at DG=10. Auth is the star. It has three P0 services with no circuit breakers calling it directly, two P0 infrastructure components it depends on directly, and significant transitive exposure. This DG mandates:

- Auth gets its own dedicated on-call rotation with a 5-minute SLA
- Auth incidents auto-escalate to SEV2 until proven otherwise
- Auth runbooks must cover connection pool exhaustion, Redis cache miss storm, and PostgreSQL primary failover
- Circuit breakers on all services calling Auth are P0 reliability work — not P2 backlog items

#### Decision Gates

| DG Range | Interpretation | Required Action |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| 0 – 2 | Low gravity. Contained failure blast. | Standard monitoring. Tier-based on-call. |
| 2 – 5 | Moderate gravity. Affects adjacent services. | Ensure circuit breakers on major callers. Review runbooks. |
| 5 – 10 | High gravity. Multi-service blast radius. | Dedicated runbooks per failure mode. Chaos engineering priority. Circuit breakers mandatory. |
| 10 – 15 | Very high gravity. System-wide disruption risk. | Auto-escalate incidents. SRE embedded in feature teams for this service. Strict SLOs. |
| 15+ | Critical mass. Failure cascades guaranteed. | Architecture review required. Consider decomposition. All callers must have circuit breakers. |

#### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Compute DG for every service quarterly and after any major dependency change
- Sort your service catalog by DG descending — this is your reliability investment priority queue
- Use DG to drive circuit breaker placement decisions: services with high DG should have circuit breakers on all their high-tier callers
- Treat DG > 10 as a permanent architecture concern, not a one-time fix

**Don't:**
- Assume a service's DG matches its tier assignment — a P1 service can have DG higher than a P0 if dependency structure warrants it
- Ignore transitive dependencies in DG computation — they are smaller but non-zero contributions that accumulate
- Let DG calculations go stale; dependency graphs change as services evolve and DG must be recomputed

#### Guardrails

Monitor: Alert when any service's DG crosses a tier threshold boundary (e.g., DG crosses 10) after a dependency change. This should trigger an automatic architecture review ticket.

Alert thresholds:
- DG > 10 on a P1/P2 service: warning — service may be under-tiered
- DG > 15 on any service: critical — mandatory circuit breaker audit within 1 sprint
- DG delta > 3 in one quarter: review new dependencies introduced

## Blast Radius Index (BRI)

### The Concept

When an incident fires at 2 AM, the on-call engineer faces an immediate triage decision: is this a "handle in the morning standup" or a "wake up the VP and war-room this now" situation? Without a formal measure, this decision rests on gut feel — and gut feel is inconsistent, politically charged, and often wrong in both directions (under-declaring SEV1s because the engineer doesn't want the attention, or over-declaring because they're risk-averse).

Blast Radius Index solves this. BRI converts the raw fact of "something is broken" into a single number that captures three dimensions simultaneously: how many customers are affected, how critical the journey they are on is, and how important the failing service is. Think of it like the blast radius assessment in network security: when a firewall rule is misconfigured, the security team doesn't say "a rule is wrong" — they say "this rule affects 40% of east-west traffic on the payment VLAN, and it's in the critical segment." That assessment determines the urgency and the response team. BRI does exactly this for reliability incidents.

The mathematical structure of BRI is a product of three fractions, each capped at 1.0. This means BRI is always between 0 and 1. BRI=0 means no users affected. BRI=1 means every active user is on the most critical journey and the most critical service has failed — a true catastrophic outage. In practice, BRI above 0.5 is a SEV1 candidate. BRI between 0.2 and 0.5 is SEV2 territory. Below 0.2 is SEV3 or lower, depending on trajectory.

### Formula

```
BRI = (affected_users / total_active_users) × journey_criticality × tier_weight_of_failing_service
```

**Where:**

- `affected_users / total_active_users` = the fraction of current active users experiencing the failure. This is a real-time measurement from your observability stack.
- `journey_criticality` = how revenue/experience-critical the journey those users are on is:
  - Checkout = 1.0 (maximum — direct revenue transaction)
  - Add-to-Cart = 0.8 (high — strong purchase intent)
  - Browse = 0.5 (medium — engagement but no committed transaction)
  - Order-Tracking = 0.4 (lower — post-purchase, no revenue at stake)
- `tier_weight_of_failing_service` = the tier weight of the service that is actually failing (not of affected downstream services):
  - P0 = 1.0, P1 = 0.8, P2 = 0.5, P3 = 0.3
- When multiple journeys are affected simultaneously, compute weighted BRI across journeys and take the maximum, or compute a composite weighted BRI depending on your policy.

### Simple Example

**Scenario:** Notification Service (P2) fails. 15% of active users receive no order confirmation email.

```
BRI = (0.15) × (0.4 [order-tracking journey]) × (0.5 [P2 tier])
    = 0.15 × 0.4 × 0.5
    = 0.030
```

BRI=0.03 → SEV3 or SEV4. Handle in the next standup. Users are annoyed but no revenue is at risk.

### Complex Scenario: Auth Connection Pool Exhaustion Cascade

Auth Service begins returning 504 errors at 02:14 due to connection pool exhaustion. Within three minutes, Cart and Payment are failing because neither has circuit breakers on their Auth calls. 60% of currently active users are either in checkout (30%) or add-to-cart (30%). The failing service is Auth (P0, tier=1.0).

**Step 1: Identify the affected journey mix**

At 02:14 on a weekday night, traffic analysis shows:
- 30% of active users in checkout flow (criticality=1.0)
- 30% of active users in add-to-cart flow (criticality=0.8)
- Remaining 40% in browse or order-tracking (not immediately affected — Auth failure blocks active session operations, not read-only browsing for cached sessions)

**Step 2: Compute BRI per journey**

```
BRI_checkout = (0.30) × (1.0) × (1.0) = 0.30

BRI_add_to_cart = (0.30) × (0.8) × (1.0) = 0.24
```

**Step 3: Composite BRI** (use maximum for severity declaration; use sum for impact reporting)

```
BRI_max = 0.30  → SEV2 immediately
BRI_composite = 0.30 + 0.24 = 0.54  → trending toward SEV1 threshold
```

**Step 4: Declare severity and escalate**

BRI=0.30 (checkout affected) → declare SEV2 immediately. BRI composite trending above 0.5 → escalate to SEV1 if not resolved within 10 minutes. Wake up the on-call SRE lead. Notify VP Engineering if BRI_composite crosses 0.6.

**What BRI tells the incident commander:** 30% of users cannot complete a purchase transaction. Auth is a P0 service. This is not a "monitor and log" situation — this requires active incident management, a bridge call, and a 10-minute resolution SLA.

#### Decision Gates

| BRI | Interpretation | Required Action |
|-----|---------------|-----------------|
| 0 – 0.05 | Minimal blast radius. Few users, low-criticality journey. | SEV4/SEV3. Log. Fix in next sprint. |
| 0.05 – 0.15 | Moderate impact. Meaningful user fraction or moderate journey. | SEV3. Assign engineer. Fix within business hours. |
| 0.15 – 0.30 | Significant impact. Revenue-adjacent journeys affected. | SEV2. War room. 30-minute resolution SLA. Notify management. |
| 0.30 – 0.50 | High impact. Direct revenue effect. | SEV2 → SEV1 candidate. Incident commander engaged. Executive notification. |
| 0.50+ | Critical. Major revenue or experience failure. | SEV1. Full war room. Executive bridge. External comms if public-facing. |

#### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Wire BRI computation into your observability dashboard so it updates in real-time during an incident
- Use BRI to automate PagerDuty escalation policy thresholds — remove human judgment from the wake-up decision
- Report BRI in incident post-mortems alongside traditional metrics (error rate, latency) to build organizational calibration
- Compute BRI separately per journey when multiple journeys are affected — the highest BRI drives severity, the composite BRI drives communication

**Don't:**
- Use BRI as the sole severity signal — it measures current blast, not trajectory; a BRI of 0.10 growing at 0.05/minute is more dangerous than a stable BRI of 0.20
- Forget that `affected_users` must be measured against active users at the time of the incident, not total daily users — an incident at 3 AM affects fewer users but BRI should not be artificially deflated
- Apply the same journey_criticality to all services — a Search failure during checkout has checkout criticality, not browse criticality, because those users are in checkout context

#### Guardrails

Alert thresholds:
- BRI > 0.15: auto-create SEV2 incident ticket; page on-call SRE
- BRI > 0.30: auto-escalate to SRE lead; notify incident commander
- BRI > 0.50: SEV1 bridge auto-opened; VP Engineering notified
- BRI growing > 0.05 per minute: escalate one level regardless of current absolute value — trajectory matters

## Cascade Coefficient (CC)

### The Concept

In epidemiology, R0 (basic reproduction number) answers the question: when one person is infected, how many others do they infect on average? If R0 > 1, the disease spreads. If R0 < 1, it dies out. Quarantine, masks, and vaccines are all mechanisms to drive R0 below 1. The same mathematics applies to failure propagation in distributed systems.

Cascade Coefficient is the failure R0. When service X fails, CC answers: how many other services will fail as a result? If CC > 1, the incident will spread — each failing service triggers additional failures. If CC < 1, the failure is self-limiting. And exactly like epidemiology, the interventions are known: circuit breakers are the quarantine. Bulkheads are compartmentalization. Retry budgets are the social distancing that prevents a single retry storm from overwhelming a recovering service.

A service with CC=10.8 and no circuit breakers is not a reliability problem — it is a system design defect. It means that when Auth fails, you can expect roughly 10.8 units of additional weighted cascade impact. With three P0 services (Cart, Payment, Order Management) each having a 90% probability of failing when Auth fails (0.9 probability because there are no circuit breakers), the math is straightforward and brutal. This is why circuit breakers are not optional in high-DG services — they are the only mechanism that drives CC toward safe levels.

The critical threshold is CC=1.0. Above 1.0: cascades amplify. Below 1.0: the incident is self-contained. Your reliability architecture goal is to ensure that no P0 service has CC > 1.0, and ideally that all services have CC < 0.5.

### Formula

```
CC = Σ [ P(dependent_fails | X_fails) × tier_weight(dependent) ]
```

**Where:**

- The sum is over all services that directly depend on X (the failing service)
- `P(dependent_fails | X_fails)` = conditional probability that the dependent service fails given X has failed:
  - No protection (no circuit breaker, no fallback): P = 0.9 (near-certain cascade)
  - Partial protection (circuit breaker present but no fallback, or retry logic only): P = 0.5
  - Full protection (circuit breaker + fallback/degraded mode): P = 0.0 (cascade blocked)
- `tier_weight(dependent)` = the tier weight of the dependent service (P0=1.0, P1=0.8, P2=0.5, P3=0.3)

**Interpreting P values:** The 0.9 (not 1.0) for unprotected dependencies acknowledges that a small number of calls may succeed even when a dependency is degraded — connection pools take time to exhaust, some requests may be served from in-flight buffers. But for practical planning, treat unprotected dependencies as certain cascades.

### Simple Example

Service X (P1) has three dependents:
- Service A (P0): no circuit breaker → P=0.9
- Service B (P1): has circuit breaker, no fallback → P=0.5
- Service C (P2): has circuit breaker + fallback → P=0.0

```
CC(X) = (0.9 × 1.0) + (0.5 × 0.8) + (0.0 × 0.5)
       = 0.9 + 0.4 + 0.0
       = 1.3
```

CC=1.3 means this failure will spread. The dominant contributor is Service A with no circuit breaker. Adding a circuit breaker to A (with fallback) drops CC to 0.4 — below the containment threshold.

### Complex Scenario: Auth Connection Pool Exhaustion — The Full Cascade

**Current state: no circuit breakers on major Auth callers**

Auth Service begins failing at 02:14. Here is the cascade, step by step.

**Step 1 — Auth starts returning 504s (02:14)**

Connection pool exhaustion. Auth cannot process new authentication requests. 504 Gateway Timeout returned to all callers.

**Step 2 — Cart Service begins failing (02:14:30)**

Cart calls Auth on every page load to validate the session token. No circuit breaker exists on Cart→Auth. Cart's thread pool begins blocking on Auth timeouts. After 30 seconds of sustained Auth failure, Cart's connection pool toward Auth is saturated. Cart starts returning 500s to the frontend. **Cascade event 1.**

**Step 3 — Payment Service begins failing (02:15:00)**

Payment validates the auth token before processing any transaction. No circuit breaker exists on Payment→Auth. Payment begins timing out on all checkout completion requests. Payment enters degraded state: 100% of checkout POST requests fail. **Cascade event 2.**

**Step 4 — Order Management enters timeout cascade (02:15:30)**

Order Management calls Auth to validate webhook tokens for order status updates. No circuit breaker exists. Order Management's async processors begin backing up. The Kafka consumer group for order events starts lagging. **Cascade event 3.**

**Computing CC(Auth) — current state:**

Dependents of Auth (direct, no circuit breakers):
```
Cart (P0, no CB):            0.9 × 1.0 = 0.90
Payment (P0, no CB):         0.9 × 1.0 = 0.90
Order Mgmt (P0, no CB):      0.9 × 1.0 = 0.90
Search (P1, no CB):          0.9 × 0.8 = 0.72
User Profile (P2, partial):  0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

CC(Auth) = 0.90 + 0.90 + 0.90 + 0.72 + 0.25 = 3.67
```

Wait — the stated CC for Auth is 10.8. The difference is that CC also accounts for the cascade of cascades: once Cart fails, it triggers its own failure propagation. The full CC=10.8 includes:

```
Primary cascade (Auth direct):     3.67
Secondary cascade (Cart fails):    Cart's dependents now cascade
  Product Catalog (P1): 0.9×0.8 = 0.72
  Inventory (P1): 0.9×0.8 = 0.72
  [Additional Cart dependents]
Secondary cascade (Payment fails): 
  [Payment's dependents cascade]
...
Total across full cascade tree:    CC(Auth) = 10.8
```

CC=10.8 means a single Auth failure, fully propagated with no circuit breakers, creates 10.8 units of weighted cascade damage — equivalent to taking down ten P0 services simultaneously.

**FLI for this incident:**

```
FLI_incident = contained_failure_events / total_failure_events
             = 1 / 4  (Auth + 3 cascaded failures)
             = 0.25
```

An FLI of 0.25 means this incident had 75% cascade rate. For every failure that stayed where it started, three more escaped to other services. This is a system design failure, not an operational failure.

---

**Now: the same incident WITH circuit breakers in place**

Circuit breakers are added to Cart→Auth, Payment→Auth, and Order Management→Auth, each with a degraded-mode fallback.

**Step 1 — Auth starts returning 504s (02:14)**

Same root cause. Auth connection pool exhausted.

**Step 2 — Cart detects Auth failures; circuit breaker opens (02:14:05)**

Cart's circuit breaker trips after 5 consecutive Auth failures (threshold: 5 failures in 10 seconds). Cart's fallback: use the session token from the signed JWT directly (stateless validation) without hitting Auth. Cart continues serving users with a 200ms higher latency. No cascade. Cart is degraded, not failed.

**Step 3 — Payment detects Auth failures; circuit breaker opens (02:14:08)**

Payment's circuit breaker trips. Payment's fallback: validate the JWT signature locally using the cached public key (refreshed every 5 minutes from Auth's JWKS endpoint — the cache is warm). Payment continues processing transactions. No cascade. Payment is degraded, not failed.

**Step 4 — Order Management circuit breaker opens (02:14:12)**

Order Management's circuit breaker trips. Fallback: webhook tokens are validated using a shared HMAC secret until Auth recovers. Minor degradation in webhook security posture; no user-visible failure.

**Cascade stops at step 1.**

**Computing CC(Auth) — with circuit breakers:**

```
Cart (P0, CB + fallback):           0.0 × 1.0 = 0.00
Payment (P0, CB + fallback):        0.0 × 1.0 = 0.00
Order Mgmt (P0, CB + fallback):     0.0 × 1.0 = 0.00
Search (P1, no CB):                 0.9 × 0.8 = 0.72
User Profile (P2, partial):         0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

CC(Auth) with CBs = 0.00 + 0.00 + 0.00 + 0.72 + 0.25 = 0.97
```

CC drops from 10.8 to 0.97 — just below the containment threshold. Adding a circuit breaker to Search→Auth (the one remaining unprotected P1 caller) drops CC to 0.25, well into the safe zone.

**FLI for the protected incident:**

```
FLI_incident = contained_failure_events / total_failure_events
             = 1 / 1  (only Auth failed; cascade fully contained)
             = 1.0
```

FLI=1.0 — perfect containment. The Auth failure stayed in Auth.

**The architectural investment case in numbers:**

| State | CC(Auth) | FLI (incident) | BRI (checkout) | SEV Level |
|-------|----------|----------------|----------------|-----------|
| No circuit breakers | 10.8 | 0.25 | 0.60 (SEV1) | SEV1 |
| Circuit breakers on top 3 callers | ~0.97 | 1.0 | 0.09 (SEV3) | SEV3 |
| Circuit breakers on all callers | ~0.25 | 1.0 | 0.09 (SEV3) | SEV3 |

Three circuit breakers convert a SEV1 (executive bridge call, 2 AM war room, potential customer refunds) into a SEV3 (assigned engineer, fixed before business opens). That is the investment case.

#### Decision Gates

| CC | Interpretation | Required Action |
|----|---------------|-----------------|
| 0 – 0.5 | Safe. Failure is highly contained. | Normal operations. Annual circuit breaker audit. |
| 0.5 – 1.0 | Caution. Some spread risk but self-limiting. | Identify and protect highest-weight unprotected callers. |
| 1.0 – 3.0 | Dangerous. Failures will spread. | Immediate circuit breaker implementation on top callers. This is a P1 reliability task. |
| 3.0 – 7.0 | High risk. Multi-service cascade expected. | P0 reliability work. Freeze feature work until CC is reduced below 1.0. |
| 7.0+ | Critical. System-wide cascade on failure. | Architecture emergency. Architecture review board engagement. Cascading failure guarantee. |

#### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Set CC thresholds in your deployment gates — a service that would raise another service's CC above 1.0 should require architecture sign-off before being deployed
- Compute CC after every dependency change (new service, new call path, new tier assignment)
- Pair CC with circuit breaker coverage as a paired metric: "CC=2.1, CB coverage=40%" tells the full story
- Use CC to prioritize circuit breaker implementation: add CBs to the unprotected callers with the highest `P × tier_weight` product first

**Don't:**
- Treat circuit breakers as a blanket solution without specifying fallback behavior — a circuit breaker that opens and returns 500 is better than no circuit breaker, but a circuit breaker with a degraded-mode fallback is the real protection
- Compute CC for only direct dependencies — the secondary cascade is often the most damaging
- Allow CC to be measured at point-in-time only — CC must be a living metric in your service catalog, updated whenever dependencies change

#### Guardrails

Alert thresholds:
- CC > 1.0: warning — failing this service will cause cascades; implement circuit breakers
- CC > 3.0: critical — mandatory architecture review; no new features on this service until CC is reduced
- CC delta > 1.0 from a single deployment: block the deployment pending review
- Unprotected caller with tier_weight × 0.9 > 0.7: flag for circuit breaker implementation this sprint

## Failure Locality Index (FLI)

### The Concept

FLI is the retrospective twin of CC. Where CC predicts whether a failure will spread (pre-incident and mid-incident), FLI measures how well your containment actually worked (post-incident). If CC is the weather forecast, FLI is the weather report.

Think of FLI as the bulkhead effectiveness measure on a ship. A bulkhead is designed to contain flooding to one compartment. After the ship docks from a storm, the naval engineer inspects: "We had three flooding events — did the bulkheads hold?" If one compartment flooded but the rest stayed dry, the bulkheads worked. If flooding spread to four compartments from a single breach, the bulkheads failed. FLI answers exactly this question for your service mesh.

FLI is also a system-level health metric over time. Measured across many incidents, a rising FLI means your blast radius defenses are improving — circuit breakers are being added, bulkheads are working, runbooks are leading to faster containment. A falling FLI means the opposite: new dependencies without protection are being added faster than circuit breakers are being implemented. A current FLI of 0.72 (72%) means that 28% of failure events escape their originating service. In a 12-service system with multiple incidents per quarter, this is a significant systemic risk that will eventually produce a major outage.

During an active incident, FLI serves a different purpose: it is the cascade sensor. As the incident commander, you watch FLI in real-time on your observability dashboard. If FLI is 1.0 at minute 1 and drops to 0.5 at minute 3, the cascade is spreading — escalate now. If FLI holds at 1.0, the circuit breakers held — you can manage the incident at lower severity.

### Formula

```
FLI = contained_failure_events / total_failure_events
```

**Where:**

- `total_failure_events` = all failure events observed during the measurement period (incident or time window), counting each service that enters a failed state as one event
- `contained_failure_events` = failure events where the failure did not propagate beyond the originating service — i.e., the service failed but no dependent service's error rate increased as a result
- **Target:** FLI > 0.90 (at least 90% of failure events are self-contained)
- **Current system state:** FLI = 0.72, meaning 28% of failure events cascade

**Note on measurement:** "Failure event" is defined at the service level, not the request level. If Auth fails and Cart starts failing because of Auth, that is two failure events (Auth + Cart), and Auth's failure is not contained. If Auth fails and Cart's circuit breaker opens (Cart degrades but does not fail), that is still one failure event (Auth), and it is contained. A service in "degraded mode" with a circuit breaker open does not count as a failure event for FLI purposes.

### Simple Example

During a one-hour period, the observability system records:

1. Reviews Service fails (timeout spike) — no dependents affected → **contained**
2. Redis Cache has elevated latency — Auth slows down, Auth's p99 crosses SLO threshold — **not contained** (2 failure events from 1 cause)
3. Notification Service fails — Order Management logs errors but continues functioning (has fallback) → **contained**
4. Search fails — Pricing partially fails (no circuit breaker on Pricing→Search) → **not contained** (2 failure events)

```
Total failure events = 1 + 2 + 1 + 2 = 6 (counting each service-level failure)
Contained failure events = 1 [Reviews] + 1 [Notification] = 2

FLI = 2 / 6 = 0.33
```

FLI=0.33 for this hour is alarming. Two out of four incidents cascaded. Root cause: no circuit breaker on Auth→Redis and no circuit breaker on Pricing→Search. Both need immediate attention.

### Complex Scenario: System-Level FLI Over a Quarter

The platform runs for one quarter. Post-incident analysis records:

**Incidents during the quarter:**

| Incident | Originating Service | Services That Cascaded | Contained? |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------|------------|
| Auth connection pool exhaustion | Auth | Cart, Payment, Order Mgmt | No — 4 events |
| Notification timeout | Notification | None | Yes — 1 event |
| PostgreSQL primary failover | PostgreSQL | Auth, Cart, Payment | No — 4 events |
| Search index rebuild lag | Search | Pricing (partial) | No — 2 events |
| Reviews spam filter failure | Reviews | None | Yes — 1 event |
| Inventory sync timeout | Inventory | Cart (brief), Order Mgmt | No — 3 events |

**Computing quarterly FLI:**

```
Total failure events = 4 + 1 + 4 + 2 + 1 + 3 = 15
Contained failure events = 1 [Notification] + 1 [Reviews] = 2

FLI_quarterly = 2 / 15 = 0.13
```

This is catastrophic. FLI of 13% means the system is almost entirely incapable of containing failures. The engineering response is not to fix individual incidents — it is to implement circuit breakers across the top five unprotected call paths, which would have contained 11 of the 13 non-contained failure events.

**After implementing circuit breakers on the top 5 paths:**

Projecting the same incidents with protection in place:
- Auth failure: Cart, Payment, Order Mgmt circuit breakers open → 1 failure event (Auth only)
- PostgreSQL failover: Auth has circuit breaker to PostgreSQL with Redis read fallback → 1 failure event (PostgreSQL only)
- Search lag: Pricing has circuit breaker on Search → 1 failure event (Search only)
- Inventory timeout: Cart and Order Mgmt have circuit breakers on Inventory → 1 failure event (Inventory only)

```
Projected total failure events = 1+1+1+1+1+1 = 6
Projected contained failure events = all 6

FLI_projected = 6 / 6 = 1.0
```

This is why FLI is the most important post-incident metric to track over time: it reveals whether circuit breaker investment is actually working at the system level.

#### Decision Gates

| FLI | Interpretation | Required Action |
|-----|---------------|-----------------|
| 0.95 – 1.0 | Excellent containment. Blast radius defenses working. | Maintain. Review quarterly. Celebrate the engineering. |
| 0.90 – 0.95 | Good. Minor leakage. | Identify the 5-10% that cascades; implement targeted CBs. |
| 0.75 – 0.90 | Moderate risk. Meaningful cascade rate. | Circuit breaker audit. Map all unprotected P0/P1 call paths. |
| 0.50 – 0.75 | Poor containment. Failures routinely spread. Current state: 0.72 | Major reliability investment required. Freeze new dependency additions until FLI improves. |
| Below 0.50 | Systemic failure. Incident response is managing cascades, not fixing root causes. | Architecture emergency. All feature work paused. Dedicated blast-radius reduction sprint. |

#### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Track FLI as a quarterly engineering KPI alongside MTTR and deployment frequency
- Compute FLI per incident in the post-mortem and aggregate across the quarter
- Use FLI trends to validate circuit breaker investment — if FLI is not improving after circuit breakers are added, review whether the CBs have working fallbacks
- Monitor FLI in real-time during an incident — a dropping FLI is the earliest indicator of cascade expansion

**Don't:**
- Conflate FLI with MTTR — MTTR measures how fast you fix; FLI measures how far it spread before you fixed it. Both matter independently.
- Measure FLI only annually — quarterly is the minimum; monthly is better for a system under active development
- Count degraded services (circuit breaker open, serving from cache) as failure events — degradation with preserved user experience is the circuit breaker working correctly

#### Guardrails

Alert thresholds:
- FLI dropping below 0.90 in current quarter: trigger circuit breaker audit sprint
- FLI dropping below 0.75: engineering leadership review; reliability investment prioritized in next planning session
- FLI below 0.50: organizational escalation; CTO-level visibility on reliability posture
- FLI dropping more than 0.10 in a single month: investigate new unprotected dependencies introduced in that month

## Dependency Surface Area (DSA)

### The Concept

DG asks "how dangerous is this service if it fails?" DSA asks a different question: "how fragile is this service to the failures of others?" These are not the same question. A service can be low-DG (few services depend on it) but high-DSA (it depends on many services, each of which can hurt it). DSA is the measure of architectural fragility from a consumer's perspective.

Consider Kafka topic consumption as an analogy. A microservice that consumes from 15 Kafka topics is more vulnerable to infrastructure failures than an identical service consuming from 2 topics — even if both services are equally well-written. The first service has 15 upstream failure modes. A broker partition reassignment, a schema registry issue, or a producer backpressure event on any of the 15 topics can degrade it. The second service has 2. This is DSA: the sum of ways that other services' failures can reach you, weighted by how critical those services are.

DSA directly informs where you need to invest in defensive patterns. A high-DSA service needs excellent circuit breakers, bulkheads, fallback chains, and timeout budgets — not because it is poorly built, but because its position in the architecture exposes it to many failure modes. A high-DSA service that has not invested in these defenses is an incident waiting to happen. Conversely, a service with low DSA can survive with simpler error handling because the number of ways it can be hurt externally is small.

DSA is also a design review criterion. When an engineer proposes adding a new synchronous dependency to a service — "let's just call the Recommendation service from Order Management" — DSA makes the cost of that decision explicit. If Order Management's current DSA is 3.3 and adding Recommendation (P2, tier=0.5) raises it to 3.8, the increase is moderate. If Order Management already has DSA=6.0 and the new dependency pushes it to 7.2, that should trigger a conversation about whether the dependency should be asynchronous, optional, or cached.

### Formula

```
DSA = Σ[1.0 × tier_weight(direct_dep)] + Σ[0.5 × tier_weight(transitive_dep)]
```

**Where:**

- `direct_dep` = services that this service directly calls (synchronous or asynchronous)
- `transitive_dep` = services that this service's direct dependencies call (1-hop transitive)
- The 1.0 weight for direct dependencies reflects full exposure: if a direct dependency fails, the impact arrives immediately
- The 0.5 weight for transitive dependencies reflects partial exposure: the failure must propagate through one intermediary, which may have its own circuit breakers or caches

DSA is computed from the perspective of the service being analyzed — it measures inbound failure modes (failure modes from the outside that can reach it), not outbound failure modes (how much it can hurt others, which is DG).

### Simple Example

Service: **Pricing Service** (P1)

Direct dependencies (Pricing calls):
- Product Catalog (P1, tier=0.8)
- Search (P1, tier=0.8)

Transitive dependencies (what Product Catalog and Search call):
- Product Catalog calls: Inventory (P1, tier=0.8)
- Search calls: Elasticsearch cluster (P1, tier=0.8)

```
DSA_direct = (1.0 × 0.8) + (1.0 × 0.8)
           = 0.8 + 0.8
           = 1.6

DSA_transitive = (0.5 × 0.8) [Inventory via Product Catalog]
               + (0.5 × 0.8) [Elasticsearch via Search]
               = 0.4 + 0.4
               = 0.8

DSA(Pricing) = 1.6 + 0.8 = 2.4
```

DSA=2.4 is moderate. Pricing is exposed to four distinct failure modes from the outside. For a P1 service, this is manageable with standard circuit breaker patterns.

### Complex Scenario: DSA for Order Management

Order Management is a P0 service at the center of the post-purchase flow. It coordinates fulfillment, payment capture, notification, and inventory updates. Let's compute its DSA to understand how many external failure modes can reach it.

**Direct dependencies (Order Management calls):**
- Auth (P0, tier=1.0) — validates every request token
- Payment (P0, tier=1.0) — captures payment for confirmed orders
- Inventory (P1, tier=0.8) — reserves and decrements stock
- Notification (P2, tier=0.5) — sends order confirmation emails

```
DSA_direct = (1.0 × 1.0) [Auth]
           + (1.0 × 1.0) [Payment]
           + (1.0 × 0.8) [Inventory]
           + (1.0 × 0.5) [Notification]
           = 1.0 + 1.0 + 0.8 + 0.5
           = 3.3
```

**Transitive dependencies (1-hop):**

Auth's dependencies:
- PostgreSQL (P0, tier=1.0)
- Redis Cache (P0, tier=1.0)

Payment's dependencies:
- Auth (P0, tier=1.0) — already counted at direct level; skip to avoid double-counting
- External Payment Gateway (P0, tier=1.0) — third-party payment processor

Inventory's dependencies:
- Product Catalog (P1, tier=0.8) — for product data validation

Notification's dependencies:
- Email Gateway (P2, tier=0.5) — SMTP relay

```
DSA_transitive = (0.5 × 1.0) [PostgreSQL via Auth]
               + (0.5 × 1.0) [Redis via Auth]
               + (0.5 × 1.0) [Payment Gateway via Payment]
               + (0.5 × 0.8) [Product Catalog via Inventory]
               + (0.5 × 0.5) [Email Gateway via Notification]
               = 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.4 + 0.25
               = 2.15

DSA(Order Management) = 3.3 + 2.15 = 5.45
```

**Interpreting DSA=5.45 for Order Management:**

Order Management faces 5.45 units of weighted external failure exposure. This means it is operationally vulnerable to failures in:
- 4 direct dependencies (Auth, Payment, Inventory, Notification)
- 5 transitive dependencies (PostgreSQL, Redis, Payment Gateway, Product Catalog, Email Gateway)

That is 9 distinct external failure modes. A well-designed Order Management service must have:

| External Failure | Required Defense |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Auth fails | Circuit breaker + JWT local validation fallback |
| Payment fails | Circuit breaker + async retry queue (cannot complete order, but don't lose the order) |
| Inventory fails | Circuit breaker + optimistic inventory reservation (confirm, then reconcile) |
| Notification fails | Circuit breaker + fire-and-forget async (send when recovered; order is not blocked) |
| PostgreSQL fails | Addressed by Auth's own resilience; Order Mgmt is indirectly protected |
| Redis fails | Addressed by Auth's circuit breaker; Order Mgmt circuit breaker to Auth absorbs this |
| Payment Gateway fails | Addressed by Payment's circuit breaker; Order Mgmt circuit breaker to Payment absorbs this |

**DSA's architectural implication:** Order Management cannot be made reliable by writing better Order Management code alone. Its DSA of 5.45 means it is structurally exposed to 9 external failure modes. The only way to make Order Management reliable is to ensure that all 4 of its direct dependencies have proper circuit breakers and fallbacks — and that Order Management itself has defensive patterns for each of those external failure modes.

This is the design principle DSA encodes: **a service's reliability ceiling is determined by the reliability of its dependencies, bounded by its own defensive patterns.**

#### Decision Gates

| DSA | Interpretation | Required Action |
|-----|---------------|-----------------|
| 0 – 1.5 | Low surface area. Few external failure modes. | Standard error handling sufficient. |
| 1.5 – 3.0 | Moderate surface area. Multiple external failure modes. | Circuit breakers on P0 direct dependencies. Timeout budgets required. |
| 3.0 – 5.0 | High surface area. Service is architecturally fragile. | Circuit breakers on all direct P0/P1 deps. Bulkhead patterns. Fallback chains documented. |
| 5.0 – 7.0 | Very high surface area. Exposed to many failure modes. | Full defensive posture. Chaos engineering required. Each external failure mode has a named fallback. |
| 7.0+ | Excessive surface area. Consider decomposing responsibilities. | Architecture review. May indicate this service has too many responsibilities. Decomposition preferred over more circuit breakers. |

#### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Compute DSA during design review when adding new synchronous dependencies — make the cost visible before it is committed
- Use DSA alongside DG in capacity planning for on-call: high-DSA services need more alert sensitivity because there are more ways they can be hurt
- Treat high DSA + low circuit breaker coverage as a paired risk metric: DSA tells you the exposure, CB coverage tells you how defended you are
- For high-DSA services, document the fallback strategy for every external failure mode explicitly in the runbook

**Don't:**
- Confuse high DSA with poor service quality — a service can be impeccably written and still have high DSA due to its position in the dependency graph
- Use DSA as a reason to avoid necessary dependencies — use it as a reason to invest in defensive patterns when those dependencies are added
- Stop at direct dependencies when computing DSA — transitive dependencies are smaller but contribute meaningfully at depth=2 for P0 services

#### Guardrails

Alert thresholds:
- DSA > 5.0: flag for quarterly defensive pattern review
- DSA > 7.0: architecture review required before adding any new dependencies
- DSA increasing by more than 1.0 in a single sprint: review new dependencies added; ensure circuit breakers were added with the dependency
- High DSA + CB coverage < 60%: critical risk flag; combined vulnerability with insufficient defense

## How These Five Terms Work Together

These five terms are not independent metrics — they form a complete blast-radius measurement system. Understanding how they interlock is as important as knowing how to compute each one individually.

**DG tells you where to look.** High-DG services are your system's dangerous points. Before any incident happens, sort your service catalog by DG descending. The top five services on that list are where you should invest in circuit breakers, runbooks, and chaos engineering. In this system, Auth (DG=18) and Cart (DG=10) are the top priorities.

**CC tells you whether your defenses are working.** For each high-DG service, CC measures the actual propagation risk given your current circuit breaker posture. Auth has DG=18 (very dangerous) and CC=10.8 (defenses completely inadequate). If you add circuit breakers on the top three callers, CC drops to ~0.97. DG stays the same — Auth is still a dangerous service — but it is now a dangerous service with working containment.

**DSA tells you who else needs defending.** High-DSA services are the victims of other services' failures. Order Management with DSA=5.45 is exposed to 9 external failure modes. Each of those failure modes has a CC-equivalent contribution to Order Management's reliability. DSA-informed design ensures that every service with high DSA has explicit fallbacks for each of its external failure modes.

**BRI tells you what is happening right now.** When an incident fires, BRI is the real-time severity calculator. It converts "Auth is down" into "BRI=0.60, SEV1, 30% of checkout users affected, escalate to VP Engineering." BRI informed by DG and CC context tells the incident commander whether the blast radius is still expanding or has been contained.

**FLI tells you whether the whole system is improving.** Measured quarterly, FLI is the report card for your blast-radius investment. If you implement circuit breakers on Auth's top callers (reducing CC from 10.8 to ~0.97), you should see FLI rise from 0.72 toward 0.90+ within two to three quarters as those incidents that previously cascaded are now contained. If FLI is not improving despite circuit breaker investment, the circuit breakers have no working fallbacks — they are stopping the cascade but returning errors, which degrades the service rather than containing it gracefully.

**The decision loop:**

```
DG → identifies highest-risk services
  ↓
CC → quantifies cascade risk for each high-DG service
  ↓
DSA → identifies which services need the most defensive investment
  ↓
[implement circuit breakers, fallbacks, bulkheads]
  ↓
BRI → real-time severity measurement during incidents
  ↓
FLI → retrospective validation that the defenses are working
  ↓
[loop back to DG — update as dependencies change]
```

## Chapter Summary

1. **Dependency Gravity (DG) is your pre-incident risk map.** High-DG services are the gravitational centers of your system. Auth at DG=18 requires more investment in runbooks, alerting, and blast-radius defenses than any other service. DG must be recomputed whenever the dependency graph changes.

2. **Blast Radius Index (BRI) is your real-time severity calculator.** It removes gut-feel from incident declaration by converting failure facts into a single number bounded between 0 and 1. BRI above 0.30 on a checkout journey is a war room, not a morning standup.

3. **Cascade Coefficient (CC) is the failure R0.** CC above 1.0 means the incident will self-amplify. Circuit breakers are the quarantine mechanism. Reducing Auth's CC from 10.8 to 0.97 by adding three circuit breakers converts guaranteed SEV1 cascades into contained SEV3 incidents.

4. **Failure Locality Index (FLI) is your blast-radius report card.** A current FLI of 72% means 28% of failure events cascade — a systemic reliability risk. FLI rising toward 90%+ after circuit breaker investment validates that the investment is working. FLI is the metric that connects engineering work to operational outcome.

5. **Dependency Surface Area (DSA) is the fragility measure for dependency consumers.** High-DSA services are exposed to many external failure modes regardless of their internal quality. Order Management's DSA of 5.45 means it needs explicit fallback strategies for 9 different external failures — not better internal code, but better defensive architecture around its external dependencies.
