# Chapter 9: Daily Practice — The Framework in Action

> "A framework that only works in planning meetings is a philosophy. A framework that works at 2 AM with your phone in your hand is a tool."

---

## How to Use This Chapter

This chapter is a working handbook. Its five sections cover the five most common SRE activities where the framework changes behavior in real time:

1. [Incident Triage](#activity-1-incident-triage) — first 5 minutes of an incident
2. [CAB Call](#activity-2-cab-call) — 15-minute change review
3. [Blameless Postmortem](#activity-3-blameless-postmortem) — structured incident review
4. [Root Cause Analysis](#activity-4-root-cause-analysis) — complex multi-service investigation
5. [Error Budget Review](#activity-5-error-budget-review) — monthly accountability meeting

Each section is formatted for **reference during the actual activity** — not just study before it. The decision tables, communication scripts, and templates are designed to be open on a second monitor while the event is in progress.

For each activity, you will find:
- What this activity is and when it happens
- What goes wrong without the framework
- The framework-driven protocol (numbered, formula at each step)
- Decision table (score → action)
- Communication script (what to say to EM, VP, or on-call team)
- Shopping cart worked example
- Reusable template

## Activity 1: Incident Triage

### What This Is

Incident triage is the first 5 minutes of an incident. An alert fires, a customer reports a problem, or a teammate notices something wrong. The triage phase answers four questions in sequence: How bad is this? Who owns it? Is it spreading? What do we do right now?

Triage happens under time pressure, with incomplete information, and often at inconvenient hours. The quality of triage determines whether the next 30 minutes are controlled or chaotic.

### What Goes Wrong Without the Framework

Without structured triage, teams rely on instinct and experience — which varies by individual, degrades under stress, and produces inconsistent severity declarations. A junior SRE may under-declare severity because the alert "doesn't look that different from last week's noise." A senior SRE may over-declare because a similar-looking alert caused a major incident six months ago. Neither response is reliable.

The most common failure mode is spending the first 5 minutes investigating the *technical details* of the failure before answering the strategic question: *Who else needs to know right now?* A 10-minute delay in notifying the right team during a cascade incident is the difference between a 20-minute MTTR and a 2-hour one.

Without BRI, severity declarations are subjective. Without CC, the on-call SRE does not know whether to monitor one service or five. Without EBV, the team does not know whether to escalate now or can wait for business hours.

### The Five-Question Triage Protocol

Work through these questions in order. The output of each question drives the next action.

---

**Question 1 — BRI: What is the severity level?**

```
BRI = (affected_user_%) × criticality_score × tier_weight
```

Compute BRI immediately from available signals. If exact numbers are unavailable, use conservative estimates.

| BRI Value | Severity | Escalation |
|---|---|---|
| > 0.70 | SEV1 | Immediate VP notification; all-hands bridge; customer communication triggered |
| 0.40–0.70 | SEV2 | EM notification; dedicated bridge; customer support notified |
| 0.15–0.40 | SEV3 | Team notification; investigation in progress; EM aware |
| < 0.15 | SEV4 | Ticket created; investigate during business hours |

**Triage decision**: Declare severity level. Open bridge (SEV1/SEV2). Begin paging.

---

**Question 2 — EBV: Is there SLA urgency?**

```
EBV = normalized_error_rate / baseline_error_rate
Time_to_breach = budget_remaining_minutes / (EBV × baseline_burn_rate)
```

EBV tells you how fast you are burning through the error budget relative to normal. A high EBV does not always mean imminent SLA breach — it depends on how much budget remains.

| Time to Breach | Action |
|---|---|
| < 2 hours | Immediate escalation regardless of BRI. Customer SLA at risk. |
| 2–24 hours | Escalate within the hour. Notify EM. Track closely. |
| 24–72 hours | Inform EM. Monitor burn rate. No emergency escalation yet. |
| > 72 hours | Note in ticket. Investigate at normal pace. |

**Triage decision**: If time-to-breach < 2 hours, escalate severity regardless of BRI. Budget breach is contractual.

---

**Question 3 — CC: Is a cascade in progress or likely?**

```
CC = Σ[P(cascade_propagation) × tier_weight]
```

You do not have time to compute CC from scratch during triage. Use the pre-computed CC values from your service catalog (see template below). Look up the CC of the failing service.

| CC Value | Cascade Risk | Action |
|---|---|---|
| > 7.0 | High cascade risk | Immediately check all downstream dependents. Pre-page their on-call. |
| 3.0–7.0 | Moderate cascade risk | Check downstream dependents. Have their on-call ready to page. |
| < 3.0 | Low cascade risk | Monitor downstream. No pre-emptive paging unless symptoms appear. |

**Triage decision**: If CC > 7.0, do not wait for cascade symptoms to appear before pre-paging. By the time symptoms appear in a service with CC=10.8, the cascade is already in progress.

---

**Question 4 — FLI (observed): Is it spreading?**

You cannot compute FLI precisely during triage. Instead, take a fast field observation:

1. Check the 3–5 services most commonly associated with the failing service in your dependency graph
2. Look for any of: elevated error rate, p99 latency increase, reduced request volume (a sign the upstream is shedding load)

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Other services normal | Failure appears contained — continue monitoring but do not escalate cascade |
| 1–2 services showing symptoms | Early cascade — page their on-call now, do not wait |
| 3+ services affected | Full cascade in progress — SEV1 regardless of original BRI; all-hands bridge |

**Triage decision**: Observed cascade changes the severity declaration. Upgrade immediately.

---

**Question 5 — Layer: Who owns the fix?**

Match the symptom pattern to the fault layer. The correct layer identification determines which team to page.

| Symptom Pattern | Probable Layer | Team |
|---|---|---|
| 5xx errors from one service, others normal | Layer 3 — Application | Service owner team |
| Connection pool exhaustion, timeout storms | Layer 5 — Resource saturation (container) | Platform/SRE |
| DNS resolution failures, latency spikes across services | Layer 1 — Network/Service mesh | Network/Infra SRE |
| Queue buildup, consumer lag | Layer 4 — Middleware | Platform/messaging team |
| Database connection errors | Layer 4 — Data tier | DB/platform team |
| Pod OOMKill, container crash loops | Layer 5 — Container resource | Platform SRE |

**Triage decision**: Page the team that owns the layer, not the team that owns the service surface.

### Shopping Cart Worked Example — Auth 504s at 2 AM

**Setup**: 2:10 AM. PagerDuty fires: Auth service returning 504 Gateway Timeout, elevated rate. No other alerts yet.

**Question 1 — BRI**:
- Affected users: checkout is 504ing for users who hit Auth. Approximately 40% of active users in flight at 2 AM are in checkout. affected_user_% = 0.40
- Criticality: checkout is business-critical. criticality_score = 1.0
- Tier weight: Auth is T1. tier_weight = 1.0
- BRI = 0.40 × 1.0 × 1.0 = **0.40 → SEV2**

Action: Open bridge. Page EM. Auth on-call already paged by PagerDuty.

**Question 2 — EBV**:
- From service catalog: Auth baseline burn = 0.0005 min/min. Budget remaining = 8 min.
- Current EBV from monitoring = 6.0 (error rate is 6× baseline)
- Current burn rate = 6.0 × 0.0005 = 0.003 min/min
- Time to breach = 8 / 0.003 = 2,667 minutes ≈ 44 hours

No immediate SLA breach crisis. Budget is not at risk in the next few hours. SEV2 stands.

**Question 3 — CC**:
- From service catalog: CC(Auth) = **10.8**
- Decision: immediately check Cart and Payment for symptoms. Pre-page Cart on-call.

**Question 4 — Observed FLI**:
- Check Cart service: also returning 504s. Cart is cascading.
- Check Payment: beginning to show timeout errors.
- Observed: 2 services affected, cascade is in progress.

Decision: Upgrade to SEV1. All-hands bridge. Page Payment on-call.

**Question 5 — Layer**:
- Auth 504s with downstream connection pool errors visible in Splunk → Layer 5 (resource saturation at container level — connection pool exhaustion)
- Layer 3 might explain Auth's own failure, but the connection pool pattern is a container-level resource issue

Decision: Page Auth team AND Platform SRE. Auth team owns the application response; Platform SRE owns the container resource diagnosis.

**Total triage time**: 4 minutes 20 seconds.

### Triage Communication Script

Use this verbatim or adapt it. The goal: give EM and team the six facts they need to make decisions, in 60 seconds.

> "We have a SEV[X]. [Service] is [symptom] — [affected user %] of active users affected, BRI=[value]. Budget has [time-to-breach] before SLA breach — [urgent/not urgent]. CC=[value], [cascade status]. I've paged [team(s)]. Bridge is open. EM: you have [timeframe] before I recommend VP notification."

**Shopping cart example**:

> "We have a SEV1. Auth is 504ing — 40% of active users affected, BRI=0.40. Budget has 44 hours before SLA breach — not urgent by time but urgent by cascade. CC=10.8, cascade is active — Cart and Payment are showing errors. I've paged Auth, Cart, and Platform SRE. Bridge is open. EM: recommend VP notification in the next 5 minutes given cascade scope."

## Activity 2: CAB Call

### What This Is

A CAB (Change Advisory Board) call is a 15–30 minute structured review that occurs before any significant change is deployed to production. The CAB determines whether the change should be approved, conditionally approved, or deferred. In traditional organizations, a CAB call is a subjective discussion: "Does this seem risky? How confident are you? What could go wrong?" The answers depend on who is in the room.

A framework-driven CAB call replaces subjective discussion with seven objective computations. The change is approved or deferred based on scores, not confidence levels.

### What Goes Wrong Without the Framework

Without structured scoring, CAB calls fall into two failure modes:

**Mode 1: Rubber stamp**. The team is under delivery pressure. The CAB approves everything quickly because there is no systematic way to distinguish risky changes from safe ones. The risky change deploys, cascades, and becomes a 2 AM postmortem.

**Mode 2: Obstruction**. A change touches a service that had a bad incident last month. The CAB intuitively blocks it. But the change is actually low-risk — different component, different failure mode. The team loses a sprint slot and stakeholder trust. The CAB becomes a bottleneck rather than a gate.

The framework-driven CAB is fast (15 minutes), consistent (same criteria for every change, every week), and defensible (the SRE can explain exactly why a change was approved or deferred).

### The Seven-Question CAB Protocol

---

**Question 1 — MRI: What approval tier does this change require?**

```
MRI = DG × CD × (1 - FLI) × (2 - OCR)
```

Compute MRI for the service being changed. Use current system values (DG, CD, FLI, OCR from service catalog).

| MRI Value | Approval Tier | Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| < 2 | Tier 1 — Self-approval | SRE review; 30-min monitoring post-deploy |
| 2–5 | Tier 2 — Team approval | Team lead sign-off; standard CAB; SRE monitoring |
| 5–10 | Tier 3 — SRE gate | Mandatory CAB; SRE bridge during deploy; EM notification |
| > 10 | Tier 4 — Director gate | Director-level approval; phased rollout required; P0 incident protocol ready |

---

**Question 2 — EBR: Does the budget support this change?**

```
EBR = min(MRI × 0.05, 0.80)
```

EBR is the expected budget burn from this change, expressed as a fraction of monthly budget. Compare against available budget.

| Budget vs. EBR | Decision |
|---|---|
| budget_remaining > 3 × EBR | Approve without budget concern |
| budget_remaining 1–3 × EBR | Approve with enhanced monitoring; document budget risk |
| budget_remaining < EBR | Defer unless emergency; document explicit budget risk acceptance |

---

**Question 3 — RV: Is the maintenance window large enough?**

```
Required window ≥ 3 × RV (rollback_time)
```

The rollback time RV must be measured, not estimated. Run a practice rollback or use the last actual rollback time for this service.

| Window vs. 3×RV | Decision |
|---|---|
| Window ≥ 3 × RV | Window is sufficient |
| Window 2–3 × RV | Marginal — require SRE to be on bridge with pre-staged rollback |
| Window < 2 × RV | Defer — window is insufficient; reschedule with appropriate window |

---

**Question 4 — CSD: What is the change footprint?**

```
CSD = Σ[tier_weight × depth_coefficient]
```

CSD measures how broadly the change touches the system. A change that modifies one microservice in isolation has low CSD. A change that modifies a shared library used by fifteen services has high CSD.

Use CSD to set the monitoring scope: all services with tier_weight > 0.1 within the affected dependency graph should be monitored post-deploy.

---

**Question 5 — SCS: Does this change break SLO coherence?**

```
SCS = SLO_new^CD / SLA_target
```

If the change modifies a service's SLO (adds a new dependency hop, changes response time guarantees), recompute SCS post-change. SCS must remain in the range 0.95–1.10 after the change.

| Post-change SCS | Decision |
|---|---|
| 0.95–1.10 | SLO chain coherent — approve |
| < 0.95 | SLO chain undermined — change will degrade customer SLA even if services hit individual SLOs. Block. |
| > 1.10 | Over-specced — individual SLOs are tighter than necessary. Not a blocker, but worth noting. |

---

**Question 6 — DSS: What is the composite deployment safety score?**

```
DSS = (1 - SCV_norm) × (1 - MRI/10) × APR × (budget_remaining/total)
```

DSS integrates all current system state data into a single gate score. This is the most important number in the CAB call.

| DSS | Decision |
|---|---|
| > 0.60 | Approve: standard monitoring |
| 0.40–0.60 | Conditionally approve: SRE standby 30 min |
| 0.20–0.40 | Conditionally approve: SRE bridge; 1-hr window; check-in required |
| 0.10–0.20 | Elevated risk approval: EM notified; SRE bridge; rollback pre-staged |
| < 0.10 | Defer unless emergency with explicit executive risk acceptance |

---

**Question 7 — SCV: Is this service already volatile?**

```
SCV = frequency × CSAI × failure_rate
```

SCV measures whether this service has been failing frequently on recent changes. A high SCV means recent change history is bad — the service is in a volatile state where additional changes carry elevated failure risk.

| SCV | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 0.20 | Service is stable. Change history is clean. |
| 0.20–0.50 | Moderate volatility. Review recent change log before proceeding. |
| > 0.50 | High volatility. Require root cause of recent failures before approving new changes. |

---

### Shopping Cart Worked Example — Cart Service Code Update, Thursday 2 PM

**Change request**: Cart Service v2.4.1 — adds coupon code validation logic. No new dependencies. Cart's DG=8, CD=5, FLI=0.72, OCR=0.457. Current budget: 65% remaining. APR=0.31.

**Q1 — MRI**:
```
MRI = 8 × 5 × (1-0.72) × (2-0.457)
    = 8 × 5 × 0.28 × 1.543
    = 17.28
```
MRI=17.28 → **Tier 4 (Director gate)**. This is surprising for a routine coupon code update. The reason: the system is fragile (FLI=0.72, OCR=0.457). The change itself may be simple, but the *system* it is being deployed into is brittle.

**Q2 — EBR**:
```
EBR = min(17.28 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.864, 0.80) = 0.80
```
EBR=0.80. Budget remaining=65%. 65% > 80%? No — EBR requires 80% but only 65% remains. **Budget risk: marginal.** Approve with documented budget risk acknowledgment.

**Q3 — RV**:
Cart rollback time (last measured) = 18 minutes. Required window = 3 × 18 = 54 minutes.
Requested window: 90 minutes. 90 ≥ 54. **Window sufficient.**

**Q4 — CSD**:
Cart (T2, tier_weight=0.8). Depth coefficient for application layer change = 1.0. No shared libraries modified.
CSD = 0.8 × 1.0 = **0.80**. Monitoring scope: Cart, Auth (upstream caller), Payment (downstream dependency).

**Q5 — SCS**:
No SLO changes in this deployment. SCS unchanged from current value ≈ 1.0. **Pass.**

**Q6 — DSS**:
SCV_norm(Cart) = 0.10 (Cart has been stable recently).
```
DSS = (1-0.10) × (1-17.28/10) × 0.31 × 0.65
    = 0.90 × (-0.728) × 0.31 × 0.65
    = negative
```

DSS is **negative** because MRI=17.28 > 10. The MRI term (1-MRI/10) = (1-1.728) = -0.728.

This is the correct and sobering result: a routine coupon code update, in a system with FLI=0.72 and OCR=0.457, is not deployable by the formula. The system is too fragile.

**Q7 — SCV**:
Cart SCV = 0.10. **Low volatility.** The change itself is clean. The problem is not Cart — it is the system state.

**CAB Decision**: Defer until system state improves (FLI > 0.80, OCR > 0.70), OR obtain Director-level approval with explicit acknowledgment that DSS is negative due to system-level fragility, not change-level risk. If approved under exception: SRE bridge mandatory, 30-minute post-deploy hold before next change, pre-staged rollback with SRE finger on trigger.

## Activity 3: Blameless Postmortem

### What This Is

A postmortem is a structured review that occurs after a significant incident is resolved. Its goal is to learn from what happened, not to assign blame. The traditional blameless postmortem asks: "What happened? Why did it happen? What do we do about it?" The framework-driven postmortem replaces open-ended discussion with six formula-driven questions that produce prioritized, defensible action items.

### What Goes Wrong Without the Framework

Traditional postmortems produce action items by committee. The team discusses "what went wrong" for 45 minutes, then writes 7–12 action items in the last 10 minutes, prioritized by whoever is most vocal. The top action items are typically the most *memorable* failure modes from the incident, not the ones with the highest return on investment.

Three months later, 40% of action items are still open. The ones that got closed were the easy ones, not the important ones. The incident class recurs, and the same postmortem gets written again.

The framework-driven postmortem forces six specific questions. Each question has a formula answer. The answers directly produce a ranked action item list, sorted by RIS — which is exactly the order the team should work them.

### The Six-Question Postmortem Protocol

---

**Question 1 — BRI_actual: Was the declared severity correct?**

```
BRI_actual = (actual_affected_user_%) × criticality × tier_weight
```

Compare BRI_actual against what was declared at triage. This is not blame — it is calibration.

| Comparison | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| BRI_actual ≈ declared | Triage was accurate | None |
| BRI_actual > declared (under-declared) | Under-declaration slowed escalation | Update triage thresholds or training |
| BRI_actual < declared (over-declared) | Over-declaration wasted resources | Note — less critical, but review |

---

**Question 2 — FLI_incident: How far did the cascade spread?**

```
FLI_incident = services_that_held / services_exposed_to_failure
```

This is a post-incident measurement, not the ongoing FLI. It answers: of all services that had exposure to this failure, what fraction held without cascading?

| FLI_incident | Action |
|---|---|
| > 0.90 | Containment was good. No circuit breaker action item. |
| 0.60–0.90 | Partial cascade. Identify which boundaries failed. Add circuit breakers. |
| < 0.60 | Major cascade. Circuit breakers mandatory action item. All affected paths to be hardened. |
| < 0.25 | Systemic cascade. Treat as architecture review trigger — the bulkhead design has failed. |

---

**Question 3 — RCR: Should we fix this or document it as known_error?**

```
RCR = (response_hours × incident_frequency) / fix_hours
```

RCR determines whether the return on fixing the root cause exceeds the investment.

| RCR Value | Decision |
|---|---|
| > 2.0 | Fix this sprint — return is 2× or more per investment hour |
| 0.50–2.0 | Fix within 90 days — return is positive but not urgent |
| < 0.50 | Document as known_error — fixing costs more than it returns at current frequency |

**Important**: a low RCR is not license to ignore the incident class forever. Set a re-evaluation trigger: "If MTBI for this class drops below [threshold], recompute RCR and escalate."

---

**Question 4 — MTBI_trend: Is incident frequency improving or worsening?**

```
MTBI = observation_period / incident_count
MTBI_trend = MTBI_this_quarter / MTBI_last_quarter
```

| Trend Ratio | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| > 1.20 | Improving — incidents less frequent | Confirm which investment drove improvement |
| 0.80–1.20 | Stable | Monitor; no emergency action |
| < 0.80 | Worsening — incidents more frequent | Treat as priority action item. Find the driver. |
| < 0.50 | Accelerating — frequency has doubled or worse | Escalate to SRE Director. Something has fundamentally changed. |

---

**Question 5 — RDR_predicted: Will the proposed fix displace the problem?**

```
RDR = new_failure_mode_rate / old_failure_mode_rate  [target < 0.10]
```

RDR evaluates whether the proposed fix truly eliminates the incident class or merely shifts it to a different failure mode. A fix that reduces the original incident class but introduces a new one with similar frequency is not a fix — it is a displacement.

Before finalizing action items, estimate the new failure mode rate that the proposed fix introduces. If RDR > 0.10, the fix introduces a failure mode that accounts for more than 10% of the original problem rate. Add an instrumentation action item to detect the new failure mode.

---

**Question 6 — OL of action items: Are we prioritizing the right fixes?**

For each action item, estimate OL (operational leverage). High-OL action items should be done first.

```
OL = estimated_Δbudget_pp / SRE_hours_required
```

Rank action items by OL before finalizing the sprint plan. Do not prioritize action items by committee vote or by how recently the associated incident occurred.

### Shopping Cart Worked Example — Auth Connection Pool Exhaustion Postmortem

**Incident summary**: Auth service connection pool to PostgreSQL was exhausted, causing 504 errors that cascaded to Cart and Payment. Duration: 47 minutes. SEV2 declared (upgraded to SEV1 when cascade confirmed).

**Q1 — BRI_actual**:
- Affected users: 38% (slightly below the 40% estimated at triage — checkout users during low-traffic window)
- BRI_actual = 0.38 × 1.0 × 1.0 = **0.38**
- Declared: SEV2 (BRI>0.40 threshold). Actual BRI=0.38 is fractionally below SEV2 threshold.
- Interpretation: triage was accurate. The SEV2 declaration was appropriate given the cascade risk. No calibration needed — incident escalated correctly.

**Q2 — FLI_incident**:
- Services exposed: Auth, Cart, Payment, Order Management (4 services with blast radius)
- Services that held without cascading: Order Management partially isolated due to read path not requiring Auth. 1 service held.
- FLI_incident = 1/4 = **0.25 → Major cascade**
- Action: circuit breakers mandatory. Auth→Cart and Cart→Payment boundaries are the priority.

**Q3 — RCR (two proposed fixes)**:

*Fix A: PgBouncer connection pooler (addresses connection pool exhaustion directly)*
- Response cost: Auth incidents ~12/year × 1.5 SRE-hours = 18 SRE-hours/year
- Fix effort: 120 hours (PgBouncer setup, testing, migration plan)
- RCR_A = 18 / 120 = **0.15 → Document as known_error. Return is below threshold.**

*Fix B: Circuit breakers at Auth→Cart and Cart→Payment*
- Response cost: cascade incidents affect 3 services, ~12 cascades/year × 1.5 hrs × 3 services = 54 SRE-hours/year
- Fix effort: 40 hours
- RCR_B = 54 / 40 = **1.35 → Fix within 90 days. Return is strongly positive.**

Decision: PgBouncer is deferred (documented as known_error with re-evaluation if frequency worsens). Circuit breakers are prioritized.

- Auth incidents this quarter: 4 incidents in 90 days = MTBI_current = 90/4 = **22.5 days**
- Auth incidents last quarter: 2 incidents in 90 days = MTBI_prior = 90/2 = **45 days**

**Q5 — RDR_predicted for circuit breaker fix**:
- Original: Auth connection pool exhaustion → cascade. Rate: ~12/year.
- Circuit breaker new failure mode: circuit breaker trips on latency (false positive), causing brief service degradation during non-Auth incidents. Estimated rate: 1–2/year based on historical latency patterns.
- RDR = 1.5 / 12 = **0.125 → slightly above threshold.**

## Activity 4: Root Cause Analysis

### What This Is

Root cause analysis (RCA) is the structured investigation of *why* an incident happened — not just what happened. In simple single-service failures, RCA is straightforward: one service failed, the logs say why. In distributed systems, RCA is a multi-service investigation that spans layers, teams, and time.

The framework-driven RCA is a seven-checkpoint investigation protocol. Each checkpoint uses a formula to rank suspicion, eliminate candidates, and confirm the root cause chain. It is designed for complex, multi-service incidents where the first reported symptom is not the root cause.

### What Goes Wrong Without the Framework

In distributed systems, the first service to alert is almost never the root cause. The service that crashes is usually a *victim* of something upstream. Without systematic investigation protocol, teams anchor on the first symptom, spend 40 minutes in the wrong service's logs, and miss the actual cause. This is especially common when the true root cause has poor instrumentation (low OCR) — the team cannot see it clearly, so they investigate what they can see.

A second failure mode: the team identifies "the last change before the incident" as the root cause without verifying whether the change's blast radius matches the observed impact. The change was to Search. But Search's CC score shows it cannot cascade to Payment. If Payment is also affected, Search is not the root cause.

### The Seven-Checkpoint RCA Protocol

---

**Checkpoint 1 — OCR_path: Can we see the problem?**

```
OCR_path = Σ(instrumented × tier_weight) / Σ(tier_weight)  [for this incident's affected path]
```

Before investigating, assess your visibility. If OCR_path < 0.70, you are working with less than 70% of the signal you need. Name the blind spots explicitly.

| OCR_path | Visibility | Action |
|---|---|---|
| > 0.90 | Good — golden signals available for most path services | Proceed to Checkpoint 2 |
| 0.70–0.90 | Partial — some blind spots | Note gaps. Use substitute signals (API gateway logs, synthetic probes, external uptime checks) |
| < 0.70 | Poor — significant blind spots | Before investigating root cause, define substitute signals for each uninstrumented service. Investigation confidence will be lower. |

---

**Checkpoint 2 — SCV recency: What changed recently?**

Compute a suspicion score for each service that had a change in the last 72 hours:

```
suspicion(service_i) = SCV(service_i) × recency_factor

recency_factor:
  deployed < 24 hours ago = 1.0
  deployed 24–72 hours ago = 0.5
  deployed > 72 hours ago = 0.1
```

Rank services by suspicion score. The highest-suspicion service is your primary investigation target.

---

**Checkpoint 3 — DG: Which suspect has the highest blast radius potential?**

From the ranked suspicion list, look up DG for the top suspects. A service with high suspicion score AND high DG is the highest-priority investigation target, because if it is the root cause, its blast radius matches the observed incident scope.

**Rule**: if the observed incident scope (number of services affected, tier weights involved) is much larger than the DG of the highest-suspicion service, that service is probably not the root cause. Look upstream for a service with higher DG.

---

**Checkpoint 4 — Layer: Is this application, middleware, container, or network?**

Identify which layer the root cause operates in. Each layer has a characteristic symptom signature:

| Symptom | Layer | Investigation target |
|---|---|---|
| Increasing error rate, logic errors, wrong response codes | Layer 3 — Application | Application logs, recent code changes |
| Connection pool exhaustion, timeout storms, queue buildup | Layer 4 — Middleware | Connection pool metrics, queue depth, Kafka lag |
| OOMKill, pod crash loops, CPU throttling, container restarts | Layer 5 — Container | kubectl describe pod, resource metrics |
| DNS failures, high latency across multiple unrelated services | Layer 1 — Network | Service mesh metrics, DNS resolution time, network flow logs |
| Certificate expiry, TLS handshake failures | Layer 2 — Security/Auth | Certificate expiry checks, TLS metrics |

Layer identification prevents cross-team miscommunication: "the application team says their code is clean, so it must be infrastructure" is a false dichotomy that wastes 30 minutes.

---

**Checkpoint 5 — CC match: Does the blast radius match the suspected service?**

```
expected_scope = f(DG, CC_of_suspected_service)
observed_scope = services_actually_affected
```

If the suspected root cause service has CC=2.0 but three high-tier services are affected, something with higher cascade propagation is involved. Reopen the suspect list.

If the suspected service has CC=10.8 and three services are affected, the blast radius matches. Confidence increases.

---

**Checkpoint 6 — MTBI comparison: Is this service incident-prone?**

Compare this service's MTBI against baseline. A service that has been incident-free for 6 months and suddenly fails is less suspicious than a service that has been degrading every two weeks.

```
MTBI_ratio = MTBI_this_period / MTBI_prior_period
```

A ratio < 0.80 means incidents are accelerating at this service — relevant context for the RCA.

---

**Checkpoint 7 — known_error: Is this a documented incident class?**

Before concluding RCA, check the known_error registry. If this incident matches a documented known_error:

- Compare observed symptoms against the known_error signature
- Check: has the mitigation for this known_error been applied? If not, why not?
- If mitigation was applied and this is a recurrence, update the known_error entry and re-evaluate the mitigation's RCR

---

### Shopping Cart Worked Example — Multi-Service Slowdown

**Setup**: 11:30 AM Tuesday. Cart, Payment, and Search are all showing p99 latency > 5 seconds. No single alert — a customer complaint triggered investigation.

**Checkpoint 1 — OCR_path**:
- Services in incident scope: Cart (instrumented), Payment (instrumented), Search (minimal instrumentation — no golden signals, only basic HTTP metrics)
- OCR_path = (0.8×1 + 0.9×1 + 0×1) / (0.8 + 0.9 + 0.6) = 1.7/2.3 = 0.74

Visibility is partial. Search is a blind spot. Substitute signal for Search: API gateway response time for /search endpoint. Use synthetic probe results if available.

**Checkpoint 2 — SCV recency**:

| Service | Last Deploy | Recency Factor | SCV | Suspicion |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Search | 6 hours ago (Elasticsearch config change) | 1.0 | 0.80 | **0.80** |
| Auth | 5 days ago | 0.1 | 0.40 | 0.04 |
| Cart | 3 days ago | 0.1 | 0.20 | 0.02 |

Highest suspicion: **Search** (suspicion score = 0.80). Primary investigation target.

**Checkpoint 3 — DG**:
- DG(Search): Search is called by Browse and Add-to-Cart, but not by Payment or Order Management directly. DG(Search) = moderate — affects Browse and Add-to-Cart paths, but Payment being affected seems inconsistent.
- The Payment service being affected needs explanation. Does anything call Search and then Payment?
- Investigation reveals: Pricing Service calls Search to get product IDs for price lookups. Pricing Service is then called by Payment. This is an undocumented dependency. DG(Search) is actually higher than the service catalog shows because of the hidden Search→Pricing→Payment chain.

This is a catalog accuracy finding — add to action items.

**Checkpoint 4 — Layer**:
- Elasticsearch config change → Layer 4 (middleware/data tier)
- The config change reduced JVM heap allocation for Elasticsearch from 8 GB to 4 GB (engineering error — they misread the config parameter)
- Symptom: Elasticsearch GC pauses increasing, causing p99 latency on all Search queries to spike from 200ms to 5+ seconds

Layer 4 investigation confirmed. Team: Search service + Platform Elasticsearch SME.

**Checkpoint 5 — CC match**:
- CC(Search in service catalog): 3.2 (moderate cascade risk)
- But the hidden Search→Pricing→Payment chain means the effective CC is higher for this incident
- Observed scope: Cart (showing slowness because it queries Search for product info), Payment (via Pricing), 2 services. Matches an undocumented DG.

Confidence: high that Search is root cause. The blast radius math now matches when the hidden dependency is included.

**Checkpoint 6 — MTBI**:
- Search this quarter: first incident (MTBI undefined)
- Search last quarter: 2 incidents
- Context: new team took ownership of Search 8 weeks ago. Elasticsearch config is less familiar to them.

This is a knowledge transfer gap, not a reliability pattern. Action item: Elasticsearch tuning runbook for the new team.

**Checkpoint 7 — known_error**:
- Check known_error registry: "Elasticsearch heap exhaustion → p99 spike" — **documented**.
- Known mitigation: maintain heap at minimum 50% of physical memory, never below 6 GB on production nodes.
- Was mitigation applied? No — the config change overrode it without the new team knowing the constraint existed.

Confirmed: root cause is Elasticsearch heap under-provisioning in a config change 6 hours ago, cascading through the undocumented Search→Pricing→Payment chain. Root cause confirmed at Checkpoint 7.

## Activity 5: Error Budget Review

### What This Is

The error budget review is a monthly meeting that accounts for how much error budget was consumed, why, and what the implications are for the next month's deployment plan. It is the financial reconciliation meeting of the SRE practice: every organization does something like it, but few do it with the discipline that the framework enables.

Without a structured review, error budget is treated as an abstract concept that everyone nominally understands but no one actively manages. Teams deploy freely early in the month, then discover in week four that budget is exhausted, then scramble to either freeze deployments or ignore the budget entirely. Neither behavior is systematic.

The framework-driven budget review runs five agenda sections, each producing a specific output that drives decisions for the next 30 days.

### Five-Section Error Budget Review Agenda

---

**Section 1 — Consumption Accounting (15 minutes)**

For each service with its own SLA or contributing to a customer SLA:

```
Budget_consumed_pp = SLO_target - actual_availability
Budget_remaining_pp = SLO_target - (SLO_target - SLA_floor) consumed fraction
```

Build a table: service, SLO target, actual availability, budget consumed, budget remaining, percent of monthly budget used.

Identify: which services consumed more than their expected pro-rated share? (Expected daily consumption = monthly budget / 30.)

---

**Section 2 — EBV Analysis (10 minutes)**

For each service that over-consumed, compute EBV during the over-consumption period:

```
EBV = normalized_error_rate / baseline_error_rate
```

A high EBV on a specific date tells you which incidents drove the budget spike. This is the "where did the money go" section of the reconciliation.

Identify: is EBV elevated due to a specific incident (recoverable) or due to ongoing elevated error rate (structural problem)?

---

**Section 3 — MTBI Trend (10 minutes)**

```
MTBI_trend = MTBI_this_month / MTBI_last_month
```

For each service with incidents this month:
- Is incident frequency improving, stable, or worsening?
- Which services have worsening MTBI? These are the highest-priority investigation items for the next sprint.
- Are this month's incidents the same class as last month's (suggesting action items from the prior postmortem are not working)?

---

**Section 4 — Investment OL Review (10 minutes)**

Review each investment made last sprint that was predicted to improve error budget position. Did it?

```
OL_actual = (budget_remaining_now - budget_remaining_pre_investment) / SRE_hours_invested
```

Compare OL_actual against OL_predicted. This closes the feedback loop on investment quality:
- OL_actual significantly lower than predicted → the investment did not work as expected. Understand why.
- OL_actual matches predicted → investment was well-understood.
- OL_actual significantly higher than predicted → unexpected compounding benefit. Document for future RIS calculations.

---

**Section 5 — Change Gate Preview (15 minutes)**

Review upcoming CRs (change requests) for the next 30 days. For each:
1. Compute EBR
2. Compare against projected budget at the time of the planned deployment
3. Flag any CR where EBR > projected budget remaining at deploy date

This produces the "deployment calendar gate" — a forward-looking view of which CRs will be safe to deploy versus which need rescheduling.

---

## Chapter 9 Summary

This chapter is a working handbook for five activities where the framework changes real-time behavior:

**Incident Triage** uses five questions in sequence: BRI (severity), EBV (SLA urgency), CC (cascade risk), observed FLI (is it spreading?), and layer identification (who owns it?). The output is a consistent severity declaration and the right teams on the bridge within 5 minutes, regardless of who is on call.

**CAB Call** uses seven computations: MRI (approval tier), EBR (budget gate), RV (window sizing), CSD (monitoring scope), SCS (coherence check), DSS (composite safety gate), and SCV (service volatility). The output is a defensible approve/conditional/defer decision that does not depend on who is in the room.

**Blameless Postmortem** uses six formula-driven questions: BRI_actual (severity calibration), FLI_incident (cascade scope), RCR (fix or defer decision), MTBI_trend (frequency direction), RDR_predicted (fix quality), and OL of action items (priority ordering). The output is a ranked action item list sorted by risk-adjusted return, not by committee vote.

**Root Cause Analysis** uses a seven-checkpoint protocol: OCR_path (visibility assessment), SCV recency (suspicion ranking), DG (blast radius match), layer identification (investigation scope), CC match (scope confirmation), MTBI comparison (service health context), and known_error check (catalog match). The output is a confirmed root cause with a blast radius explanation and new dependency discoveries.

**Error Budget Review** uses a five-section agenda: consumption accounting, EBV analysis, MTBI trend, investment OL review, and change gate preview. The output is a forward-looking deployment gate and a prioritized sprint focus, derived from actual budget data.

The framework has now completed its full cycle: from structural analysis (Chapters 2-3) through health signals and efficiency (Chapters 4-6) through change governance and lifecycle (Chapter 7) through composite scoring (Chapter 8) to daily practice (Chapter 9). Every formula you have learned maps to a decision a real SRE makes in a real situation. The framework does not replace SRE judgment — it gives judgment a quantitative foundation.
