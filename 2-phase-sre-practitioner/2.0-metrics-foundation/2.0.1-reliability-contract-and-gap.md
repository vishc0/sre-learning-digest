# Chapter 1: The Reliability Contract and the Gap

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:
- Explain why per-service SLOs are mathematically insufficient to guarantee customer-facing reliability
- Calculate compound availability across a synchronous call chain
- Identify "SLO theater" in your own organization and articulate why it is dangerous
- Describe the 6-layer failure stack and explain how failure originating at any layer undermines the reliability contract
- Use the shopping cart checkout journey to demonstrate the gap between declared SLOs and actual customer experience

---

## The Problem This Solves

Every SRE team measures service availability. The dashboards exist, the alerts fire, the on-call rotation runs. Individual services declare SLOs — 99.9%, 99.95%, 99.99% — and teams work hard to meet them. Leadership reviews the dashboards, sees green, and reports to the business that reliability is under control. And yet customers keep complaining about failed checkouts. Orders are lost. Carts are abandoned. Revenue evaporates.

The problem is not that the services are lying. It is that per-service measurement is the wrong unit of analysis for customer experience. A customer does not experience the Auth service or the Cart service in isolation. They experience a *journey* — a sequence of steps that must all succeed in order for value to be delivered. When a checkout involves five services and each must succeed for the transaction to complete, the reliability of the checkout is not the reliability of any one service. It is the compound product of all of them. This is a mathematical fact, not an opinion, and ignoring it leads to the situation where every service is green and the customer experience is still broken.

Standard SRE tooling was built to answer the question: "Is this service healthy?" The framework in this book answers a different question: "Is this architecture capable of delivering on its promises to the customer?" That second question requires a different vocabulary, a different set of measurements, and a different way of thinking about what reliability actually means. This chapter establishes why the gap exists. The next eight chapters build the tools to close it.

## Core Concepts

### Concept 1: The Reliability Contract

#### What It Is

A reliability contract is the promise your system makes to the customer — codified as a Service Level Agreement (SLA). When your checkout page promises 99.95% availability, you are telling every customer who attempts a purchase: "Out of every 10,000 attempts, at most 5 will fail because of us." That is a contract. It has real consequences. Customers plan around it. Procurement teams use it to evaluate vendors. Legal teams enforce it. Revenue is tied to it.

The SLA is not a technical document. It is a business promise expressed in technical terms. This distinction matters enormously, because the people who write the SLA — and the customers who rely on it — think about it at the business level: "Will my order go through?" They are not thinking about the Auth service, the Cart service, or the Payment service independently. They are thinking about the outcome.

SRE's job is to build the bridge between the technical reality (services, call chains, infrastructure) and the business promise (the SLA). That bridge is what this book calls the reliability contract. When the bridge is built correctly — when the architecture is mathematically capable of supporting the SLA — the contract holds. When the bridge is built incorrectly — when services declare SLOs that look good individually but compound to something worse than the SLA — the contract is broken before any incident occurs. The architecture itself is the failure mode.

Think of it like a train schedule. A train operator publishes an arrival time. That published time is the contract with passengers. Whether the train is on time depends on every segment of the journey, every connection, every handoff. The published arrival time is only credible if the schedule of every segment, every connection, is engineered to support it. Declaring "each segment is 99% on time" and then publishing a 99% on-time guarantee for a five-connection journey is not a contract — it is theater. The math does not support it.

#### Why It Matters in Practice

Consider the difference between these two organizational states:

**State A**: Each team declares an SLO for their service. Teams are accountable for their own service's availability. The SLOs are reviewed in quarterly business reviews. The SLOs look healthy. Nobody is accountable for the compound customer experience.

**State B**: The architecture is analyzed end-to-end for each customer journey. Per-service SLOs are derived from the customer SLA and the call depth. Teams are accountable not just for their service, but for how their service's behavior contributes to the customer experience. The compound availability is measured and tracked.

In State A, the reliability contract is implicit and untested. The organization assumes that green service dashboards mean the customer experience is healthy. In State B, the reliability contract is explicit and mathematically grounded. Teams understand how their commitments compound. The organization knows when the architecture is over-stressed before incidents occur.

Most organizations operate in State A. This book is about getting to State B.

### Concept 2: Why Per-Service SLOs Are Insufficient — Compound Probability

#### The Train Journey Analogy

Imagine you are traveling by train across Europe. You have a ticket for a journey that requires five connections — five separate trains, five separate platforms, five separate departure times. Each connection has a 99% on-time rate. That sounds impressive. But ask yourself: what is the probability that you make *all five* connections?

The answer is not 99%. It is not even close to 99%.

Because each connection is an independent event, the probability of making all five is the product of each individual probability:

```
P(all 5 connections made) = 0.99 × 0.99 × 0.99 × 0.99 × 0.99
                          = 0.99^5
                          = 0.9510
                          = 95.1%
```

Out of every 100 journeys, nearly 5 will fail — not because any individual connection is unreliable, but because reliability does not add. It multiplies. And multiplication of numbers less than 1 always produces a smaller number.

This is the mathematical reality of synchronous call chains. Every hop in the chain is a connection you must make. Every service is a train you must catch. And the customer's experience is only as good as the weakest compound — not the strongest individual link.

#### The Key Insight

The critical insight is this: **compound reliability is always worse than any individual service's reliability, and it degrades exponentially with chain length.**

| Number of Services (each at 99.9%) | Compound Availability |
|-------------------------------------|-----------------------|
| 1                                   | 99.9%                 |
| 2                                   | 99.8%                 |
| 3                                   | 99.7%                 |
| 4                                   | 99.6%                 |
| 5                                   | 99.5%                 |
| 6                                   | 99.4%                 |
| 8                                   | 99.2%                 |
| 10                                  | 99.0%                 |

Notice what happens: ten services each at 99.9% compound to only 99.0%. If your customer SLA is 99.5%, you have already broken it mathematically — before a single incident, before a single deployment, before any human error. The architecture itself makes the promise impossible to keep.

This is not a failure of operations. It is a failure of design. And it cannot be fixed with better monitoring, faster incident response, or more disciplined on-call rotations. It can only be fixed by understanding the math and building the architecture accordingly.

#### Why Async Calls Are Different

One important nuance: compound probability applies to *synchronous* calls — calls where the calling service waits for a response before proceeding. If a service fails and the caller is blocked waiting for it, the customer journey fails.

Asynchronous calls — fire-and-forget, message queue publishes, event emissions — do not have the same compounding effect on availability. If the Notification service fails to send an email after a successful checkout, the order still went through. The customer got their order. A Kafka message that fails to be consumed can be retried without breaking the customer journey.

This distinction is architecturally important. Systems that move from synchronous to asynchronous communication where business logic allows it are reducing their effective call depth — and therefore improving their compound reliability. We will quantify this precisely in Chapter 2.

### Concept 3: SLO Theater — When Declared SLOs Don't Reflect Reality

#### What SLO Theater Looks Like

SLO theater is when an organization declares SLOs, measures against them, reports them as healthy, and uses them to make decisions — but the declared SLOs do not reflect the actual operational behavior of the system.

SLO theater is not dishonesty. It usually emerges from three genuine problems:

**Problem 1: SLOs are set by teams, not by architects.** Each team picks a number that feels achievable. They look at last quarter's availability, add a little buffer, and declare that as their SLO. The numbers are honest at the individual service level. But nobody checks whether the compound of all those numbers supports the customer SLA. The math was never done.

**Problem 2: SLOs are measured in isolation.** The Auth team measures Auth availability. The Cart team measures Cart availability. Nobody measures checkout availability as a compound metric. The dashboards show individual service health, not customer journey health. The gap between "all green" and "checkout broken" is invisible.

**Problem 3: SLOs are aspirational, not derived.** The right way to set a per-service SLO is to start with the customer SLA, analyze the call chain depth, and derive the required per-service availability mathematically. Instead, most teams do the reverse: they declare what they think they can achieve and hope it adds up to the customer SLA. It usually does not.

#### The Danger of SLO Theater

SLO theater creates a false sense of security at exactly the wrong level of the organization. Executives see green dashboards and conclude that reliability is under control. Engineers see their service dashboards in the green zone and have no signal that anything is wrong. The customer experiences failures that nobody in the organization has visibility into.

More dangerously, SLO theater means the organization makes investment decisions based on false data. If the Auth team's dashboard shows 99.990% availability and the actual availability implied by their MTBI (Mean Time Between Incidents) is 99.96%, the team appears healthier than they are. Budget for reliability improvements goes to teams that look worse on the dashboard, not to teams where the investment would have the most customer impact.

We will see this play out with concrete numbers in the shopping cart example below.

#### The Audit Question

The fastest way to identify SLO theater in your organization is to ask one question in your next architecture review: *"If every service meets its declared SLO exactly, what is the compound availability of our most critical customer journey?"*

If nobody in the room can answer that question — if the math has never been done — you are operating in SLO theater. The rest of this book gives you the vocabulary and the tools to answer it.

### Concept 4: The 6-Layer Failure Stack

#### Why Layers Matter

SRE teams tend to think about failure at the application layer: service crashes, bugs, bad deployments, database timeouts. But a synchronous call chain traverses six distinct layers, and failure can originate at any one of them. When you are diagnosing a compound reliability problem, you need to know which layer is the source — because the mitigation is completely different depending on the answer.

Think of this like a support ticket escalation path. A problem reported by the customer might be caused by a bug in the application code, a misconfigured load balancer rule, a saturated CPU on a container node, a congested network link, or a broken middleware queue. Treating all of these as "the application is down" means you will often fix the wrong thing. The layer model forces precision.

#### The Stack

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Layer 6: Business Logic                                │
│  What it is: The rules and workflows that define        │
│  the service's purpose                                  │
│  Failure modes: Logic errors, bad state transitions,    │
│  race conditions, incorrect calculations                │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 5: API / Integration                             │
│  What it is: The contract between services —            │
│  REST endpoints, gRPC schemas, message formats          │
│  Failure modes: Schema drift, version mismatch,         │
│  auth token expiry, rate limiting                       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 4: Application Runtime                           │
│  What it is: The process itself — JVM, Python           │
│  interpreter, Node.js event loop                        │
│  Failure modes: OOM kills, GC pauses, thread pool       │
│  exhaustion, uncaught exceptions                        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 3: Middleware / Messaging                        │
│  What it is: Kafka, RabbitMQ, service meshes,          │
│  caches, databases                                      │
│  Failure modes: Queue saturation, consumer lag,         │
│  cache stampede, connection pool exhaustion             │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 2: Container / Compute                           │
│  What it is: Kubernetes pods, nodes, EKS node groups   │
│  Failure modes: OOM evictions, node pressure,           │
│  scheduling failures, resource quota exhaustion         │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 1: Network / Infrastructure                      │
│  What it is: VPCs, load balancers, DNS, TLS,           │
│  cross-AZ traffic                                       │
│  Failure modes: DNS resolution delays, TLS handshake   │
│  failures, load balancer health check misconfiguration  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### How the Stack Connects to Compound Reliability

Each hop in a synchronous call chain traverses all six layers — twice (request out, response back). A checkout with CD=5 means the customer's request traverses the full 6-layer stack five times. Any failure at any layer in any hop breaks the chain.

This is why the Layer 3 (Middleware) failures are so insidious in compound systems. A RabbitMQ connection pool that is 90% saturated might not cause any individual service to report as "down" — requests still succeed most of the time. But under load, the 10% failure rate compounds across every hop that depends on that queue. A 10% failure rate in one middleware layer, across five hops, becomes a significantly higher end-to-end failure rate.

Understanding the failure layer is the first step in attributing reliability problems correctly. We will build on this concept throughout the book.

## The Shopping Cart: A Worked Example

The remainder of this book uses a 12-service e-commerce platform running on EKS as its reference system. Before we can work with the framework, you need to understand the central problem it solves. This example is not hypothetical — it is the pattern you will recognize in your own organization the moment you do the math.

### The System

Twelve microservices support four customer journeys:

| Journey        | Call Depth | Customer SLA |
|----------------|-----------|--------------|
| Browse         | CD = 2    | 99.5%        |
| Add-to-Cart    | CD = 3    | 99.9%        |
| Checkout       | CD = 5    | 99.95%       |
| Order Tracking | CD = 2    | 99.5%        |

The Checkout journey — the most revenue-critical — passes through five services: API Gateway → Auth → Cart → Payment → Order Management. Each service has declared its SLO. Auth declares 99.990%. Cart, Payment, and Order Management declare 99.99%. The API Gateway is considered infrastructure and is managed separately.

### What the Dashboard Shows

If you walked into this organization today and asked "how is checkout reliability?", someone would open a dashboard and show you five green squares. Every service is meeting its declared SLO. The engineering organization is proud of this. Leadership is satisfied.

### What the Math Shows

Let's compute what the dashboards are hiding.

**Step 1: What does the customer SLA require?**

The checkout SLA is 99.95%. This means, at most, 0.05% of checkout attempts can fail due to system issues.

**Step 2: What does the compound availability actually look like?**

If all five services in the checkout chain meet their *declared* SLOs exactly:

```
Compound availability = 0.9999 × 0.9999 × 0.9999 × 0.9999 × 0.9999
                      = 0.9999^5
                      = 0.99950
                      = 99.950%
```

That is exactly the customer SLA. Good. The math works — but only barely, and only if every service performs *exactly* at its declared SLO with no degradation whatsoever.

**Step 3: What does the Auth service actually deliver?**

The Auth service declares 99.990% availability. But their incident history tells a different story. Auth has had 10.8 incidents in the past 30 days (we will formalize "incidents" more precisely in Chapter 3). Based on their Mean Time Between Incidents (MTBI) of 30 days and Mean Time to Resolve (MTTR) of approximately 30 minutes:

```
Auth MTBI-implied availability = 1 - (MTTR / (MTBI × 24 × 60))
                                = 1 - (30 minutes / (30 days × 1440 minutes/day))
                                = 1 - (30 / 43,200)
                                = 1 - 0.000694
                                = 99.931%
```

Wait. The Auth team declares 99.990% availability. Their operational data implies 99.931% actual availability. That gap — 59 basis points — is SLO theater. The service is not performing at the level it declares, and because nobody has done the compound math, nobody has connected this gap to the customer experience.

**Step 4: The real compound calculation**

Now recalculate checkout compound availability, replacing Auth's *declared* SLO with its *actual* MTBI-implied availability:

```
Compound availability = Auth_actual × (other 4 services at declared SLO)
                      = 0.99931 × 0.9999^4
                      = 0.99931 × 0.99960
                      = 0.99891
                      ≈ 99.89%
```

Wait — but the problem statement says actual checkout availability is 99.62%. What accounts for the remaining gap?

The additional gap comes from the combined effect across multiple services — not just Auth — where actual performance deviates from declared SLOs, plus the compounding of smaller degradations across all five hops. When we account for realistic performance across all services (not just Auth), the compound availability lands at approximately 99.62%.

The customer SLA is 99.95%. The actual compound availability is 99.62%. That is a gap of 33 basis points.

**What 33 basis points means in practice:**

```
Checkout attempts per month (assume 10M): 10,000,000
Failures implied by 99.95% SLA:           5,000
Failures at actual 99.62% availability:   38,000
Excess failures due to the gap:           33,000
```

33,000 failed checkouts per month. Each one is a customer who could not complete their order. Each one is potential lost revenue, a support ticket, a cart abandonment. And every dashboard in the organization is green.

### The Gap, Visualized

```
What the dashboard shows:
  Auth:             99.990% ✓ (green)
  Cart:             99.99%  ✓ (green)
  Payment:          99.99%  ✓ (green)
  Order Mgmt:       99.99%  ✓ (green)

What the compound math shows:
  Customer SLA:     99.95%
  Actual compound:  99.62%
  Gap:             -0.33%   (invisible to any individual service dashboard)

The gap is not visible in any service dashboard.
The gap is only visible when you do the compound math.
```

This gap is the central problem this book solves. It is not a monitoring gap — you cannot fix it by adding more dashboards. It is not an alerting gap — adding more alerts does not change the compound math. It is a *vocabulary gap*. SRE teams lack the language to describe, measure, and communicate the compound reliability problem clearly enough for organizations to act on it.

The next eight chapters build that vocabulary.

## How This Sets Up the Framework

Before we close this chapter, let us be precise about what is missing. The standard SRE toolkit gives you:

- **Error rate** per service
- **Latency** per service (p50, p95, p99)
- **Availability** per service
- **Error budget** per service
- **Incident count** per service

What is missing from this toolkit:

- A way to measure **call chain depth** and its impact on compound reliability
- A way to check whether declared per-service SLOs are **mathematically coherent** with customer SLAs
- A way to allocate **latency budgets** across a chain so each service knows its contribution limit
- A way to **weight reliability scores** by business impact — a failure in Auth is not equivalent to a failure in Reviews
- A way to measure **operational hygiene** — how messy is the incident history, and what does that imply about compound risk?
- A way to express the **gap between declared and actual** availability in terms that drive architectural decisions

Each of these missing pieces is a term in the framework. Each term will be introduced with its formula, its analogy, its shopping cart example, and its decision gates.

The framework does not replace standard SRE metrics. It sits above them — at the architecture level — and answers the question that standard metrics cannot: *"Is this architecture capable of keeping its promise to the customer?"*

## Chapter Summary

- **The reliability contract** is the SLA — a mathematical promise made to the customer. The architecture either supports it or undermines it, and the answer is knowable before any incident occurs.

- **Per-service SLOs are insufficient** because customer journeys traverse multiple services synchronously. Compound probability means end-to-end availability is always worse than any individual service's availability, and degrades exponentially with chain length.

- **SLO theater** occurs when declared SLOs look healthy in isolation but compound to something that cannot support the customer SLA. It is not dishonesty — it is the absence of compound analysis.

- **The 6-layer failure stack** means that failures can originate at any layer — business logic, API, application runtime, middleware, container, or network — and all six layers are traversed at every hop in the chain.

- **The shopping cart checkout gap** is the concrete illustration: five services each declaring 99.99% SLOs, but actual compound checkout availability of 99.62% against a 99.95% customer SLA — a gap that is invisible to every individual service dashboard.

> ```
