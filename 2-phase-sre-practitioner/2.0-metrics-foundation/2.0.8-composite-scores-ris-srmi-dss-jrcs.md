# Chapter 8: Composite Scores — Combining the Framework Into Four System-Level Indicators

> "Individual gauges tell you whether a specific system is healthy. Composite scores tell you whether your *engineering practice* is healthy."

---

## Chapter Overview

Chapters 2 through 7 gave you 25 individual instruments — each one answering a specific, bounded question about a specific aspect of reliability. CD tells you how many failure hops exist in your call chain. FLI tells you whether failures are staying put or spreading. EBV tells you how fast you are burning through your error budget. OCR tells you how much of the system you can actually see.

Each instrument is valuable in isolation. But when a VP of Engineering asks "Is our system getting more reliable or less reliable?", no single instrument can answer that. When a principal SRE asks "Which backlog item should I prioritize this sprint?", ranking by gut feel produces inconsistent results. When an on-call SRE at 2 AM is deciding whether to approve an emergency deployment, they need a single score that synthesizes system state — not seven separate metrics to compute under pressure.

This chapter introduces the four composite scores that combine the framework's individual instruments into system-level indicators. Each composite answers one of the biggest strategic questions in reliability engineering:

| Composite Score | Question It Answers | Primary Audience |
|---|---|---|
| **RIS** — Reliability Investment Score | Where should we invest SRE resources this sprint? | Engineering team, SRE lead |
| **SRMI** — System Reliability Maturity Index | How mature is our overall reliability practice? | VP Engineering, SRE Director |
| **DSS** — Deployment Safety Score | Is it safe to deploy right now, given system state? | On-call SRE, release engineer |
| **JRCS** — Journey Reliability Coherence Score | Is this customer journey actually reliable end-to-end? | Product engineering, customer experience |

Think of the individual terms from Chapters 2-7 as the instruments in an aircraft cockpit — altimeter, airspeed, heading, fuel, engine temperature. Each one is essential. But the four composite scores are the four displays the pilot actually watches during cruise: overall flight path, fuel reserve, weather ahead, and systems health summary. The raw instruments are there when you need to drill into a specific problem. The composites are what you monitor to know whether to drill in at all.

## 8.1 RIS — Reliability Investment Score

### 8.1.1 What Question Does RIS Answer?

**"Which item in our SRE backlog gives us the best reliability return for the risk and effort involved?"**

Every SRE team has more work than capacity. The backlog always has more items than sprints. Without a systematic prioritization method, teams default to whatever is most visually alarming (the bright red dashboard), whatever the loudest stakeholder requested, or whatever the team finds technically interesting. None of these criteria optimize for reliability outcomes.

RIS is the portfolio management tool that imposes mathematical discipline on backlog prioritization. It quantifies the risk-adjusted return on every potential SRE investment. A high-RIS item means: the cost of the current situation is high (you are losing a lot of SRE time to incidents), the efficiency of fixing it is high (the fix investment returns more than it costs), and the risk of making the change is manageable (you are not introducing a worse problem while solving this one).

RIS is used by SRE leads and engineering managers during sprint planning to answer the question: "Given that we can only do two things this sprint, which two things?"

### 8.1.2 Formula and Components

```
RIS = (RCR × OL) / MRI
```

**RCR — Reactive Cost Ratio** (from Chapter 6)

```
RCR = (response_hours × incident_frequency) / fix_hours
```

RCR measures the cost of *not fixing* something relative to the cost of fixing it. An RCR of 1.0 means every hour you spend fixing this incident class is matched by an hour you spend *reacting* to it. An RCR of 3.0 means for every hour the fix would take, you are currently losing three hours to incidents. Anything above 0.50 should be on your fix list.

**OL — Operational Leverage** (from Chapter 6)

```
OL = Δ(error_budget_remaining_pp) / SRE_hours_invested
```

OL measures the return per SRE-hour invested. A high OL means a small investment produces a large improvement in error budget position. Structural changes (removing a dependency from a call chain, implementing circuit breakers at a high-DG service) tend to have high OL because they solve problems permanently. Operational changes (tuning a timeout, increasing a pod count) tend to have lower OL because the benefits are marginal and often temporary.

**MRI — Modification Risk Index** (from Chapter 7)

```
MRI = DG × CD × (1 - FLI) × (2 - OCR)
```

MRI measures how dangerous it is to make a change to the system in its current state. A high MRI means the system is brittle (failures spread easily, visibility is poor), so any change — even a good one — carries elevated risk of triggering a cascade. In the context of RIS, a high MRI in the denominator *penalizes* investments that require touching a high-risk, high-blast-radius service. It rewards investments that either operate in low-risk territory (low DG, high FLI) or that reduce MRI itself.

**The trade-off RIS captures**: RIS rewards items where the *cost of inaction* (RCR) is high and the *efficiency of the fix* (OL) is high, but *penalizes* items where fixing it means touching a brittle, high-blast-radius part of the system. The ratio surfaces items that are cheap to fix, cheap to do safely, and expensive not to fix.

### 8.1.3 Worked Example — Shopping Cart Backlog

The shopping cart system has four candidate investments on the backlog. Let us compute RIS for three of them to demonstrate the ranking logic.

**Investment A: API Gateway JWT Validation (removes Auth from checkout call chain)**

The current checkout call chain has CD=5. Implementing JWT validation at the API gateway means the gateway validates the token directly, eliminating the Auth service as a required runtime dependency for checkout. This reduces CD from 5 to 4 and removes the Auth service's DG=18 blast radius from the checkout path.

Computing the components:

- *RCR*: Auth incidents occur approximately twice per month, each requiring 1.5 SRE-hours to respond. Annual response cost = 2 × 12 × 1.5 = 36 hours. Fix effort = 80 hours. RCR = 36/80 = **0.45**
- *OL*: Removing a hop from CD=5 to CD=4 restructures the SLO math. Required_SLO per service drops, budget widens substantially. OL estimate = **15** (structural, permanent improvement)
- *MRI*: This change involves Auth (DG=18) and the API gateway. We use the MRI of the ongoing situation without the fix — the risk the team accepts every day by NOT making this change: MRI = 18 × 5 × (1-0.72) × (2-0.457) = 18 × 5 × 0.28 × 1.543 = **38.9**

```
RIS_A = (0.45 × 15) / 38.9 = 6.75 / 38.9 = 0.17
```

**Investment B: Circuit Breakers (FLI improvement from 0.72 to 0.90)**

Circuit breakers at the Auth→Cart and Cart→Payment boundaries would prevent cascade propagation. The FLI improvement reduces the MRI for all future changes.

Computing the components:

- *RCR*: Cascade incidents occur approximately 3 times per month, each requiring 2 SRE-hours. Annual cascade cost = 3 × 12 × 2 = 72 hours. Fix effort = 40 hours. RCR = 72/40 = **1.80**
- *OL*: Circuit breakers reduce cascade incidents and improve error budget consumption. OL estimate = **12** (structural, affects all services simultaneously)
- *MRI*: Same ongoing system risk: MRI = **38.9**

```
RIS_B = (1.80 × 12) / 38.9 = 21.6 / 38.9 = 0.56
```

**Investment C: Alert Redesign (APR improvement from 0.31 to 0.80)**

The current alert system fires 69% noise — 69 out of every 100 alerts are not actionable. SRE engineers spend time investigating false positives, which delays response to real incidents and erodes trust in the alerting system.

Computing the components:

- *RCR*: Estimated 21 SRE-hours per week consumed by noisy alerts. Annual cost = 21 × 52 = 1,092 hours. Fix effort = 160 hours to audit, rewrite, and tune all alert definitions. RCR = 1,092/160 = **6.83**
- *OL*: Alert redesign improves APR, which reduces MTTR (faster detection of real incidents). OL estimate = **6** (operational improvement, not structural)
- *MRI*: Alert redesign does not modify any service code or configuration. There is no change to the deployment graph. MRI for this investment ≈ **1** (near-zero deployment risk)

```
RIS_C = (6.83 × 6) / 1 = 40.98 / 1 = 40.98
```

**RIS Ranking**

| Investment | RCR | OL | MRI | RIS | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alert Redesign | 6.83 | 6 | 1 | **40.98** | 1st |
| Circuit Breakers | 1.80 | 12 | 38.9 | **0.56** | 2nd |
| API Gateway JWT | 0.45 | 15 | 38.9 | **0.17** | 3rd |

### 8.1.4 What the Score Tells You

The RIS ranking contains a counter-intuitive insight that pure operational experience tends to miss: **pure operational improvements with no code changes often have the best risk-adjusted return.**

Alert redesign (Investment C) ranked first despite having the lowest OL of the three candidates. The reason: RCR was enormous (6.83 — the team is bleeding SRE-hours to noise every week), and MRI was near zero (no production code touched, no cascade risk). The combination overwhelms the moderate OL.

Circuit breakers ranked second. High RCR (cascades are expensive), high OL (structural improvement), but penalized by the same high MRI as Investment A because both investments require touching a brittle, high-blast-radius system.

API Gateway JWT ranked last. Not because it is a bad investment — it is excellent strategically — but because RCR for Auth-only incidents is currently low, and MRI is high. The correct interpretation: do this investment after circuit breakers improve FLI. Once FLI rises to 0.90, MRI drops to 18 × 5 × 0.10 × 1.543 = 13.9, and Investment A's RIS rises to (0.45 × 15) / 13.9 = 0.49, making it competitive with circuit breakers.

**RIS as a conversation tool**: When a stakeholder asks "Why aren't you doing X?", RIS provides a defensible, quantitative answer. "Investment X has RIS=0.17. Investment Y has RIS=40.98. We are doing Y first because it returns 240 times more reliability improvement per unit of risk. We will revisit X after FLI improves."

### 8.1.5 How RIS Changes Over Time

RIS is not static. As you make investments, the inputs change:

- As RCR decreases (fewer incidents), the numerator shrinks — the urgency of a previously high-priority item may drop
- As MRI decreases (FLI improves, OCR improves), the denominator shrinks — structural investments become more attractive
- As system health improves, the entire RIS landscape re-ranks

Run RIS calculations at every sprint planning. The backlog ranking from last quarter may not be the right ranking this quarter. A service that was too risky to touch when FLI=0.72 may become accessible when FLI=0.90.

### 8.1.6 Best Practices

**Compute RIS for every backlog item, not just the top candidates.** The surprise is often in the middle of the list — items no one was excited about turn out to have high RIS because their RCR is high and their MRI is low.

**Use OL estimates, not precise calculations.** OL requires knowing how much error budget improvement a change will produce — hard to compute exactly. Use rough bands: structural changes that reduce CD = 10-20, circuit breakers = 8-15, scaling investments = 5-10, operational changes = 3-8. Precision is not required; order-of-magnitude ranking is.

**Never use RIS alone.** RIS is a prioritization tool, not a veto tool. An investment with RIS=0.10 might still be mandatory for compliance, regulatory, or contractual reasons. RIS tells you the efficiency ordering; it does not override judgment.

**Recompute MRI before finalizing the sprint.** MRI uses live system data (FLI, OCR). If FLI worsened since the last RIS calculation (a cascade incident, a new dependency), the ranking may shift.

## 8.2 SRMI — System Reliability Maturity Index

### 8.2.1 What Question Does SRMI Answer?

**"Is our reliability practice getting stronger or weaker over time — and how far are we from where we need to be?"**

RIS answers "what to do next." SRMI answers "how far have we come and how far do we have to go." It is the quarterly executive health report metric. Its primary audience is engineering leadership — VP Engineering, SRE Director, CTO — who need to understand trajectory and maturity without reviewing 25 individual metrics.

SRMI synthesizes five fundamental properties of a mature reliability practice:

1. **Failure containment** (FLI): mature systems keep failures local
2. **Observability coverage** (OCR): mature systems can see everything
3. **Alert precision** (APR): mature systems only page when something matters
4. **Toil discipline** (TAF): mature systems do not require more human effort as traffic grows
5. **Cascade control** (CC_max): mature systems have bounded blast radius

A high SRMI means all five are healthy simultaneously. Because it is a ratio of health indicators over risk indicators, it rises steeply when multiple good things happen at once — and it falls quickly when any one indicator deteriorates. This makes it a leading indicator of systemic fragility, not just a lagging measure of incident outcomes.

### 8.2.2 Formula and Components

```
SRMI = (FLI × OCR × APR/0.85) / (TAF × CC_max/10)
```

**Numerator — Practice Health**

- **FLI** (Failure Localization Index): proportion of incidents contained at origin. Higher = more mature.
- **OCR** (Observability Coverage Ratio): proportion of service criticality instrumented with golden signals. Higher = more mature.
- **APR/0.85** (Alert Precision Ratio, normalized): ratio of actionable alerts. Divided by 0.85 so that a team at the target precision (85%) contributes a factor of 1.0 to the numerator. A team below 0.85 reduces the numerator; a team above 0.85 boosts it.

**Denominator — Practice Risk**

- **TAF** (Toil Amplification Factor): ratio of toil growth to traffic growth. Lower = more mature. A TAF below 1.0 means toil is growing slower than traffic (good). A TAF above 1.0 means every traffic increase amplifies human work (a scaling crisis in progress).
- **CC_max/10** (Maximum Cascade Coefficient, normalized): the highest cascade propagation risk across any service in the system. Divided by 10 to normalize against the scale seen in practice. A CC_max of 10 contributes a denominator of 1.0. A CC_max of 2 contributes a denominator of 0.2, boosting SRMI. A CC_max of 15 contributes a denominator of 1.5, suppressing SRMI.

**Why a ratio structure?** Because reliability maturity is not just about how good things are — it is about how good things are relative to how risky the system currently is. A team with perfect OCR but a CC_max of 25 (uncontrolled cascade risk) is not mature. The ratio structure penalizes high-numerator scores that are accompanied by high-denominator risks.

### 8.2.3 Worked Example — Shopping Cart Current State

**Computing SRMI_current**

Using the shopping cart's current measurements:

- FLI = 0.72 (72% of incidents contained at origin — cascade is happening 28% of the time)
- OCR = 0.457 (45.7% of critical service tier weight instrumented)
- APR = 0.31 (31% alert precision — 69% noise rate)
- TAF = 0.62 (toil growing at 62% of traffic growth rate — below 1.0, but not under control)
- CC_max = 10.8 (Auth service cascade coefficient — extremely high)

```
SRMI_current = (FLI × OCR × APR/0.85) / (TAF × CC_max/10)
             = (0.72 × 0.457 × 0.31/0.85) / (0.62 × 10.8/10)
             = (0.72 × 0.457 × 0.3647) / (0.62 × 1.08)
             = (0.72 × 0.1666) / 0.6696
             = 0.1200 / 0.6696
             = 0.179
```

**Computing SRMI_target**

A mature reliability practice for a system of this complexity would achieve:
- FLI = 0.95 (95% of incidents contained at origin)
- OCR = 0.95 (95% of critical service weight instrumented)
- APR = 0.85 (85% alert precision — APR/0.85 = 1.0)
- TAF = 0.40 (toil growing at 40% of traffic rate — automation is working)
- CC_max = 2.0 (cascade coefficient bounded — circuit breakers and bulkheads in place)

```
SRMI_target = (0.95 × 0.95 × 1.0) / (0.40 × 0.20)
            = 0.9025 / 0.08
            = 11.28
```

**The gap in plain language**: Current SRMI is 0.179. Target SRMI is 11.28. The current practice is operating at 1.6% of its mature target. This single number tells VP Engineering: "We are in the early stages of a reliability transformation. We have a 63× improvement journey ahead of us."

This is not alarming — it is clarifying. The number gives leadership a rational basis for investment: the team needs sustained investment across all five dimensions, not a single-quarter fix.

### 8.2.4 The Quarterly Roadmap — SRMI as a Progress Tracker

SRMI is most powerful as a trend metric. The improvement trajectory becomes the team's roadmap, expressed as a single number that leadership can track quarter over quarter.

**Q0 (Current baseline)**: SRMI = 0.179

**Q1 target — Circuit breakers + instrumentation sprint**
Investment: implement circuit breakers at Auth→Cart and Cart→Payment boundaries (FLI: 0.72→0.85); complete instrumentation for three uninstrumented services (OCR: 0.457→0.70).

```
SRMI_Q1 = (0.85 × 0.70 × 0.3647) / (0.62 × 1.08)
         = (0.85 × 0.70 × 0.3647) / 0.6696
         = 0.2171 / 0.6696
         = 0.324
```

Quarter-over-quarter improvement: 0.179 → 0.324 (+81%). The circuit breakers and instrumentation produce a near-doubling of SRMI even with APR still low.

**Q2 target — Alert redesign + OCR completion**
Investment: complete the alert redesign (APR: 0.31→0.70); complete instrumentation for remaining services (OCR: 0.70→0.90); begin TAF reduction program (TAF: 0.62→0.50).

```
SRMI_Q2 = (0.85 × 0.90 × 0.70/0.85) / (0.50 × 0.40)
         = (0.85 × 0.90 × 0.824) / 0.20
         = 0.631 / 0.20
         = 3.15
```

Quarter-over-quarter improvement: 0.324 → 3.15 (+872%). The alert redesign produces the largest SRMI jump because it simultaneously increases the numerator (APR) and reduces the denominator's effective impact by improving the team's ability to see and respond to real signals.

**Q3 target — TAF reduction + CC_max reduction**
Investment: automation program reduces TAF (0.50→0.40); JWT validation at API gateway reduces Auth dependency, and circuit breaker tuning reduces CC_max (10.8→2.0); APR reaches target (0.70→0.85).

```
SRMI_Q3 = (0.90 × 0.95 × 1.0) / (0.40 × 0.20)
         = 0.855 / 0.08
         = 10.69
```

Quarter-over-quarter improvement: 3.15 → 10.69 (+239%). The CC_max reduction from 10.8 to 2.0 (enabled by circuit breakers and architecture cleanup) produces the denominator collapse that drives SRMI into mature territory.

**SRMI roadmap summary**

| Quarter | FLI | OCR | APR | TAF | CC_max | SRMI | vs. Target |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Q0 (now) | 0.72 | 0.457 | 0.31 | 0.62 | 10.8 | 0.179 | 1.6% |
| Q1 | 0.85 | 0.70 | 0.31 | 0.62 | 10.8 | 0.324 | 2.9% |
| Q2 | 0.85 | 0.90 | 0.70 | 0.50 | 10.8 | 3.15 | 28% |
| Q3 | 0.90 | 0.95 | 0.85 | 0.40 | 2.0 | 10.69 | 95% |
| Target | 0.95 | 0.95 | 0.85 | 0.40 | 2.0 | 11.28 | 100% |

This table is the nine-month SRE transformation roadmap. It tells leadership exactly which levers move SRMI and by how much — making the investment case concrete and measurable.

### 8.2.5 What the Score Tells You

**SRMI < 0.5**: Early stage. The team is reactive. Most incidents cascade. Visibility is partial. Expect significant SRE-hours consumed by toil and noise. Priority: FLI and OCR.

**SRMI 0.5–2.0**: Developing stage. Circuit breakers and instrumentation are in place. Cascades are less frequent. Visibility is improving. Priority: APR and TAF.

**SRMI 2.0–7.0**: Maturing stage. The team is primarily proactive. Alerts are mostly actionable. Automation is reducing toil. Priority: CC_max reduction and structural improvements.

**SRMI > 7.0**: Mature stage. The practice is healthy. Focus shifts to maintaining these scores as the system evolves, new services are added, and traffic patterns change.

### 8.2.6 How SRMI Changes Over Time

SRMI is a quadratic amplifier: when multiple inputs improve simultaneously, the composite improvement exceeds the sum of individual improvements. This is the "reliability flywheel" effect:

- Better FLI → fewer cascades → faster MTTR → better APR (engineers tune alerts on real incident patterns, not noise)
- Better OCR → faster root cause identification → lower MTTR → lower EBV burn → more budget available for investment
- Better APR → less alert fatigue → engineers catch real signals faster → better FLI (incidents caught before they cascade)

This means SRMI growth is not linear. It is slow in Q1 (only one or two inputs improving), then accelerates as multiple inputs improve simultaneously. Set quarterly targets, not monthly, to avoid measuring the flat part of the ramp and concluding the program is not working.

### 8.2.7 Best Practices

**Report SRMI at the quarterly business review alongside NPS and revenue metrics.** A declining SRMI is a leading indicator of reliability degradation that will eventually show up in customer satisfaction and SLA breach costs.

**Investigate SRMI regressions before they compound.** If SRMI drops between quarters, identify which input changed (FLI? CC_max? APR?) and trace the root cause. A CC_max increase usually means a new service was added without circuit breaker patterns. An APR decrease usually means alert debt accumulated.

**Do not optimize individual inputs at the expense of the composite.** It is possible to game SRMI by, for example, reducing CC_max on low-traffic services while ignoring high-DG services. The composite will rise slightly, but the real risk is unchanged. Always look at which inputs changed, not just the composite direction.

## 8.3 DSS — Deployment Safety Score

### 8.3.1 What Question Does DSS Answer?

**"Given the current state of the system — not just this change, but the entire deployment graph, budget position, and observability state — is it safe to deploy right now?"**

DSS was introduced in Chapter 6 as part of the Change Governance framework. Its role as a composite score deserves deeper treatment here, because the key insight DSS encodes is one that traditional change management frameworks miss entirely:

**A safe change deployed into an unhealthy system is not a safe deployment.**

A code change that passes all tests, has a short rollback time, and has been reviewed by three engineers is still dangerous if: the error budget is nearly depleted (no margin for error), the alert system is 69% noise (the team will not notice degradation quickly), or the system is already in a cascade-prone state (CC_max=10.8). DSS captures all of these dimensions simultaneously.

### 8.3.2 Formula and Components

```
DSS = (1 - SCV_norm) × (1 - MRI/10) × APR × (budget_remaining / total_budget)
```

**Component interpretation**:

- **(1 - SCV_norm)**: The inverted Service Change Volatility score for this specific service. Higher recent failure rate → lower SCV → lower DSS. A service that has been failing on recent deployments (SCV high) is penalized.
- **(1 - MRI/10)**: The inverted Modification Risk Index, normalized. High blast radius + low containment → MRI approaches or exceeds 10 → this term approaches or drops below zero. When MRI > 10, DSS becomes negative — a hard signal that the system is too brittle to deploy into safely.
- **APR**: Alert precision. A team that cannot distinguish real alerts from noise has reduced ability to detect deployment-induced degradation. Low APR penalizes DSS because low APR means lower confidence in the deployment feedback loop.
- **(budget_remaining / total_budget)**: Error budget fraction remaining. A nearly-depleted budget means there is almost no room to absorb any additional failures. Deploying when budget is 5% remaining is equivalent to performing surgery on a patient with almost no reserve capacity.

### 8.3.3 Worked Example — Shopping Cart Deployment Week

The shopping cart team plans to deploy on Monday and tracks DSS through the week. Starting values: APR=0.31, SCV_checkout=0.10 (normalized), MRI_checkout=3.45.

**Monday — Start of week**
- Budget remaining: 95% of monthly budget
- DSS = (1-0.10) × (1-3.45/10) × 0.31 × 0.95
- DSS = 0.90 × 0.655 × 0.31 × 0.95
- DSS = 0.90 × 0.655 × 0.2945
- DSS = **0.174**

**Wednesday — After two deployments, one minor incident**
- Budget remaining: 72% (two deployments consumed budget; minor incident burned more)
- DSS = 0.90 × 0.655 × 0.31 × 0.72
- DSS = **0.132**

**Friday — After a significant cascade incident**
- Budget remaining: 45% (cascade incident burned significantly)
- DSS = 0.90 × 0.655 × 0.31 × 0.45
- DSS = **0.082**
- Interpretation: SRE bridge monitoring required for any deployment. The budget erosion from the cascade has made the system too fragile for routine deployment cadence.

**Sunday — Budget critically low**
- Budget remaining: 30% with 10 days left in the month
- DSS = 0.90 × 0.655 × 0.31 × 0.30
- DSS = **0.055**
- Interpretation: Block deployments. At current EBV burn rate, the team will breach SLA before month end if any additional incidents occur. Wait for budget reset or obtain executive override with explicit risk acceptance.

**The deployment week progression**

| Day | Budget Remaining | DSS | Deployment Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monday | 95% | 0.174 | Approve with standard monitoring |
| Wednesday | 72% | 0.132 | Approve with enhanced monitoring |
| Friday | 45% | 0.082 | Require SRE bridge monitoring |
| Sunday | 30% | 0.055 | Block — await budget or exception |

### 8.3.4 DSS Action Thresholds

| DSS Value | Interpretation | Required Action |
|---|---|---|
| > 0.60 | System healthy, budget strong | Standard deployment with post-deploy monitoring |
| 0.40–0.60 | Moderate risk — monitor closely | Require SRE standby for 30 min post-deploy |
| 0.20–0.40 | Elevated risk | Require SRE bridge; 1-hour hold before next deployment |
| 0.10–0.20 | High risk | SRE on bridge; EM notification; rollback plan pre-staged |
| < 0.10 | Critical state | Block all non-emergency deployments |
| Negative | System brittle (MRI > 10) | Freeze deployments; address CC_max and FLI first |

### 8.3.5 The Key Insight: DSS Decouples "Change Safety" from "System Safety"

Traditional change management asks: "Is this change safe?" DSS asks a better question: "Is this change safe, **given the current system state**?"

A change with DSS=0.60 on Monday might have DSS=0.08 on Friday if the system absorbed two incidents in between. The change itself did not change. The system absorbed the budget. The correct response to a DSS drop is not to make the change "safer" (it was already safe) — it is to wait for the system to recover.

This is the core behavioral change DSS drives: **teams stop asking "can we deploy?" and start asking "should we deploy now, or wait until the system state improves?"**

### 8.3.6 Best Practices

**Compute DSS in real time, not at deploy time.** If your deployment pipeline can query DSS via API before executing, deployments can be automatically gated. A CI/CD pipeline that blocks a deployment when DSS < 0.10 is more reliable than an SRE manually checking the dashboard.

**Distinguish DSS from change quality.** DSS does not evaluate whether the code change is well-written, tested, or reviewed. That is the job of the code review process and test suite. DSS evaluates whether the *system* is ready to absorb a change.

**Track DSS trend through the sprint.** A team whose DSS is chronically low (below 0.20 every week) has a systemic problem, not a deployment timing problem. The fix is MRI reduction (circuit breakers, instrumentation) or APR improvement — not fewer deployments.

## 8.4 JRCS — Journey Reliability Coherence Score

### 8.4.1 What Question Does JRCS Answer?

**"Is this customer journey actually reliable from the customer's perspective, not just from the perspective of individual service SLOs?"**

JRCS is the most customer-centric of the four composite scores. Every other metric in this framework looks at services in isolation or in the context of a change: Is Auth healthy? Is the checkout deployment safe? Is our overall practice mature?

JRCS looks at the entire path a customer traverses to complete a business outcome — Browse → Add-to-Cart → Checkout → Payment → Order Confirmation — and asks: given everything we know about failure containment, observability, cascade risk, and SLO coherence across this path, what is the probability that this journey works for the customer?

This is a different question from "is each service healthy?" A system can have every individual service hitting its SLO target while the customer journey is failing at an unacceptable rate. This happens when: SLOs are not coherently set across the chain (SCS < 0.95), failures do not stay contained (FLI is low), key services on the path are not visible (OCR_path is low), or cascade risk on the path is high (CC_max).

### 8.4.2 Formula and Components

```
JRCS = SCS × FLI × OCR_path × (1 - CC_max/10)
```

**SCS** (SLO Coherence Score): Measures whether the per-service SLOs, when compounded across the call chain, actually deliver the customer-facing SLA. SCS = SLO^CD / SLA. A value of 0.95-1.10 means the chain is coherent. A value below 0.95 means the math says the chain will breach the SLA even if every individual service hits its individual SLO. SCS appears in JRCS because it captures structural reliability — the shape of the chain, not the momentary health of services.

**FLI** (Failure Localization Index): When a failure occurs somewhere on this journey path, does it stay at the failing service or cascade across the path? Low FLI means a single service failure affects the entire journey. JRCS uses the system-wide FLI as a proxy for path-specific containment.

**OCR_path** (Observability Coverage Ratio for this specific path): This is a path-specific variant of OCR, measuring what fraction of service-tier-weight on this specific journey path has golden signal instrumentation. OCR_path may differ from system-wide OCR because some paths traverse better-instrumented services than others. The checkout path may have lower OCR_path than the browse path if the browse path traverses newer, better-instrumented services.

**(1 - CC_max/10)**: The cascade risk term. When CC_max > 10, this term becomes negative, making JRCS negative — a critical signal that the cascade risk on this journey exceeds the safety margin. This is not a theoretical concern; it means that a single failure at the high-CC_max service will propagate to multiple stops on the customer journey, making the journey fail even if the directly-failed service recovers quickly.

### 8.4.3 Worked Example — Checkout Journey, Current State

The shopping cart checkout journey spans CD=5 hops: API Gateway → Auth → Cart → Payment → Order Management. Auth has CC_max=10.8 — the highest cascade coefficient in the system.

**Computing JRCS_checkout, current state**:

- SCS: For CD=5 and SLA=99.95%, each service must hold Required_SLO = 99.95%^(1/5) = 99.99%. Auth's declared SLO = 99.990%. SCS = (99.990%^5) / 99.95% ≈ 99.95% / 99.95% ≈ 1.0 (chain is SLO-coherent on paper)
- FLI = 0.72
- OCR_checkout_path = 0.60 (estimated — the checkout path includes Auth, which is instrumented, but Order Management has minimal instrumentation)
- CC_max = 10.8

```
JRCS_checkout = 1.0 × 0.72 × 0.60 × (1 - 10.8/10)
              = 1.0 × 0.72 × 0.60 × (1 - 1.08)
              = 1.0 × 0.72 × 0.60 × (-0.08)
              = -0.035
```

**The JRCS is negative.**

This is the most alarming finding in the entire framework. A negative JRCS means the cascade risk exceeds the safety margin — the checkout journey is at systemic risk of cascade failure even without any immediate incident occurring. The CC_max of 10.8 on Auth means that a failure at Auth (the most downstream dependency on the checkout path) will cascade to Cart, Payment, and potentially Order Management before circuit breakers can trip, because there are no circuit breakers.

The SLOs say checkout should be at 99.95% availability. The customer is experiencing something far worse.

### 8.4.4 The Circuit Breaker Investment — JRCS Impact

After investing in circuit breakers at the Auth→Cart and Cart→Payment boundaries, CC_max drops from 10.8 to 2.0 (Auth failures no longer cascade past Cart because Cart has a circuit breaker).

```
JRCS_after_circuit_breakers = 1.0 × 0.72 × 0.60 × (1 - 2.0/10)
                             = 1.0 × 0.72 × 0.60 × 0.80
                             = 0.346
```

JRCS goes from -0.035 to 0.346 — the single circuit breaker investment rescues the checkout journey from the "systemically at risk" zone to the "developing" zone. This validates why circuit breakers ranked second in the RIS analysis and why RIS and JRCS should be interpreted together.

### 8.4.5 JRCS After Full Investment Portfolio

After the nine-month investment program (FLI→0.90, OCR_checkout_path→0.90, CC_max→1.5):

```
JRCS_mature = 1.0 × 0.90 × 0.90 × (1 - 1.5/10)
            = 1.0 × 0.90 × 0.90 × 0.85
            = 0.689
```

### 8.4.6 JRCS Interpretation Scale

| JRCS Value | Journey Health | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Negative | Critical | Cascade risk exceeds safety margin. Journey is systemically fragile even without active incidents. Immediate CC_max reduction required. |
| 0–0.25 | Poor | Multiple reliability gaps. Customer experience is unreliable. Significant investment needed across 2+ dimensions. |
| 0.25–0.50 | Developing | Core problems addressed but journey still fragile. Circuit breakers in place but observability or containment gaps remain. |
| 0.50–0.75 | Maturing | Journey is mostly reliable. Edge cases and low-OCR gaps exist but customer impact is manageable. |
| > 0.75 | Healthy | Journey reliability is production-grade. All inputs strong. Focus on maintaining as system evolves. |

**Target JRCS for a healthy customer journey: > 0.80**

### 8.4.7 JRCS Across Multiple Journeys

JRCS should be computed for every customer journey in the system, not just the most critical one. Common journeys in a commerce system:

| Journey | Typical CD | Key Risk | JRCS Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Browse → Product Detail | 2–3 | Search cache misses | Low |
| Add to Cart | 3 | Cart service availability | Medium |
| Checkout (full) | 5 | Auth cascade | High |
| Order History | 2 | Database read replica | Low |
| Return/Refund | 4 | Payment service rollback | High |

Services that appear in multiple high-priority JRCS journeys (Auth appears in Add-to-Cart AND Checkout AND Return/Refund) are the highest-leverage investment targets because improving their FLI and CC_max improves multiple JRCS scores simultaneously.

### 8.4.8 Best Practices

**Assign JRCS to product owners, not just SRE teams.** JRCS is expressed in customer-experience language — journey reliability — not infrastructure language. Product owners should own the JRCS target for their journeys and treat it as a feature-parity concern alongside conversion rate and latency.

**Compute JRCS monthly, not weekly.** The inputs (FLI, OCR_path, CC_max) change slowly with investment. Weekly fluctuations are noise. Monthly trends are signal.

**When JRCS is negative, stop all new feature deployments on that journey.** A negative JRCS is not a performance optimization problem or a technical debt item — it is a systemic reliability emergency. The journey is not production-safe. Treat it with the urgency of an ongoing SEV1.

**Use JRCS to scope instrumentation priorities.** OCR_path for specific journeys helps prioritize which services to instrument first. If the checkout path has OCR_path=0.60 and the browse path has OCR_path=0.90, the next instrumentation sprint should focus on uninstrumented checkout-path services.

## 8.5 Using the Four Composites Together

The four composite scores are designed to be used as a system, not in isolation. They answer different questions and drive different conversations:

```
SRMI ──► quarterly executive review
  │
  ├──► SRMI declining? → investigate: which input fell?
  │     └──► FLI fell? → CC_max rose? → which journey? → JRCS
  │     └──► APR fell? → alert debt → RIS: alert redesign priority?
  │     └──► TAF rose? → toil automation needed → RIS: automation ROI?
  │
RIS ──► sprint planning
  │
  ├──► RIS high for item X? → approve sprint slot
  └──► RIS low for item X? → understand why before deferring
        └──► MRI too high? → sequence behind FLI improvement
        └──► RCR too low? → genuine low priority
        └──► OL too low? → is this a structural or operational fix?

JRCS ──► customer experience review
  │
  ├──► JRCS negative? → emergency: CC_max reduction before next release
  └──► JRCS below target? → which input is limiting?
        └──► CC_max? → circuit breakers
        └──► FLI? → bulkheads and timeout hardening
        └──► OCR_path? → targeted instrumentation sprint

DSS ──► every deployment decision
  │
  └──► DSS below threshold? → wait, or escalate for exception
        └──► Budget depleted? → freeze until reset
        └──► APR low? → fix alerts before next deploy
        └──► MRI high? → improve FLI/OCR before next deploy
```

The four composites tell one coherent story about the system:
- **Are we investing in the right things?** (RIS)
- **Is the investment working?** (SRMI trend)
- **Can we safely change the system?** (DSS)
- **Are customers experiencing the reliability we think we have?** (JRCS)

## Chapter 8 Summary

This chapter introduced the four composite scores that synthesize the framework's 25 individual terms into system-level indicators.

**RIS (Reliability Investment Score)** = (RCR × OL) / MRI. Prioritizes the SRE backlog by risk-adjusted return. Alert redesign ranked first in the shopping cart example because enormous RCR and near-zero MRI overwhelmed modest OL. Pure operational improvements often have the best RIS.

**SRMI (System Reliability Maturity Index)** = (FLI × OCR × APR/0.85) / (TAF × CC_max/10). Measures overall practice maturity. Shopping cart current SRMI=0.179, target=11.28 — a 63× improvement journey achievable in 3 quarters through sequenced investment. SRMI is a trend metric; its trajectory is the team's roadmap.

**DSS (Deployment Safety Score)** = (1-SCV_norm) × (1-MRI/10) × APR × (budget_remaining/total). Synthesizes system state into a pre-deployment gate. The key insight: a safe change in an unhealthy system is not a safe deployment. DSS decouples "change quality" from "deployment safety."

**JRCS (Journey Reliability Coherence Score)** = SCS × FLI × OCR_path × (1-CC_max/10). Measures customer journey reliability end-to-end. Shopping cart checkout JRCS is currently negative — the cascade risk at Auth exceeds the safety margin. Circuit breakers alone rescue JRCS from -0.035 to 0.346.

The four composites answer the four biggest strategic reliability questions and drive four distinct conversations: sprint planning (RIS), quarterly review (SRMI), every deployment (DSS), customer experience review (JRCS).
