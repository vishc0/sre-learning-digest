## Introduction: The CAB Table Has a Math Problem

Most change advisory boards operate on gut feel dressed up as process. An engineer presents a change, someone asks "what's the blast radius?", the answer is "should be fine," and the change is approved because the service owner looks confident and the meeting is running long. Two hours later, checkout is down.

The seven metrics in this chapter replace gut feel with computable signals. They are not bureaucracy generators — they are the engineering substrate that makes CAB meetings short, decisions defensible, and post-mortems less frequent. An SRE who knows these numbers walks into a CAB call knowing the answer before the question is asked. An SRE who does not is guessing in public.

The organizing metaphor for this chapter is a **Formula 1 race weekend**. A pit stop looks simple from the stands — four tires changed in 2.3 seconds. But behind that execution is weeks of measurement: tire degradation curves, fuel load calculations, undercut timing, safety car probability models, and practiced rollback procedures (the driver re-entering the pits if something went wrong). Every term in this chapter maps to a piece of that preparation. The change is the race. The metrics are the pit wall.

We will use the shopping cart platform throughout: 12 services across four priority tiers, two P0 database-backed services under active pressure (Auth at 99.62% actual vs. 99.95% SLA target), and a system health profile that is, frankly, concerning. These are not abstract numbers. Work through the computations as if you are the SRE on the CAB bridge call at 2 PM Thursday.

## Term 1: Service Change Volatility (SCV)

### The Concept

SCV is the deployment risk fingerprint of a service over time. It answers a single question: *how much risk does this service's change pattern generate per week?*

Think of it as a car's pit stop complexity score across a season. A team that changes tires, adjusts wing angles, and replaces the front nose assembly every three laps has a high complexity score — not because any single action is wrong, but because the aggregate of frequent, high-impact interventions creates compounding risk. One misaligned tire nut at stop 12 does not exist in isolation; it exists in a system that has been under continuous modification pressure for 45 laps.

SCV captures three orthogonal dimensions of deployment risk. First, *frequency*: how often does the service change? A service that deploys once per quarter has low inherent exposure. A service deploying daily has seven times the weekly event surface. Second, *complexity*: not all changes are equal. Swapping a config flag is categorically different from rewriting an API contract. The Change Surface Attributed Index (CSAI) is the multiplier that weights change complexity — it ranges from 0.2 for a config flip to 2.0 for a coordinated multi-service deployment. Third, *reliability*: the change failure rate is the historical rollback rate, which is the most honest signal of how well the team's deployment process handles the service's actual change surface.

High SCV does not mean the service is broken. It means the service is volatile under current conditions. Auth could have low SCV during a freeze period and high SCV during an active migration sprint. SCV is a current-state measurement, not a verdict.

### Formula

```
SCV = deploy_freq_per_week × mean_CSAI × change_failure_rate
```

**Components:**

| Variable | Definition | Units |
|---|---|---|
| `deploy_freq_per_week` | Number of deployments to this service per week (rolling 4-week average) | deploys/week |
| `mean_CSAI` | Mean Change Surface Attributed Index across deployments in the period | dimensionless multiplier |
| `change_failure_rate` | Fraction of deployments requiring rollback = `deployments_requiring_rollback / total_deployments` | 0.0–1.0 |

**CSAI values by change type:**

| Change Type | CSAI | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Config-only | 0.2 | Environment variable, feature flag, timeout value — no binary change |
| Code update | 0.5 | New logic, dependency bump, bug fix — binary changes, no contract break |
| Schema or API change | 1.0 | Database migration, endpoint signature change — consumer impact possible |
| Multi-service coordinated | 2.0 | Requires simultaneous or sequenced changes across service boundaries |

**SCV Interpretation Bands:**

| SCV Value | Risk Level | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| < 0.5 | Stable | Low deployment pressure; standard pipeline sufficient |
| 0.5 – 2.0 | Moderate | Elevated monitoring warranted; review change sequencing |
| > 2.0 | High Risk | Deployment freeze review; investigate failure rate root cause |

### Simple Example: Auth Service — Routine Sprint

**Given:**
- deploy_freq_per_week = 3 (three code updates this sprint)
- mean_CSAI = 0.40 (mix: two code updates at 0.5, one config at 0.2 → mean = (0.5 + 0.5 + 0.2) / 3 = 0.40)
- change_failure_rate = 0.10 (1 of 10 recent deploys required rollback)

**Computation:**
```
SCV = 3 × 0.40 × 0.10
    = 3 × 0.04
    = 0.12
```

**Interpretation:** SCV = 0.12. Stable band. Auth is deploying frequently but with a controlled change type mix and a low rollback rate. Standard pipeline is sufficient.

### Complex Scenario: Search Service During Elasticsearch Upgrade

Search is upgrading from Elasticsearch 7.x to 8.x. This is not a routine sprint. The index mapping format changed, the query DSL has breaking changes, and two downstream consumers (Product Catalog and Recommendation) depend on the Search response contract.

**Given:**
- deploy_freq_per_week = 2 (two migration-phase deploys per week, deliberately gated)
- mean_CSAI = 1.5 (blend of schema change at 1.0 and multi-service coordination at 2.0 → mean = (1.0 + 2.0) / 2 = 1.5)
- change_failure_rate = 0.375 (3 of 8 migration-phase deploys required rollback in staging validation)

**Computation:**
```
SCV = 2 × 1.5 × 0.375
    = 2 × 0.5625
    = 1.125
```

**Interpretation:** SCV = 1.125. Moderate band — approaching the high-risk threshold. This is the correct outcome: Search during an Elasticsearch migration is genuinely elevated risk. The 37.5% staging rollback rate is the dominant driver. Before promoting to production, the team should investigate why rollbacks are occurring in staging and reduce the failure rate below 0.25 to bring SCV under 0.75.

**CAB implication:** A service presenting SCV > 1.0 should prompt the CAB chair to ask: "What is driving the rollback rate in staging, and what changes to the deployment procedure reduce it?" The SCV number surfaces the question that gut feel might miss.

### Decision Gates

| SCV Range | Interpretation | Required Action |
|---|---|---|
| < 0.5 | Stable | Standard canary deploy; no additional gate |
| 0.5 – 1.0 | Moderate | SRE review of deployment plan before promotion |
| 1.0 – 2.0 | Elevated | SRE on bridge during deploy window; rollback procedure validated |
| > 2.0 | High Risk | CAB must review; deployment freeze candidate; root cause investigation required |

### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Compute SCV on a rolling 4-week window to smooth sprint-boundary noise
- Track SCV per service in your change management tooling so trends are visible at CAB
- When SCV rises, investigate the change_failure_rate first — it has the largest leverage
- Use SCV to set monitoring sensitivity: high-SCV services get tighter alert thresholds during deploy windows
- Include SCV in the CAB ticket as a required field, not an optional annotation

**Don't:**
- Use SCV as a penalty for active services — a service with SCV = 1.5 during a major migration is behaving correctly for its context
- Conflate low deploy_freq with low risk — a service that deploys once per quarter with a 50% rollback rate has a different risk profile than SCV alone shows
- Substitute a point-in-time CSAI value for the mean — cherry-picking the lowest change type understates the real risk
- Accept a team's self-reported change_failure_rate — compute it from the deployment pipeline's rollback events directly

### Guardrails

- SCV > 2.0: automatic flag in change management system; CAB chair notified before ticket progresses
- SCV computed from pipeline data, not team estimates
- CSAI assigned by change type classification in the deployment template, not by the submitting engineer
- Staging rollback rate is a valid input to mean_CSAI calculation when production data is insufficient

## Term 2: Migration Risk Index (MRI)

### The Concept

MRI is the CAB approval tier calculator. It translates the structural properties of a change into a specific governance requirement: notify SRE, require two CAB approvers, require VP Engineering sign-off, or block the change entirely.

Return to the pit stop analogy. Not all pit stops are equal. Changing front tires during a safety car window on lap 20 is a standard stop. Changing the front wing, rebalancing brake bias, and switching from wet to slick tires simultaneously in a live race window is a multi-variable intervention where each action changes the context for the next one. MRI measures the complexity of that simultaneous intervention.

The key insight is this: **Auth is always high-MRI not because its code is bad, but because its blast radius is enormous.** Auth has a dependency graph score of 18 — meaning 18 downstream services are affected if Auth degrades. The MRI formula encodes this reality. A perfect team with perfect tooling deploying Auth will still produce a higher MRI than a mediocre team deploying the Notification service, because MRI measures structural risk, not team quality.

This is a profoundly important distinction. MRI prevents the cognitive trap of "our Auth team is senior, so this change is low risk." The blast radius is real regardless of team seniority. The governance tier is dictated by the number, not the confidence of the people presenting the change.

MRI has two risk-reduction levers: Feature Launch Infrastructure (FLI) reduces blast radius by enabling graduated rollout, so the full blast radius is never simultaneously exposed. Operational Capability Rating (OCR) captures the system's ability to detect and recover from the change — a system with high OCR observability can catch problems earlier. Both levers appear in the formula, and improving them is the architectural investment case for reducing MRI over time.

### Formula

```
MRI = DG × CD × (1 - FLI) × (2 - OCR_weighted)
```

**Components:**

| Variable | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| `DG` | Dependency Graph score — number of downstream services affected | Architecture map; updated quarterly |
| `CD` | Customer Dependency score — critical journey count × SLA weight | Journey criticality register |
| `FLI` | Feature Launch Infrastructure score — 0.0 (no graduated rollout) to 1.0 (full dark launch capability) | Platform capability audit |
| `OCR_weighted` | Operational Capability Rating weighted across the impacted journey — 0.0 (blind) to 1.0 (full observability) | OCR computed from ch04 |

**Why the formula structure works:**
- `(1 - FLI)`: When FLI = 1.0, this term goes to zero, zeroing the entire MRI. Perfect feature flagging makes blast radius instantaneous exposure zero.
- `(2 - OCR_weighted)`: When OCR = 1.0, this multiplier is 1.0 (no benefit). When OCR = 0.0, this multiplier is 2.0 (doubles the risk). Observability gaps amplify risk; this is mathematically encoded.

**MRI Approval Tiers:**

| MRI Range | Governance Required |
|---|---|
| < 1.0 | SRE notification only |
| 1.0 – 3.0 | CAB with 2 approvers |
| 3.0 – 8.0 | VP Engineering approval + defined maintenance window |
| ≥ 8.0 | BLOCKED — architectural prerequisites must be met first |

### Simple Example: Auth Service Aurora DB Migration (Current State)

This is the clearest demonstration of why MRI matters. Auth wants to migrate to Aurora. The team is confident. The staging tests passed. But the numbers tell a different story.

**Given:**
- DG = 18 (Auth has 18 downstream dependents — the highest in the platform)
- CD = 5 (Checkout journey at weight 5 — highest single-journey weight)
- FLI = 0.72 (current platform FLI — partial dark launch capability)
- OCR_weighted = 0.457 (current weighted OCR across checkout journey)

**Computation:**
```
Step 1: (1 - FLI) = 1 - 0.72 = 0.28
Step 2: (2 - OCR_weighted) = 2 - 0.457 = 1.543
Step 3: MRI = DG × CD × (1 - FLI) × (2 - OCR_weighted)
            = 18 × 5 × 0.28 × 1.543
            = 90 × 0.28 × 1.543
            = 25.2 × 1.543
            = 38.87
```

**MRI = 38.9. BLOCKED.**

This is not a close call. MRI = 38.9 is nearly five times the block threshold of 8.0. The Aurora migration cannot proceed under current system conditions. The CAB does not need to deliberate — the number makes the decision.

### What Must Improve to Reach the VP Tier (MRI < 8)?

This is where MRI becomes an architectural investment tool. The question is not "can we get approval anyway?" The question is "what architectural improvements reduce MRI to an acceptable tier?"

**Target:** MRI < 8.0 (VP Engineering tier)

We cannot change DG (Auth genuinely has 18 dependents — that is architecture, not measurement error). We need to improve FLI and OCR, and potentially reduce CD by timing the deployment outside the peak checkout window.

**Scenario: FLI → 0.90, OCR → 0.85, CD → 4 (off-peak window, reduced journey criticality)**

```
Step 1: (1 - FLI) = 1 - 0.90 = 0.10
Step 2: (2 - OCR_weighted) = 2 - 0.85 = 1.15
Step 3: MRI = 18 × 4 × 0.10 × 1.15
            = 72 × 0.10 × 1.15
            = 7.2 × 1.15
            = 8.28
```

**MRI = 8.28. Still VP tier — just above the 8.0 block threshold.**

This is the correct outcome. An Auth Aurora migration on a platform with 18 dependents should be hard. Even with dramatically improved observability (OCR 0.457 → 0.85), much better feature flagging (FLI 0.72 → 0.90), and a lower-criticality timing window (CD 5 → 4), the MRI barely clears the block threshold. To reach VP tier requires all three improvements simultaneously.

**To reach VP tier comfortably (targeting MRI ≈ 4.0):**
```
With FLI = 0.95, OCR = 0.90, CD = 4:
Step 1: (1 - 0.95) = 0.05
Step 2: (2 - 0.90) = 1.10
Step 3: MRI = 18 × 4 × 0.05 × 1.10
            = 72 × 0.05 × 1.10
            = 3.6 × 1.10
            = 3.96
```

MRI = 3.96. VP tier. Achievable — but it requires FLI at 0.95 (near-perfect dark launch capability) and OCR at 0.90 (excellent observability). These are multi-quarter infrastructure investments, not sprint tasks. MRI makes the investment case concrete: until the platform reaches FLI ≥ 0.95 and OCR ≥ 0.90, the Auth Aurora migration is either VP-tier-with-conditions or blocked.

### Decision Gates

| MRI Range | Decision | Required Actions Before Scheduling |
|---|---|---|
| < 1.0 | SRE notification | Email SRE on-call team; standard pipeline |
| 1.0 – 3.0 | CAB: 2 approvers | Submit CAB ticket 5 business days prior; rollback plan required |
| 3.0 – 8.0 | CAB: VP Engineering + window | Architecture review; runbook with rollback RV measured; EBR check |
| ≥ 8.0 | BLOCKED | Identify architectural prerequisites; re-evaluate after FLI/OCR improvements |

### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Treat MRI ≥ 8.0 as an architectural signal, not an approval process failure
- Update DG and CD on a quarterly cadence — dependency graphs drift as services evolve
- Invest in FLI infrastructure proactively; it is the most leveraged MRI reducer for high-DG services
- Show MRI trend over time in architecture reviews — a rising MRI means the system is accruing structural debt
- Compute MRI before writing the CAB ticket, not during the CAB meeting

**Don't:**
- Argue that a team's experience or service quality offsets MRI — structural risk is structural
- Use point-in-time OCR for MRI when a rolling average is available — OCR fluctuates
- Compute MRI only for changes the team is nervous about — routine changes to high-DG services also generate high MRI
- Treat the block tier as negotiable — if MRI ≥ 8.0, the change cannot proceed regardless of business pressure

### Guardrails

- MRI computed at CAB ticket submission time, not at time of change execution
- DG and OCR sourced from platform registry, not self-reported by submitting team
- Changes with MRI ≥ 8.0 require explicit architectural remediation plan before re-submission
- VP Engineering approval for MRI 3.0–8.0 must be documented in the change ticket with timestamp

## Term 3: Change Surface Delta (CSD)

### The Concept

CSD measures the footprint of a change — how much of the system is being simultaneously modified, weighted by the depth of each modification. If MRI tells you the governance tier, CSD tells you the deployment strategy.

The distinction is important. Two changes can have identical MRI values but completely different deployment strategies based on CSD. A single schema change to Auth (high DG, moderate CSD) needs a different approach than a coordinated update touching Auth, Cart, Payment, and Order Management simultaneously (high DG, high CSD). MRI gates approval; CSD dictates execution.

Think of CSD as the construction site footprint. You can have a high-risk project (building a skyscraper — MRI analogue) that still has a small physical footprint (drilling a single foundation shaft — low CSD). Or you can have a moderate-risk project (renovating an office floor) with a massive footprint (every room simultaneously exposed to construction dust and noise — high CSD). The governance is different; the operational impact pattern is different.

The expand-contract pattern is CSD's primary deployment strategy for large footprints. When CSD exceeds 15, the discipline of expand-contract is not optional: you first deploy the new capability alongside the old (expand), verify consumers are working with both versions, then remove the old capability (contract). This prevents the simultaneous exposure of a large change surface from creating cascading failures.

### Formula

```
CSD = Σ [ tier_weight(service_i) × change_depth_coefficient_i ]
```

**Tier Weights by Priority:**

| Priority Tier | Tier Weight |
|---|---|
| P0 | 1.0 |
| P1 | 0.8 |
| P2 | 0.5 |
| P3 | 0.3 |

**Change Depth Coefficients:**

| Change Type | Coefficient | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Config-only | 0.2 | No binary change; no consumer impact |
| Code update | 0.5 | Logic change; same interface contracts |
| Schema change | 1.5 | Data shape modified; consumers must handle both versions |
| API contract change | 2.0 | Interface broken; consumers must upgrade |
| Multi-service coordinated | 2.5 | Cross-boundary simultaneity; failure modes multiply |

**CSD Size Bands:**

| CSD Range | Size Classification | Required Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| < 5 | Small | Standard deployment |
| 5 – 15 | Medium | Consider expand-contract for schema/API changes |
| 15 – 30 | Large | Expand-contract required |
| > 30 | Massive | Phased deployment only; no simultaneous exposure |

### Simple Example: Auth Schema Change Contribution

Auth is updating its user session schema to add a `device_fingerprint` field, and simultaneously updating the API contract so downstream consumers receive the new field. Both the schema migration and the API contract update are independently dangerous depth layers.

**Services and depth layers:**
1. Auth — schema change: tier_weight = 1.0, coefficient = 1.5
2. Auth — API contract update: tier_weight = 1.0, coefficient = 2.0

```
CSD = (1.0 × 1.5) + (1.0 × 2.0)
    = 1.5 + 2.0
    = 3.5
```

**CSD = 3.5. Small footprint.** Standard deployment is sufficient for Auth-side changes alone.

### Complex Scenario: Auth Aurora DB Migration — Full CSD

The Aurora migration is not just an Auth-internal change. Auth's session schema changes cascade to every service that reads Auth tokens. Cart, Payment, and Order Management must each update their Auth client libraries to handle the new token format.

**Services and depth layers:**
1. Auth — schema change: P0, tier_weight = 1.0, coefficient = 1.5
2. Auth — API contract update: P0, tier_weight = 1.0, coefficient = 2.0
3. Cart — consumer code update: P0, tier_weight = 1.0, coefficient = 0.5
4. Payment — consumer code update: P0, tier_weight = 1.0, coefficient = 0.5
5. Order Management — consumer code update: P0, tier_weight = 1.0, coefficient = 0.5

**Computation:**
```
CSD = (1.0 × 1.5) + (1.0 × 2.0) + (1.0 × 0.5) + (1.0 × 0.5) + (1.0 × 0.5)
    = 1.5 + 2.0 + 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.5
    = 5.0
```

**CSD = 5.0. Medium footprint — at the boundary of expand-contract territory.**

This is the correct signal: a change touching four P0 services including an API contract update is not small, but it is not massive either. The medium classification means the team should consider expand-contract for the Auth API contract specifically — deploy Auth with both old and new token formats simultaneously, verify Cart/Payment/Order Management have upgraded their clients, then remove the old format.

The fact that CSD = 5.0 sits right at the small/medium boundary is itself a governance signal: this is the minimum viable change surface for the Aurora migration. If the team discovers additional services that also need updates, CSD climbs firmly into medium territory and expand-contract becomes mandatory.

### Decision Gates

| CSD Range | Classification | Deployment Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| < 5 | Small | Standard pipeline; simultaneous deployment acceptable |
| 5 – 15 | Medium | Review schema/API changes for expand-contract; phased by risk layer |
| 15 – 30 | Large | Expand-contract required; deployment sequenced by dependency order |
| > 30 | Massive | Phased only; no service updated until predecessor is stable for 24h |

### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Enumerate every service and every change depth layer separately in the CSD calculation
- Include consumer-side updates in CSD even if the consumers are maintained by other teams
- Use CSD as the deployment sequencing input: highest-depth changes deploy first, consumers follow
- When CSD > 15, write an explicit expand-contract plan before submitting the CAB ticket
- Track CSD per change request in your CMDB to identify patterns of large-footprint releases

**Don't:**
- Count a service once when it has multiple change depth layers — each layer is a separate line item
- Treat consumer updates as trivial because they are only code updates — they still contribute 0.5 per P0 consumer
- Use CSD in isolation without MRI — a low-CSD change can still have high MRI (single high-DG service)
- Skip the expand-contract pattern because "the consumers can update simultaneously" — they will not all succeed simultaneously

### Guardrails

- CSD > 30: automatic phased deployment requirement; no simultaneous multi-service exposure
- CSD 15–30: expand-contract plan required as an attachment to the CAB ticket
- CSD enumeration must include all consuming services, not just the origin service
- Schema and API contract changes have mandatory coefficient minimums (1.5 and 2.0 respectively) — these cannot be self-classified as lower

## Term 4: Rollback Velocity (RV)

### The Concept

RV is the total elapsed time to restore the pre-change system state. It is the most honest single number in change governance because it is measured, not estimated. Everything else in the change management process can be argued or negotiated. RV is empirical. Either you restored the system in 12 minutes in staging, or you did not.

The maintenance window rule is simple and non-negotiable: **window_duration ≥ 3 × RV**. If it takes 12 minutes to roll back Auth in staging, the production maintenance window must be at least 36 minutes. The 3× multiplier accounts for the reality that production rollbacks are slower than staging: more traffic to drain, more database connections to close gracefully, more downstream services to notify, and an operations team that is managing an active incident rather than executing a planned procedure.

Think of RV as the escape hatch measurement in a submarine. The submarine (your service) can dive deep (deploy a complex change), but before it dives, the crew measures exactly how long it takes to emergency surface (roll back). If the dive window is 4 hours and the emergency surface time is 3 hours, that is not a safe dive — there is only 1 hour of margin, and underwater conditions are never exactly like the surface tests. The 3× rule ensures you have enough window to roll back, verify the rollback is complete, and still have time to restore the downstream effects before anyone notices.

RV must be measured in staging with a production-like data volume and traffic pattern. An estimate is not RV. "It should take about 10 minutes" is not RV. RV is the stopwatch measurement from the rollback command execution to the moment when all health checks pass and the previous version is confirmed serving traffic.

### Formula

```
RV = measured total time to restore pre-change state
     (from rollback initiation to health check confirmation)
```

**Window Duration Rule:**
```
window_duration ≥ 3 × RV
```

**RV is not a formula — it is a measurement protocol:**

1. Execute rollback procedure in staging under production-like load
2. Start timer at rollback command execution (not at decision to roll back)
3. Stop timer when all downstream health checks pass and traffic is confirmed on previous version
4. Record RV with date, staging environment config, and data volume
5. Re-measure if staging environment configuration changes materially

### Simple Example: Auth Service Code Update

Auth is deploying a code update. The deployment team measured the rollback procedure in staging last week.

**Staging measurement:**
- Rollback command executed: T+0:00
- Auth pods on previous version serving traffic: T+4:00
- Downstream services (Cart, Payment) health checks passing: T+8:00
- Database connection pool re-established to previous schema: T+12:00
- All health checks confirmed green: T+12:00

**RV = 12 minutes**

**Window calculation:**
```
window_duration ≥ 3 × RV
window_duration ≥ 3 × 12 minutes
window_duration ≥ 36 minutes
```

The maintenance window must be at least 36 minutes. If the business requires a shorter change window (e.g., the low-traffic window between 02:00–02:30 is only 30 minutes), the change cannot proceed in that window. Either find a longer window, or improve the rollback procedure to reduce RV below 10 minutes.

### Complex Scenario: Auth Aurora DB Migration RV

Database migrations have longer rollback procedures than code rollbacks because data written by the new schema must be handled. The RV for the Aurora migration requires explicit staging measurement.

**Staging measurement components:**
- Halt new writes: T+0:00–T+2:00 (2 minutes — connection draining)
- Restore previous Aurora snapshot: T+2:00–T+18:00 (16 minutes — snapshot restore time)
- Validate data integrity post-restore: T+18:00–T+23:00 (5 minutes — row count and checksum validation)
- Restart Auth with previous configuration: T+23:00–T+26:00 (3 minutes — pod restart and readiness probe)
- Re-establish downstream health checks: T+26:00–T+30:00 (4 minutes — Cart, Payment, Order Management reconnect)
- Confirm all checkout journey health checks passing: T+30:00–T+32:00 (2 minutes)

**RV = 32 minutes**

**Window calculation:**
```
window_duration ≥ 3 × 32 minutes = 96 minutes
```

The Aurora migration requires a minimum 96-minute maintenance window. A 2-hour window (120 minutes) provides adequate margin. A 1-hour window is insufficient.

**Implication:** If the only available maintenance window is 90 minutes (common in retail environments with tight low-traffic windows), the team has two options: reduce RV by optimizing the snapshot restore step (using read replica promotion instead of snapshot restore can reduce RV to ~20 minutes, making the window requirement 60 minutes), or negotiate a longer window.

### Decision Gates

| RV vs. Window Duration | Status | Action |
|---|---|---|
| window ≥ 3 × RV | Safe | Proceed with change |
| 2 × RV ≤ window < 3 × RV | Marginal | SRE escalation required; explicit rollback decision timeline defined |
| window < 2 × RV | Unsafe | Change blocked; find larger window or reduce RV |
| RV not measured (estimated only) | Invalid | Change blocked; RV measurement required before scheduling |

### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Measure RV in staging at least once per quarter for all P0 services, even if no migration is planned
- Include RV measurement in your deployment runbook as a required field, not an optional note
- Distinguish between code rollback RV and data migration rollback RV — they are different procedures
- Optimize rollback procedures before optimizing deployment procedures — the rollback is the safety net
- Store RV measurements with version, date, and staging environment configuration

**Don't:**
- Accept "estimated" RV in a CAB ticket — require the staging measurement timestamp
- Measure RV during business hours in staging if production rollback would occur during off-hours — team composition and system load will differ
- Include decision time in RV — RV starts when the rollback command is executed, not when the team decides to roll back
- Assume RV is stable across minor infrastructure changes — a new database version or network topology change can alter RV significantly

### Guardrails

- RV measurement age > 90 days: remeasurement required before scheduling a P0 change
- RV measurement based on estimation rather than actual staging measurement: change blocked
- window < 3 × RV: automatic block in change management system
- Data migration RV must include data integrity validation step — not just service restart

## Term 5: Service Compatibility Window (SCW)

### The Concept

SCW is the duration during which two versions of a service must coexist simultaneously. It is the governing constraint for any change that affects interfaces between services — API contracts, message formats, database schemas accessed by multiple services, or event stream schemas.

The fundamental insight is this: in a distributed system, you cannot update all consumers and producers simultaneously. Some consumer will be on the old version while the producer has already moved to the new version, or vice versa. The SCW is the maximum duration of that overlap. Any protocol or schema change requires an explicit SCW plan — a description of what both versions are, how the service handles requests from both old and new consumers during the window, and when the old version can be retired.

Think of SCW as the bilingual signage period in a major city when an official language changes. You cannot flip every sign simultaneously. For a period of weeks or months, signs must display both the old and new names. Bus drivers must understand both. GPS maps must route to either. The SCW for a city-wide language change might be 18 months. If you remove the old signs on day 1, half the population gets lost. The SCW is not a failure of planning — it is the correct acknowledgment of distributed system reality.

SCW = max(deploy_time, consumer_upgrade_time, data_migration_time). It is the maximum of the three durations, not the sum, because these phases may overlap — but the longest one sets the total window.

### Formula

```
SCW = max(deploy_time, consumer_upgrade_time, data_migration_time)
```

**Components:**

| Variable | Definition |
|---|---|
| `deploy_time` | Time to deploy the new version to all producer instances |
| `consumer_upgrade_time` | Time for all consumers to deploy their updated clients and pass health checks |
| `data_migration_time` | Time to migrate all data to the new schema format, including backfill |

**When SCW applies:** Any change involving:
- API endpoint signature change (added required field, changed type, removed field)
- Message schema change (Kafka topic, RabbitMQ message format)
- Database schema change accessed by multiple services
- Event contract change (CloudEvents, internal event bus)

**When SCW does not apply:** Config-only changes, internal code refactoring with no interface change, dependency version bumps with no API changes.

### Simple Example: Auth API Contract Change (Token Format Update)

Auth is updating the JWT token format to add a `device_fingerprint` claim. Cart, Payment, and Order Management parse Auth tokens. All three must update their Auth client libraries before the new token format becomes the default.

**Components:**
- deploy_time = 5 minutes (Auth new version deployed to all pods; rolling update)
- consumer_upgrade_time = 30 minutes (Cart: 10 min, Payment: 12 min, Order Management: 8 min — sequential with health checks)
- data_migration_time = 0 minutes (JWT tokens are stateless; no data migration required)

**Computation:**
```
SCW = max(5, 30, 0)
    = 30 minutes
```

**SCW = 30 minutes.** For 30 minutes, Auth must serve both old-format tokens (for consumers that have not upgraded yet) and new-format tokens. The Auth service must be deployed with a compatibility flag that generates both formats simultaneously. Only after all consumers confirm new-format token validation can the old format be retired.

### Complex Scenario: Auth Aurora Migration — Full SCW

The Aurora migration involves a database schema change (adding columns, modifying indexes), which means the Auth service itself must be compatible with both old and new schema during the migration window. Additionally, all downstream consumers must upgrade their Auth client libraries.

**Components:**
- deploy_time = 5 minutes (Auth new version deployed)
- consumer_upgrade_time = 30 minutes (Cart, Payment, Order Management client library updates)
- data_migration_time = 240 minutes (4 hours — backfilling `device_fingerprint` for existing sessions, updating indexes, running validation queries on 50M+ session records)

**Computation:**
```
SCW = max(5, 30, 240)
    = 240 minutes
    = 4 hours
```

**SCW = 4 hours.** This is the governing constraint. For 4 hours during the Aurora migration, Auth must maintain compatibility with both the old and new schema simultaneously. This means:

1. Auth cannot drop the old columns until the data migration completes (4 hours)
2. Auth must handle queries from consumers that expect the old response format during the consumer upgrade window (30 minutes, which is nested inside the 4-hour window)
3. The deployment window must be at least 4 hours — the data migration is the bottleneck

**Operational implication:** A 96-minute maintenance window (3 × RV for Auth) is insufficient for the Aurora migration because SCW = 4 hours. The actual window must satisfy max(3 × RV, SCW) = max(96 minutes, 240 minutes) = 4 hours. This conflict between RV-driven window requirements and SCW-driven window requirements is the source of many failed change plans — both constraints apply, and the window must satisfy both.

### Decision Gates

| SCW Duration | Required Plan Elements |
|---|---|
| 0 (no interface change) | SCW plan not required |
| < 30 minutes | Compatibility flag in service config; consumer upgrade order documented |
| 30 min – 4 hours | Formal dual-version compatibility plan; consumer rollout sequence; rollback procedure for partial upgrade |
| > 4 hours | Architecture review required; consider whether the migration can be decomposed |
| > 24 hours | Executive sign-off required; extended dual-version support plan |

### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Compute SCW before scheduling the change window — it may be the binding constraint on window length
- Design compatibility layers into your service before the migration, not during it
- Use feature flags to control when each consumer switches to the new format
- Monitor the consumer upgrade progress in real-time during the SCW — if a consumer upgrade fails, you need to detect it before the old format is retired
- Document the dual-version behavior explicitly: what happens when the service receives an old-format request after the new version is deployed?

**Don't:**
- Retire the old format before all consumers have confirmed upgrade completion
- Assume consumers will upgrade simultaneously — they will not
- Use SCW interchangeably with deploy_time — the data migration is frequently the binding constraint
- Skip the SCW calculation for "minor" API changes — adding a required field is a breaking change that requires full SCW planning

### Guardrails

- Any API contract or schema change: SCW plan required as a CAB ticket attachment
- SCW > 4 hours: architecture review required; decomposition options must be evaluated
- Consumer upgrade progress must be monitored with explicit success criteria (not just "all consumers deployed")
- Old format retirement step must be a separate change ticket, not part of the initial deployment

## Term 6: Error Budget Reservation (EBR)

### The Concept

EBR is the budget gate. It answers the question: does the system have enough reliability margin to absorb this change?

Error budget is the allowed downtime per SLA per month. If the checkout journey SLA is 99.95%, the monthly budget is approximately 21.6 minutes of allowed degradation (30 days × 24 hours × 60 minutes × 0.0005). Every incident, every degraded deployment, every latency spike that violates SLA thresholds consumes budget. EBR reserves a portion of the remaining budget against the risk of the proposed change before the change executes.

The analogy is a surgery team's blood bank protocol. Before a high-risk surgery, the anesthesiologist reserves units of blood against the possibility of significant blood loss. The blood is not used unless needed — but it must be available. If the blood bank does not have enough units to cover the reservation, the surgery is rescheduled until supply is restored. EBR applies the same logic to error budget: if the remaining budget is less than the EBR, the change is rescheduled to the next window.

EBR is proportional to MRI — high-risk changes reserve more budget. The formula `min(MRI × 0.05, 0.80)` ensures that even extremely high-MRI changes cannot reserve more than 80% of the remaining budget (preserving a floor for operational headroom), and that low-MRI changes reserve proportionally less.

### Formula

```
EBR = min(MRI × 0.05, 0.80)
      expressed as fraction of monthly budget

Gate: if budget_remaining < EBR → change blocked until next window
```

**Components:**

| Variable | Definition |
|---|---|
| `MRI` | Migration Risk Index (computed from Term 2) |
| `0.05` | 5% of budget per MRI unit — calibrated to reserve meaningful margin for moderate-risk changes |
| `0.80` | Cap — no single change can reserve more than 80% of remaining budget |
| `budget_remaining` | Fraction of monthly budget not yet consumed = `(budget_total - budget_consumed) / budget_total` |

**Checkout budget reference:**
- Monthly budget = 30 × 24 × 60 × (1 - 0.9995) = 43,200 × 0.0005 = 21.6 minutes
- Each minute of checkout degradation = 1/21.6 ≈ 4.6% of monthly budget

### Simple Example: MRI = 2 Change (Two-Approver Tier)

A Cart configuration change has been computed with MRI = 2.0 (code update to Cart, moderate complexity, within the two-approver CAB tier).

**EBR Computation:**
```
EBR = min(2.0 × 0.05, 0.80)
    = min(0.10, 0.80)
    = 0.10
```

**EBR = 10% of monthly budget must remain after this change.**

**Budget check — two scenarios:**

Scenario A: Budget remaining = 37% (8 minutes of 21.6 minutes remaining)
```
budget_remaining (0.37) ≥ EBR (0.10) → PASS
Change may proceed.
```

Scenario B: Budget remaining = 8% (1.7 minutes of 21.6 minutes remaining)
```
budget_remaining (0.08) < EBR (0.10) → BLOCKED
Insufficient budget remaining to absorb the risk of this change.
Change rescheduled to next month's window.
```

### Complex Scenario: The Budget Gate in a Live CAB Session

It is Thursday afternoon CAB. Three change requests are on the table. The checkout error budget currently has 8 minutes remaining of its 21.6 minute monthly budget — that is 37% remaining.

**CR-1: Cart code update (MRI = 2.0)**
```
EBR = min(2.0 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.10, 0.80) = 10%
budget_remaining = 37% ≥ 10% → PASS
```

**CR-2: Payment gateway update (MRI = 5.0)**
```
EBR = min(5.0 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.25, 0.80) = 25%
budget_remaining = 37% ≥ 25% → PASS
```

Note: if CR-1 runs first and consumes some budget during deployment (even a successful deployment may consume minutes if latency degrades briefly), the remaining budget might drop to 32%, which still passes CR-2's 25% EBR.

**CR-3: Auth API update (MRI = 8.5, hypothetically)**
```
EBR = min(8.5 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.425, 0.80) = 42.5%
budget_remaining = 37% < 42.5% → BLOCKED
```

**CAB decision:** CR-1 and CR-2 can proceed. CR-3 is blocked for this window. The Auth change is rescheduled to next month, or to a later window this month if budget is restored by the SLA reset date.

**The Aurora migration EBR (current MRI = 38.9):**
```
EBR = min(38.9 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(1.945, 0.80) = 80%
```

Even if 99% of the budget remained, the Aurora migration would require 80% reservation. A change this risky demands near-full budget availability. This is the correct behavior — the EBR cap prevents the formula from producing impossible requirements (> 100%), while still ensuring that extremely high-risk changes can only proceed when the system is in exceptional health.

**After architecture improvements (MRI = 5.0, from FLI/OCR investments):**
```
EBR = min(5.0 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.25, 0.80) = 25%
```

With MRI = 5.0 achieved through platform improvements, the Aurora migration requires only 25% budget remaining. If 37% is available, the change can proceed. This demonstrates that architectural investment in FLI and OCR has a direct, computable impact on deployment flexibility — it does not just lower MRI, it lowers the budget barrier for scheduling the change.

### Decision Gates

| EBR vs. Budget Remaining | Status | Action |
|---|---|---|
| budget_remaining ≥ EBR + 0.20 (comfortable margin) | Green | Proceed; standard monitoring |
| budget_remaining ≥ EBR (passes gate, no margin) | Yellow | Proceed with heightened SRE attention; any incident triggers immediate change hold |
| budget_remaining < EBR | Red | Change blocked; reschedule to next window |
| budget_remaining < 0.10 (under 10%) | Freeze | All non-critical changes blocked regardless of EBR |

### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Check EBR at CAB ticket submission and again at change execution time — budget can change between submission and execution
- Use EBR to sequence multiple changes: lower-EBR changes first, higher-EBR changes when budget is fullest (start of month)
- Treat EBR as a rolling calculation — budget consumed during a previous change in the same window reduces available budget for subsequent changes
- Communicate EBR results to the business: "We cannot deploy the Auth update this month because the checkout journey has insufficient budget margin. This is the mathematical consequence of the incidents on day 7 and day 14."
- Track EBR blocking events over time — a pattern of monthly EBR blocks indicates the system's incident rate is exceeding its deployment ambition

**Don't:**
- Compute EBR only against total budget — compute against remaining budget at execution time
- Override EBR gates based on business urgency — the budget is the budget; urgency is what makes the next incident worse
- Conflate EBR blocking with change rejection — EBR blocking is a timing decision, not a quality judgment
- Use a static budget remaining figure in a live CAB session — pull the live value from your SLO monitoring system

### Guardrails

- Budget remaining < 10%: all non-critical deployments blocked automatically
- EBR check performed at execution time (not just submission time) for any change with a scheduling lag > 24 hours
- EBR blocking reason documented in change ticket with exact budget figures at time of block
- MRI cap at 0.80 applied automatically — no single change reserves more than 80% of remaining budget

## Term 7: Deployment Safety Score (DSS)

### The Concept

DSS is the composite deployment confidence score. It synthesizes SCV, MRI, APR, and budget remaining into a single number that determines the deployment strategy — standard canary, SRE on bridge, maintenance window required, or block.

If MRI is the approval gate and EBR is the budget gate, DSS is the execution gate. A change can have passed CAB approval (MRI gate), have sufficient budget reserved (EBR gate), and still be unsafe to deploy because the combination of service volatility, system health, and team readiness creates an unacceptable execution risk.

Think of DSS as the launch commit decision in rocket launches. Each sub-system has passed its individual go/no-go: engines checked, telemetry checked, weather checked, range clear. DSS is the flight director's composite read — not "are all systems individually green?" but "given the current combination of all systems, is the risk of this launch acceptable for this mission?" A launch where three systems are marginally green can produce a no-go even though no individual system is red.

DSS captures four orthogonal risk dimensions. SCV represents the service's historical volatility — has it been well-behaved recently? MRI represents structural blast radius — how bad could a failure get? APR (Automated Pipeline Reliability, from ch04) represents the quality of the deployment pipeline itself — can we trust the mechanism that will execute this change? Budget remaining represents system health margin — do we have room to absorb an unexpected failure?

The APR term is frequently the most surprising to engineers new to DSS. A change that would be low-risk on a healthy pipeline becomes high-risk when APR is low — because a low APR means the pipeline itself has a significant failure probability. The risk of the deployment mechanism failing is as real as the risk of the change content failing.

### Formula

```
DSS = (1 - SCV_normalized) × (1 - MRI/10) × APR × budget_remaining_fraction

where: SCV_normalized = min(SCV/5.0, 1.0)
```

**Components:**

| Variable | Definition | Range |
|---|---|---|
| `SCV_normalized` | SCV scaled to 0–1 range (capped at 5.0 SCV = 1.0 normalized) | 0.0–1.0 |
| `MRI/10` | MRI normalized against the block threshold of 10 | 0.0–1.0+ (capped at 1.0 for BLOCKED) |
| `APR` | Automated Pipeline Reliability from ch04 | 0.0–1.0 |
| `budget_remaining_fraction` | Fraction of monthly error budget not yet consumed | 0.0–1.0 |

**DSS Deployment Strategy Bands:**

| DSS Range | Deployment Strategy |
|---|---|
| > 0.70 | Standard canary — no special monitoring |
| 0.40 – 0.70 | SRE on bridge during deployment window |
| 0.20 – 0.40 | Maintenance window required; enhanced rollback readiness |
| < 0.20 | Block — system conditions do not support this deployment |

### Simple Example: Cart Code Update — Current System State

Cart code update under current system conditions (APR = 0.31, budget remaining = 37%).

**Given for Cart:**
- SCV_cart = 0.10 (Cart is stable — low deploy frequency, low rollback rate)
- SCV_normalized = min(0.10/5.0, 1.0) = min(0.02, 1.0) = 0.02
- MRI_cart = 3.45 (after architecture improvements — using the improved scenario from Term 2)
- APR = 0.31 (current system APR)
- budget_remaining = 0.37

**Computation:**
```
Step 1: (1 - SCV_normalized) = 1 - 0.02 = 0.98
Step 2: (1 - MRI/10) = 1 - 3.45/10 = 1 - 0.345 = 0.655
Step 3: APR = 0.31
Step 4: budget_remaining = 0.37

DSS = 0.98 × 0.655 × 0.31 × 0.37
    = 0.6419 × 0.31 × 0.37
    = 0.1990 × 0.37
    = 0.0736
```

**DSS = 0.074. BLOCK.**

This is the critical insight: a routine Cart code update — the most boring change in the platform — produces a block-level DSS under current system conditions. The culprit is APR = 0.31. The deployment pipeline has a 69% failure probability. Multiplied across all other terms, even a low-risk change cannot achieve a safe DSS when the execution mechanism itself is unreliable.

This is not a policy failure. This is the math correctly identifying that deploying anything when your pipeline succeeds only 31% of the time is risky, regardless of the change content.

### Complex Scenario: Cart Code Update After System Improvements

Same Cart code update, but the team has invested in APR improvement (to 0.80) and the budget picture is better (95% remaining — early in the month).

**Given:**
- SCV_normalized = 0.02 (unchanged)
- MRI_cart = 3.45 (unchanged)
- APR = 0.80 (improved pipeline reliability)
- budget_remaining = 0.95 (early month, minimal budget consumption)

**Computation:**
```
Step 1: (1 - SCV_normalized) = 0.98
Step 2: (1 - MRI/10) = 0.655
Step 3: APR = 0.80
Step 4: budget_remaining = 0.95

DSS = 0.98 × 0.655 × 0.80 × 0.95
    = 0.6419 × 0.80 × 0.95
    = 0.5135 × 0.95
    = 0.488
```

**DSS = 0.487. SRE on bridge.**

The same Cart code update, with the same SCV and MRI, moves from BLOCK to SRE-on-bridge purely through pipeline reliability improvement and budget timing. This is the correct operational reality: system readiness matters as much as change content.

**The standard canary ceiling for this change:**

To achieve standard canary (DSS > 0.70), compute the maximum possible DSS with SCV_normalized = 0.02 and MRI = 3.45:
```
Maximum DSS = 0.98 × 0.655 × 1.0 × 1.0 = 0.642
```

The maximum achievable DSS for this Cart change with MRI = 3.45 is 0.642. Standard canary (> 0.70) is not achievable under any conditions while MRI remains at 3.45. To reach standard canary, the team must reduce Cart's MRI below approximately 2.0 through architectural improvements. DSS reveals not just today's status but the architectural ceiling.

### Decision Gates

| DSS Range | Required Deployment Strategy | SRE Action |
|---|---|---|
| > 0.70 | Standard canary | Monitor dashboards; no active bridge required |
| 0.40 – 0.70 | SRE on bridge | SRE joins deployment bridge call; holds rollback authority |
| 0.20 – 0.40 | Maintenance window required | Full incident command posture; pre-positioned rollback |
| < 0.20 | Block | Change does not execute; root cause of low DSS identified |

### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Compute DSS at execution time, not only at CAB approval time — APR and budget change daily
- Use DSS to determine SRE staffing for deployment windows; it makes the staffing decision objective
- Investigate low DSS to identify which term is the dominant driver — each term has a different remediation path
- Track DSS over time for each service; a trending-down DSS is an early warning of increasing deployment risk
- Communicate DSS to the business: "This change is in maintenance-window territory because our pipeline APR is 0.31. The investment case for APR improvement directly translates to shorter deployment windows and lower SRE staffing overhead."

**Don't:**
- Override a block-level DSS based on change content confidence — the DSS reflects system conditions, not change quality
- Treat DSS as permanent policy: a change in block territory today may be standard canary next month when APR improves
- Ignore the APR term — it is frequently the dominant DSS driver and is actionable through pipeline investment
- Compute DSS without pulling live budget remaining — a stale budget figure produces an incorrect DSS

### Guardrails

- DSS < 0.20: automatic block in change management system; requires SRE director override to proceed
- DSS computed from live pipeline data (APR) and live SLO consumption (budget remaining) — no cached values
- DSS block decisions logged with the DSS value and each component value at time of block
- SRE on bridge deployments (DSS 0.40–0.70) require pre-execution rollback rehearsal confirmed within 24 hours

## How These Terms Work Together

The seven terms form a sequential decision pipeline. They are not independent — each term informs the next, and all seven must be computed before any governance decision is final.

```
Change Request Submitted
         │
         ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  SCV Computation        │ ──── Is this service volatile right now?
│  (Service fingerprint)  │     High SCV → elevated monitoring during deploy
└──────────┬──────────────┘
           │
           ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  MRI Computation        │ ──── What governance tier does this require?
│  (Approval gate)        │     MRI ≥ 8 → BLOCKED before proceeding
└──────────┬──────────────┘
           │ (if MRI < 8)
           ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  CSD Computation        │ ──── What is the change footprint?
│  (Deployment strategy)  │     CSD > 15 → expand-contract required
└──────────┬──────────────┘
           │
           ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  RV Measurement         │ ──── Is the maintenance window long enough?
│  (Window constraint)    │     window < 3×RV → reschedule or enlarge window
└──────────┬──────────────┘
           │ (if window ≥ 3×RV)
           ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  SCW Computation        │ ──── How long must dual-version coexistence last?
│  (Compatibility plan)   │     SCW > window → window must extend to match SCW
└──────────┬──────────────┘
           │
           ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  EBR Computation        │ ──── Does the system have budget margin for this?
│  (Budget gate)          │     budget_remaining < EBR → reschedule to next window
└──────────┬──────────────┘
           │ (if budget passes EBR)
           ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  DSS Computation        │ ──── What deployment posture does this require?
│  (Execution gate)       │     DSS < 0.20 → block; 0.20–0.40 → maintenance window
└──────────┬──────────────┘
           │
           ▼
    Change Executes
    with appropriate
    deployment posture
```

**The binding constraint principle:** At each stage, the most restrictive output governs. MRI blocking overrides everything downstream. A passed MRI but failed EBR blocks at the budget gate. A passed EBR but block-level DSS blocks at the execution gate. The chain is only as strong as its most restrictive link.

**The architectural investment principle:** Each term reveals a specific investment opportunity. High MRI despite low SCV → invest in FLI and OCR infrastructure. Low DSS despite manageable MRI → invest in APR (pipeline reliability). High SCW despite simple changes → invest in backward compatibility patterns. The framework does not just govern changes — it makes the investment case for platform improvements computable and communicable to leadership.

## End-to-End Scenario: Thursday Afternoon CAB — Payment Service Update

It is 14:00 Thursday. A senior engineer from the Payments team submits a CAB ticket for a Payment Service code update. The change is described as "routine" — updating the payment gateway SDK from version 3.1.2 to 3.2.1. The CAB chair asks you, the on-call SRE, to evaluate it using the framework.

**System state at 14:00:**
- APR = 0.31 (pipeline has been unreliable this sprint — three failed deployments in five days)
- FLI = 0.72 (current platform feature launch infrastructure)
- OCR_weighted = 0.457 (checkout journey observability)
- Checkout budget consumed: 8 minutes of 21.6 minutes = 63% consumed, 37% remaining
- Payment Service: DG = 12 (downstream: Order Management, Notification, Fraud Detection, analytics pipeline, and 8 others)
- Checkout journey CD = 5 (P0, SLA = 99.95%)

---

**Step 1: SCV — Is Payment volatile right now?**

The deployment pipeline shows Payment has deployed 2 times this week. Both were code updates (CSAI = 0.5). One required rollback (50% failure rate).

```
SCV = 2 × 0.5 × 0.50 = 0.50
```

SCV = 0.50. Exactly at the stable/moderate boundary. The single rollback this week is a yellow flag. Payment is not in a high-volatility state, but it is not clean either.

---

**Step 2: MRI — What governance tier?**

Payment is a P0 service in the checkout journey. DG = 12, CD = 5, FLI = 0.72, OCR = 0.457.

```
MRI = 12 × 5 × (1 - 0.72) × (2 - 0.457)
    = 12 × 5 × 0.28 × 1.543
    = 60 × 0.28 × 1.543
    = 16.8 × 1.543
    = 25.93
```

**MRI = 25.93. BLOCKED.**

The CAB chair may be surprised. This is a "routine" SDK update. But the math is not wrong — Payment has 12 downstream dependents, anchors the 99.95% SLA checkout journey, and the current system has poor observability and immature dark launch capability. At MRI = 25.93, this change cannot proceed regardless of how routine the SDK update appears.

**The CAB conversation at this point:**

CAB Chair: "MRI is 25.93 — that is blocked. What would it take to bring it under 8?"

You: "Payment has DG = 12 — we cannot change the architecture without a multi-quarter decomposition effort. To reach MRI < 8, we need FLI ≥ 0.90, OCR ≥ 0.80, and we should time this outside peak checkout hours to reduce effective CD."

```
Scenario: FLI = 0.90, OCR = 0.80, CD = 4 (off-peak window):
MRI = 12 × 4 × (1 - 0.90) × (2 - 0.80)
    = 48 × 0.10 × 1.20
    = 48 × 0.12
    = 5.76
```

With platform improvements and better timing, MRI = 5.76. VP Engineering tier. Achievable — but it requires a multi-quarter platform investment and scheduling coordination.

**For completeness, compute the remaining terms assuming the improved MRI = 5.76:**

---

**Step 3: CSD — What is the deployment footprint?**

Payment SDK update: code update to Payment only. No schema change, no API contract change, no consumer updates required.

```
CSD = 1.0 × 0.5 = 0.5
```

CSD = 0.5. Small footprint. Standard deployment sequencing is sufficient.

---

**Step 4: RV — Is the window long enough?**

The team measured Payment rollback in staging last month: 18 minutes (pod restart, health checks, gateway reconnect verification).

```
window_duration ≥ 3 × 18 = 54 minutes
```

The proposed maintenance window is Saturday 02:00–04:00 (120 minutes). 120 ≥ 54. **PASS.**

---

**Step 5: SCW — How long must dual-version coexist?**

This is a code update only — no API contract change, no schema change. SCW does not apply.

---

**Step 6: EBR — Does the budget support this?**

With MRI = 5.76:
```
EBR = min(5.76 × 0.05, 0.80)
    = min(0.288, 0.80)
    = 0.288 = 28.8%
```

Budget remaining at current measurement: 37%.

```
budget_remaining (0.37) ≥ EBR (0.288) → PASS (margin: 8.2%)
```

EBR passes, but with minimal margin. Any incident between now and Saturday that consumes more than 8.2% of budget (approximately 1.8 minutes of checkout degradation) would block this change. One P0 incident blocks the Saturday window.

---

**Step 7: DSS — What deployment posture?**

```
SCV_normalized = min(0.50/5.0, 1.0) = min(0.10, 1.0) = 0.10
(1 - SCV_normalized) = 0.90
(1 - MRI/10) = 1 - 5.76/10 = 0.424
APR = 0.31 (current; assuming no improvement by Saturday)
budget_remaining = 0.37

DSS = 0.90 × 0.424 × 0.31 × 0.37
    = 0.3816 × 0.31 × 0.37
    = 0.1183 × 0.37
    = 0.044
```

**DSS = 0.044. BLOCK.**

Even with MRI improved to 5.76 through platform work, the current APR of 0.31 drives DSS below 0.20. The deployment pipeline is the binding constraint on DSS — no change can achieve a safe execution posture when the pipeline fails 69% of the time.

---

**Final CAB Decision:**

The Payment Service SDK update is blocked on two grounds:
1. **MRI = 25.93** under current conditions — requires FLI improvement to 0.90 and OCR to 0.80 before rescheduling (multi-quarter infrastructure investment)
2. **DSS = 0.044** even under improved MRI — requires APR improvement from 0.31 to at least 0.60 before the execution risk is acceptable

**Conditions for scheduling:**
- Platform achieves FLI ≥ 0.90 (automated dark launch for P0 services)
- Checkout journey OCR improves to ≥ 0.80
- APR improves to ≥ 0.60 (pipeline reliability investment)
- Change scheduled in off-peak window (Saturday 02:00–04:00)
- VP Engineering approval obtained (MRI 3.0–8.0 tier)
- RV re-measured in staging within 30 days of scheduled window
- Budget remaining ≥ 29% at execution time

**The CAB chair closes the ticket:** "Not because the change is bad, but because the platform is not in a condition to absorb it safely. The framework gives us the exact investments required to schedule this in the next quarter."

This is the purpose of the framework — not to block changes, but to make the blocking conditions explicit and the path forward computable.

### Exercise 6.1: Notification Service Schema Change — Low Tier, High Risk?

The Notification Service (P2) wants to add a `notification_channel` field to its message schema. This is a schema change affecting the message queue consumers (User Profile and Order Management subscribe to notification events). Current system state: FLI = 0.72, OCR_weighted = 0.457.

Notification Service properties:
- DG = 4 (User Profile and Order Management are direct consumers; two analytics sinks)
- CD = 1 (Notification affects the Browse journey marginally; lowest criticality weight)
- FLI = 0.72 (platform-wide)
- OCR_weighted = 0.457 (platform-wide)

**Compute MRI:**
```
MRI = DG × CD × (1 - FLI) × (2 - OCR_weighted)
    = 4 × 1 × (1 - 0.72) × (2 - 0.457)
    = 4 × 1 × 0.28 × 1.543
    = 4 × 0.28 × 1.543
    = 1.12 × 1.543
    = 1.729
```

**MRI = 1.73. Two-approver CAB tier.**

**What does this reveal?** Even a P2 (low-tier) service with minimal journey impact produces a two-approver CAB requirement when the platform's FLI and OCR are poor. MRI = 1.73 is not dangerous — it is not close to the VP or block tier — but it is above the SRE-notification-only threshold of 1.0.

The insight for low-tier services: **poor platform health (low FLI, low OCR) elevates governance requirements across all services, not just P0 services.** A platform with FLI = 0.95 and OCR = 0.90 would produce:
```
MRI = 4 × 1 × 0.05 × 1.10 = 0.22 → SRE notification only
```

The same Notification schema change would require only an SRE email on a well-instrumented platform. The investment in platform FLI and OCR reduces governance overhead for every service, at every tier — not just for the high-risk P0 migrations. This is the compounding return on platform infrastructure investment: a team deploying a P2 service should not need two CAB approvers, but they do today because the platform infrastructure is immature.

### Exercise 6.2: Budget-Constrained Change Sequencing

It is the 22nd of the month. The checkout error budget has 15% remaining (3.24 minutes of 21.6 minutes). Three change requests are in the CAB queue for the same weekend window:

- **CR-A:** Cart config update, MRI = 2.0
- **CR-B:** Payment code update, MRI = 5.0
- **CR-C:** Inventory (P1) config update, MRI = 1.0

**Step 1: Compute EBR for each.**

```
EBR_A = min(2.0 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.10, 0.80) = 10%
EBR_B = min(5.0 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.25, 0.80) = 25%
EBR_C = min(1.0 × 0.05, 0.80) = min(0.05, 0.80) = 5%
```

**Step 2: Check against available budget (15%).**

```
CR-A: 15% ≥ 10% → PASS
CR-B: 15% ≥ 25% → BLOCKED (15% < 25%)
CR-C: 15% ≥ 5%  → PASS
```

**Initial decision:** CR-A and CR-C proceed. CR-B is blocked for this window.

**Step 3: Optimal sequencing for CR-A and CR-C.**

CR-C runs first (EBR = 5%, lower risk — preserve maximum budget for CR-A). Assume CR-C completes successfully. Budget remaining: approximately 14% (slight consumption from deployment latency events).

CR-A runs second. After CR-A, budget might drop to approximately 12% remaining.

**Step 4: Can CR-B proceed after CR-A and CR-C succeed?**

```
Budget remaining ≈ 12%. EBR_B = 25%. 12% < 25% → Still BLOCKED.
```

CR-B remains blocked regardless of CR-A and CR-C outcomes. The budget gate is deterministic.

**Options for CR-B:**
1. Wait until month reset (8 days) when budget refreshes to 100% — at month start, EBR_B = 25% would pass trivially
2. Reduce MRI (if possible) to lower the EBR requirement — MRI 5.0 → MRI 2.0 would reduce EBR from 25% to 10%, which would pass at 15% budget remaining
3. Accept the deferral; use the time to improve platform FLI/OCR so the next scheduling attempt is easier

The correct sequencing answer: CR-C first, then CR-A, then CR-B deferred to next month.

## Chapter Summary

- **SCV** measures a service's deployment risk fingerprint as the product of change frequency, change complexity (CSAI), and historical rollback rate. It contextualizes change risk in terms of recent behavior — not change content. A service with SCV > 2.0 is under deployment pressure regardless of the quality of the current change. The rollback rate is the highest-leverage driver to investigate when SCV is elevated.

- **MRI** gates CAB approval tiers by translating blast radius (DG), journey criticality (CD), and platform maturity (FLI, OCR) into a computable governance level. MRI ≥ 8 blocks changes regardless of team confidence. Auth with DG = 18 produces MRI = 38.9 under current conditions — not because the team is incompetent, but because the structural blast radius is enormous. Improving FLI and OCR reduces MRI across every service simultaneously, making platform infrastructure investment computable in governance terms.

- **CSD** determines deployment strategy by measuring change footprint across all affected services and change depth layers. A single service contributing multiple depth layers (schema change + API contract update) produces multiple CSD line items. CSD > 15 mandates expand-contract; CSD > 30 mandates phased deployment. CSD prevents the common mistake of treating a four-service consumer cascade as a single-service change.

- **RV** is the single empirical measurement in the framework. Window ≥ 3 × RV is non-negotiable and must be measured in staging, not estimated. Read replica promotion eliminates the snapshot restore bottleneck, reducing Auth RV from 32 to 24 minutes. Optimizing the rollback procedure before optimizing the deployment procedure is the correct priority order — the rollback is the safety net.

- **SCW** is the binding constraint for any interface change. It equals the maximum of deploy time, consumer upgrade time, and data migration time. For the Auth Aurora migration, SCW = 240 minutes because the data migration drives it — not the 5-minute deploy or the 30-minute consumer upgrade. SCW must be computed before committing to a maintenance window length; it frequently overrides RV-derived window requirements.

- **EBR** is the budget gate: the system must have sufficient reliability margin to absorb the risk of the change. EBR = min(MRI × 0.05, 0.80). High-MRI changes require more budget margin; the cap at 0.80 prevents impossible requirements. Scheduling high-EBR changes early in the monthly budget cycle (days 1–10) is a legitimate risk reduction strategy. EBR blocking is a timing decision, not a quality judgment.

- **DSS** is the execution gate that synthesizes all factors into a deployment posture decision. Low APR is frequently the dominant DSS driver and is often overlooked because it is a platform condition, not a change property. A routine Cart code update blocked by DSS = 0.074 is the framework correctly identifying that the deployment mechanism (APR = 0.31) is the risk — not the change content. DSS also reveals architectural ceilings: a change with MRI = 3.45 cannot exceed DSS = 0.642 regardless of APR or budget, meaning standard canary requires MRI reduction below 2.0.

### Question 3: Judgment Scenario (Binding Constraint Identification)

The Order Management service (P0, DG = 9) is planning a Kafka schema migration for its order event topic. The migration changes the event format — all four consumers (Notification, Inventory, Recommendation, Analytics) must update their consumer code.

**Known facts:**
- RV measured in staging: 15 minutes
- Proposed maintenance window: Saturday 02:00–03:00 (60 minutes)
- Consumer upgrade times: Notification 5 min, Inventory 8 min, Recommendation 5 min, Analytics 20 min
- Data migration (backfill of historical events in new format): 180 minutes
- Budget remaining: 45%
- MRI (computed): 4.2

1. Identify the binding constraint that prevents this change from executing in the proposed Saturday window.
2. Compute SCW and show which component drives it.
3. Propose a decomposition strategy that makes this change feasible in a 60-minute window.

**Expected answer elements:**

1. The binding constraint is SCW, not RV. The 60-minute window satisfies the RV constraint (3 × 15 = 45 min < 60 min — passes). But SCW = 180 minutes, and 60 minutes cannot accommodate 180 minutes of dual-version coexistence.

2. SCW = max(deploy_time, consumer_upgrade_time, data_migration_time) = max(~5 min, 20 min [Analytics is the slowest consumer], 180 min) = **180 minutes**. The historical event backfill is the binding SCW component.

3. Decomposition strategy: separate the schema migration from the backfill. Phase 1 (60-minute window): deploy Order Management with dual-format event emission (old and new format simultaneously). Update all four consumers to accept both formats in the same window. No backfill — existing historical events remain in old format. Consumer upgrade SCW = max(5, 20, 0) = 20 minutes. Phase 1 fits in the 60-minute window with margin. Phase 2 (separate 3–4 hour window, scheduled independently): run the historical event backfill as an offline batch job. All consumers already handle both formats, so Phase 2 is operationally low-risk. The decomposition converts a single 180-minute constraint into a 20-minute Phase 1 constraint and a separate, lower-risk Phase 2. Both phases have their own MRI, EBR, and DSS computations.
