# Availability & Continuity Management: The Complete Framework

## What This Is

When a board member asks "what happens to this company if our primary data center goes offline for 72 hours?" — that question is not answered by an engineer. It is answered by a Director or VP who has already done the math, built the plan, tested it, and can state the financial exposure, the recovery timeline, and the investment required to reduce that exposure.

This framework covers the full continuum from engineering High Availability (HA) decisions through Disaster Recovery (DR) planning to Business Continuity Planning (BCP) and board-level risk governance. It treats availability not as a technical metric but as a financial commitment and a governance obligation.

This is not a theory document. It is a working framework covering:
- How to translate uptime percentages into dollar risk that a board can act on
- How to design HA architecture that matches the actual cost the business is willing to pay
- How to build a DR program that survives an audit and a real disaster
- How to run DR drills, Game Days, and tabletop exercises that produce real findings
- How to map dependencies and eliminate single points of failure before they eliminate your service
- How to report availability posture to executives and regulators in language they understand
- What each role owns — from SRE on-call through VP of Operations through Chief Risk Officer

## The Three Failure Modes of Continuity Programs

Most organizations fail at availability and continuity management in one of three patterns:

**Failure Mode 1: Engineering HA without business alignment**
The platform team builds sophisticated multi-AZ deployments and cross-region replication. The business has never stated what RTO and RPO they actually need. The engineering investment is either insufficient (because the real requirement is 4-hour RTO but the team built for 24-hour) or wasteful (because the business would accept 48-hour RTO but the team paid for hot standby). No one has done the math. The gap is discovered during an actual disaster.

**Failure Mode 2: DR plans that exist on paper and nowhere else**
The organization has a DR plan. It was written three years ago by someone who has since left. It has never been tested. It references infrastructure that no longer exists. When an actual DR scenario occurs, the plan is consulted, found to be wrong, and abandoned within the first hour. The team improvises while the clock runs. The plan is updated afterward with what was actually done — but it is still not tested.

**Failure Mode 3: Testing culture without remediation investment**
The organization runs annual DR tests. The tests consistently reveal gaps — actual RTO of 6 hours vs. a target of 2 hours, backup restoration failures, manual steps with no documented owner. The findings are written up. No budget is allocated to close them. The same gaps appear in the next year's test. The testing program becomes risk theater: it demonstrates awareness of the problem while the problem persists.

This framework addresses all three. Chapters 1–2 address Failure Mode 1. Chapters 3–5 address Failure Mode 2. Chapters 6–8 address Failure Mode 3.

## Key Definitions (Used Consistently Across All Files)

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Availability** | The percentage of time a service meets its defined performance and functionality criteria; expressed as annual downtime budget |
| **High Availability (HA)** | Architecture design that eliminates or reduces single points of failure to maintain service continuity through component failures |
| **Disaster Recovery (DR)** | The process of restoring IT services after a catastrophic failure that HA mechanisms cannot absorb; DR activates when HA fails |
| **Business Continuity (BC)** | The broader discipline of maintaining essential business functions during and after any disruptive event — IT, people, facilities, supply chain |
| **Business Continuity Plan (BCP)** | The documented, tested plan for how the organization sustains critical business functions through a disruptive event |
| **RTO** | Recovery Time Objective — the maximum acceptable elapsed time from failure declaration to service restoration |
| **RPO** | Recovery Point Objective — the maximum acceptable data loss expressed as time (how old can the recovered data be?) |
| **MTPD** | Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption — the business limit beyond which the disruption causes irreversible harm; MTPD sets the ceiling for RTO |
| **WRT** | Work Recovery Time — the time required to reprocess transactions, reconcile data, and restore operational state after systems are technically restored; WRT + RTO must not exceed MTPD |
| **RPA** | Recovery Point Actual — the data loss actually achieved during a DR test; compared against RPO to measure gap |
| **Failover** | The process of switching service delivery from a primary system to a secondary (standby) system |
| **Failback** | The process of returning service delivery from the secondary system back to the restored primary after DR |
| **Tabletop Exercise** | A discussion-based exercise where participants walk through a scenario verbally; no systems are actually changed |
| **Game Day** | A chaos engineering exercise where failure is injected into production or production-like systems to test actual recovery behavior |
| **Site Risk** | The aggregate risk of a physical or logical location failing — calculated as probability × impact × MTPD exposure |

## Relationship to Incident Management and Change Management

Availability & Continuity Management does not operate in isolation. The three disciplines form a governance triad:

**[Incident Management]** handles failures in real time. When HA mechanisms fail and a DR scenario begins, the Incident Commander hands off to the DR Activation Lead. The DR Activation Playbook in this framework picks up where the Incident Command Playbook ends.

**[Change Management]** is the primary source of preventable availability risk. Uncontrolled changes are responsible for approximately 70% of production incidents in mature IT organizations (ITIL research, Gartner 2023). The DR plan must include a change freeze protocol that activates during and after DR events.

**[Problem Management]** drives the long-term elimination of recurring availability failures. When DR tests reveal the same gaps year after year, that is a Problem Management failure — the findings are not being converted into permanent solutions.

Where these frameworks are referenced, they appear in brackets: `[Incident Management]`, `[Change Management]`, `[Problem Management]`.

## Why This Domain Is Risk Governance, Not Engineering

A principal SRE thinks about availability in terms of architecture — redundancy, replication lag, failover time, blast radius.

A Director of Operations thinks about availability in terms of risk — what is the probability of a disruption this quarter, what is the financial exposure if it occurs, what is the cost to reduce that exposure by X%, and does the board accept the residual risk?

The engineering conversation is: "We need three nines."
The governance conversation is: "The cost of four nines is $2.4M/year. The expected annual loss from three nines is $800K. We are recommending we accept three nines and invest the $1.6M delta in security instead. Do you accept this risk?"

That second conversation requires this framework. The board does not fund engineering projects. It funds risk reduction. This document teaches you to speak that language.
