# ch05 — Business Continuity Planning

## BCP is Broader Than DR

Disaster Recovery is a subset of Business Continuity. DR addresses the question: how do we restore IT systems after a failure? Business Continuity Planning (BCP) addresses the larger question: how does the organization continue functioning when any essential input — systems, people, facilities, suppliers, communications — is disrupted?

A DR plan that activates successfully but cannot be operated because the people who run it cannot get to the facility, cannot reach each other, and cannot access the tools they need is a failed BCP.

The scope difference:

| Domain | DR Addresses | BCP Addresses |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| IT systems | Yes | Yes (as one component) |
| People | No | Yes — key person risk, succession, cross-training |
| Facilities | No | Yes — alternate work locations, facility failure |
| Communications | Partially | Yes — when normal channels (email, Slack, phone) are the disruption |
| Suppliers | No | Yes — critical vendor failure, supply chain disruption |
| Regulators | Minimally | Yes — required notifications, regulatory interaction during disruption |
| Customers | Minimally | Yes — communication plan, service continuity commitments |

## Business Impact Analysis: The Foundation of BCP

The BIA process described in [ch04] produces the data that drives BCP design. This chapter builds on that output.

From the BIA, you have:
- Critical business functions and their MTDPs
- Financial and regulatory impact at each disruption time interval
- Enabling systems for each function (IT scope)
- Initial priority order

BCP adds the non-IT dependencies to this picture:
- Who performs each critical function (people dependency)
- Where that work is performed (facility dependency)
- What external suppliers enable it (supplier dependency)
- How internal and external parties communicate during disruption (communications dependency)

## Recovery Priority Order: The Sequencing Logic

Not all critical functions recover simultaneously. Resources — people, systems, communications — are constrained during a disruption. Recovery must be sequenced.

### Sequencing Principles

**Principle 1: Life safety first**
Any function related to physical safety of employees or customers is first priority, before any business function.

**Principle 2: Regulatory and compliance functions next**
Functions required to meet regulatory reporting deadlines or maintain legally required records activate before revenue-generating functions. Regulatory penalties for missed filings can exceed operational losses from delayed revenue.

**Principle 3: Revenue-critical functions by MTPD**
Order by how quickly irreversible harm begins. The function with the shortest MTPD activates first.

**Principle 4: Support functions that enable the above**
Identity and access management, monitoring, logging, and communication infrastructure must activate before the business functions that depend on them.

**Principle 5: Standard operations last**
Internal tools, reporting, analytics, and non-critical functions recover only after critical functions are stable.

### Example Recovery Sequence

| Priority | Function | MTPD | Rationale |
|----------|----------|------|-----------|
| 1 | Employee safety notifications | Immediate | Life safety |
| 2 | Regulatory status communications | 1 hour | FCC Part 4 notification window |
| 3 | Enterprise notification delivery | 2 hours | Primary revenue service; SLA breach at 4 hours |
| 4 | Customer authentication | 2 hours | Prerequisite for all customer-facing functions |
| 5 | Billing and invoice processing | 8 hours | Financial impact but not immediate SLA breach |
| 6 | Internal communications (email, Slack) | 24 hours | Disruptive but not business-stopping |
| 7 | Analytics and reporting | 48 hours | Non-critical to operational continuity |

Document this sequence formally. Validate it with business stakeholders — they must understand why their function is where it is and agree with the prioritization.

## People Continuity

### Key Person Risk

Key person risk exists when: operational or technical knowledge resides in one person's head and has not been documented, cross-trained, or structured into runbooks.

Identify key persons by asking: "If this person were unavailable for 30 days starting tomorrow, what would break?"

Categories of key person risk:

**Technical key persons**: Engineers who are the sole operators of a critical system, the sole authors of a critical codebase component, or the sole holders of vendor relationship credentials and escalation contacts.

**Process key persons**: Managers or analysts who are the sole negotiators, sole approvers, or sole contacts for critical business relationships (key customer accounts, regulatory relationships, insurance brokers).

**Organizational key persons**: Leaders whose relationships and institutional knowledge cannot be replaced quickly — typically roles where internal relationships and external reputation are the primary value.

### Key Person Risk Matrix

| Person (Role, Not Name) | Knowledge/Function Held | Backup Documented? | Cross-trained? | Bus Factor Risk |
|------------------------|------------------------|-------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Platform Lead SRE | Production runbooks, vendor escalation contacts | Partially | No | Critical |
| Database Administrator | Schema management, DR restore procedures | No | No | Critical |
| VP of Regulatory Affairs | FCC relationship, reporting procedures | No | No | High |

The "bus factor" (how many people would need to be hit by a bus for this knowledge to disappear) should be 2 or higher for any function above Tier 3.

### Succession Plans

For leadership roles, succession planning addresses:
- Who can perform the role in the first 24 hours of an emergency?
- Who can perform the role for 30+ days?
- What decisions does the role make that require explicit delegation authority?

Succession plans do not require permanent organizational changes. They require pre-authorization: "If [role] is unavailable, [alternate] has authority to [specific decisions] during the period of unavailability."

### Cross-Training Requirements

Cross-training is the operational mitigation for key person risk. For each critical key person:
- Document their critical procedures in runbook format
- Train a second person to execute those procedures
- Validate the training with a supervised exercise before the key person is unavailable
- Rotate on-call coverage to ensure the second person regularly exercises the knowledge

**T-Mobile context**: On a 15-person SRE team managing the notification platform, cross-training requirements should ensure that any critical operational procedure can be performed by at least 2 team members independently. Single-owner runbooks are a BCP risk.

## Facility Continuity

### Facility Failure Scenarios

| Scenario | Duration | Mitigation |
|----------|----------|------------|
| Building access denied (safety incident, law enforcement, hazmat) | Hours to days | Pre-negotiated alternate work location; work-from-home capability |
| Office facility loss (fire, flood, structural failure) | Weeks to months | Permanent alternate site; distributed remote work |
| Data center facility failure | Hours to weeks | Cloud failover; colocation with geographic separation |
| Regional infrastructure failure (power grid, communications) | Hours to days | Distributed workforce; mobile connectivity (T-Mobile context: cellular as backup) |

### Work-From-Home Capability Assessment

A BCP that relies on "everyone works from home" must validate:
- Do all critical role employees have home internet adequate for their function?
- Do all employees have devices capable of running required applications?
- Can critical security controls (VPN, DLP, endpoint management) be enforced on home networks?
- Are there role-specific requirements (physical access to hardware, classified materials, regulated data) that cannot be satisfied remotely?

**The hybrid reality**: Most organizations discovered during 2020 that work-from-home capability was substantially better than their pre-pandemic BCP assumed — and that some functions (requiring physical hardware access, secure rooms, or regulated data access) were genuinely incompatible with remote work.

### Alternate Site Arrangements

**Hot alternate site**: A fully equipped facility that can accommodate the workforce immediately. Staffed with maintained equipment. Highest cost; fastest activation.

**Warm alternate site**: A facility with physical infrastructure but requiring equipment setup or activation. Days to activate.

**Cold alternate site**: A contracted space with physical premises only. Requires equipment delivery and setup. Weeks to activate.

**Reciprocal agreement**: A formal arrangement with another organization to share facilities in mutual emergency situations. Cost-effective but dependent on the partner not needing the space at the same time.

**Cloud-hosted alternate site**: For knowledge worker roles, a combination of cloud-based work tools (Microsoft 365, Salesforce, Jira), VPN access, and cloud-hosted critical applications eliminates the need for a physical alternate site for many functions.

## Supplier Continuity

### Critical Supplier Identification

A critical supplier is any vendor whose failure causes a disruption in a Tier 1 or Tier 2 business function.

Identify critical suppliers by mapping each business function to its external dependencies:
- Cloud providers (AWS, Azure, GCP)
- Network providers (internet transit, MPLS circuits, last-mile)
- SaaS platforms (Salesforce, ServiceNow, PagerDuty, Okta)
- Payment processors (Stripe, Adyen, FIS)
- Data providers (market data, reference data, fraud scoring)
- Professional services with embedded operational roles (managed SOC, offshore operations)

### Supplier Risk Assessment

| Supplier | Function Enabled | Tier | Single Source? | Contractual SLA | Their DR Capability Verified? |
|----------|-----------------|------|----------------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| AWS us-east-1 | All cloud services | 1 | No (can shift to us-west-2) | 99.99% | Yes (published SLA) |
| Primary MPLS carrier | Headquarters connectivity | 2 | Yes | 99.9% | No |
| PagerDuty | Incident alerting | 1 | Yes | 99.99% | No |

The "Single Source?" column identifies supplier SPOFs. A supplier SPOF requires either a backup supplier pre-qualified and ready to activate, or an explicit accepted-risk record stating the organization accepts the exposure.

### Supplier SLA Limitations

A vendor SLA does not prevent outages. It defines compensation after an outage occurs. No financial credit compensates for a mission-critical outage during a DR event when you needed the vendor to be available.

The relevant question about a critical supplier is not "what SLA do they have?" — it is "what is their actual historical availability, and do they have a tested DR capability I can verify?"

Request evidence of supplier DR testing from Tier 1 and Tier 2 critical suppliers. Suppliers that cannot provide this evidence should be treated as higher-risk.

### Pre-Qualified Alternates

For each single-source critical supplier:
- Identify a viable alternate supplier
- Document the activation steps to switch to the alternate
- Negotiate a standby agreement or verify the alternate can be activated without a prior relationship
- Test the activation steps at least annually

## Communication Plan

### The Communication Plan Problem

When normal communication channels (email, Slack, Microsoft Teams, phone systems) fail, how do you coordinate the response?

This is not hypothetical. Email servers go down during data center failures. Slack has had major outages. Phone systems fail during facility evacuations. The communication tools most needed during a crisis are often hosted on the same infrastructure that is failing.

### Communication Hierarchy

Design communication with channel redundancy:

| Priority | Channel | Condition for Use |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Designated crisis conference line (external, not on company infrastructure) | Primary channel when normal comms are unavailable |
| 2 | Personal mobile phones (call, SMS) | When conference system is unavailable |
| 3 | Secondary messaging platform (e.g., personal Signal or WhatsApp group) | Pre-established out-of-band channel |
| 4 | In-person at designated rally point | Last resort; required for facility evacuation scenarios |

### Notification Chains

Define who contacts whom, in what sequence, using what channel:

**Internal notifications**:
- VP of Operations → CTO, CRO, CEO within 30 minutes of DR declaration
- Operations Manager → all on-call team members immediately
- Communications Lead → PR/Communications leadership within 60 minutes if customer impact is anticipated
- Legal/Compliance Lead → Regulatory affairs team within 60 minutes

**External notifications**:
- Customer communications: which customers, what message, what channel (status page, email, phone), who approves content
- Regulatory notifications: FCC Part 4 (T-Mobile), FFIEC (financial services), HIPAA breach reporting — each has specific timelines and required content
- Partner/vendor notifications: critical integration partners who must know about the disruption to manage their side

**Communication templates**: Draft standard messages for each scenario type in advance. During an active DR event, communication quality degrades under stress. Pre-drafted templates prevent both over-disclosure and under-disclosure.

### Regulatory Notification Requirements

| Regulation | Trigger | Timeline | Content Required |
|-----------|---------|----------|-----------------|
| FCC Part 4 (Telecom) | Network outage affecting 900K+ user-minutes, or outage affecting special facilities (911, hospitals) | Initial notification: within 24 hours. Final report: within 30 days | Affected area, estimated users affected, cause, restoration time |
| FFIEC (Banks) | IT failure affecting core banking functions | Board notification: within 1 business day. Regulator: varies by charter, typically 2–5 days | System affected, duration, customer impact, remediation |
| HIPAA (Healthcare) | Breach of PHI, including availability failures that expose PHI | Notice to HHS: within 60 days of discovery | Affected individuals, PHI categories, cause, mitigation |
| SEC (Public Companies) | Material cybersecurity incident | 8-K filing: within 4 business days of determining materiality | Nature of incident, material impact, remediation |

The BCP must include these notification workflows as first-class procedures with named owners, approved message templates, and explicit deadline tracking.

## Regulatory BCP Requirements by Industry

### Telecom: FCC Requirements

FCC rules require network reliability plans and outage reporting. For T-Mobile:
- Network outage data must be retained and reportable
- Disaster recovery capabilities must be documented and periodically reviewed
- Geographic diversity for critical routing infrastructure is expected
- DR plans must account for major disaster scenarios (hurricanes, earthquakes in covered service areas)

### Financial Services: FFIEC SR 11-7 and Business Continuity Requirements

The FFIEC's Business Continuity Planning handbook specifies:
- Annual BCP testing required
- BCP must cover all critical business processes, not just IT
- RTO/RPO targets must be documented and tested
- Third-party service provider continuity must be addressed (vendor BCP review)
- Board-level BCP governance is an examination item

### Healthcare: HIPAA Contingency Planning

HIPAA §164.308(a)(7) requires covered entities to have:
- Data backup plan
- Disaster recovery plan
- Emergency mode operation plan
- Testing and revision procedures
- Application and data criticality analysis (equivalent to BIA)
