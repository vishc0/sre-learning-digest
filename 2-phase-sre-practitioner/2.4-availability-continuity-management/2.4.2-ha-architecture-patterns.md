# ch02 — High Availability Architecture Patterns

## The Director's Job in HA Architecture

A Director of Operations does not design HA architectures. Engineers do that. The Director's job is to:

1. Understand what each pattern costs and what it buys
2. Match the pattern to the service tier and the business's RTO/RPO requirements
3. Identify single points of failure that engineering may have normalized
4. Sign off on the HA design as fit for the business commitment
5. Ask the question every architecture review forgets: "What fails first, and how long does recovery take?"

This chapter gives you the vocabulary, the decision matrix, and the questions to ask.

---

## The Four HA Patterns

### Pattern 1: Active-Active

**How it works**: Two or more fully operational instances run simultaneously and share the load. Any instance can handle any request. If one instance fails, traffic routes to the remaining instances with no failover step required.

**Analogy**: Two TIBCO EMS brokers in a cluster both processing messages. If one dies, the other continues without pause — no failover event, no failover time.

**RTO**: Near-zero (seconds, limited by health check detection time)
**RPO**: Near-zero (data is shared or replicated synchronously)
**Cost multiplier**: 2x–3x (you pay for two fully provisioned production environments)

**When to use**:
- Mission-critical services where any failover time is unacceptable (payment, auth, telecom routing)
- Services with stateless request patterns or where session state is externalized
- When the business has committed to four or five nines

**When NOT to use**:
- Stateful services where write coordination across instances is complex (split-brain risk)
- When the cost of 2x infrastructure is not justified by the business value of the service
- When you haven't solved data consistency — active-active without consistent data is worse than active-passive

**Key risk**: Split-brain. If the network partition between the two active nodes causes each to believe it is the only surviving node, both write independently and data diverges. Solving split-brain is a hard distributed systems problem. Assume it will happen and design for detection and reconciliation.

---

### Pattern 2: Active-Passive (Hot Standby)

**How it works**: A primary instance handles all traffic. A secondary instance runs and is fully provisioned but handles no traffic. When the primary fails, traffic is redirected to the secondary. The secondary becomes primary.

**Analogy**: RabbitMQ classic mirroring — one master queue, one mirrored queue. The mirror does no work until the master fails, then it is promoted.

**RTO**: Minutes (1–5 minutes with automated failover; 15–30 minutes with manual failover)
**RPO**: Seconds to minutes (depends on replication lag between primary and standby)
**Cost multiplier**: 1.5x–2x (standby infrastructure is paid for but idle)

**When to use**:
- Business-critical services where 1–4 hour RTO is acceptable
- When active-active complexity (split-brain, write coordination) is not justified
- Regulated environments where the standby must be verified before accepting traffic

**When NOT to use**:
- When RTO must be sub-minute (the failover detection and traffic redirect takes time)
- When the cost of idle standby infrastructure is not justified for the service tier

**Key risk**: Failover validation. When the standby takes over, is it actually serving traffic correctly? Automated failover can switch to a broken standby. Validation gates in the failover sequence are non-negotiable.

---

### Pattern 3: N+1 Redundancy

**How it works**: Deploy N instances to handle normal load, plus 1 additional instance as reserve capacity. If any single instance fails, the N remaining instances absorb the load. The +1 ensures no capacity deficit during single failures.

**Analogy**: A T-Mobile cell site with three sectors (N=3) and one additional radio unit on standby (+1). If one sector fails, the other three cover coverage without degradation.

**RTO**: Near-zero (health checks remove failed instances from the pool automatically)
**RPO**: Near-zero (stateless workloads) or depends on data replication (stateful)
**Cost multiplier**: 1.25x–1.5x (pays for 1 extra instance above minimum required)

**When to use**:
- Stateless application tiers (web servers, API gateways, microservices)
- When you need cost-efficient protection against single instance failure
- Kubernetes deployments with replica sets — N+1 is the natural Kubernetes HA model

**When NOT to use**:
- When multiple simultaneous failures are plausible (multi-AZ failure, cascading)
- When the +1 instance doesn't have enough capacity to absorb a failed node's load at peak

**Key risk**: +1 is insufficient at peak load. If N instances each run at 70% capacity and one fails, the remaining N-1 must absorb that 70%/N load increase. If you are already at 70% utilization, losing one instance puts remaining at 80%+. Do the math at peak, not average.

---

### Pattern 4: Geographic Redundancy (Multi-Region)

**How it works**: Service is deployed in two or more geographically separated data centers or cloud regions. A failure in one region does not affect the other. Traffic routing (via DNS, CDN, or global load balancer) directs users to the healthy region.

**Analogy**: T-Mobile's network with both west coast and east coast POPs. If a natural disaster takes out the Seattle POP, traffic routes to Chicago without customer impact.

**RTO**: Seconds to minutes (with DNS-based failover; DNS TTL is a material constraint)
**RPO**: Seconds to minutes (depends on cross-region replication mode)
**Cost multiplier**: 2x–4x (full second environment in another region)

**When to use**:
- When a regional failure (natural disaster, major cloud provider failure, power grid event) is a credible risk
- When regulatory requirements mandate geographic separation (FFIEC, DORA, FCC network diversity requirements)
- When latency for users in remote regions is a product requirement (serve from nearest region)

**When NOT to use**:
- When regional failure probability does not justify the cost
- When cross-region data replication creates data consistency problems not yet solved
- When the second region would have higher latency than acceptable for your workload

**Key risk**: Replication lag and data loss at failover. Cross-region replication is almost always asynchronous. When Region A fails and you failover to Region B, the last seconds or minutes of transactions written to Region A but not yet replicated to Region B are lost. RPO is the measure of this loss. Know your replication lag in real time.

## RTO vs. Cost Trade-Off Matrix

| Pattern | RTO | RPO | Cost Multiplier | Min Nines Achievable |
|---------|-----|-----|----------------|----------------------|
| Active-Active | < 30 seconds | Near-zero | 2x–3x | 99.999% |
| Active-Passive (automated) | 1–5 minutes | Seconds | 1.5x–2x | 99.99% |
| N+1 (single region) | < 30 seconds | Near-zero (stateless) | 1.25x–1.5x | 99.9%–99.99% |
| Geographic Redundancy | 1–15 minutes | Seconds–minutes | 2x–4x | 99.99% |
| Backup & Restore (no HA) | Hours | Hours | 1x (baseline) | 99%–99.9% |

> **What the VP must decide**: For each Tier 1 service, which pattern does the business pay for? This decision determines your achievable availability ceiling. You cannot commit to four nines and fund three-nines architecture.

## Single Point of Failure Identification Methodology

An SPOF is any component whose failure causes complete service unavailability, regardless of the sophistication of the surrounding architecture.

HA patterns eliminate SPOFs from specific tiers. But SPOFs can hide outside the components you are protecting.

### The SPOF Audit Process

**Step 1: Draw the full service dependency map**
Document every component the service touches from end-user request to database write and back. Include: DNS, load balancer, API gateway, application servers, caches, databases, message queues, third-party APIs, identity providers, monitoring systems.

**Step 2: For each component, ask**
- What happens to the service if this component fails completely?
- What happens if this component is degraded (slow but not completely down)?
- Is there a secondary path that activates automatically, or does recovery require human action?

**Step 3: Score each SPOF**
- Probability of failure: Low (< once per year) / Medium (1–3 times/year) / High (> 3 times/year)
- Impact of failure: Minor (degraded, not down) / Major (partial outage) / Critical (complete outage)
- Detection time: Auto-detected in < 1 min / Detected in 1–10 min / Detected manually > 10 min
- Recovery time: Automated < 5 min / Semi-automated 5–30 min / Manual > 30 min

**Step 4: Prioritize by risk**
SPOF Risk Score = Probability × Impact × Recovery Time  
Address Critical × High probability × Long recovery first.

### Common Hidden SPOFs

Engineers typically protect application and database layers. SPOFs survive in:

- **DNS**: A single DNS provider with no secondary. DNS failures are catastrophic and historically underinvested.
- **TLS certificates**: Expired certificates cause immediate outages. A single cert management system with no renewal alerting is an SPOF.
- **Shared services**: A single authentication provider (LDAP, OAuth), shared message broker, or shared logging pipeline that multiple services depend on.
- **CI/CD pipeline**: The deployment pipeline itself. If it fails during an incident response requiring an emergency deploy, you are doubly blocked.
- **Monitoring and alerting**: If your PagerDuty account or alerting pipeline fails, you lose visibility into all other failures simultaneously.
- **Vendor APIs**: A third-party payment processor, identity verification service, or mapping API with no fallback path.
- **People**: A single engineer who holds operational knowledge that exists nowhere else — the human SPOF. Cross-reference [ch07 — Dependency Mapping].

## Kubernetes High Availability

Kubernetes is not inherently HA. The default installation creates multiple SPOFs that must be explicitly addressed.

### Multi-AZ Deployment

The fundamental Kubernetes HA requirement: nodes must span multiple availability zones. If all nodes are in us-east-1a and us-east-1a has an outage, your cluster is down.

**Pod anti-affinity rules** ensure replicas of the same workload are not co-located on the same node or AZ:

```yaml
affinity:
  podAntiAffinity:
    requiredDuringSchedulingIgnoredDuringExecution:
    - labelSelector:
        matchExpressions:
        - key: app
          operator: In
          values: [my-service]
      topologyKey: topology.kubernetes.io/zone
```

`requiredDuringScheduling` means the pod will not be scheduled if the constraint cannot be satisfied — it enforces AZ spread, it does not merely prefer it.

### PodDisruptionBudgets

A PodDisruptionBudget (PDB) limits how many pods of a given workload can be simultaneously unavailable during voluntary disruptions (node drains, cluster upgrades):

```yaml
apiVersion: policy/v1
kind: PodDisruptionBudget
spec:
  minAvailable: 2   # At least 2 replicas must be available at all times
  selector:
    matchLabels:
      app: my-service
```

Without PDBs, a node drain during a cluster upgrade can terminate all replicas of a service simultaneously if they happen to be on adjacent nodes.

### Control Plane HA

The Kubernetes control plane (API server, etcd, scheduler, controller manager) must also be HA. In managed Kubernetes (EKS, GKE, AKS), the cloud provider handles control plane HA. In self-managed clusters, etcd must run with a minimum of 3 nodes (quorum requires N/2+1 nodes to be healthy for writes).

**etcd quorum math**: 3 nodes tolerate 1 failure. 5 nodes tolerate 2 failures. 7 nodes tolerate 3 failures. Never run even numbers — split votes are unresolvable.

### Node Redundancy

**Node groups must span AZs**. When using EKS with managed node groups or Karpenter, configure zone awareness explicitly. Karpenter's `topologySpreadConstraints` at the workload level combines with node provisioner zone configuration.

**Cluster autoscaler consideration**: The cluster autoscaler makes cost-efficiency decisions that can conflict with HA. If it removes nodes to save cost and those nodes hold your only remaining replica in a given AZ, you have a latent SPOF. Set minimum node counts per AZ for critical workloads.

## Database High Availability

Database HA introduces data consistency challenges that application HA does not.

### Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Replication

**Synchronous replication**: Every write is acknowledged only after it is confirmed on both primary and replica. RPO = 0 data loss. RTO = seconds (automated failover). Cost: latency on every write (the replica acknowledgment is in the write path). Geographic distance increases latency.

**Asynchronous replication**: The primary acknowledges the write immediately. The replica receives it with a lag (typically milliseconds to seconds, but can spike under load or network issues). RPO = non-zero (up to the replication lag at the time of failure). RTO = seconds. Cost: potential data loss at failover.

**Decision rule**: Use synchronous for financial, compliance, or mission-critical data. Use asynchronous when write latency matters more than zero RPO.

### Quorum Systems

Distributed databases (Cassandra, DynamoDB, MongoDB replica sets, etcd) use quorum for write confirmation. The quorum write requires acknowledgment from a majority of replicas before returning success.

In Cassandra with replication factor 3: a QUORUM write requires acknowledgment from 2 of 3 replicas. If one replica is down, writes still succeed. If two are down, writes fail — the database protects consistency at the cost of availability. This is the CAP theorem in practice: at a partition, you choose consistency or availability.

> **What the board asks**: "Can we lose data in a DR event?" The answer depends entirely on your replication mode. Know it. State it explicitly in board reporting.

### Split-Brain Risk

In active-passive database setups, if the network link between primary and replica breaks:
- The replica cannot confirm the primary is dead (it might just be a network partition)
- Automated failover may promote the replica to primary
- The original primary, now isolated, continues accepting writes
- When connectivity is restored, two primaries have diverged write histories

Split-brain is catastrophic for data integrity. Mitigations:
- Fencing tokens: primary must obtain a lease from a quorum system before writing
- STONITH (Shoot The Other Node In The Head): automated system that powers off the isolated node
- Majority quorum: writes require quorum confirmation — an isolated node without quorum cannot write

## Network High Availability

Network failures are the most common cause of HA failures in cloud environments. Most HA architectures protect application and database layers while leaving the network path as an SPOF.

### BGP Multi-Homing

For physical or co-location infrastructure: BGP multi-homing connects your edge routers to two or more upstream transit providers. If one transit provider fails or has a routing event, traffic routes through the surviving provider automatically. BGP convergence time is typically 30–300 seconds.

**In telecom environments**: T-Mobile's network operations already operate with BGP redundancy at the carrier level. The risk is in the enterprise and cloud-connected segments where single-provider connections exist.

### CDN Failover

CDN providers (Cloudflare, Akamai, Fastly) offer automatic origin failover: if the primary origin returns errors above a threshold, the CDN routes to a secondary origin. CDN failover is invisible to end users — the failover happens at the CDN edge, not at the DNS level, and completes in seconds rather than DNS TTL intervals.

**CDN as availability layer**: A well-configured CDN can continue serving cached content during a complete origin outage, maintaining partial availability (read operations succeed, write operations fail). This is valuable for read-heavy consumer applications.

### DNS-Based Load Balancing and Failover

DNS failover (AWS Route 53, Azure Traffic Manager) routes traffic based on health check results. If a health check fails, the DNS record is updated to point to the secondary endpoint.

**Critical constraint**: DNS TTL determines how long clients continue sending traffic to the failed endpoint. A TTL of 300 seconds means clients may continue hitting a dead endpoint for up to 5 minutes after DNS has updated. During those 5 minutes, your "automated DNS failover" has not actually failed over for those clients.

Design rule: Set DNS TTL to 60 seconds for mission-critical services. Accept the increased DNS query volume as the cost of faster failover.

## The Cost of HA: Infrastructure Cost Multiplier per Pattern

| Service Tier | Target Availability | Recommended Pattern | Infrastructure Cost Multiplier | Annual Engineering Investment |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Tier 1 (Mission-Critical) | 99.99%+ | Active-Active + Geo Redundancy | 3x–5x | High (dedicated SRE team, automated runbooks) |
| Tier 2 (Business-Critical) | 99.9%–99.95% | Active-Passive + N+1 application tier | 1.5x–2.5x | Medium (shared SRE team, runbooks for known failures) |
| Tier 3 (Standard) | 99%–99.9% | N+1 or single AZ with backup | 1x–1.5x | Low (on-call rotation, manual runbooks) |

**Director decision framework**: For each service, the question is not "how much HA can we build?" It is "what is the minimum HA pattern that satisfies our business commitment, and is that investment justified by the downtime cost it prevents?"

A Tier 2 service with $200K/hour downtime exposure and 8 hours/year expected downtime without HA has an expected annual downtime cost of $1.6M. If N+1 + Active-Passive architecture costs $400K/year incremental, the risk-adjusted ROI is 4:1. Fund it.

A Tier 3 service with $5K/hour downtime exposure and 20 hours/year expected downtime has an expected annual cost of $100K. If Active-Passive costs $300K/year incremental, the ROI is negative. Do not fund it.
