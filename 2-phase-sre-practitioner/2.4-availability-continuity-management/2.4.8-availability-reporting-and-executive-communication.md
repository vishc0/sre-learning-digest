# ch08 — Availability Reporting and Executive Communication

## The Reporting Hierarchy

Availability reporting serves three distinct audiences with different needs:

**Operations teams** need real-time dashboards — current availability, active incidents, error budget burn rate, SPOF count. Granularity is seconds to hours. Format is system dashboards and alert channels.

**Executive leadership** needs monthly operational reports — availability vs. target, downtime cost, trend, and the leading indicators that predict next month's performance. Granularity is days. Format is a 1–2 page summary with supporting tables.

**Board and regulators** need quarterly and annual reports — resilience posture, risk acceptance, investment adequacy, regulatory compliance. Granularity is months. Format is a structured board document with risk language and investment decisions.

The VP of Operations writes for all three audiences. The skill is knowing what each audience needs to govern, and giving them exactly that — nothing more.

## Monthly Availability Report

### What Goes In

The monthly availability report is an operational governance document. Its purpose is to enable the VP and their leadership team to identify trends, catch budget erosion early, and decide whether to escalate.

**Section 1: Executive Summary**
Two to three sentences. What was availability this month? Was it within target? Is there a trend that requires attention?

**Section 2: Availability by Service Tier**

| Service | Tier | Target | Actual | Minutes Down | Incidents | Budget Remaining (Annual) |
|---------|------|--------|--------|-------------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Notification Platform | 2 | 99.95% | 99.93% | 31 min | 2 | 4.8 hr of 4.38 hr |
| Auth Service | 1 | 99.99% | 99.99% | 0 min | 0 | 52.6 min of 52.6 min |
| Billing API | 2 | 99.95% | 99.98% | 9 min | 1 | 3.4 hr of 4.38 hr |

Note: A service in the table that has consumed more than its monthly allocation of the annual budget should be flagged. In the example above, the Notification Platform has consumed 31 minutes more than its proportional annual budget — equivalent to exceeding budget for the period.

**Section 3: Incident Contributions to Downtime**

| Date | Service | Duration | Severity | Primary Cause | Action Item Status |
|------|---------|----------|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| [date] | Notification Platform | 22 min | Sev2 | RabbitMQ broker restart; lag spike | PIR complete; fix in sprint |
| [date] | Notification Platform | 9 min | Sev2 | Deployment rollout caused config error | Fix deployed |

**Section 4: Trend Analysis**
Three-month and twelve-month rolling availability. Is the trend improving, stable, or degrading? A degrading trend 3 months before a major commercial event (product launch, peak season) requires immediate action.

**Section 5: Error Budget Status**

| Service | Annual Budget | Used YTD | Remaining | Burn Rate | Projected Year-End |
|---------|--------------|----------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Notification Platform | 4.38 hr | 2.1 hr (48%) | 2.28 hr | 0.42 hr/mo | 4.8 hr (BREACH) |

The projected year-end calculation — (used ÷ months elapsed) × 12 — projects whether the service will breach its annual availability target at the current burn rate. A projected breach triggers executive attention and a change governance review. Cross-reference [Change Management].

**Section 6: Leading Indicators**
Leading indicators predict future availability. Include:
- MTBI (Mean Time Between Incidents) trend — is the system becoming more stable?
- SPOF count trend — is the team eliminating SPOFs or accumulating them?
- DR test status — are tests current? Are findings being closed?
- Dependency complexity score — is the dependency graph growing (adding risk) or shrinking (simplifying)?

## Board-Level Risk Reporting

### What Board Members Ask

Board members are not operators. They are risk governors. Their questions are:

1. "What is our current resilience posture?" (Are we adequately protected against disruption?)
2. "What is our largest single availability risk?" (Where would we be most hurt?)
3. "Are we meeting our regulatory obligations?" (Will we face fines or enforcement?)
4. "Is the current investment level appropriate?" (Are we over- or under-spending on resilience?)
5. "What would it cost to improve?" (What investment would move us from current state to target state?)

Note what board members do not ask: deployment architecture, Kubernetes configurations, replication lag. They ask about risk, obligation, and investment.

### Board Reporting Format

**Quarterly board resilience report structure**:

```
BOARD RESILIENCE SUMMARY — Q[X] FY[YYYY]

OVERALL POSTURE: [Green / Yellow / Red]
[One sentence rationale for the rating]

AVAILABILITY PERFORMANCE
[Table: service, commitment, actual, trend, status]

CRITICAL RISKS (Board Action Required)
[Any risks above the board's stated risk tolerance that require a decision or acknowledgment]

REGULATORY COMPLIANCE
[Status against FCC Part 4, applicable financial regulations, contractual SLA obligations]
[Any regulatory findings or upcoming audit requirements]

INVESTMENT STATUS
[DR program investments approved: $X. Spend to date: $Y. Expected completion: [date].]
[New investment required: [item], [cost], [risk if not funded].]

ACCEPTED RISKS ON RECORD
[List of risks formally accepted by business owners in the period; status of annual review]

DR TESTING STATUS
[Last full DR test: [date]. Result: [pass/fail]. Critical gaps: [N]. Open remediation: [status].]
[Next scheduled test: [date].]
```

### The Resilience Scorecard

The resilience scorecard gives the board a service-by-service risk picture in a single table:

| Service | Tier | HA Pattern | SPOF Count | DR Test Status | BCP Coverage | Overall Risk |
|---------|------|-----------|------------|----------------|--------------|--------------|
| Notification Platform | 2 | N+1 + Warm Standby | 3 SPOFs identified | Last test: 8 months ago; 2 gaps open | Partial | Yellow |
| Auth Service | 1 | Active-Active | 0 SPOFs | Last test: 3 months ago; 0 gaps | Full | Green |
| Billing API | 2 | Active-Passive | 1 SPOF | Last test: 14 months ago | None | Red |

Color coding:
- Green: HA matches tier; 0 SPOFs; DR test < 6 months; 0 critical gaps; BCP coverage complete
- Yellow: 1–2 SPOFs; DR test 6–12 months; 1–2 low-impact gaps; BCP coverage partial
- Red: Any of: 3+ SPOFs unresolved; DR test > 12 months; critical gap unresolved > 90 days; no BCP coverage

### Translating Technical Risk to Board Language

**Engineer's language**: "We have asynchronous database replication with a lag of up to 15 seconds."

**Board language**: "In a primary data center failure scenario, we may lose up to 15 seconds of transaction data. For the Notification Platform, that represents approximately 6,250 messages. Our current data recovery process can reprocess those messages from source in approximately 45 minutes, which is within our 2-hour RTO target."

The translation rule: engineers describe the technical condition; the VP translates it into business consequence and recovery posture.

## Regulatory Reporting

### FCC Part 4 (T-Mobile Context)

FCC Part 4 requires network outage reporting when:
- The outage affects 900,000 or more user-minutes (calculated as duration in minutes × affected users)
- The outage affects a Special Facility (911 call centers, hospitals, emergency services, military installations)
- The outage degrades interconnection with other carriers

**Reporting timeline**:
- Notification of outage: within 24 hours of determining the outage qualifies
- Detailed written report: within 30 days

**Required notification content**:
- Company name and contact information
- Network type (wireline, wireless, cable, etc.)
- Geographic area affected
- Estimated number of users affected
- Date and time outage began
- Estimated date and time of restoration
- Cause of outage (if known)

The DR plan must include FCC reporting as an explicit step with named owner, message template, and timeline tracking. Cross-reference the communication plan in [ch05].

### SLA Regulatory Reporting

For enterprise customer contracts with availability SLAs, the reporting obligation includes:
- Monthly availability report delivered to customer by the agreed date
- SLA credit calculation and payment within the contracted period
- Root cause explanation for any incident causing credit
- Prevention commitment for recurring incidents

The VP of Operations owns the SLA report delivery process. Missed SLA reports (the report itself, not just the SLA) can trigger contract penalties.

### Availability Evidence for Auditors

Auditors (internal, external, regulatory) require evidence that:
1. Availability targets are documented and formally approved
2. Availability is actually measured and reported
3. DR testing is current and findings are tracked to resolution
4. BCP covers the required scope
5. Regulatory notifications were made within required timelines

Evidence package per audit cycle:
- Availability reports for the period (12 months for annual audits)
- DR test reports with actual vs. target metrics and findings
- Action item tracker showing open and closed remediation items
- BCP document with evidence of annual review
- Regulatory notification log with timestamps and content

Retain audit evidence for the longer of: (a) 7 years (general enterprise standard), or (b) the regulation-specific requirement (FCC: varies; HIPAA: 6 years from creation or last effective date).

## Leading Indicators: Seeing Problems Before They Become Incidents

Availability reporting typically focuses on lagging indicators — what happened last month. A mature reporting framework also tracks leading indicators that predict what will happen next month.

### Leading Indicator Set

**MTBI (Mean Time Between Incidents) trend**
If MTBI is decreasing — incidents are becoming more frequent — this predicts future availability problems before they show up in the availability numbers. A decreasing MTBI is an early warning signal.

**SPOF count over time**
Track the number of identified SPOFs by severity. A growing SPOF count means the team is finding but not eliminating SPOFs — the architecture is accumulating risk. A decreasing SPOF count means the remediation program is working.

**Dependency complexity score**
Count the number of direct dependencies per Tier 1 and Tier 2 service. A growing dependency count increases the probability that a dependency failure causes an outage. Architecture that adds dependencies without eliminating others is increasing availability risk.

**Change failure rate** (from [Change Management])
The percentage of production changes that cause an incident or rollback. A rising change failure rate predicts upcoming availability events. Cross-reference [Change Management] metrics.

**DR test coverage**
Percentage of Tier 1 and Tier 2 services with a DR test in the last 12 months. Coverage below 80% for Tier 1 services is a risk indicator. Coverage of 100% is the target.

**Action item age**
Average age of open DR test action items. A rising average age means findings are not being closed. Findings that are not closed are risks that persist.

## Presenting a DR Investment Request

The VP of Operations will periodically need to request budget for DR improvements. This is not a technical proposal — it is a risk reduction investment case.

### Investment Request Format

**Executive summary**: We have identified a gap between our current DR capability and our business commitment. Closing this gap requires an investment of $X. The business risk of not closing this gap is $Y in potential annual loss.

**Risk evidence**: Show the gap in business terms. "Our tested RTO for the Notification Platform is 3.5 hours. Our committed RTO is 2 hours. In the last 18 months, we have had 2 events that would have triggered this gap. Each event carries $400K in SLA credit exposure."

**Investment options**: Present options at different cost points with the residual risk at each:

| Option | Investment | Achieved RTO | Residual Risk |
|--------|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Do nothing | $0 | 3.5 hours (untested) | $800K expected annual SLA exposure |
| Runbook improvements only | $30K (one-time) | 2.5 hours | $300K expected annual SLA exposure |
| Warm standby upgrade | $200K/yr | 45 minutes | $50K expected annual SLA exposure |
| Hot standby | $600K/yr | 15 minutes | < $10K expected annual SLA exposure |

**Recommendation**: State the recommended option and why it is the best risk-adjusted choice at the organization's risk tolerance.

**Cost of inaction**: State explicitly what happens if no investment is made. "At current trajectory, we will breach our Notification Platform SLA commitment in Q3. SLA credit exposure is $400K. Additionally, two customers have indicated that continued SLA performance below target will trigger contract renegotiation."

This format gives the board or budget committee everything they need to make a risk-informed decision.

## When to Declare Force Majeure

Force majeure is a legal concept: events beyond the organization's reasonable control that excuse performance under a contract. In availability management, declaring force majeure affects SLA credit obligations.

### Force Majeure Criteria

An event qualifies as force majeure when:
1. The event was not caused by, and could not have been prevented by, the organization
2. The event falls within the contractually defined force majeure categories (typically: natural disasters, government action, war, acts of God, infrastructure failures of third-party providers outside the organization's control)
3. The organization took reasonable steps to mitigate impact and restore service as quickly as possible

**What force majeure is not**: A planned maintenance event gone wrong. A configuration error. A vendor outage from a vendor the organization chose without adequate risk assessment. A predictable failure that the organization chose not to mitigate.

### Force Majeure Declaration Process

1. **Legal review**: Force majeure declarations have contractual consequences. Legal must be involved before any declaration.
2. **Customer communication**: Notify affected customers of the claimed force majeure event, the impact, and the recovery timeline.
3. **Regulatory notification**: Check whether the triggering event requires a separate regulatory notification (FCC Part 4, FFIEC, etc.).
4. **Documentation**: Document the evidence that the event meets force majeure criteria — for both the customer communication and any future dispute.
5. **SLA credit exclusion**: Formally exclude the downtime from SLA credit calculations as permitted by the contract.

**Board notification**: Any event large enough to trigger force majeure consideration is large enough for board notification.
