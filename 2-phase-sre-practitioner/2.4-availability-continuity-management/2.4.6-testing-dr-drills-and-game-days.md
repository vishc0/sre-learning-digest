# ch06 — Testing: DR Drills and Game Days

## The Fundamental Rule

An untested DR plan is a liability, not an asset. It creates confidence that does not exist, delays the discovery of gaps until a real disaster, and produces a false picture for executives and regulators.

The VP of Operations owns the testing program. Not the engineering team. Not the audit team. The VP owns the schedule, the scope, the evidence standard, and the consequence of gaps found.

---

## The Testing Taxonomy

DR testing exists on a spectrum from lowest fidelity (cheapest, safest, least useful) to highest fidelity (most expensive, most risk, most useful):

### Level 1: Tabletop Exercise

**What it is**: A structured discussion. Participants walk through a DR scenario verbally. No systems are changed. No traffic is moved.

**What it validates**: Logical correctness of the plan. Role clarity. Decision authority. Communication flows. Gaps in the documented plan (missing steps, unclear ownership, undefined criteria).

**What it does not validate**: Whether the technical steps actually work. Whether the automation fires. Whether the actual RTO is achievable.

**Duration**: 2–4 hours
**Frequency**: Quarterly for Tier 1 services; annually for Tier 2
**Cost**: Low — time only
**Regulatory credit**: Partial — auditors accept tabletop evidence as partial BCP testing compliance but require higher-level tests for full credit

**Best use**: New DR plans (before any technical testing). New team members. New scenarios (ransomware, multi-region failure). Governance check before annual DR test.

### Level 2: Simulation (Chaos Injection Without Production Traffic)

**What it is**: Failure is injected into a production-like environment (staging, shadow, or canary environment). The recovery procedure is executed. No real user traffic is affected.

**What it validates**: Automation fires correctly. Detection works. Runbook steps execute as documented. Actual time required for each step is measured.

**What it does not validate**: Behavior under real production load. Behavior when the failure is unexpected (as opposed to the engineered scenario). Integration with real external dependencies.

**Duration**: Half-day to full day
**Frequency**: Quarterly for Tier 1 automated failover; semi-annual for Tier 2
**Cost**: Medium — engineering time + environment cost
**Regulatory credit**: Moderate — demonstrates technical capability in controlled conditions

### Level 3: Partial DR Test (Fail One Component)

**What it is**: A specific DR scenario is triggered in production. One component (a database, a service, an AZ) is deliberately failed. The DR procedure executes. Real user traffic is rerouted.

**What it validates**: Production behavior during failover. Customer impact visibility. Actual RTO with real traffic. Alert and escalation chain. Communication plan activation.

**What it does not validate**: Full-environment recovery. Scenarios requiring complete primary site loss.

**Duration**: Half-day including monitoring and validation
**Frequency**: Semi-annual for Tier 1 mission-critical components
**Cost**: High — production impact risk. Must have abort criteria and rollback plan.
**Regulatory credit**: Substantial — demonstrates real recovery capability

### Level 4: Full DR Test (Full Environment Failover)

**What it is**: The complete primary environment is taken offline (or declared unavailable). All traffic shifts to the DR environment. All DR runbooks execute. The full recovery procedure runs from end to end.

**What it validates**: End-to-end DR capability. Actual RTO against all services simultaneously. Data integrity at actual RPO. Communication plan completeness. All dependencies in the DR environment. WRT (work recovery time).

**What it does not validate**: Scenarios where the DR environment itself fails simultaneously.

**Duration**: Full day to multiple days (including validation, stabilization, and failback)
**Frequency**: Annual minimum for Tier 1; required by most regulated industries
**Cost**: Very high — production downtime risk, engineering time, potential customer impact
**Regulatory credit**: Full — satisfies most regulatory BCP testing requirements

> **What the board asks**: "When was our last full DR test, and what did we find?" If the answer is more than 18 months ago or "we've never done one," that is a board-level risk finding.

## Tabletop Exercise Design

### Scenario Selection

Choose scenarios that:
- Are credible (plausible given your infrastructure and threat landscape)
- Have not been recently experienced (rehearse what you haven't faced)
- Stress-test decision authority (ambiguous trigger criteria force the group to debate)
- Include dependencies across teams (require coordination, not just individual performance)

Recommended scenario rotation:
- Q1: Regional cloud provider failure
- Q2: Data corruption or ransomware
- Q3: Key person unavailability + partial system failure (compound scenario)
- Q4: Regulatory notification failure (the communications and governance scenario)

### Participant Roles

| Role | Responsibility During Exercise |
|------|-------------------------------|
| Facilitator | Presents scenario, injects complications, keeps time, documents decisions |
| VP of Operations | Makes activation decisions, tests authority limits |
| DR Activation Lead | Works through the activation playbook |
| Communications Lead | Drafts customer and regulatory notifications |
| Technical Lead (SRE) | Works through technical recovery steps |
| Business Unit VP | Provides business impact context, makes risk decisions |
| Observer/Documenter | Records decisions made, gaps found, timeline |

Keep exercises to 8 or fewer participants for full decision participation. More than 10 people produces an audience, not an exercise.

### Facilitation Structure

**Phase 1 — Scenario injection (15 minutes)**: Present the scenario. Allow participants to ask clarifying questions about the scenario (not about the plan — that comes later). Establish what facts are known at the moment of discovery.

**Phase 2 — Initial response (30 minutes)**: Walk through the first 30 minutes. Who is notified? Who declares? What is communicated externally? Surface decision authority gaps.

**Phase 3 — Recovery execution (60 minutes)**: Walk through the recovery steps. For each step, challenge: who owns this? what if the primary owner is unavailable? what if this step fails?

**Phase 4 — Complication injection (15 minutes)**: Add a second problem. "During recovery, you discover the backup is 4 hours older than expected. What do you do?" Complications reveal how the plan holds up under real conditions.

**Phase 5 — Debrief (30 minutes)**: What decisions were made correctly? What decisions were unclear? What plan gaps were discovered? What assumptions were proven wrong?

### Findings Documentation

Every finding from a tabletop produces:
- **Finding**: What gap or uncertainty was discovered
- **Owner**: Who is responsible for closing it
- **Action**: What specifically must be done
- **Timeline**: When it must be closed
- **Verification**: How completion is confirmed

Findings without owners and deadlines are not findings — they are observations. Observations do not close gaps.

## Game Day Design and Execution

Game Days go beyond tabletops by injecting real failure into real or production-like systems. The Chaos Engineering discipline (originated at Netflix, now widespread) provides the methodology.

### Game Day Principles

**Start with a steady state hypothesis**: Define what "normal" looks like in measurable terms before injecting failure. If you don't know what normal is, you can't identify what abnormal looks like.

**Minimize blast radius**: Begin with the smallest possible failure that tests the hypothesis. Scale up only when smaller failures are fully understood and controlled.

**Run in production or not at all**: Testing only in staging gives you staging-only confidence. The failure modes in production — load, data volume, external integrations, customer traffic patterns — do not exist in staging.

**Abort criteria are non-negotiable**: Define in advance what conditions cause immediate abort. If a real customer impact exceeds the blast radius boundary, abort the game day and recover. Never extend a game day because it "might teach us something." The learning from an uncontrolled game day is not worth the customer impact.

### Pre-Game Readiness Check

Before injecting any failure:

- [ ] All on-call engineers are aware and briefed
- [ ] Rollback procedure is documented and tested
- [ ] Monitoring dashboard is confirmed working and visible
- [ ] Communication channels are open (war room or bridge established)
- [ ] Customer support team is briefed on potential impact
- [ ] Abort criteria are defined and agreed by all participants
- [ ] Business stakeholder approval obtained (for Tier 1 services)
- [ ] Change advisory board notified (per [Change Management] policy)
- [ ] Time window confirmed (never during peak hours for Tier 1)

### Failure Injection Patterns

| Scenario | Injection Method | What It Tests |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Node failure | `kubectl delete node` or instance termination | Pod rescheduling, replica behavior, autoscaler |
| AZ failure | Remove security group rules from subnet; terminate all instances in AZ | Multi-AZ failover, data replication |
| Network latency | `tc netem delay` on target hosts | Timeout handling, retry logic, circuit breakers |
| CPU saturation | Stress tool on target pods/nodes | Autoscaling, load shedding, HPA behavior |
| Database connection failure | Drop database connections; revoke access temporarily | Connection pooling, retry, circuit breaker |
| DNS failure | Modify DNS records to point to black hole | DNS TTL behavior, failover readiness |
| Certificate expiry | Use near-expired certificate in staging | TLS error handling, renewal alerting |

### Measuring Game Day Success

Success in a Game Day is measured by findings, not by passing. A Game Day where nothing breaks and no gaps are discovered is either evidence of a mature system (rare) or evidence of insufficient failure magnitude (common).

Measure:
- Time from failure injection to automated detection (MTTD)
- Time from detection to automated recovery (if applicable)
- Time from detection to human acknowledgment (MTTA)
- Total time to full service restoration (actual RTO contribution)
- Customer-visible impact (was blast radius controlled?)
- Plan accuracy (did actual behavior match documented expected behavior?)

## DR Drill Cadence

### Annual Full DR Test: The Non-Negotiable

For Tier 1 services and all regulated environments, an annual full DR test is the minimum. Many regulations (FFIEC, FCA) explicitly require it. The test must be documented with evidence.

**Annual DR test planning timeline**:
- **6 months before**: Define scope, scenarios, and success criteria
- **4 months before**: Brief all stakeholders; schedule communication to customers if planned downtime is required
- **2 months before**: Run a tabletop exercise to validate the plan before executing it live
- **1 month before**: Pre-game readiness check; confirm all tooling, contacts, and procedures are current
- **Test day**: Execute per plan; document every step with timestamps
- **1 week after**: Publish DR test report (see template)
- **1 month after**: All critical findings (gaps where actual RTO/RPO exceeded target) must have remediation plans with funding approved

### Quarterly Testing for Mission-Critical Services

For services where a DR gap is catastrophic (payment infrastructure, 911-adjacent services, core network), quarterly testing is necessary — but it does not need to be full DR tests each quarter:

| Quarter | Test Type | Scope |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Q1 | Full DR test | All Tier 1 services |
| Q2 | Partial DR test | Top 3 highest-SPOF-risk scenarios |
| Q3 | Simulation | Automated failover validation |
| Q4 | Tabletop | New scenarios; BCP communications plan |

## What to Measure During a DR Test

| Metric | Measurement Method | Target |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Actual RTO | Timestamp from declaration to validation checklist complete | ≤ RTO target |
| Actual RPO | Data age of first transaction post-recovery vs. last clean transaction pre-failure | ≤ RPO target |
| MTTD (detection time) | Time from failure injection to alert | < 5 minutes for automated alerting |
| Runbook accuracy | Count of runbook steps that required deviation | 0 deviations from documented steps |
| Communication plan execution | Were all required notifications made within SLA? | 100% within timeline |
| Blast radius control | Did the failure propagate beyond the planned scope? | 0 unplanned dependencies affected |
| WRT | Time from technical restoration to full operational state | ≤ WRT target |

## DR Test Report: What Executives and Auditors Need to See

The DR test report is a governance document, not a technical log. It answers:
- What was tested?
- What did we commit to achieving?
- What did we actually achieve?
- What gaps exist?
- What is being done about those gaps?

## The Lessons Learned Pipeline

A DR test finding that does not result in a closed action item is evidence that the testing program is theater.

Build a pipeline:
1. **DR test finding** → documented with severity (Critical / High / Medium / Low)
2. **Finding → action item** → assigned owner, due date, success criteria
3. **Action item → design change** → engineering implements the fix
4. **Design change → re-test** → the specific finding is re-tested in the next exercise
5. **Re-test → closure** → finding is documented as closed with evidence

Critical findings (where actual RTO/RPO exceeded target by more than 50%) require:
- Funding approved within 30 days of test
- Implementation complete before next annual DR test
- Interim compensating control documented if implementation will take longer than 90 days

**The accountability mechanism**: The DR test report is reviewed by the board annually. A board that sees the same critical finding appear in successive annual reports will ask why it was not closed. The VP of Operations is accountable for that answer.
