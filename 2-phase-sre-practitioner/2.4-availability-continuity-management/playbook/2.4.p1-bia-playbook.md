# Business Impact Analysis (BIA) Playbook

## Purpose

The BIA is the analytical foundation of the entire availability and continuity program. It answers the question that engineering alone cannot answer: "What does the business actually need?" This playbook guides the VP of Operations through a structured BIA process that produces defensible RTO/RPO targets with documented business owner approval.

The BIA is run annually and whenever a new Tier 1 or Tier 2 service launches, a major product changes function, or a regulatory requirement changes.

---

## BIA Team

| Role | Responsibility in BIA |
|------|----------------------|
| VP of Operations | Facilitates and owns the process; synthesizes findings |
| Business Unit VPs | Provide function criticality and MTPD input for their domain |
| Finance | Provides revenue impact quantification |
| Legal/Compliance | Identifies regulatory obligations and timelines |
| Technology Lead | Maps business functions to enabling systems |
| Internal Audit | Reviews completeness and validity |

Do not run the BIA with only IT participants. The BIA is a business exercise. Technology's role is to map systems to functions, not to define function criticality.

---

## Phase 1: Preparation (2 Weeks Before Interviews)

### Step 1: Define the BIA scope

Which business units are in scope? Which geographic locations? Which service tiers?

For an initial BIA, scope all Tier 1 and Tier 2 services plus any service with regulatory obligations. Standard (Tier 3) services can be assessed with a lighter-touch self-assessment survey.

### Step 2: Send pre-interview questionnaire to business stakeholders

Send to each business unit VP 2 weeks before their interview:

```
BIA Pre-Interview Questionnaire

1. List the 5–10 most important activities your team performs 
   (not tools — activities that produce business outcomes).

2. For each activity, estimate: what is the financial impact of a 4-hour interruption?
   A 24-hour interruption? A 1-week interruption?

3. For each activity, estimate: is there a regulatory, contractual, or SLA obligation 
   that would be breached if this activity were interrupted for more than [X] hours?

4. Which of these activities cannot be performed manually if the supporting system is unavailable?

5. Are there specific dates/periods where interruption would be significantly worse 
   than average? (quarter-end, peak season, regulatory filing dates)
```

Questionnaire responses inform the interview. They prevent the interview from being the first time the business stakeholder has thought about these questions.

### Step 3: Prepare the impact quantification model

Build a financial model before the interviews. Include:
- Revenue per hour by business unit (from finance data)
- SLA credit rates for enterprise customer contracts
- Regulatory fine schedules for applicable regulations
- Industry benchmark downtime costs for reference

The model allows the VP to translate business stakeholder responses ("this is very important") into financial quantification ("at $X revenue per hour, a 4-hour disruption costs $Y").

## Phase 2: Business Unit Interviews

### Interview Structure (90 minutes per business unit)

**Block 1 — Function identification (20 minutes)**

Walk through the pre-interview questionnaire responses. For each identified function:
- Is this still accurate?
- Is there a function we missed?
- Of these functions, which three would you be most concerned about losing for 24 hours?

Record functions in a structured format, not free-form notes.

**Block 2 — Impact quantification (30 minutes)**

For each critical function, quantify impact over time:

| Function | 1-hr impact | 4-hr impact | 8-hr impact | 24-hr impact | 72-hr impact |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
| [Function] | [$ + regulatory] | | | | |

For each time interval, ask: "At what point does this become irreversible? At what point do we lose customers, face regulatory action, or cannot recover fully?"

The point of irreversibility is the MTPD.

**Block 3 — Dependency and workaround identification (20 minutes)**

For each critical function:
- "If the supporting system were unavailable for 2 hours, what manual workaround would your team use?"
- "How long could you sustain the manual workaround?"
- "At what point does the manual workaround fail or become impractical?"

The duration of the manual workaround extends the effective MTPD — if a team can manually process transactions for 4 hours before the workaround breaks down, the MTPD is effectively 4 hours, not zero.

**Block 4 — Priority ranking (10 minutes)**

"If your team could only recover three of these functions in the first 4 hours of a DR event, which three would you choose?"

Priority ranking from business stakeholders directly feeds the recovery sequence in [ch05].

**Block 5 — Confirmation (10 minutes)**

Read back the top 3 critical functions, their MTPD estimates, and the business rationale. Confirm accuracy. Ask the business stakeholder to explicitly state: "I agree that [function] has an MTPD of [X hours] based on [reason]." Record this confirmation — it becomes the documented business owner approval.

## Phase 3: Technology Mapping

After completing all business unit interviews, the technology team maps each identified critical function to its enabling systems.

**Mapping format**:

| Business Function | Business Unit | MTPD | Enabling Systems | System Tier (Current) | Recommended Tier |
|------------------|--------------|------|-----------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Enterprise notification delivery | Platform Operations | 4 hours | Notification API, RabbitMQ, MySQL, Redis | Tier 2 | Tier 2 (confirmed) |
| Customer authentication | All business units | 2 hours | Okta, LDAP, Session Store | Tier 1 | Tier 1 (confirmed) |
| Regulatory reporting (FCC) | Regulatory Affairs | 20 hours | Reporting DB, FCC submission tool | Tier 3 | Tier 2 (reclassify — regulatory) |

Where the current system tier does not match the business-derived MTPD, flag for reclassification. The reclassification requires a VP of Operations decision and may require investment in HA or DR improvement for the affected system.

---

## Phase 4: RTO/RPO Derivation

From the BIA data, derive RTO and RPO targets using the formula from [ch04]:

**RTO derivation**:
- MTPD (from business interview): the ceiling
- WRT estimate (from technology team): how long to restore operational state after technical recovery
- Maximum RTO = MTPD − WRT

**RPO derivation**:
- "How much data could your team manually re-enter or recover if needed?" → sets maximum manual recovery window
- "What is the business cost of losing [X hours] of transactions?" → validates RPO acceptability

**Document the derivation**, not just the result:

```
Service: Notification Platform
MTPD (business-derived): 4 hours
  Source: Enterprise SLA breach at 4 hours (contractual); confirmed by VP of Products, [date]
WRT estimate: 2 hours
  Basis: Failed message reprocessing (600K messages at 2 min per 1000 = ~20 min automation 
         + 90 min validation and duplicate management + 30 min stakeholder confirmation)
Maximum RTO: 4 - 2 = 2 hours
RPO target: 30 minutes
  Basis: Business can re-queue last 30 minutes of messages from source systems if needed;
         beyond 30 minutes, source systems no longer retain the data
```

---

## Phase 5: Gap Analysis and Investment Identification

Compare derived RTO/RPO targets against current tested recovery capability.

For each service with a gap:
- Quantify the risk: probability of a DR event × financial exposure per event
- Identify investment options with costs and achievable RTO/RPO per option
- Make a recommendation

Package the gap analysis as an investment brief for VP review.

---

## Phase 6: Board Presentation

The BIA results that require board attention:
1. Any service where business-derived MTPD is shorter than current recovery capability (gap exists)
2. Any service reclassification that changes investment requirements
3. New regulatory requirements identified during the BIA
4. Residual risks accepted during the prior BIA period — confirmation or withdrawal of acceptance

**Board presentation format (see [ch08])**:
- 1 executive summary slide: overall BIA findings
- 1 risk table: services with gaps, financial exposure, investment required
- 1 investment table: options and costs at each tier
- Decision request: approve investment / accept risk / direct further analysis

The board does not need the full BIA detail. They need to see: what risk exists, what it costs to reduce it, and whether the proposed investment is appropriate.

---

## BIA Document Retention and Review

The BIA is an audit artifact. Retain all interview notes, questionnaire responses, and derived targets for 7 years (or per your regulatory requirement, whichever is longer).

Annual BIA review: update only what has changed. A full re-run is not required if:
- No new Tier 1 or Tier 2 services were launched
- No regulatory requirements changed
- No significant revenue or business model changes occurred

In that case, distribute the prior BIA to business stakeholders and ask: "Has anything changed that would affect these targets?" Document the review and the responses.
