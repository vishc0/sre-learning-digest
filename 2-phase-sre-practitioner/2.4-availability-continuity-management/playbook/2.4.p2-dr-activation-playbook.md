# DR Activation Playbook

## Purpose

This playbook governs the transition from incident response to disaster recovery mode. It defines who declares, when they declare, what steps execute in what order, how validation gates work, and what constitutes a successful DR completion.

**This playbook begins where [Incident Management] ends.** When an incident is determined to be unrecoverable through standard means, the Incident Commander transfers to DR Activation.

---

## Declaration Criteria

DR is declared when ANY of the following conditions are met:

| Condition | Threshold | Verification |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Primary site unavailable | Complete loss of access or connectivity for > 15 minutes | Confirmed by infrastructure team and network operations |
| Service unreachable from multiple locations | Failures from ≥ 2 independent network paths | Verified via external monitoring probe and customer confirmation |
| Data corruption detected | Unrecoverable data corruption or loss confirmed | DBA or data team confirmation; restoration path identified as infeasible |
| Ransomware encryption detected | Any confirmed encryption of production systems or backup storage | Security team confirmation |
| Regional cloud failure declared by provider | Provider has declared a regional impairment for the production region | Verified from provider status page and AWS/Azure support confirmation |
| Estimated recovery time exceeds RTO × 2 | Incident in progress with current MTTR estimate > 2× the service RTO | IC estimates and engineering lead confirmation |

**Who can declare DR**: VP of Operations, Director of Operations, or designated on-call Operations Lead (when VP/Director are unavailable). The declaration authority must be pre-designated and listed in the contact chain — "whoever is available" is not acceptable.

---

## Phase 0: Pre-Declaration (0–5 Minutes)

**Trigger**: Incident Commander signals that the incident may be a DR scenario.

### Actions

1. **Operations Lead joins the incident bridge** (if not already present)
2. **Assess against declaration criteria** — use the table above; do not apply judgment to thresholds
3. **Verify infrastructure status**: Is primary site completely unavailable or is this a partial failure?
4. **Confirm DR environment status**: Is the standby environment healthy and reachable? (This check prevents failing over to a broken standby.)
5. **Check DR readiness gate**:
   - [ ] DR runbooks are accessible from current location
   - [ ] Contact list is accessible from current location
   - [ ] DR environment access credentials are confirmed working
   - [ ] DNS/traffic management credentials are confirmed working

If any DR readiness gate item fails, resolve it before declaring. A declaration without the tools to execute is premature.

---

## Phase 1: Declaration and Notification (Minutes 0–15)

**Clock starts at the moment of declaration.**

### Actions — Minute 0: Formal Declaration

1. Operations Lead states explicitly: **"DR is declared for [service name] at [time]. Declaration authority: [name]."**
2. Scribe records declaration timestamp, declarant name, and trigger condition
3. DR Activation Lead is assigned (may be the Operations Lead or a designated senior engineer)
4. Incident Commander in the existing incident bridge is notified of DR transition — they maintain incident communications while DR activation begins on a separate bridge

### Minute 1–5: Notification Chain

**Tier 1 services** — notify immediately:
- [ ] CTO or VP of Engineering (text message; do not wait for email)
- [ ] CEO or designated executive on-call (text message)
- [ ] Chief Communications Officer or Head of PR (if customer-visible impact is anticipated)
- [ ] Legal counsel (for regulated services or force majeure assessment)
- [ ] Customer Success or Account Management leads (for enterprise customer notification)

**Tier 2 services** — notify within 15 minutes:
- [ ] Director of Operations
- [ ] VP of the affected business unit
- [ ] Account management if affected enterprise customers have SLA implications

### Minute 5–15: External Notifications

**Customer status page update** (publish within 15 minutes of declaration):
```
[Time] — We are investigating an issue affecting [service]. 
Impact: [what customers are experiencing].
We have activated our disaster recovery procedures. 
Next update in [30 minutes].
```

**FCC Part 4 assessment** (T-Mobile): Does this event meet the 900,000 user-minute threshold or affect a Special Facility? If yes, FCC notification must be filed within 24 hours. Flag for regulatory team immediately.

## Phase 2: Failover Execution (Minutes 15–RTO Target)

Failover steps are service-specific and must be documented in the service DR runbook. The generic sequence below applies to most cloud-hosted services with warm or hot standby DR strategy.

### Pre-Failover Validation Gate

Before redirecting any traffic, confirm:
- [ ] Standby environment health check: all services responding
- [ ] Standby data currency: confirm replication state — what is the data lag?
- [ ] Standby capacity: is the standby sized to handle production traffic?
- [ ] Configuration parity: confirm standby configuration matches production configuration
- [ ] DNS TTL: confirm current DNS TTL (if TTL is high, clients may not respect redirect for several minutes)

**If standby validation fails**: Do not proceed. Document what failed. Escalate to VP of Operations — the situation is worse than a standard DR event and may require incident escalation rather than DR activation.

### Failover Sequence (Generic)

**Step 1: Database promotion** (for warm/hot standby)
- Promote replica to primary
- Confirm replica is accepting writes
- Record timestamp and data lag at promotion time (this determines your actual RPO)

**Step 2: Application activation**
- Scale up standby application tier to production capacity
- Confirm all application instances are healthy (health check endpoints returning 200)
- Confirm application can connect to the promoted database

**Step 3: Traffic redirect**
- Update DNS records to point to standby load balancer
- Update CDN origin if CDN is in use
- Update Route 53 health check targets
- Confirm DNS propagation via external probe from multiple geographic locations

**Step 4: Integration reconnection**
- Reconnect message queue consumers to standby queue infrastructure
- Confirm downstream service integrations are routing to standby endpoints
- Confirm monitoring agents are pointing to standby services

**Step 5: Validation — before declaring DR active**
- [ ] End-to-end synthetic transaction from external probe (not from inside the network): confirm a real transaction completes successfully
- [ ] SLA-critical functions confirmed operational (list in service DR runbook)
- [ ] Error rates at baseline
- [ ] Latency within acceptable range (within 150% of normal baseline)
- [ ] No new alerts firing that indicate incomplete recovery

## Phase 3: Customer Communication (Ongoing, Parallel to Failover)

Communications Lead manages all external communication. This is a dedicated role during DR activation — not a task shared with the technical recovery lead.

### Communication Timeline

| T+0 | Status page: investigating (see template above) |
| T+15 min | Status page update: DR activated, recovery in progress |
| T+30 min | Customer email to enterprise accounts if Tier 1 service; include impact scope and expected resolution |
| T+RTO | Status page: resolved; post-incident update |
| T+24 hr | Full incident report to affected enterprise customers; SLA credit statement if applicable |

### Communication Principles

- State what you know; state what you don't know; state when you will know more
- Never state a recovery time you are not confident in — a missed recovery time commitment compounds trust damage
- Regulatory notifications (FCC, FFIEC) follow their own templates and must not be simplified; use the pre-drafted regulatory templates

---

## Phase 4: DR Validation and Declaration of Recovery

### Validation Gate — Recovery Complete

Recovery is not complete until ALL of the following are confirmed:

- [ ] Synthetic transaction test: end-to-end transaction completes successfully from external probe
- [ ] Error rate: production error rate is at or below pre-incident baseline
- [ ] Latency: P99 latency is within 150% of pre-incident baseline
- [ ] All critical integrations confirmed: downstream services confirmed receiving data
- [ ] All monitoring restored: all critical dashboards and alerts operational
- [ ] Data integrity spot check: confirm data written before the event is accessible and correct
- [ ] FCC/regulatory notification confirmed if required

**Declaration of recovery**: "DR recovery declared complete for [service] at [timestamp]. Actual RTO: [elapsed time]. Actual RPO: [data lag at promotion time]. DR Activation Lead: [name]."

Record the declaration. Notify all parties in the notification chain that recovery is complete.

---

## Phase 5: Stabilization (Post-Recovery, First 2 Hours)

Do not declare victory and stand down. The first 2 hours after recovery are high-risk — integration points may fail as traffic patterns normalize, the standby environment may have latent configuration issues under load.

- Monitor error rates and latency every 5 minutes for the first hour
- Have the rollback plan available if issues emerge
- Do not execute non-emergency changes during stabilization
- Keep the war room bridge open with skeleton crew for the full stabilization period

---

## Phase 6: DR Test Report and Handoff

Immediately after stabilization, the DR Activation Lead documents:
- Exact declaration time and declarant
- Each failover step with actual timestamps
- Actual RTO: (recovery declaration time) − (DR declaration time)
- Actual RPO: data lag at database promotion, converted to time
- Any steps that deviated from the runbook, and why
- Any validation gate failures during the process
- Items to address before failback

This becomes the evidence record for the DR event, analogous to a post-incident review for an incident. File within 24 hours of recovery.

---

## Abort Criteria

Abort the DR failover process and return to incident mode if:
- Standby environment fails validation (is not healthy or is also experiencing failures)
- Data corruption is discovered in the standby environment
- Failover is causing cascading failures in services that were previously healthy
- Leadership directs abort pending additional information

To abort: stop failover sequence at current step. Do not partially commit traffic. Document the abort reason, time, and state. Return to incident management bridge with full status.
