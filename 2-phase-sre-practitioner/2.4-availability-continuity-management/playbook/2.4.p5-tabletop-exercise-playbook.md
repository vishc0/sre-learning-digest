# Tabletop Exercise Playbook

## Purpose

This playbook guides the design and facilitation of tabletop exercises for DR and BCP validation. A tabletop is a structured discussion-based exercise — no systems are changed, no traffic is moved. Its value is in surfacing plan gaps, decision authority ambiguities, and communication flow failures before a real event exposes them.

---

## When to Use This Playbook

- Quarterly tabletop for Tier 1 services (required)
- Annual tabletop for Tier 2 services
- Whenever a new DR scenario is added to the plan
- After a real DR event (as part of lessons learned)
- When a new team member joins who owns a DR role
- As the preparation exercise 4–8 weeks before an annual full DR test

---

## Exercise Design: 4 Weeks Before the Exercise

### Step 1: Define Objectives

What does this exercise need to validate? Be specific.

Examples:
- "Validate that all participants know their DR activation roles"
- "Test the communications plan — specifically the sequence and content of customer notifications"
- "Surface gaps in the failback procedure after the new database migration"
- "Validate the vendor escalation procedure for AWS outage scenario"

Write 2–3 specific objectives. Exercises with vague objectives ("make sure the team knows the DR plan") produce vague findings.

### Step 2: Select the Scenario

Choose a scenario that:
- Is credible for your environment (not exotic)
- Has not been recently experienced (testing known scenarios produces overconfidence)
- Stresses the objective you identified
- Forces cross-team coordination (siloed exercises produce limited findings)

**Scenario library** (rotate annually):

| Scenario | What It Stresses |
|----------|-----------------|
| Primary cloud region fails (all services in us-east-1 unavailable) | Full DR activation, geographic redundancy |
| Database corruption discovered at 2am | Data recovery, RPO reality, off-hours decision authority |
| Ransomware detected across production systems | Isolation decisions, backup integrity, regulatory notification |
| Senior SRE lead and DR lead both unavailable simultaneously | People continuity, succession, bus factor |
| Critical vendor (authentication provider) experiences 6-hour outage | Vendor SPOF, dependency, customer communication |
| Multiple systems fail during scheduled maintenance window | Change management integration, risk governance |
| Regulatory notification deadline at risk | Communications plan, regulatory escalation |

### Step 3: Build the Scenario Narrative

Write a 1-page scenario document. Include:
- The initial observable condition (what is seen first)
- Time pressure (when does the situation escalate?)
- Information deliberately withheld at the start (reveal it as injects)
- What a "good response" looks like without stating it explicitly
- 2–3 complication injects to use if the exercise is moving too smoothly

### Step 4: Assign Roles

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Facilitator | Reads the scenario, manages time, injects complications, stays neutral |
| Documenter | Records decisions, action items, and gaps in real time |
| VP of Operations | Makes activation decisions; tests authority limits |
| DR Activation Lead | Works through the technical activation sequence |
| Communications Lead | Drafts notifications; tests communication chain |
| Business Stakeholder | Provides business context; tests BIA-derived priorities |
| Observer(s) | Watch and provide feedback in debrief; do not participate in scenario decisions |

Maximum: 8 active participants. Above 8, add observers rather than active participants.

## Exercise Facilitation: Day Of

### Pre-Exercise Setup (30 Minutes Before)

- [ ] All participants in the room or on video conference
- [ ] Documenter has note-taking template ready
- [ ] DR plan documents accessible to all participants
- [ ] Timer visible to all
- [ ] Facilitator has complication injects prepared but not visible to participants

### Opening (10 Minutes)

1. State the exercise objectives explicitly
2. Establish ground rules:
   - "Discuss as if this is real. Do not solve it by saying 'we would just call AWS.'"
   - "There are no right answers here. We are looking for gaps."
   - "You can reference any document. We want to see how the plan actually works."
   - "The facilitator will inject additional information. Treat it as real."
3. State what will not be tested today (scope limits)
4. Confirm documentation consent (notes will be used for improvement actions)

### Phase 1 — Scenario Injection (10 Minutes)

Read the initial scenario condition. Allow participants to ask clarifying questions about the scenario facts (not about the DR plan). Provide only the facts established in the scenario narrative — withhold information designated for later injects.

Prompt questions to get the discussion started:
- "What is your first action?"
- "Who makes the declaration decision, and what do they need to see first?"
- "What do you tell customers right now?"

### Phase 2 — Initial Response (25 Minutes)

Let the group work. Observe and document. Do not guide. When the discussion stalls, use a prompt:
- "You've now been in this situation for 15 minutes. What has been communicated externally?"
- "The on-call engineer cannot be reached. What happens now?"
- "Your status page update from 10 minutes ago said 'investigating.' It's been 25 minutes. What's next?"

Record every decision with the rationale provided.

### Phase 3 — Recovery Execution (20 Minutes)

Walk the group through the recovery runbook steps verbally. For each step:
- Who executes it?
- What is the expected output?
- What if that output is not achieved?
- What is the timeline to complete this step?

Surface and record every place where the answer is "I don't know" or "it depends" — those are findings.

### Phase 4 — Complication Injection (15 Minutes)

Inject the pre-prepared complication. Examples:
- "Your database replica is 4 hours behind, not 15 minutes. How does this change your approach?"
- "The communications lead has lost internet connectivity. Who sends the customer notification?"
- "AWS is reporting the secondary region also has degraded service. What do you do?"

Complications reveal how the plan holds up when assumptions fail.

### Phase 5 — Debrief (30 Minutes)

The debrief is the highest-value part of the exercise. Structure it:

**What went well?** (5 minutes)
- "Where did the team demonstrate clear decision authority?"
- "What parts of the plan worked as designed?"

**What was unclear?** (10 minutes)
- "Where was decision authority ambiguous?"
- "Where did the plan not have a clear answer?"

**What was missing?** (10 minutes)
- "What information was needed that the plan didn't provide?"
- "What contacts or credentials were unavailable?"

**Action items** (5 minutes)
- Documenter reads back all identified findings
- Each finding gets an owner and a proposed timeline

---

## Findings Documentation Format

For each finding:

```
Finding ID: [YYYY-MM-DD]-[##]
Exercise: [scenario name]
Type: [Gap / Ambiguity / Missing Documentation / Training Need / Process Failure]
Description: [What the exercise revealed was missing or unclear]
Risk if not addressed: [What could go wrong in a real DR event]
Owner: [Name and role]
Action required: [Specific, actionable remediation]
Due date: [within 30 days for Critical; within 90 days for High; next exercise for Medium/Low]
Verification: [How completion will be confirmed]
Status: [Open / In Progress / Closed]
```

Critical findings (those where a real DR event would result in MTPD breach or regulatory violation) require VP of Operations sign-off and tracking to closure.

---

## Exercise Report (Publish Within 5 Business Days)

The exercise report goes to:
- VP of Operations
- All exercise participants
- Operations leadership team
- Compliance/audit if required by regulatory obligation

Report sections:
1. Exercise objectives and scenario summary
2. What was tested and what was not
3. Key decisions made (and whether they were correct per plan)
4. Findings table (all findings with owner, timeline, status)
5. Plan updates required as a result
6. Recommended scenario for next exercise

---

## Quality Standards for Tabletop Exercises

A tabletop exercise that produces zero findings is not a successful exercise — it is a sign that the scenario was too easy or the group was too comfortable. A mature, tested DR plan should produce 2–5 findings per exercise. A new DR plan should produce 10–20.

Measure exercise quality by:
- Number of findings vs. expected range for plan maturity
- Decision authority: were all decisions made by the correct role?
- Finding close rate: what percentage of prior exercise findings are closed?
- Participant engagement: did all participants contribute decisions (not just observers)?
