# Failback Playbook

## Purpose

Failback is the controlled process of returning service delivery from the DR environment back to the restored primary environment after a DR event. It is the final phase of disaster recovery and is frequently under-planned.

Failback carries unique risks: the primary environment has been offline during the DR period, it may have missed configuration changes made to the DR environment during the disaster, and the data state of the two environments has diverged.

A rushed or poorly planned failback can cause a second outage worse than the original.

---

## Failback Is Not the Inverse of Failover

The failover was an emergency response under time pressure. The failback is a planned, validated operation executed when the primary is confirmed restored and stable.

Key differences:
- **Failover**: Activated immediately under pressure. Some validation may be abbreviated.
- **Failback**: Planned with a defined maintenance window. Full validation is required. No shortcuts.
- **Failover**: Minimizes time to customer restoration.
- **Failback**: Minimizes risk of a second disruption.

The failback timeline is not driven by urgency (the DR environment is serving customers successfully). It is driven by readiness. Do not failback before the primary is ready.

---

## Phase 1: Primary Environment Restoration Validation

Before beginning failback planning, the primary environment must pass all of the following gates:

### Infrastructure Validation

- [ ] Root cause of the original DR event has been identified and confirmed resolved
- [ ] Primary environment infrastructure is fully provisioned and healthy
- [ ] All hardware/software that failed has been replaced or repaired
- [ ] Network connectivity to the primary site is confirmed from multiple paths
- [ ] Monitoring agents on the primary are active and reporting to the monitoring platform

### Application Validation

- [ ] All application services in the primary environment are running and passing health checks
- [ ] Application configuration in the primary environment is current — matches the DR environment's configuration at the time of failback (accounting for any configuration changes made during the DR period)
- [ ] All secrets, certificates, and credentials are valid and will not expire within 30 days
- [ ] Container images in the primary environment's registry are current

### Data Validation (Most Critical)

This is the highest-risk step in the failback process.

- [ ] Data in the primary environment matches the data in the DR environment at a confirmed point in time
- [ ] If the primary environment's data predates the DR period (as it typically does), a data synchronization plan is documented and approved
- [ ] Data integrity checks have been run and passed
- [ ] Any data written to the DR environment during the DR period is accounted for in the sync plan
- [ ] The data sync plan has been reviewed by the DBA and the business owner (not just engineering)

**The data gap**: When the primary failed and DR was activated, data was continuing to be written to the DR environment. When the primary is restored, it has the pre-failure data state. Failback requires synchronizing the data written during the DR period back to the primary before it becomes the writer again.

Failure to reconcile this data gap means: the primary serves stale data after failback, and any data written to the DR environment during the gap is effectively lost.

## Phase 2: Failback Planning

### Define the Failback Maintenance Window

Failback requires a maintenance window. This means:
- Scheduled customer communication in advance (not the same as an incident notification)
- Planned brief service interruption during the traffic cutover
- Business stakeholder approval for the timing

Recommended windows: overnight or weekend. Avoid business peak hours, quarter-end, or high-volume event periods.

Minimum window duration: 2× the expected cutover time, to allow for issues during execution.

### Define the Data Synchronization Strategy

Options for data sync during failback:

| Strategy | How It Works | Risk | Duration |
|----------|-------------|------|---------|
| Replicate DR data to primary, then cut over | Use replication to bring primary current, then switch writers | Low — primary is current before cutover | Hours to days depending on data volume |
| Stop writes, sync, restart | Pause write traffic briefly, sync final delta, redirect | Very low — no possibility of split-write | Brief write pause (minutes) |
| Accept delta loss (exceptional, requires business approval) | Accept that data written during DR period cannot be reconciled | High — data loss | Immediate, but requires explicit approval |

For most scenarios, the "Replicate DR to primary, then cut over" approach is safest. The primary runs in read-only or standby mode, receiving replication from the DR environment, until it is current. Cutover happens only when replication lag is zero.

### Failback Runbook

Write the failback runbook during Phase 1 (while the primary is being restored) — not during the cutover window. The runbook must include:
- Each step in sequence
- Expected output for each step
- Who executes each step
- Rollback instruction for each step (if the step fails, how to reverse it)
- Time estimate per step

---

## Phase 3: Pre-Failback Checklist (Night Before)

- [ ] Failback runbook reviewed and confirmed current by DR Activation Lead
- [ ] All engineers who will execute the failback have confirmed availability
- [ ] Primary environment re-validated (all Phase 1 checks re-run within 24 hours of failback)
- [ ] Data sync confirmed current (replication lag < 1 minute)
- [ ] Customer communication sent: scheduled maintenance, expected duration
- [ ] Support team briefed and prepared for potential contact volume increase
- [ ] Rollback plan confirmed: if failback causes issues, can we quickly return to DR environment?
- [ ] Monitoring dashboards for both environments are accessible
- [ ] War room bridge established for the failback window
- [ ] Abort authority identified and confirmed available

---

## Phase 4: Failback Execution

### Step 1: Final Data Sync

Pause new writes to the DR environment (or place it in read-only mode). Allow replication to primary to catch up to zero lag. Confirm:
- [ ] Replication lag = 0
- [ ] No in-flight transactions pending
- [ ] Data checksums match between DR and primary (for critical tables)

### Step 2: Application Drain

Gracefully drain in-flight requests from the DR environment:
- Stop accepting new connections to the DR load balancer
- Allow in-flight requests to complete (configurable drain timeout — typically 30–60 seconds)
- Confirm queue depth is zero before proceeding

### Step 3: Traffic Redirect to Primary

- Update DNS records to point to primary load balancer (confirm TTL is set low for rapid propagation)
- Update CDN origin if applicable
- Update Route 53 health checks
- Confirm DNS propagation via external probes from multiple locations

### Step 4: Primary Validation

Before declaring failback complete:
- [ ] Synthetic transaction from external probe: confirms end-to-end transaction completes on primary
- [ ] Error rate on primary: within baseline
- [ ] Latency on primary: within baseline
- [ ] No alerts firing from primary environment
- [ ] Write traffic confirmed on primary (check database write metrics)
- [ ] DR environment confirmed in read-only or standby mode (no dual-write risk)

### Step 5: Monitoring Period

Maintain active monitoring for 2 hours post-failback:
- Keep war room bridge open with skeleton crew
- Check error rates and latency every 5 minutes for the first hour
- Confirm downstream integrations are all routing to primary

If error rates exceed 2× baseline at any point during the 2-hour monitoring window → execute rollback to DR environment.

---

## Phase 5: Stakeholder Notification and Closure

### Internal Notifications

- VP of Operations → executive team: "Failback complete at [timestamp]. Service fully restored to primary."
- Operations Lead → customer success and support: "Primary environment restored. Monitor for any customer-reported issues."
- DR Activation Lead → monitoring team: "DR monitoring stand-down. Primary is primary."

### Customer Notifications

- Status page update: "Services fully restored. All systems operating normally."
- Enterprise customer account teams: personal notification to affected account contacts that full restoration is confirmed.

### Regulatory Notifications

If the original DR event triggered FCC Part 4 or other regulatory notifications:
- Confirm whether a follow-up or restoration notification is required under the regulation
- File the final report (FCC Part 4 requires a final written report within 30 days)

---

## Phase 6: DR Environment Decommission and Reset

After confirming primary stability (minimum 72 hours), return the DR environment to standby mode:

- Restore DR environment to its pre-activation configuration (standby/read-only)
- Confirm data replication from primary to DR has re-established and is current
- Confirm DR environment monitoring is active
- Update DR runbooks with any lessons learned from the actual DR event and failback
- Schedule the post-DR retrospective (within 2 weeks of failback completion)

---

## Failback Lessons Learned

The failback is a unique opportunity to assess the full DR lifecycle. The post-DR retrospective must address:

1. Was the DR activation decision made at the right time?
2. Were the RTO and RPO targets met during the DR event?
3. Were there data reconciliation issues during failback?
4. Did the failback runbook execute as planned?
5. What would we do differently next time?

Findings from the post-DR retrospective feed back into the DR plan, the failback runbook, and the next annual DR test design. This is the learning pipeline that converts real disasters into program improvement.
