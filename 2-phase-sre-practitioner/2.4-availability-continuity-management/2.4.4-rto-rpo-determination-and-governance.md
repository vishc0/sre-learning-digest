# ch04 — RTO/RPO Determination and Governance

## Definitions That Cannot Be Loose

The four most misused terms in availability management:

**RTO (Recovery Time Objective)**: The maximum elapsed time from the moment a disaster is formally declared to the moment the service is restored to users. The clock starts at declaration, not at the moment of failure.

**RPO (Recovery Point Objective)**: The maximum acceptable data loss expressed as elapsed time. An RPO of 1 hour means: after recovery, the oldest state we can accept is data from 1 hour before the failure. Any data written in that 1-hour window may be lost.

**MTPD (Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption)**: The business-defined upper limit on how long the organization can function without a given service before the disruption causes irreversible harm. MTPD sets the absolute ceiling for RTO. You cannot negotiate an RTO that exceeds MTPD — the business limit is the limit.

**WRT (Work Recovery Time)**: After systems are technically restored, the time required to reprocess transactions, reconcile data, and return to operational state. RTO + WRT must not exceed MTPD.

### Why WRT Is Chronically Ignored

Most DR plans state an RTO. Almost none state a WRT. The result: the plan declares recovery complete when services are technically available, but operations teams are still reconciling 6 hours of backlogged transactions, manually re-entering data lost in the RPO window, and working through failed message queues. The business is not actually operational — and that time counts against MTPD.

**Example**: A telecom notification platform declares DR recovery after 90 minutes (within 2-hour RTO). But 600,000 messages failed delivery during the outage window. Reprocessing, validating, and managing duplicates takes 4 additional hours. The business has been disrupted for 5.5 hours — the WRT of 4 hours was invisible to the DR plan. If MTPD is 4 hours, the recovery itself caused an MTPD breach.

**Rule**: Always state MTPD = RTO + WRT. Design your recovery architecture with WRT in the model, not as an afterthought.

## The Business Impact Analysis Process

BIA is the analytical foundation of RTO/RPO determination. It is not an IT exercise. It is a business exercise that IT facilitates.

### BIA Phase 1: Critical Function Identification

**Participants**: Business unit leaders, operations managers, compliance officers, finance leadership. Not IT — not yet.

**Output**: A list of critical business functions, not systems. The distinction matters.

A "critical function" is a business activity that, if interrupted, causes measurable financial, operational, regulatory, or reputational harm. Examples:
- Processing customer payments (financial services)
- Routing 911 calls (telecom)
- Issuing notifications to enterprise customers (notification platform)
- Filing regulatory reports on time (any regulated industry)

The system (database, API, platform) that supports the function is identified in the next phase. Starting with systems produces incomplete coverage — business units often rely on systems the IT team has classified as non-critical.

### BIA Phase 2: Impact Quantification

For each critical function, quantify the impact of disruption over time:

| Time Window | Financial Impact | Operational Impact | Regulatory Impact | Reputational Impact |
|------------|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|---------------------|
| 0–1 hour | Low | Medium | None | None |
| 1–4 hours | High | High | Low (if reported) | Low |
| 4–8 hours | Very High | Critical | Medium | Medium |
| 8–24 hours | Critical | Business-stopping | High (reporting trigger) | High |
| 24+ hours | Irreversible | Business continuity emergency | Critical | Severe |

The time at which impact moves from "recoverable" to "irreversible" defines the MTPD.

**Financial impact quantification**:
- Direct revenue loss: (transactions per hour × average transaction value) × outage hours
- SLA credit exposure: (contract value × SLA credit percentage) × affected contracts
- Regulatory fine exposure: per applicable regulation (FCC Part 4, FFIEC, HIPAA, etc.)

### BIA Phase 3: System-to-Function Mapping

Map each critical function to its enabling systems. This is where IT joins the BIA process.

| Critical Function | Enabling Systems | Tier Classification |
|-------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Enterprise notification delivery | Notification API, RabbitMQ, Database, DNS, CDN | Tier 1 |
| Customer authentication | Identity Provider, LDAP, Session Store | Tier 1 |
| Billing and invoice generation | Billing Platform, Payment Gateway, Database | Tier 1 |
| Internal employee communications | Email, Slack | Tier 3 |

Systems with multiple critical functions mapped to them are automatically candidates for Tier 1 classification regardless of their original engineering tier.

### BIA Phase 4: RTO/RPO Derivation

From the MTPD of each critical function, derive the maximum acceptable RTO:

**Rule**: RTO must be less than (MTPD − WRT)
**Rule**: RPO must be less than the data loss the business can reconcile manually in the WRT window

Example derivation for notification platform:
- MTPD: 4 hours (enterprise SLA breach at 4 hours of notification failure)
- WRT: 2 hours (estimated time to reprocess failed messages and validate delivery)
- Maximum RTO: 4 − 2 = 2 hours
- RPO: 30 minutes (business can re-queue notifications from the last 30-minute window manually if needed)

These are the targets. Engineering now must design an architecture that achieves them.

## The RTO/RPO Paradox: The Negotiation Framework

The paradox: the business wants zero RTO and zero RPO (continuous availability, no data loss). Engineering can theoretically build toward this. The cost curve asymptotes to infinity as you approach zero/zero. Every additional nine of availability and every additional second of RPO improvement costs exponentially more.

The resolution is a negotiation framework, not a technical argument.

### Step 1: Present the cost curve

Show the infrastructure and operational cost at each RTO/RPO point:

| RTO Target | RPO Target | DR Strategy | Annual Infrastructure Cost | Annual Ops Cost |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| 24 hours | 4 hours | Backup & Restore | $50K | $100K |
| 4 hours | 1 hour | Pilot Light | $150K | $150K |
| 1 hour | 15 min | Warm Standby | $400K | $200K |
| 15 min | 5 min | Hot Standby | $800K | $300K |
| < 5 min | < 1 min | Active-Active | $1.5M | $500K |

### Step 2: Present the cost of not meeting each target

Show what it costs the business if RTO is not met — i.e., if the service is down longer than the target:

- Missing 4-hour RTO by 2 hours: $400K in additional SLA credits + $200K in lost revenue
- Missing 15-minute RTO by 30 minutes: $750K in direct loss + regulatory notification required

### Step 3: Let the business make the risk-cost decision

Present the table:

| Option | Annual Investment | RTO/RPO | Business Risk if Met | Business Cost if Missed |
|--------|-----------------|---------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Baseline (no DR) | $0 | N/A | $3M expected annual loss | Full exposure |
| Pilot Light | $300K/yr | 4hr / 1hr | $400K expected residual | $200K per missed incident |
| Warm Standby | $600K/yr | 1hr / 15min | $100K expected residual | $750K per missed incident |
| Hot Standby | $1.1M/yr | 15min / 5min | $20K expected residual | $750K per missed incident |

The business chooses based on risk appetite and budget. The VP of Operations ensures the choice is made explicitly and documented — not by default.

### Step 4: Document the accepted residual risk

Whatever target is chosen, document what risk remains and who has accepted it:

```
Accepted Risk Record — Notification Platform DR
RTO target: 1 hour (Warm Standby strategy)
RPO target: 15 minutes
Accepted residual risk: Events exceeding 1-hour duration will breach enterprise SLA for affected customers.
Estimated frequency: 0–2 events/year based on historical data.
Estimated financial exposure per breach: $150K in SLA credits.
Annual expected residual cost: $0–$300K.
Business owner acceptance: [VP of Products, signature]
Date accepted: [date]
Next review: [date + 12 months or following SLA renegotiation]
```

This document protects the VP of Operations when an MTPD is breached. It demonstrates that the residual risk was known, quantified, accepted by the business, and reviewed on a defined cadence.

## Governance: Who Approves RTO/RPO Targets

RTO/RPO targets are business commitments, not engineering parameters. The approval chain must reflect this.

| Level | Role | Responsibility |
|-------|------|---------------|
| Approval | Board Risk Committee or CRO | Accept residual risk at the portfolio level; review annually |
| Ownership | VP of Operations | Set targets per service, manage gap, report to board |
| Accountability | Service Owner (business unit VP) | Confirm MTPD for their function; accept residual risk for their service |
| Implementation | VP of Engineering / SRE Lead | Design and operate architecture that achieves the target |
| Audit | Internal Audit / Compliance | Verify targets are tested, documented, and meet regulatory minimums |

### Review Cadence

RTO/RPO targets are not set-and-forget. Triggers for review:

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Annual budget cycle | Full BIA re-run for Tier 1 services; spot check Tier 2 |
| New regulatory requirement | Immediate review for affected services |
| New product launch or major feature | BIA for new service; re-run for services it depends on |
| SLA renegotiation with customer | Review targets for affected services |
| DR test reveals gap > 50% between actual and target | Immediate review and investment decision |
| Acquisition or integration of new company | BIA for all acquired systems |

### Regulatory Minimums

Certain industries have regulatory floors that override business decisions:

| Regulation | Requirement | Industry |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| FCC Part 4 | Network outages affecting 900K+ user-minutes must be reported within 24 hours. Implies ability to quantify impact, which requires monitoring and logging that can survive a DR event. | Telecom |
| FFIEC | Financial institutions must maintain BCP with tested recovery capability. RTO for critical systems typically required to be ≤ 24 hours; many large banks commit to 4 hours. | Financial Services |
| HIPAA §164.308(a)(7) | Covered entities must have a DR plan, data backup plan, and emergency mode operation plan. RPO and RTO not prescribed, but must be documented and tested. | Healthcare |
| DORA (EU) | ICT third-party risk management, incident reporting within 4 hours for major incidents, annual DR testing. | Financial Services (EU) |
| PCI DSS | Cardholder data must have recovery procedures and tested backups. No mandated RTO, but annual testing required. | Any entity handling card data |

**T-Mobile obligation**: FCC Part 4 requires outage reporting within 24 hours for qualifying network events. The DR plan must include the FCC notification step — declaration authority, notification content, and notification timeline — as a first-class item alongside technical recovery steps.

## RTO/RPO Testing: Validating the Paper Target

The most dangerous document in DR planning is an untested DR plan with RTO/RPO targets that have never been validated.

### The Test Hierarchy

| Test Type | What It Validates | RTO/RPO Evidence It Produces |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Tabletop exercise | Logical correctness of the plan; role clarity; decision points | None — no actual recovery |
| Simulation (chaos injection without traffic) | Automated detection, failover mechanics, monitoring behavior | Partial — measures failover in controlled conditions |
| Partial DR test (fail one component) | Recovery of a specific scenario; validates runbook accuracy | Scenario-specific RTO/RPO evidence |
| Full DR test (full environment failover) | End-to-end recovery capability; actual vs. target RTO/RPO | Strongest evidence; required for regulatory compliance |

### Measuring Actual RTO During a DR Test

To produce credible RTO evidence:
1. Start the DR clock at the moment of failure declaration (not at the moment the failure was injected)
2. Stop the clock only when all validation checklist items pass — not when services are technically up
3. Record the exact time of each step in the recovery sequence
4. Calculate: (Declaration time) → (Service restoration time) = Actual RTO
5. Compare against target RTO; document the gap

### The "Untested Backup" Problem

"We have backups" is not a DR capability. "We have tested and validated backups that we have successfully restored from, with a documented restoration time of X hours" is a DR capability.

Untested backups fail at restoration with alarming frequency:
- Backup corruption that went undetected (no restore validation in the backup job)
- Backup files present but missing database schema or configuration
- Backup restoration tool incompatible with a newer database version
- Backup storage inaccessible due to credential rotation or access policy change

**Rule**: A backup you have never restored from is not a backup. It is a storage cost center that creates false confidence.

**Minimum backup testing standard**: Quarterly restoration test to a sandboxed environment. Measure actual restoration time. Validate data integrity with checksums and spot queries. Document results.

## Board-Level Reporting: Current Capability vs. Commitment

The board-level RTO/RPO report answers three questions:
1. What did we commit to?
2. What can we actually achieve (based on test evidence)?
3. What does the gap between commitment and capability cost the business if we have a real DR event?

### Board Report Format

```
DR Capability Report — Q[X] FY[YYYY]
Reporting Period: [dates]
Prepared by: VP of Operations
Board Review: [date]

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
[2–3 sentences: overall posture, any significant gaps, investment status]

SERVICE RECOVERY CAPABILITY — TIER 1 SERVICES

| Service | RTO Committed | RTO Tested | RPO Committed | RPO Tested | Test Date | Status |
|---------|--------------|-----------|--------------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| [Svc] | 15 min | 22 min | 5 min | 8 min | [date] | GAP |
| [Svc] | 1 hr | 55 min | 30 min | 20 min | [date] | MET |

GAPS AND INVESTMENT REQUIREMENTS
[Table of gaps, risk if gap persists during real DR, investment required to close]

RESIDUAL RISK ACCEPTED BY BUSINESS
[List of accepted risks with approval dates]

NEXT DR TEST
[Date, scope, services included]
```

This format gives the board what they need to govern: commitment vs. capability, gap visibility, and investment decision context.
