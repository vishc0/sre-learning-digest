# ch01 — Availability as a Business Requirement

## The Core Reframe

Engineers measure availability as a percentage. Boards measure it as money.

When a principal SRE says "we achieved 99.95% availability last quarter," the board hears a number without meaning. When a VP of Operations says "we experienced 10.9 hours of downtime last quarter, which cost us $3.4M in direct revenue loss and $1.2M in SLA credits, against a target of zero hours and a budget of 4.38 hours," the board can act on that.

The reframe is not cosmetic. It changes what decisions get made. An engineering target is optimized by engineers within an engineering budget. A business commitment is governed by the business, funded by the P&L, and protected by the risk framework. These are different accountability structures.

Your job at Director/VP level is to convert between the two — fluently, in both directions, in real time.

## The Cost of Downtime Model

Downtime has two cost categories. Most organizations measure only the first.

### Direct Costs

| Cost Type | Description | Measurement |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Lost revenue | Transactions that cannot complete during the outage | Revenue/hour × outage duration |
| SLA credits | Contractual payments to affected customers | Per-contract credit formula × affected customers |
| Emergency labor | Overtime, contractor fees, vendor support escalation | Headcount × hours × premium rate |
| Recovery operations | Cloud resource scale-up, data restoration fees | Actual spend tracked during incident |
| Regulatory fines | Penalties from FCC, FFIEC, HIPAA for availability failures | Statutory scale, not easily predictable |

### Indirect Costs

Indirect costs are real but harder to quantify. They often exceed direct costs over a 12-month horizon.

| Cost Type | Description | Measurement |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Brand damage | Customer perception shift measured in NPS decline | NPS point × customer lifetime value formula |
| Customer churn | Customers who cancel or reduce service post-outage | Churn rate increase × average contract value |
| Lost new business | Deals that don't close because of perceived reliability risk | Pipeline conversion decline |
| Engineering distraction | Engineering hours redirected from product to firefighting | Sprint capacity lost × sprint velocity × opportunity cost |
| Talent attrition | Engineers and operators who leave organizations with chronic outage cultures | Replacement cost × roles lost |

### The Downtime Cost Formula

```
Annual Downtime Cost = (Revenue/Hour × Downtime Hours) 
                     + (SLA Credit Rate × Affected Customers × Outage Hours)
                     + Emergency Labor
                     + Indirect Cost Multiplier (typically 2x–4x direct costs)
```

The indirect cost multiplier is contested — use 2x in conservative board presentations, 3x in risk assessments, and note the assumption explicitly.

## The Board Conversation: Translating Uptime Percentages

The nine-count system is unintuitive to non-technical executives. Your job is to translate it before being asked.

| Availability | Annual Downtime | What This Means Operationally |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| 99% (two nines) | 87.6 hours/year | Nearly 4 full days offline; acceptable only for non-critical internal tools |
| 99.9% (three nines) | 8.76 hours/year | About 9 hours; common for business-critical applications; one major incident can consume the entire budget |
| 99.95% | 4.38 hours/year | About 4.5 hours; competitive for SaaS and enterprise platforms |
| 99.99% (four nines) | 52.6 minutes/year | Under an hour annually; requires serious architectural investment; typical for payment and telecom infrastructure |
| 99.999% (five nines) | 5.26 minutes/year | Five minutes annually; requires hot standby or active-active with sub-second failover; carrier-grade requirement |

**The translation rule**: Never say "three nines." Say "8.76 hours per year of acceptable downtime, which at our revenue rate translates to $X million in maximum tolerable exposure."

**The framing rule**: Present availability targets as availability budgets. A budget implies governance: someone owns it, it can be spent wisely or wastefully, and overspending has consequences.

> **What the VP must decide**: What availability target will the business commit to for each service tier? This is a business decision, not an engineering preference. Get it in writing.

## Availability Tiers: Classification Methodology

Not every service deserves four nines. Treating all services as equally critical destroys the investment case for protecting the genuinely critical ones.

### The Three-Tier Model

**Mission-Critical** — Tier 1
- Definition: Service failure directly and immediately impacts revenue, regulatory compliance, or customer safety
- Examples: Payment processing, telecom call routing, fraud detection, 911 dispatch
- Availability target: 99.99% or higher
- RTO requirement: Minutes, not hours
- DR strategy: Hot standby or active-active
- Cost implication: 3x–5x infrastructure cost vs. standard

**Business-Critical** — Tier 2
- Definition: Service failure disrupts business operations but does not immediately halt revenue or create regulatory risk
- Examples: CRM, order management, notification platforms, internal developer tools for production teams
- Availability target: 99.9%–99.95%
- RTO requirement: 1–4 hours
- DR strategy: Warm standby
- Cost implication: 1.5x–2.5x infrastructure cost vs. standard

**Standard** — Tier 3
- Definition: Service failure is inconvenient but has minimal financial or regulatory impact
- Examples: Internal wikis, analytics dashboards, non-production environments, reporting systems
- Availability target: 99%–99.9%
- RTO requirement: 4–24 hours
- DR strategy: Backup and restore
- Cost implication: Baseline

### Classification Methodology

Use this five-question scoring to classify services:

1. **Revenue impact**: Does a 1-hour outage directly cost more than $50K? (+2 points)
2. **Regulatory risk**: Is this service subject to regulatory availability requirements? (+2 points)
3. **Customer-facing**: Do end customers interact directly with this service? (+1 point)
4. **Dependency depth**: Do 5 or more other services depend on this service? (+1 point)
5. **Recovery complexity**: Would recovery require more than 2 hours of manual work? (+1 point)

Score 5–7: Mission-Critical (Tier 1)
Score 3–4: Business-Critical (Tier 2)
Score 0–2: Standard (Tier 3)

Run this classification annually and whenever a new service launches or an existing service's business function changes materially.

## Five Nines vs. Four Nines vs. Three Nines: Operational Reality

The difference between availability tiers is not just architectural — it is organizational, operational, and cultural.

### Three Nines (99.9%): 8.76 Hours/Year

**Architecture**: Multi-AZ deployment with automated failover. Single region. Replication lag of seconds to minutes acceptable.

**Operations**: On-call rotation handles most incidents within the 8.76-hour budget. One Sev1 incident that runs 4–5 hours consumes more than half the annual budget. Budget management becomes a real constraint.

**Staffing**: Standard on-call rotation. Engineers can investigate before acting.

**Cost**: Baseline. No exotic architecture. Reasonable infrastructure spend.

**Suitable for**: Business-critical applications where business hours recovery is acceptable. Most enterprise SaaS platforms.

### Four Nines (99.99%): 52 Minutes/Year

**Architecture**: Active-passive hot standby across multiple availability zones or regions. Failover time measured in seconds to 2 minutes. Synchronous or near-synchronous replication required for RPO compliance.

**Operations**: Incidents cannot run long. A single 30-minute incident consumes 57% of the annual budget. Automated detection, automated escalation, and pre-tested runbooks are not optional. Manual investigation is a budget violation.

**Staffing**: Senior on-call engineers only. Runbooks for every known failure mode. 24/7 coverage with immediate escalation paths.

**Cost**: 2x–4x infrastructure cost over three nines, depending on architecture. Significant engineering investment in automation.

**Suitable for**: Payment rails, authentication services, core telecom infrastructure, regulated financial services platforms.

### Five Nines (99.999%): 5 Minutes/Year

**Architecture**: Active-active across multiple geographic regions. Zero human intervention in the failover path — humans are too slow. Automated circuit breakers, health checks, and traffic shifting must complete in under 30 seconds. Data consistency is a hard engineering problem at this tier.

**Operations**: Five minutes per year means a single undetected 10-minute incident puts you in breach for the year. The operations model is continuous verification, not reactive response.

**Staffing**: Dedicated SRE teams per service. Automated response for all known failure modes. Human intervention is the exception, not the norm.

**Cost**: 5x–10x baseline. Carrier-grade infrastructure. This is why telco networks cost what they do.

**Suitable for**: 911 infrastructure, core network routing, real-time payment clearing. Rarely justified for enterprise software applications.

> **What the board asks**: "Why can't everything be five nines?" Answer: "Because five nines for our full application portfolio would cost approximately $X more per year than three nines. Our downtime risk at three nines is $Y per year. The math does not support the investment for non-mission-critical services."

## The Availability Budget: Commitment vs. Tolerance

Every availability target implicitly defines a downtime budget. The Director/VP job is to make this budget explicit and govern it actively.

### Two Numbers to Establish Upfront

**Committed availability** — what you have promised customers and regulators. This is your contractual obligation. Breaching it has financial and legal consequences.

**Tolerable availability** — what the business can actually absorb without catastrophic impact. This is your engineering target. It should be higher than committed (i.e., more aggressive) to provide margin.

### The Budget Governance Model

Treat downtime budget like financial budget:

1. **Allocate** the annual downtime budget at the start of the fiscal year, by service tier
2. **Track** actual consumption in real time — every outage minute is a budget draw
3. **Report** monthly: budget remaining, burn rate, projected year-end position
4. **Alert** at 50% consumption mid-year — this triggers a review
5. **Escalate** at 80% consumption — this triggers a change freeze review and executive notification
6. **Govern** at 100% consumption — any further downtime is a breach; executive decision required on risk acceptance or emergency investment

**The change freeze trigger** — cross-reference [Change Management]: When the downtime budget reaches 80%, the change freeze threshold tightens. New changes require VP approval rather than standard CAB approval. This is not punitive — it is risk management. The remaining budget is too thin to absorb an avoidable failure.

### Connecting Budget to Board Reporting

The availability budget becomes the frame for quarterly board reporting:

- Q1: Budget 2.19 hours (of 8.76 annual). Used: 0.7 hours. Remaining: 8.06 hours. On track.
- Q2: Budget 2.19 hours. Used: 1.8 hours. Remaining: 6.26 hours. Alert: 80% of Q2 budget consumed in single incident.
- Q3: Budget 2.19 hours. Used: 0.3 hours. Remaining: 5.96 hours. On track. Recovery from Q2 anomaly.
- Q4: Remaining budget: 5.96 hours. Forecast: 5.2 hours. Projected year-end: 2.8 hours used (actual 99.97%).

That is a board-ready narrative. It tells a story, identifies the anomaly, and projects forward.
