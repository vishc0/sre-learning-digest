# Chapter 1 — Foundations: Change Management vs. Release Management

## Why Change Management and Release Management Are Distinct but Inseparable

Most organizations treat Change Management and Release Management as adjacent concerns managed by different teams — sometimes even different departments. That structural separation is the first failure. These two disciplines govern the same moment in a system's lifecycle: the transition of something from one state to another in production. Separating them creates coordination gaps where risk hides.

The distinction is real but not organizational. It is functional:

**Change Management is the governance system.** It answers: Who authorizes this? What risk is acceptable? What controls are required? What gets documented? It is the set of rules, processes, and bodies (the CAB, the RFC, the PIR) that determine whether a change happens, when it happens, and what happens when it fails.

**Release Management is the engineering system.** It answers: What artifact is moving? Through what pipeline? With what deployment strategy — canary, blue-green, rolling? What is the rollback mechanism and how long does it take to execute? It is the set of pipelines, tooling, and practices that determine how a change physically moves from one environment to another.

Both fail without the other.

A release pipeline with no change governance is an operational accident waiting to happen. You have the engineering capability to move software quickly and reliably — and no mechanism to decide whether this particular change should move, whether the blast radius has been assessed, whether a rollback plan exists that someone has actually tested. Speed without governance produces Failure Mode 2 from the README: engineering velocity without guardrails. The first low-probability, high-impact change will take you down.

Change governance without release engineering rigor is bureaucratic theater. You have an RFC process, a CAB, change tickets in ServiceNow. But the rollback plan in the RFC says "redeploy the previous version" without specifying how long that takes, who executes it, or whether the database schema migration is reversible. The CAB approved the change based on documentation that does not reflect what will actually happen. The governance creates audit evidence but does not reduce risk.

The analogy that makes this concrete: Change Management is the flight plan approval system. Before a commercial aircraft departs, the flight plan is filed, reviewed, and approved — route, altitude, fuel load, alternate airports, crew qualifications. Release Management is the aircraft and crew: the engineering systems, the preflight checklist, the instruments, the pilot's training. You need both. A flight plan without a functioning aircraft is paper. A functioning aircraft without an approved flight plan is an unauthorized flight. The FAA (your governance body) does not care how good your aircraft is if the plan was not approved. The passengers (your customers) do not care how thorough the approval process was if the aircraft malfunctions at altitude.

At Director/VP level, your job is to ensure these two systems are designed, staffed, and measured as one integrated discipline — not to manage them as separate bureaucratic tracks.

## The Change Taxonomy

Every change that touches production falls into one of three categories. The category determines the approval path, the documentation requirements, and the governance overhead. Getting the taxonomy right is the foundation of everything else.

### Standard Change: Pre-Approved, Pre-Validated, Repeatable

A Standard Change is a change that has been formally assessed, documented, tested, and approved by the CAB as a change type — not as a specific instance. Once a procedure is in the Standard Change catalog, any team can execute it without per-instance CAB review, as long as they follow the documented procedure.

**The criteria for Standard Change designation** — all six must be met:

1. **Risk assessment completed and documented.** The change type has been formally assessed using the impact × likelihood framework. The assessment is on file and current (reviewed within the last 12 months).
2. **Procedure written and peer-reviewed.** A step-by-step execution procedure exists, has been reviewed by at least one SME outside the authoring team, and is stored in a version-controlled location.
3. **Success and failure criteria defined.** There is an objective, observable definition of what "this change worked" looks like and what "this change failed" looks like. Not "the service is healthy" — specific metrics, checks, or outputs.
4. **Rollback tested at least once.** The rollback procedure has been executed, not just documented. Someone has actually run it and confirmed it restores the prior state within the expected time.
5. **Execution time is bounded and predictable.** The change takes a known amount of time. A certificate rotation that takes 3 minutes on a Tuesday took 3 minutes the last 20 times. A database migration that "should take 30 minutes" is not Standard — it introduces execution time variability that cannot be pre-approved.
6. **Approved by CAB as a category.** The CAB has reviewed the change type and formally added it to the Standard Change catalog. Individual teams cannot self-designate Standard Changes.

**Standard Change catalog governance:** The CAB owns the catalog. It is reviewed quarterly with two questions: What new procedures have teams developed that qualify? What existing procedures have had failures and should be demoted back to Normal?

**Examples at enterprise scale:**
- Automated certificate rotation for internal services where the rotation is fully scripted, idempotent, and verifiably self-testing
- Kubernetes HPA threshold adjustments within pre-approved bounds (e.g., min replicas 2-10, max replicas 10-50, CPU target 60-80%)
- Cache TTL changes for internal caches within defined ranges (e.g., ±20% of baseline TTL)
- Replica count scaling within pre-approved min/max bands for stateless services
- DNS record updates for internal services following a documented naming pattern with a defined TTL

**What Standard Change is NOT:** Anything involving a new target (a service, environment, or region not previously included in this procedure). Anything involving schema changes. Anything requiring coordination across three or more teams. Anything where execution time is not predictable within a 10% band.

**Director metric: Standard Change percentage.** Track what fraction of all changes in a quarter are Standard. Elite organizations reach 60-70% Standard. If your Standard Change percentage is below 30%, one of two things is true: your CAB is overloaded reviewing changes that should be pre-approved, or your teams have not invested in building documented, tested, repeatable procedures. Both are your problem to fix.

### Normal Change: RFC Process and CAB Review

A Normal Change is any planned change that is not pre-approved (not Standard) and not time-critical (not Emergency). Normal is the default classification for anything new, non-repeatable, higher-risk, or lacking a tested rollback.

Normal Changes require:
- An RFC submitted at least 5 business days before the planned implementation
- CAB review at the weekly meeting (or an ad-hoc session for time-sensitive cases)
- Documented blast radius assessment
- Documented rollback plan with tested steps
- Maintenance window coordination

**Director callout: Normal Change lead time is your biggest deployment frequency bottleneck.** If teams submit an RFC and wait 10 business days for approval, they will never reach Elite deployment frequency. The solution is not to weaken Normal Change controls — it is to aggressively expand the Standard Change catalog so that more work flows through the zero-friction path, and to run a CAB that reviews RFCs in hours, not days.

### Emergency Change: Declaration, Compressed Review, Mandatory PIR

An Emergency Change is a change required to resolve a production crisis or meet a hard deadline that cannot wait for the next CAB cycle. The operative word is "cannot." Not "we would prefer not to" — cannot.

**Declaration criteria — at least one must be met:**
- An active production incident where this change is the required mitigation and waiting for Normal process will prolong customer impact
- A confirmed security vulnerability with active exploitation in the wild
- A regulatory or compliance deadline with legal consequence if missed
- Customer data integrity is at risk and immediate action is required

**What is not an emergency:** A planned change where the team ran out of preparation time. A change to meet an internal deadline that is not externally committed. A fix for a non-production-impacting bug that someone wants to push on a Friday. These descriptions cover the majority of emergency change abuse in most organizations.

**Emergency change frequency as a health metric.** If your emergency change percentage exceeds 20% of all changes, your Normal change process is too slow or too painful. If it exceeds 40%, your teams have learned to game the system because the Normal path is effectively unusable. Both require fixing the Normal process, not rationalizing the emergency percentage.

## Release vs. Deployment: The Artifact vs. the Act

This distinction is consistently blurred in organizations that have not formalized their release management practice. It matters operationally because it determines where decisions are made and who makes them.

**A Release is a versioned, immutable artifact.** A container image tagged `api-gateway:v2.3.1`. A Helm chart at version `4.1.2`. An IaC module pinned to `v1.8.0`. A database migration script versioned in your migration toolchain. The release is complete when the artifact is built, signed, tested in pre-production, and approved for production use. The release decision is made in your CI pipeline, typically triggered by a merge to main after passing all automated gates.

**A Deployment is the act of placing a release into a target environment.** Deployment happens in your CD pipeline. It is the moment the artifact meets production traffic. One release can produce many deployments across many stages: canary (1% of traffic), expanding canary (10%), staging validation, regional production, global production. Each deployment is a separate act with its own go/no-go criteria.

**The release decision and the deployment decision are separate governance gates.** A release is approved for production. A deployment is executed against a specific target at a specific time. The CAB approves releases (via the RFC process). The on-call engineer and the deployment pipeline approve deployments (via automated canary checks, smoke tests, and manual hold gates).

**Director callout:** "Has this been released?" and "Has this been deployed?" are different questions with different answers and different implications.

- "Has this been released?" = Has the artifact been approved for production use? If yes, you can deploy it. If no, stop.
- "Has this been deployed?" = Is this artifact currently serving traffic in production? If yes, at what percentage? If no, why not?

A release approved for production might be deployed to 5% of traffic (canary stage) while the prior release still serves 95%. During an incident, you need to know both: what is approved (the release question) and what is actually running (the deployment question). Conflating them delays diagnosis.

This distinction also matters for rollback planning. Rolling back a deployment is fast — switch traffic back to the prior release, which is still deployed and healthy. Rolling back a release requires redeployment of the prior artifact. They have very different time-to-restore characteristics, and your RFC should specify which rollback path is being planned for.

## The DORA Four Metrics as the VP's Compass

The DevOps Research and Assessment (DORA) program produces the most rigorously validated benchmarks for software delivery performance. The 2023 DORA State of DevOps report, covering 36,000 respondents, provides four key metrics with performance tier benchmarks that function as an executive compass for any change and release program.

These metrics are not aspirational targets. They are diagnostic tools. They tell you which quadrant of the speed-stability space you occupy, which failure mode you are in, and where to apply intervention.

### The Four Metrics

**Deployment Frequency** — How often does code reach production?

| Tier | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| Elite | Multiple times per day |
| High | Weekly to monthly |
| Medium | Monthly to 6 months |
| Low | Fewer than once every 6 months |

Deployment Frequency is constrained by your Normal change lead time, your CAB throughput, your maintenance window availability, and the ratio of Standard to Normal changes. Low Deployment Frequency almost always traces to one of these four constraints.

**Lead Time for Changes** — How long from code commit to production?

| Tier | Lead Time |
|------|-----------|
| Elite | Less than 1 hour |
| High | 1 day to 1 week |
| Medium | 1 week to 1 month |
| Low | 1 to 6 months |

Lead Time is a compound metric: it includes development time, code review, automated testing, RFC preparation, CAB review, maintenance window scheduling, and deployment execution. Each stage is a candidate for reduction. The CAB review and window scheduling stages are where change governance most directly constrains this metric.

**Change Failure Rate** — What percentage of changes cause a degradation or require rollback?

| Tier | Rate |
|------|------|
| Elite | 0–5% |
| High | 10–15% |
| Medium | 15–45% |
| Low | 46% or higher |

Change Failure Rate is the primary output metric of your entire change program. It measures whether your governance and engineering practices are actually reducing risk. A Low CFR means roughly half your changes cause problems — your CAB is not catching risk, your testing is not catching regressions, and your blast radius assessments are not informing your deployment strategies.

**Mean Time to Restore (MTTR)** — How long to restore service after a failure?

| Tier | MTTR |
|------|------|
| Elite | Less than 1 hour |
| High | Less than 1 day |
| Medium | Less than 1 week |
| Low | 1 week or more |

MTTR is constrained by rollback plan quality, blast radius clarity, and the availability of people who know the system. A failed change with a tested rollback plan and a clear blast radius map restores in minutes. A failed change with no rollback plan and an unknown blast radius restores in hours — or days.

### Why All Four Matter Together

Deployment Frequency and Lead Time measure velocity. Change Failure Rate and MTTR measure stability. These are not competing axes — they are complementary measurements of the same system.

A Director who watches only velocity metrics (Deployment Frequency, Lead Time) will eventually produce a catastrophic change and not understand why. They optimized for speed and stripped out the controls that would have caught the failure.

A Director who watches only stability metrics (Change Failure Rate, MTTR) will strangle deployment frequency and lose engineering talent to organizations that ship faster. They optimized for safety in a way that made the organization uncompetitive.

**The 2023 DORA finding that changes everything:** Elite performers deploy 973 times more frequently than Low performers — AND have three times lower Change Failure Rate. They are faster AND safer. This is not a paradox. It is the proof that speed and stability are achieved through the same interventions: automation, blast radius reduction, and Standard Change expansion. Not by choosing one over the other.

> **Director Decision Point**
>
> Your DORA baseline is the most important thing you learn in your first 90 days. You cannot improve what you have not measured. Week 1: pull Change Failure Rate and Deployment Frequency for the last 6 months. Do not wait for a report — query your change management tooling, your deployment pipeline, and your incident management system directly.
>
> That number tells you where the dysfunction is before you have attended a single CAB meeting or read a single runbook. If CFR is above 15%, you have a risk assessment or testing problem. If Deployment Frequency is in the Low tier, you have a process friction problem. If both are bad, you have a program that was built to generate audit evidence, not to deliver software safely.

## Why Organizations Fail: Bureaucratic Theater vs. Engineering Discipline

The pathology is recognizable. The symptoms appear in organizations across industries, scales, and technology stacks. Understanding the pattern is what separates a Director who can fix a broken program from one who perpetuates it.

### Theater Symptoms

The CAB meeting runs every Tuesday for 90 minutes. There are 15 RFCs on the agenda. The CAB chair asks each team to "walk us through your change." No one has read the RFCs before the meeting. Each RFC gets 4 minutes regardless of risk level. A Kubernetes HPA adjustment gets the same review depth as a database schema migration. Approvals are granted because the submitter is credible, not because the risk assessment is sound.

Emergency changes consistently exceed 35% of all changes. Teams learned three years ago that the Normal path takes 12 business days end-to-end. The Emergency path takes 2 hours. The incentive is obvious.

Change tickets are created on Monday for changes that happened Friday. The ticket exists to satisfy the audit requirement. The change happened without any of the risk reduction steps the RFC process was designed to enforce.

### Engineering Discipline Symptoms

RFC review for low-risk changes (P3/P4 impact, Low likelihood) is partially automated. A tool checks for required fields, validates that the blast radius assessment references a specific service dependency map, and confirms that the rollback plan names a specific command and estimated duration. Low-risk RFCs that pass automated checks are approved within 24 hours without a CAB meeting.

The Standard Change catalog is reviewed quarterly. Last quarter, 11 new procedures were added. Three existing procedures were demoted because they had failures in the prior 6 months. The CAB now spends its meeting time on the 40% of changes that are genuinely high-risk, not on rubber-stamping procedures that have been executed safely 200 times.

Change Failure Rate is tracked per team per quarter and presented in the engineering leadership review. Teams with CFR above 10% are reviewed for process gaps, not blamed for incidents.

### The Tell: Emergency Change Percentage

Count your emergency change percentage. Divide emergency changes by total changes for the trailing 90 days.

- Below 5%: your Normal process is functioning. Emergency path is used for genuine emergencies.
- 5-20%: watch this number. Some process friction exists but it is manageable.
- 20-40%: your Normal process is too slow or too painful. Investigate lead time and team behavior. The emergency path is being used as a workaround.
- Above 40%: your CAB is theater. Teams have learned to route around it. Fix the Normal process or emergency change rate will never come down.

The fix for high emergency change rate is never to tighten emergency controls — it is to reduce Normal change friction so teams have no incentive to abuse the emergency path.

## The Speed-Stability Paradox: How Elite Performers Resolve It

The conventional wisdom says you must choose: deploy fast or deploy safely. Every senior engineer has been in an organization that made this choice — usually implicitly, usually wrong.

The conventional wisdom is wrong. The 2023 DORA data is definitive: Elite performers ship 973x more frequently than Low performers and have 3x lower change failure rate. The relationship between speed and stability, at the Elite tier, is positive. Faster delivery is safer delivery — not because Elite teams skip controls, but because their controls are automated, their blast radius is bounded, and their rollback paths are tested.

**The paradox resolves through three specific mechanisms:**

**1. Automation of Standard Changes.** Manual processes do not scale and do not reduce risk consistently. A certificate rotation procedure executed manually by 12 different engineers will have 12 variations. The same procedure executed by a script runs identically every time and completes in 2 minutes instead of 45. Standard Changes automated into pipelines remove human error and remove CAB overhead simultaneously.

**2. Blast radius reduction through deployment strategy.** A canary deployment that exposes 1% of traffic to a new release limits the blast radius to 1% of customers and 1% of transactions. If the canary fails, automatic rollback triggers before the blast radius expands. The same change deployed directly to 100% of production has a 100% blast radius at the moment of failure. Speed and safety both improve when the deployment strategy limits exposure.

**3. Standard Change catalog expansion.** The more changes that flow through the Standard path (pre-approved, automated, tested rollback), the more CAB bandwidth concentrates on genuinely novel, high-risk changes. This is the compounding flywheel: each new Standard Change procedure reduces CAB load, increases team autonomy, and improves deployment frequency — while maintaining the governance record required for compliance and audit.

**The T-Mobile scale reality check:** A failed change that affects 1% of traffic during a canary deployment is a learning event. The blast radius is ~250,000 customers, it lasts minutes before automatic rollback, and no major SLA is breached. That same change, deployed directly to production, affects 25 million customers. The incident takes hours. The regulatory notification may be required. The postmortem runs for weeks.

The choice between canary and direct deployment is not a choice between speed and safety. The canary deployment is both faster (automated rollback vs. manual incident response) and safer (bounded blast radius vs. full production impact). The Director who understands this builds deployment strategy into the change governance framework — not as an optional best practice, but as a required control for P1 and P2 services.

## Summary: What Chapter 1 Establishes

| Concept | Director's Working Definition |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Change Management | The governance system: who approves, what risk is acceptable, what gets logged |
| Release Management | The engineering system: what artifact moves, through what pipeline, with what deployment strategy |
| Standard Change | Pre-approved change type; no per-instance CAB review; requires catalog governance |
| Normal Change | Planned, requires RFC and CAB review; the default classification |
| Emergency Change | Production-critical, bypasses CAB cycle; requires compressed review and mandatory PIR |
| Release | Versioned, immutable artifact approved for production use |
| Deployment | The act of placing a release into a target environment; separate decision from release |
| DORA Four | Deployment Frequency + Lead Time (velocity) + Change Failure Rate + MTTR (stability) — watch all four |
| Speed-Safety | Not a trade-off at the Elite tier — both improve through automation, blast radius reduction, catalog expansion |
