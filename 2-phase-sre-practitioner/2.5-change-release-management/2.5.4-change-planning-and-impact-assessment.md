# Chapter 04: Change Planning and Impact Assessment

**Audience**: SRE Manager / Director / VP of Operations  
**Lens**: The RFC is the change owner's proof of readiness. The Director's job is to ensure the process produces complete, accurate proofs — not to write the RFCs.

---

## 1. RFC Structure: The Change Owner's Proof of Readiness

The RFC is not paperwork. It is the change owner's formal statement that they have thought through the failure modes. A well-written RFC contains its own refutation: here is what we are doing, here is what could go wrong, here is how we detect it, and here is exactly what we do about it. The RFC is complete when an engineer who did not write the change could implement it and execute rollback from the document alone, without asking the author any questions.

The inverse test is useful: if the RFC contains any phrase like "contact the team" or "investigate further" or "revert as needed," it is not complete. Those are placeholders for thinking that has not happened yet.

### RFC Sections — Expanded

**Section 1: Change Summary**  
One paragraph. Non-technical. Readable by a product manager, a communications manager, or an executive who needs to make a customer notification decision. What is changing, why, what happens to customers if it fails, and what the recovery plan is in plain language. The change owner who cannot write this paragraph does not understand their own change well enough to implement it in production.

**Section 2: Business Justification**  
Why is this change happening? What customer or business outcome does it enable or protect? Changes without a business justification are candidates for the question "why are we doing this at all?" Not every change is a feature delivery — maintenance, security patching, and debt reduction are valid justifications — but the justification must be stated.

**Section 3: Technical Description**  
Precise. Specifically: which services, which configuration files, which code paths, which infrastructure components, which versions, which environments. "Update the authentication service" is not a technical description. "Upgrade `auth-service` from image `v2.14.1` to `v2.15.0` in the `prod-us-east` EKS cluster, updating the Kubernetes Deployment manifest and the ConfigMap for session token TTL from 900 to 1800 seconds" is a technical description.

**Section 4: Scope and Blast Radius**  
Primary services affected. Secondary services affected via dependency (minimum two hops — see Section 2 of this chapter). Environments. Customer segments. Estimated customer-visible impact if the change fails mid-execution: not "some customers may be affected" but "customers in the middle of an active checkout flow will receive a 503 for up to N minutes; customers who have not yet started checkout will see no impact."

**Section 5: Risk Assessment**  
Impact score (Low/Medium/High) × Likelihood score (Low/Medium/High) = overall risk tier. Impact is assessed at worst-case execution failure, not at expected execution. Mitigating factors already in place (circuit breakers, redundant instances, feature flags) that reduce either score are listed explicitly. Risk is not argued away — it is documented honestly and mitigated specifically.

**Section 6: Implementation Steps**  
Numbered. Owner assigned per step by name, not role. Estimated duration per step. Go/no-go verification criteria at each step: "After step 3: confirm `auth-service` pod health checks are green in Datadog and error rate has not increased above baseline for 5 consecutive minutes. If not met within 10 minutes of step 3 completion, execute rollback from step R1."

**Section 7: Rollback Plan**  
Specific trigger criteria — not "if something goes wrong" but "if error rate exceeds 5% at any step, or if step N has not completed within its estimated duration + 50% buffer, or if any downstream service alerts." Step-by-step rollback procedure with the same granularity as implementation steps. Estimated rollback duration (measured, not estimated from intuition). Data integrity considerations for the rollback state. Named rollback owner with confirmed availability during the window.

**Section 8: Test Evidence**  
Staging test results: pass/fail, timestamp, test scope. Load test results if the change affects throughput or latency. Unit test coverage percentage for changed code paths. Chaos test results if applicable. Attestations ("we tested it and it works") are not evidence. Screenshots, log excerpts, or CI/CD pipeline links are evidence.

**Section 9: Maintenance Window**  
Start time, end time, timezone, primary window, and backup window if the primary is unavailable. Justification for timing must be data-backed: pull traffic volume by hour from Splunk and show that the proposed window is below 15% of peak. "Saturday night is low traffic" is not data-backed justification — it is assumption. Traffic patterns for telecom services do not follow retail intuition.

**Section 10: Approval Chain**  
Peer reviewer: name, date reviewed, and whether any concerns were raised and resolved. Architect sign-off if required (see Section 6 of this chapter for criteria). Security sign-off if required. CAB submission timestamp. Without a documented peer review, the RFC is not ready for CAB.

## 2. Impact Assessment: Upstream/Downstream Service Mapping

Impact assessment starts before the RFC is written. The change owner draws the dependency map first — not from memory, but from authoritative sources: the service catalog, APM tooling (Datadog, Dynatrace), or Splunk service maps. Memory is not authoritative. Engineers routinely underestimate blast radius by 30–50% when working from mental models instead of verified dependency data.

### Upstream: What Feeds the Changed Service

Upstream dependencies matter during the change window. If an upstream service behaves unexpectedly during execution — sends malformed payloads, spikes in request volume, degrades its own response time — how does that interact with the change in progress? A Kafka consumer service being upgraded while its upstream topic is experiencing a partition rebalance is a compounding failure scenario. The RFC must address it.

Key upstream questions:
- What happens if the upstream service restarts during our change window?
- What happens if upstream traffic doubles during our change window (another team's release, a business event)?
- Is there a shared queue or cache between us and the upstream that could be affected by our change?

### Downstream: What Depends on the Changed Service

Downstream dependencies are where blast radius lives. If the change causes degradation — even brief, even partial — which downstream services cascade? At what rate? With what customer-visible impact?

For RabbitMQ topology changes: every consumer service is downstream. A queue name change, a binding modification, or a prefetch count adjustment ripples through every consumer in the topology. Map them all.

For Kafka changes: partition reassignment or topic configuration changes affect consumer group offsets and consumer throughput. Every consumer group subscribed to the affected topics is in the blast radius.

For Kubernetes changes (EKS cluster config, node group changes, RBAC modifications): every workload running in the cluster is potentially downstream. Scope it to the specific namespace or workload if the change is scoped — but confirm the scope claim is accurate.

### Customer Journey Coverage

Which customer-facing journeys traverse the changed component? This is the question that connects technical impact to business impact.

At T-Mobile context:
- A change to the authentication service touches every customer journey — literally every flow that requires a logged-in state. Blast radius is the entire authenticated customer base. The RFC must reflect this and the maintenance window must be chosen accordingly.
- A change to the voicemail transcription service touches only customers using visual voicemail and only for that feature. Blast radius is scoped and the RFC scope should be scoped accordingly.

Overclaiming blast radius (saying everything is affected when it is not) creates false urgency and erodes trust in impact assessments. Underclaiming blast radius (saying the scope is narrow when it is not) creates surprise failures. Accurate blast radius assessment is a skill that improves with practice and degrades when teams stop doing it systematically.

### The Two-Hops Rule

For most changes: assess direct dependencies (one hop) and their direct dependencies (two hops). Stop there. The marginal information from three hops and beyond is usually low relative to the effort.

Exceptions that require full-depth assessment:
- Changes to DNS resolution infrastructure
- Changes to IAM/RBAC systems (authorization changes propagate unpredictably)
- Changes to core network routing or service mesh configuration
- Changes to shared platform databases or caches (Redis clusters, shared PostgreSQL instances)

For these, full depth is not optional. A misconfigured CoreDNS change in an EKS cluster can make every service in every namespace unable to resolve endpoints. Two hops is insufficient.

## 3. Rollback Plan Requirements

The rollback plan is either specific or it is worthless.

"Revert the change" is not a rollback plan. It is the absence of a rollback plan with the word "plan" added to make it look like one.

The CAB chair who accepts "revert the change" as a rollback plan is accepting that nobody has thought about what rollback actually involves for this specific change. They are approving a change with an unknown recovery time, unknown data state after rollback, and unknown blast radius of the rollback itself.

### Elements of a Valid Rollback Plan

**Trigger criteria**: Specific, observable, measurable. Not "if something goes wrong" — that phrase belongs in a project kickoff document, not a production change plan.

Good trigger criteria: "Execute rollback if: (a) error rate on the `auth-service` exceeds 5% as measured in Datadog for 3 consecutive minutes after step 4, OR (b) step 5 has not completed within 45 minutes of start (estimated duration 30 min × 1.5 buffer), OR (c) the on-call engineer observes any downstream service alerting on elevated 5xx rates during the change window."

**Rollback steps**: Numbered. Owner-assigned. Same granularity as the implementation steps. If implementation has eight steps, rollback likely has five to eight steps of its own. Rollback step 1 is not "undo step 8" — it is a specific action with a specific expected outcome.

**Estimated rollback duration**: Not an estimate based on feel — a measured duration from staging. If the rollback has never been timed in staging, the rollback plan has never been tested. An untested rollback plan is a theoretical rollback plan. Theoretical rollback plans fail under pressure.

**Data integrity consideration**: This is the section most rollback plans skip, and it is the section that produces the worst production incidents.

For any change involving:
- Database schema migrations: what is the data state after rollback? Records written after the migration began and before rollback completes — are they readable in the pre-migration schema? Are they lost? Are they corrupted? The RFC must answer this explicitly.
- Queue topology changes (RabbitMQ, Kafka): messages in-flight during the change window — what happens to them on rollback? Are they re-queued? Are they lost? Are they duplicated?
- Configuration changes: what is the service state immediately after rollback? Are there cached values that need to be flushed?

**Rollback owner**: Named person with confirmed availability. Not the on-call rotation in general — a specific engineer who has reviewed the rollback plan and can execute it without looking it up.

### Rollback Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|-------------|-------------|
| "Rollback not applicable" | Almost never true. Every change can be assessed for rollback complexity. If rollback is genuinely impossible (irreversible data transformation), that must be documented and accepted explicitly, not declared not applicable. |
| Rollback duration exceeds maintenance window | If the change window is 90 minutes and rollback takes 120 minutes, the team will be executing rollback in a production outage during business hours. This is a change design problem. |
| Rollback plan never tested in staging | Untested rollback plans fail at the worst time. Require staging rollback execution evidence the same way you require staging implementation evidence. |
| Rollback that is more complex than implementation | This is a sign the change needs to be broken into smaller phases. Each phase should have a simple, fast rollback. |

> **Director Callout**: If the rollback plan for a proposed change is substantially more complex than the implementation plan, the change design is wrong. Break it into phases where each phase is independently rollback-able. "We cannot roll back step 5 without also rolling back steps 3 and 4" is a change sequencing problem, not an acceptable constraint.

## 4. Communication Plan for Significant Changes

Every P1 and P2 change requires a communication plan as part of the RFC. The communication plan answers: who is notified, when, through what channel, and with what content.

### Stakeholder Notification Matrix

| Stakeholder | When Notified | Channel | Content |
|-------------|---------------|---------|---------|
| Customers (if customer-facing impact expected) | 24–48 hours before window | Status page, in-app banner | Maintenance window, expected impact, estimated duration |
| Customer-facing teams (support, CX, retail) | 48 hours before window | Email + Teams/Slack | Script for customer inquiries, expected impact description, escalation path |
| On-call engineers (primary + backup) | 72 hours before window | Direct notification + PagerDuty schedule confirmation | Change plan summary, rollback trigger criteria, who to contact |
| Downstream service owners | 72 hours before window | Email + direct Slack message | Your service is downstream of [X]. During the window [Y-Z], expect [behavior]. Here is what to do if you observe [specific symptom]. |
| Engineering leadership | 1 week before (for P1 changes) | Email briefing | Business justification, customer impact, risk tier, contingency plan |
| Operations NOC/bridge | Day of, 30 min before window | Phone bridge + ticketing system update | Change is starting, expected duration, what to watch, escalation contact |

**During the change**:
- P1 changes: dedicated Slack/Teams channel (#change-CHG-XXXXX). Status updates every 30 minutes. Go/no-go decision published to the channel after each major step.
- P2 changes: updates at the start of the window, at 50% completion, and at completion. Rollback notification if triggered.
- Rollback triggered: immediate notification to all stakeholders via the notification matrix, with estimated rollback completion time and next steps.

**After the change**:
- Success: notification to all stakeholders within 1 hour of completion. Status page updated if it was modified. Change ticket updated with actual vs. planned execution time (this data feeds future planning).
- Failure with rollback: notification to all stakeholders within 15 minutes of rollback completion. PIR scheduled within 24 hours. Preliminary root cause in the ticket within 2 hours.

> **Director Decision Point**: At T-Mobile scale, automated communication is essential. Manual status updates are error-prone and delayed. Invest in change management tooling that automates stakeholder notifications based on the communication plan in the RFC. This is not a luxury — it is a reliability investment that pays back in incident reduction.

## 5. Change Windows: Definition, Approval, and Exceptions

### Standard Change Windows by Risk Tier

| Risk Tier | Standard Window | Rationale |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| P1 (Critical impact) | 2:00 AM – 6:00 AM local time for primary customer region | Verified low-traffic window based on traffic telemetry. Maximize recovery time before business-hours traffic. |
| P2 (Major impact) | Overnight (10 PM – 6 AM) or Saturday 10 PM – Sunday 6 AM | Reduced traffic, full SRE team availability for escalation during business hours |
| P3 (Minor impact) | Business hours with team on standby | Impact is scoped. Team availability for immediate response is prioritized over traffic volume. |
| P4 (Minimal impact) | Any time | Minimal customer impact justifies flexibility. |

**Low traffic defined**: For a 24/7 telecom operation like T-Mobile, "low traffic" does not mean "no traffic." It means below 15% of 30-day peak volume for the affected customer journey. Pull this from Splunk before selecting a window. Intuition about traffic patterns in telecom is unreliable. Network traffic, authentication events, and API call volume follow patterns that differ from retail or banking.

### Window Governance

The change calendar is managed by the Release Manager. No two P1 or P2 changes affecting the same service or shared infrastructure may share the same maintenance window unless explicitly coordinated in the RFC (joint change plan, single implementation team, single rollback decision point).

Conflict resolution: when two teams submit conflicting windows, the CAB Chair resolves via risk tier priority (higher risk tier gets the preferred window) and then by submission order (first submitted gets priority). Teams notified immediately.

### Window Exception Approval

A window exception is required when a P1 or P2 change must be implemented outside its standard window (e.g., a P2 change during business hours for a customer-impacting security patch with an active exploit in the wild). Exception requires:
- Director approval (documented in the change ticket)
- Customer communication plan filed before the window opens
- On-call team confirmed available for the full exception window
- Business justification for why the standard window is not viable

Exception approval does not bypass CAB review. The change still goes through CAB. The exception is to the timing, not to the review process.

## 6. Peer Review Requirements Before CAB Submission

Every RFC requires a peer review by an engineer who did not write the change before CAB submission. Not a manager review, not a verbal walkthrough — a documented technical review by a peer who can evaluate the implementation steps and rollback plan independently.

### Peer Review Scope

The peer reviewer is responsible for attesting to:
- Technical accuracy of implementation steps (would these steps actually produce the described outcome?)
- Rollback plan credibility (could this rollback succeed in the estimated timeframe?)
- Test evidence completeness (does this evidence actually validate the change?)
- Blast radius coverage (are the downstream dependencies identified accurate and complete?)

The peer reviewer is not responsible for assessing business justification or approving the change — that is CAB's role. The peer reviewer's job is to catch technical errors before the RFC reaches CAB.

### When Architect Review Is Required

Architecture review required for:
- Changes to shared infrastructure (EKS cluster configuration, Kafka cluster topology, RabbitMQ cluster configuration, shared database schemas)
- Platform-level components that are dependencies for 3 or more services
- Any change that introduces a new architectural pattern not currently in use in the production environment
- Migrations from one major version to another for any middleware platform

Architecture review is not a bottleneck — it is time-boxed. The architect has 48 hours to review and respond. No response within 48 hours = architecture review is escalated to the CAB Chair for decision.

### When Security Review Is Required

Security review required for any change touching:
- Authentication or authorization logic
- Network boundaries (new ingress rules, new VPC peering, firewall rule modifications)
- Encryption configuration (TLS versions, cipher suites, certificate management)
- Customer data handling (access controls, data retention, data export)
- Any integration with a new external system or third-party API

Security review is non-negotiable. A CAB that approves a change touching authentication without a security sign-off is accepting liability that belongs in the security organization's court.

### Enforcement

An RFC submitted to CAB without a documented peer review is returned to the submitter by the CAB Chair before the meeting. It is not added to the CAB agenda. The change owner is notified: "This RFC has not completed peer review. Resubmit with peer review documentation. Next CAB opportunity is [date]."

The consequence of returning incomplete RFCs is that submitters learn quickly to complete the process before submission. The cost of a CAB meeting spent on an incomplete RFC is paid by every member of the CAB — engineer time that is used for risk review, not for investigating RFC completeness.

## 7. Change Calendar Governance

The change calendar is the single source of truth for all planned production changes. It is not a view in a Jira board, not a spreadsheet in SharePoint, not a mental model held by the Release Manager. It is a governed, accessible, authoritative calendar.

### Calendar Requirements

Every entry must include:
- Change ID (links to RFC in the change management system)
- Service(s) affected (primary and secondary)
- Maintenance window (start, end, timezone)
- Change owner
- Risk tier
- CAB approval date and decision record ID
- Peer reviewer name

The calendar must be:
- Accessible to all engineering teams (read access) to enable self-service conflict checking before RFC submission
- Write-controlled (Release Manager and CAB Chair only add/modify entries)
- Integrated with the change management tooling (ServiceNow, Jira, or equivalent) for automated conflict detection

### Automated Conflict Detection

The change management tool should flag automatically when:
- Two changes affecting the same service have overlapping windows
- A change is submitted for a declared freeze period
- A change window falls within 72 hours of a major business event on the business calendar (earnings announcements, product launches, T-Mobile Tuesdays, Super Bowl — high-traffic events are documented)

Conflict flags are reviewed by the Release Manager and resolved before the CAB meeting. The CAB is not the place to discover window conflicts.

### Freeze Period Enforcement

Freeze periods are declared in the change calendar in advance. The change management tooling enforces the freeze at RFC submission stage: any RFC submitted for a window that falls within a freeze period is automatically returned with the freeze notification. No manual enforcement required, no change owner "not knowing" about a freeze.

Typical freeze periods:
- End-of-quarter business close periods
- Holiday peak traffic seasons (for consumer-facing services: Thanksgiving through New Year's; for T-Mobile: T-Mobile Tuesdays)
- 72 hours surrounding a major product launch
- Post-incident stability periods (declared by the Director after a Severity 1 incident: "No P1 or P2 changes for 48 hours while we stabilize")

### Director Weekly Scan

Ten minutes before the weekly CAB meeting: scan the upcoming two-week change calendar. Flag anything that looks anomalous:
- Changes with no peer review documented
- Changes scheduled during business events that should have been flagged
- Multiple high-risk changes scheduled in the same week without coordination
- Services with a recent change failure history that have another change scheduled without a PIR completed

This scan is not a deep technical review — it is the Director applying pattern recognition to the calendar before the team spends CAB time on individual RFCs. Surface the anomalies early, not during RFC review.

> **Director Decision Point**: The change calendar is a governance document. If your teams treat it as optional administrative overhead rather than as a coordination tool, you have a culture problem, not a process problem. The way to resolve it is not to make the calendar mandatory through enforcement — it is to make it useful. A change calendar that accurately shows conflicts, freeze periods, and upcoming high-risk windows is one that teams consult voluntarily. Build toward usefulness.
