# Chapter 03: Change Advisory Board (CAB)

**Audience**: SRE Manager / Director / VP of Operations  
**Lens**: You set the governance model. Engineering executes inside it.

---

## 1. CAB Purpose: Risk Review, Not Approval Theater

The CAB has one job: surface the risks the change owner is too close to see, validate that the rollback plan is credible, confirm the blast radius assessment is realistic, and verify the maintenance window is appropriate. That is the entire mandate.

The CAB is a structured risk review, not a sign-off ceremony.

What CAB is NOT:
- A bureaucratic gate that proves process compliance without adding safety
- A leadership sign-off session where executives approve changes they have not read
- A rubber-stamp body for changes that are already partially implemented or that someone already promised to a customer
- A place where the change owner verbally explains the RFC to members who are seeing it for the first time

**CAB failure pattern — Approval Theater**: The meeting starts. The RFC is displayed for the first time. The change owner walks the room through each section verbally. Questions are generic ("looks good to me"). Approvals happen in the last five minutes by people who have not reviewed the document. Risks are not challenged. Rollback plans are vague. The CAB chair hits "Approve" in ServiceNow and the meeting ends. This is not governance. This is liability theater — it creates the appearance of oversight without delivering any of the safety.

The way to identify approval theater: look at your approval/defer/reject ratios. A CAB that never defers or rejects is not doing risk review. It is processing paperwork.

> **What the VP Asks**: "Show me our CAB approval, deferral, and rejection rates for the last 90 days, broken down by risk tier. If the rejection rate for P2 changes is zero, walk me through why."

**The Director's framing**: The CAB exists to catch the things the change owner is too close to see. Proximity bias is real — the engineer who has been working on a change for three weeks cannot see it clearly anymore. The CAB provides the outside view. If your CAB is not rejecting or deferring 10–15% of changes across a rolling 30-day window, it is not doing its job. That is not a target to game — it is a diagnostic signal. A 0% deferral rate means either every RFC submitted to your CAB is flawlessly prepared (statistically improbable) or your CAB members are not reading the RFCs before the meeting.

## 2. CAB Composition

### Who Must Be There

| Role | Responsibility in CAB |
|------|----------------------|
| CAB Chair (Director or designated SRE Lead) | Owns the meeting, enforces RFC quality standards, records decisions, final tie-breaker |
| Release Manager | Change calendar integrity, maintenance window conflicts, release train visibility |
| Domain SME for affected system | Technical accuracy of implementation steps, blast radius validation, rollback credibility |
| Security Representative | Required for any change touching authentication, authorization, encryption, network boundaries, or customer data handling |

**Quorum rule**: Minimum three members, including the CAB Chair and at least one domain SME. Without quorum, all changes defer to the next cycle. No exceptions. Changes approved without quorum are not approved — they are deferred and the submitting team needs to understand that.

### Who Should Be There (Situational)

- **Platform/Infrastructure Lead**: for any change to shared infrastructure (EKS cluster config, Kafka cluster, RabbitMQ topology, DNS, load balancer configuration)
- **DBA**: for any change involving schema migration, index modification, or data transformation in production
- **On-call team lead**: whoever will own the pager during the change window should understand what is being implemented and what rollback looks like

### Who Should Never Be There

- Executives who will not read the RFC before the meeting and are present for organizational visibility
- Representatives from teams with no dependency on the changed system
- Anyone attending because they were CCed on an email chain and showed up by default

CAB meeting time is expensive. Every person in that room who has not read the RFC before the meeting is a drag on the process, not a contribution to it.

### At T-Mobile Scale: Subcommittee Structure

At 200+ services with weekly change volumes that can reach hundreds of RFCs, a single monolithic CAB is not scalable. The governance model at this scale:

- **Domain CABs** (Network, Platform, Application, Data): review changes within their domain weekly. Each domain CAB is chaired by a Principal SRE or Senior Manager.
- **Enterprise CAB**: reviews P1 and cross-domain changes. Chaired by the Director. Meets weekly; emergency session available within 1 hour for Severity 1 risk changes.
- **Escalation criteria**: any change with estimated customer impact > 5% of active user base, any change affecting more than three domains simultaneously, any schema change to a platform-level database

Domain CAB chairs are accountable to the Director. If a domain CAB is rubber-stamping changes, the Director sees it in the metrics.

## 3. Change Submission Requirements: What the RFC Must Contain

The RFC is the change owner's proof of readiness — not the CAB's job to fill in the gaps. Every section of the RFC is the change owner's responsibility to complete accurately. The CAB's job is to challenge and validate, not to discover.

### Required RFC Sections

1. **Change Summary**: One paragraph, non-technical, readable by any stakeholder. What is changing, why, and what happens if it does not.

2. **Business Justification**: What customer or business outcome does this change enable? Changes without a business justification are candidates for "why are we doing this at all?"

3. **Technical Description**: Precise. Which services, which configurations, which code paths, which infrastructure components are changing. "Update the service" is not a technical description.

4. **Scope and Blast Radius**: Primary services affected. Secondary services affected via dependency (two hops minimum). Environments. Customer segments. Estimated customer impact if the change fails mid-execution.

5. **Risk Assessment**: Impact score (Low/Medium/High) × Likelihood score (Low/Medium/High) = risk tier. List of mitigating factors already in place that reduce either score.

6. **Implementation Steps**: Numbered. Owner assigned per step. Estimated duration per step. Go/no-go verification criteria at each step ("after step 3, confirm [specific metric] returns to baseline before proceeding to step 4").

7. **Rollback Plan**: Specific trigger criteria (not "if something goes wrong"). Step-by-step rollback procedure with the same granularity as implementation steps. Estimated rollback duration. Data integrity considerations. Named rollback owner.

8. **Test Evidence**: Staging test results with pass/fail. Load test results if applicable. Unit test coverage percentage for changed code paths. Chaos test results if applicable. Screenshots or log excerpts, not assertions.

9. **Maintenance Window Request**: Start time, end time, timezone, primary and backup window. Justification for timing (traffic data, not preference).

10. **Approval Chain**: Peer reviewer name and date. Architect sign-off if required. Security sign-off if required. CAB submission timestamp.

### Enforcement

Incomplete RFC = automatic deferral. The CAB Chair is responsible for reviewing RFC completeness before the meeting, not during it. A CAB that spends meeting time discovering missing RFC sections has a submission process problem.

> **Director Callout**: If your teams are consistently submitting incomplete RFCs, you have one of two problems: a training gap (teams do not know what a complete RFC looks like) or a template gap (the RFC template does not make the requirements clear enough). Check `templates/rfc-template.md`. If teams skip required sections, track it as a process adherence issue. Address it in 1:1s with the team leads, not in a CAB meeting. The CAB meeting is not the right venue for teaching RFC quality.

## 4. CAB Meeting Cadence

**Weekly standard CAB**: Fixed day, fixed time, same week every week. Cadence predictability is a forcing function for change planning. Teams that know the CAB meets every Tuesday at 10 AM will plan their RFC submissions accordingly. Unpredictable CAB schedules create pressure to use the Emergency CAB process, which inflates emergency change rates artificially.

**RFC submission deadline**: Five business days before the CAB meeting. This gives CAB members three days to pre-read RFCs and submit clarifying questions asynchronously before the meeting. Clarifying questions get answered before the meeting starts. The meeting is for decisions, not for discovery.

**Pre-CAB async review**: All RFCs distributed to CAB members the day they are submitted (Day 0). Async comment window closes 24 hours before the meeting (Day 4). Meeting time is for decisions that require synchronous discussion — not for reading RFCs aloud.

**Emergency CAB convening**: Triggered by CAB Chair within 1 hour of request. Minimum attendance: CAB Chair + one domain SME with authority to assess blast radius. Async approval from remaining required members acceptable within 2 hours of Emergency CAB conclusion. Emergency CAB decisions require retrospective review at the next standard CAB (was this genuinely an emergency, or did process failure create artificial urgency?).

### Standard CAB Meeting Structure (60 minutes)

| Block | Duration | Content |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Status review | 5 min | Changes implemented since last CAB — did any fail? PIR status. |
| RFC review | ~50 min | Priority order: P1 impact first, then P2, then P3/P4. Max 10 min per RFC. |
| Calendar review | 5 min | Upcoming freeze dates, high-risk business events, conflict flags. |

No RFC gets unlimited discussion. If a decision cannot be made in 10 minutes, the RFC is deferred — not debated further. Deferral means the RFC needs more work, not more meeting time.

## 5. Decision Framework: Approve / Approve with Conditions / Defer / Reject

### Approve

RFC is complete. Risk assessment is realistic. Rollback plan is specific and credible. Maintenance window is appropriate. Implementation steps are detailed enough that a different engineer could execute them.

### Approve with Conditions

RFC is approved pending resolution of specific, named conditions before implementation begins. Conditions must be binary (either the condition is met or it is not):
- "Approved pending staging test results for error scenario X — results must be attached to this ticket before the change window opens"
- "Approved pending security team sign-off on the new API endpoint added in step 4"
- "Approved pending confirmation from the DBA that the index migration rollback time is within the 90-minute maintenance window"

Conditions without binary verification criteria are not conditions — they are wishes.

### Defer

RFC is not ready. The change approach may be correct but the submission is insufficient, the risk is underestimated, the rollback plan is vague, or the maintenance window is wrong. Owner must resubmit. Deferral reason must be documented specifically enough that the owner can fix it without ambiguity.

Bad deferral reason: "Rollback plan needs work."  
Good deferral reason: "Rollback plan does not address data integrity for the 15-minute window between step 3 (schema migration) and step 5 (application deployment). Data written in that window may not be readable after rollback. Owner must document the data state and whether rollback is destructive to that data before resubmission."

### Reject

The change approach itself is wrong — not the timing, not the RFC quality, but the approach. Rejection is rare (a CAB with a high rejection rate has an upstream problem with how changes are scoped before they reach RFC stage). Every rejection must include a path forward:

"This change as designed deploys directly to 100% of production instances without a staged rollout. Given the blast radius (downstream dependency on six customer-facing services), the risk tier is unacceptable. Recommend phased approach: canary to 1% → 5% → 25% → 100%, with automated health gate between phases. Resubmit with progressive delivery design."

### CAB Decision Record

Every decision is recorded with rationale. Not just the outcome:

| Field | Value |

## 6. Virtual CAB vs. In-Person: When Asynchronous Approval Works

Synchronous CAB time is a constrained resource. Use it for decisions that require synchronous discussion. Everything else should be async.

| Change Tier | CAB Mode | Mechanism |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| Standard Changes (pre-approved patterns) | No CAB needed | Automated pipeline gate, no human CAB review |
| P4 Normal Changes | Async | RFC submitted to Jira/ServiceNow, tagged reviewers, 48-hour comment window, auto-approved if no objections |
| P3 Normal Changes | Async | Same as P4, 72-hour window, CAB Chair must explicitly approve |
| P2 Normal Changes | Hybrid | RFC distributed async, decision made synchronously in weekly CAB meeting |
| P1 Normal Changes | Synchronous | Always synchronous, always recorded, always requires explicit CAB Chair approval |
| Emergency Changes | Emergency CAB | Synchronous with minimum quorum, retrospective at next standard CAB |

The Director decision here: move to async-default for Standard Changes and P4. Reserve synchronous CAB time for P2 and P1. This is how you scale CAB governance from 20 changes per month to 200+ changes per month without increasing the meeting burden proportionally. The scaling lever is the Standard Change catalog — the more well-understood, low-risk changes you have pre-approved patterns for, the less synchronous CAB time you need.

## 7. What Good CAB Failure Looks Like

Failure modes that are easy to miss because they look like process compliance:

**Rubber-stamping**: A 0% rejection and 0% deferral rate over any 30-day period is statistically impossible with real risk review at any meaningful change volume. If you see this, sit in on a CAB meeting without announcing that you're doing a process audit. Observe how much time is spent on each RFC, whether rollback plans are challenged, whether blast radius claims are verified.

**Approving without reading**: RFCs submitted at the start of the meeting, approved 10 minutes later. The only way to approve a complex RFC in 10 minutes is to not read it. Look at the timestamp between RFC submission and CAB decision in your ticketing system. If P2 changes are approved within minutes, you have a rubber-stamp problem.

**Vague rollback plan acceptance**: Rollback plans that say "revert the change" or "contact the on-call team" are not rollback plans. If your CAB is accepting these, the CAB is not doing its job. Every rollback plan should answer: revert how? How long does revert take? What happens to data created after the change went in? Who executes the rollback?

**Emergency change overuse**: If more than 15–20% of your changes go through the Emergency CAB process, your normal process is broken — either the change windows are too infrequent, the submission deadline is too long, or teams have learned that Emergency CAB is faster and easier than the standard process. Track emergency change frequency monthly and investigate any spike.

**Missing feedback loops**: The same service fails its change three consecutive times. The CAB approves each change individually without connecting the pattern. Change quality by team and service is not tracked. Post-implementation reviews are not feeding back into CAB decision criteria. You are approving future failures without learning from past ones.

## 8. Director/VP CAB Chair Responsibilities

### Before the Meeting

- Review all submitted RFCs for completeness. Return incomplete RFCs to owners before the meeting — do not let the meeting discover missing sections.
- Flag any RFC that warrants deeper pre-read. Send a note to the relevant SME: "CHG-08821 — Kafka partition reassignment touching the order processing topic. Please pre-read the blast radius section specifically."
- Check the change calendar for conflicts with the proposed windows.
- Confirm domain SME attendance. A CAB meeting without the right SME for a P1 change is a meeting that should not happen.

### During the Meeting

- Timekeeper: maximum 10 minutes per RFC. If a decision cannot be reached, defer. Deferral is not failure — it is the process working.
- Risk challenger: ask the questions the change owner hopes nobody asks:
  - "What happens if step 3 takes three times longer than estimated? Is the maintenance window still viable?"
  - "You have downstream dependency on the payment service. Is the payment team aware and on standby?"
  - "Your rollback plan says 22 minutes. Have you timed it in staging? What is the actual measured time?"
  - "What is the customer-visible impact if you execute rollback at step 5?"
- Decision recorder: every decision recorded in the ticketing system before the meeting ends. Not "Approved" — the full decision record with rationale and conditions.

### After the Meeting

- Change calendar updated with approved windows within 24 hours of the meeting.
- Approval/deferral/rejection notifications sent to change owners with specific guidance.
- Deferred/rejected owners have a clear path to resubmission: what needs to change, what the resubmission deadline is.
- Emergency change retrospectives scheduled for any Emergency CABs held since the last standard meeting.

### Director-Level CAB Metrics (Monthly Report)

| Metric | Target | Action Threshold |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| Change volume | Trending baseline | > 20% spike warrants investigation |
| Approval rate | 70–80% | > 90% = rubber-stamping risk |
| Deferral rate | 10–15% | < 5% = CAB not challenging adequately |
| Rejection rate | 2–5% | 0% over 60 days = upstream scoping problem |
| Emergency change rate | < 10% of total | > 20% = normal process broken |
| Change failure rate (for CAB-approved changes) | < 10% | > 15% = CAB not catching blast radius or rollback quality issues |
| PIR completion rate for failed changes | 100% | < 80% = learning loop broken |

The CAB metrics report goes to your peers (other Director/VP-level leaders) and your manager. It is not an internal SRE document — it is a governance accountability artifact.

> **What the VP Asks in QBR**: "Last quarter we had 340 changes through the standard CAB. 28 failed post-implementation. Of those 28, how many had incomplete rollback plans that we approved anyway? How many had blast radius assessments that we accepted but that turned out to be wrong? What did we change in our review criteria as a result?"
