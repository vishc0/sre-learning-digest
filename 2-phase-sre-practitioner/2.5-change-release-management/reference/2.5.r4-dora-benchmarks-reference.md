# DORA Benchmarks Reference
## 2023 State of DevOps Report

---

## 1. What DORA Is and Why These Four Metrics

**DORA** (DevOps Research and Assessment) is a research program originally founded by Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, and Gene Kim. It was acquired by Google in 2018 and now publishes as part of the Google Cloud DevOps team. The annual *State of DevOps Report* is the largest longitudinal study of software delivery performance — 2023 represents the tenth year of data collection across tens of thousands of organizations.

**Why these four metrics matter**: DORA's core finding is that software delivery performance is measurable, predictive, and improvable. The four metrics — Deployment Frequency (DF), Lead Time for Changes (LTC), Change Failure Rate (CFR), and Mean Time to Restore (MTTR) — were selected because they predict organizational outcomes: profitability, market share, employee satisfaction, and customer satisfaction. Organizations that score Elite on these metrics outperform their peers on every business outcome DORA tracks.

**Why a change manager cares**: Three of the four metrics are directly controlled by change management process. CFR is the direct output of change quality. MTTR is controlled by incident and rollback process. LTC is partially controlled by CAB review cycle time. A change management program that improves these three metrics also improves the organization's DORA tier.

---

## 2. The Four Metrics — 2023 Benchmark Values

### Deployment Frequency (DF)

How often does the organization successfully deploy code to production?

| Tier | Benchmark |
|------|-----------|
| Elite | Multiple deploys per day |
| High | Between once per day and once per week |
| Medium | Between once per week and once per month |
| Low | Less than once per month (DORA 2021 found some Low performers deploy less than once every six months) |

### Lead Time for Changes (LTC)

How long does it take for a committed code change to reach production?

| Tier | Benchmark |
|------|-----------|
| Elite | Less than one hour |
| High | Between one day and one week |
| Medium | Between one week and one month |
| Low | Between one month and six months |

### Change Failure Rate (CFR)

What percentage of deployments to production result in a degraded service requiring hotfix, rollback, or incident response?

| Tier | Benchmark |
|------|-----------|
| Elite | 0–5% |
| High | 5–15% |
| Medium | 15–45% |
| Low | 46% or higher |

### Mean Time to Restore (MTTR)

How long does it take to restore service when a failure occurs?

*(For change management purposes: MTTR measured specifically for change-induced failures, not all incidents.)*

| Tier | Benchmark |
|------|-----------|
| Elite | Less than one hour |
| High | Less than one day |
| Medium | Less than one week |
| Low | More than one week |

## 3. What Each Tier Looks Like Operationally

### Elite

**The organization**: Deploys to production tens or hundreds of times per day per team. Engineers commit small changes frequently. Production deployments are unremarkable events — not scheduled maintenance windows, not cross-team coordination exercises. Rollback is automated or a single command. Monitoring catches failure within minutes.

**Change management profile**: Most deployments are Standard Changes or bypass traditional CAB entirely in favor of automated quality gates (test coverage, static analysis, canary health checks) built into the pipeline. CAB exists for infrastructure, shared platform, and compliance-required changes. The CAB review cycle is days, not weeks.

**Examples**: Google, Netflix, Amazon (internal tools). In telecom: some individual product teams at operators that have adopted trunk-based development and progressive delivery, though few operators have achieved Elite organization-wide.

---

### High

**The organization**: Deploys multiple times per week. Changes are grouped into releases but releases are small (days of work, not weeks). Deployment is planned but not ceremonial — team-level coordination, not company-wide change windows. Rollback is documented and tested. MTTR is hours.

**Change management profile**: Normal CAB for most production changes. Standard Change catalog exists and is actively maintained. Emergency Changes are uncommon (less than 5% of all changes). PIR completion rate is high. DORA metrics are measured but not all teams have achieved High tier individually.

**Realistic target**: This is where a strong Director/VP of Operations drives their organization within 12–18 months of a deliberate improvement program.

---

### Medium

**The organization**: Deploys weekly or monthly. Releases bundle multiple features. Change windows are scheduled and often lengthy. CAB reviews are lengthy and document-heavy. Emergency Changes are more common than they should be (often 10–20% of all changes). MTTR is days.

**Change management profile**: RFC process exists but is often seen as bureaucratic overhead. Peer review quality is inconsistent. Standard Change catalog is sparse or out of date. PIR completion rate is below 70%. Engineering teams bypass change process when they can, creating audit gaps. DORA metrics may not be formally measured.

**Common plateau**: 2023 DORA found that Medium is where many organizations plateau — they have invested in DevOps practices but have not driven the organizational changes needed to reach High.

---

### Low

**The organization**: Deploys rarely (quarterly or less). Releases are large, high-risk events. Change windows span weekends. Rollback is theorized but rarely tested. MTTR is weeks — a major incident may take a week to diagnose, patch, validate, and deploy a fix. Engineering and operations are often separate organizations with adversarial dynamics.

**Change management profile**: Change process is either absent (undocumented heroics) or so heavy (multiple weeks of review for any change) that it drives shadow IT and change process evasion. Emergency Changes are high — teams declare emergency to bypass the slow Normal process.

**2023 finding**: Low performers are more common than Medium performers. The DORA distribution is bimodal — organizations tend to be either doing well or doing poorly, with fewer in the middle than the research team expected.

## 4. The Stability-Velocity Insight

**This is the single most important finding from DORA research for change management programs.**

The intuitive assumption — which many operations organizations operate on — is that deploying more frequently means more risk: more changes, more chances for failure, more incidents.

**DORA data consistently disproves this.** Elite performers simultaneously achieve:
- The highest deployment frequency (most changes going to production)
- The lowest change failure rate (fewest of those changes causing problems)

The mechanism: small, frequent, well-tested changes are less risky than large, infrequent, complex releases. A large release bundles many changes together. When it fails, diagnosis is difficult (which of the 47 changes caused this?), rollback is complex (we cannot roll back just the broken part), and MTTR is long.

**For a change manager, the implication is direct**: A change management program that reduces deployment frequency to reduce risk is counterproductive. The goal is not fewer changes — it is smaller, better-tested, faster-rollback changes. The program should optimize for quality and reversibility, not for volume reduction.

**The CFR-DF correlation**: Elite performers achieve both high DF and low CFR not because they have lower standards but because they have automated quality gates (test coverage, canary analysis, health check gates) that catch failures before they reach all customers. The CAB review is supplemented or replaced by technical controls that are faster and more consistent than human review of large change documents.

## 5. How to Measure Each Metric

### Deployment Frequency

**Definition**: Count of successful deployments to production per service per time period.

**Formula**: `DF = count(successful production deployments) / time period`

**Data sources**: CI/CD pipeline (GitLab, GitHub Actions, Jenkins, Azure DevOps) — deployment events tagged with environment=production and status=success. Change management tool (ServiceNow, JSM) — RFC records with status=Implemented.

**Common instrumentation gaps**:
- Manual deployments not captured in CI/CD pipelines — these inflate actual frequency but are invisible to the metric
- Rollbacks counted as deployments — a rollback is not a deployment; tag separately
- Counting by commit rather than deployment — a commit that never reaches production inflates the metric

**Measurement frequency**: Daily or weekly per team. Monthly aggregate for organization report.

**Director review cadence**: Monthly trend per team. Quarterly comparison against DORA tier targets.

---

### Lead Time for Changes

**Definition**: Time from a code commit being merged to main/trunk to that commit successfully running in production.

**Formula**: `LTC = deployment timestamp − merge timestamp` (median across all deployments in the period)

**Data sources**: Git platform (merge timestamp) + CI/CD pipeline (production deployment timestamp). Requires linking commit SHA through pipeline to deployment event.

**Common instrumentation gaps**:
- Measuring from "code complete" rather than "merge to main" — inflates LTC by including review/approval time, which is correct; do not exclude it
- Measuring only emergency changes — cherry-picked high-urgency changes have artificially short LTC; measure all changes
- Not accounting for queued CAB review time — if a change sits waiting for next CAB meeting, that wait time is included in LTC and correctly counts against the metric

**Measurement frequency**: Per deployment event. Report median (not mean — outliers from blocked deployments distort the mean).

**Director review cadence**: Monthly, broken down by team and change type. Track separately: LTC for Standard Changes vs. Normal CAB changes vs. Emergency Changes. The delta shows where process is adding latency.

---

### Change Failure Rate

**Definition**: Percentage of production deployments that result in a service degradation requiring remediation.

**Formula**: `CFR = count(deployments requiring hotfix, rollback, or incident) / count(total production deployments) × 100`

**Data sources**: Change management tool (rollback flag in RFC post-implementation section) + Incident management tool (incidents tagged with root cause = recent change). Requires a defined window for "attributable to a change" — standard is any incident opened within 24 hours of a deployment.

**Common instrumentation gaps**:
- Under-counting: incidents not linked to changes in the ITSM tool — requires disciplined RCA process that traces incident root cause back to change records
- Over-counting: counting all incidents during a deployment window rather than only those caused by the change
- Not counting rollbacks as failures — a rollback is by definition a failed change; it must count in CFR

**Measurement frequency**: Per sprint or monthly. Require a minimum of 20 deployments in the period for the metric to be statistically meaningful.

**Director review cadence**: Monthly per team. Quarterly trend. Teams with CFR above 15% (above High tier) should have a dedicated improvement review in that quarter.

---

### Mean Time to Restore (MTTR)

*(For change management purposes: measured specifically for change-induced failures.)*

**Definition**: Time from detection of a change-induced service degradation to full restoration of service.

**Formula**: `MTTR = sum(restoration time − detection time) / count(change-induced incidents)`

**Data sources**: Incident management tool (incident open timestamp, incident resolve timestamp, root cause = change). Monitoring system (alert fire timestamp for detection, if more precise than incident open time).

**Common instrumentation gaps**:
- Using incident open time rather than detection time — if alert fired 10 minutes before someone opened the incident, MTTR is understated by 10 minutes; use alert timestamp where available
- Measuring resolution rather than restoration — restoration is when customers are unaffected; resolution is when the incident record is closed. For DORA purposes, use restoration
- Not distinguishing change-induced MTTR from all-cause MTTR — a database hardware failure has a different MTTR driver than a bad deployment; keep them separate

**Measurement frequency**: Per incident. Monthly aggregate.

**Director review cadence**: Monthly. Track separately: MTTR for incidents resolved by rollback vs. hotfix vs. workaround. Rollback-resolved incidents should have the shortest MTTR — if they do not, the rollback process has gaps.

## 6. Target-Setting and Improvement Timelines

### From Low to Medium: 6–12 months

**Primary driver**: Establishing the basics — CI/CD pipeline for most services, documented rollback for all changes, RFC process that is consistently followed, incident process that links to change records.

**What does not work**: Mandating DORA metrics from the top without changing the underlying process. Measuring without acting on the measurements.

**What works**: Standardizing on a single CI/CD platform. Requiring rollback documentation in RFCs. Making change failure rate visible at team level. Assigning one team to demonstrate the improved process as a model for others.

---

### From Medium to High: 12–18 months

**Primary driver**: Reducing batch size. Organizations at Medium are deploying too infrequently and too large. The improvement comes from breaking releases into smaller units, not from deploying the same large releases faster.

**For CFR**: Add automated canary analysis or health-check gates in the pipeline. Establish a Standard Change catalog so routine changes do not consume CAB capacity.

**For LTC**: Reduce CAB review cycle time by scheduling CABs more frequently or creating an async approval process for low-risk changes. The bottleneck for High → Elite is almost always CAB review latency for Normal changes.

**For MTTR**: Invest in rollback automation. The organizations that achieve Elite MTTR (under one hour) have rollback that does not require human decision-making for the initial step — health check failure triggers automatic traffic rerouting or version rollback, then humans investigate.

---

### From High to Elite: 18–36 months

**Primary driver**: Automation replacing human gates. Elite performers have not eliminated oversight — they have moved oversight earlier (design review, automated quality gates) and later (post-deployment monitoring, blameless PIR) and removed it from the deployment critical path.

**What distinguishes Elite from High in change management**: CAB still exists at Elite, but it reviews platform and infrastructure changes, not individual application deployments. Application deployments are governed by automated gates (test pass rate, canary health, dependency scan) and are executed by teams without CAB involvement.

**Realistic note for T-Mobile scale**: Organization-wide Elite performance is rare in regulated telecommunications. Individual teams — particularly those owning greenfield services with no legacy infrastructure dependency — can reach Elite. The strategic goal for a VP of Operations is moving the organizational median from Medium to High, with selected teams achieving Elite as proof points.

## 7. Industry Context from 2023 DORA Report

**The bimodal distribution**: 2023 DORA found that software delivery performance does not distribute normally. Elite and Low performers are both more common than Medium and High. Organizations tend to cluster at the extremes rather than the middle. The implication: reaching High performance requires a deliberate, sustained improvement program — organizations do not drift into High, they invest into it.

**Burnout and performance are linked**: 2023 DORA extended its model beyond the four metrics to include team well-being. Teams with high change failure rates and long MTTR also report higher burnout. The operational implication: improving change quality is not just a performance initiative, it is a team health initiative.

**AI adoption finding (2023 first year of data)**: Teams using AI assistance in code review and testing reported higher deployment frequency but saw no improvement in CFR relative to non-AI teams. DORA's interpretation: AI helps generate code faster, but the quality controls downstream of code generation (testing, review, staged deployment) are the actual CFR driver. A change management program cannot rely on AI adoption to reduce CFR — it requires the same controls regardless.

**Platform engineering effect**: Teams with access to a mature internal developer platform (self-service infrastructure, automated environment provisioning, standard deployment pipelines) consistently score higher across all four metrics than teams without. For a Director/VP building a DevOps program, investment in platform capabilities returns DORA improvement faster than investment in process change alone.
