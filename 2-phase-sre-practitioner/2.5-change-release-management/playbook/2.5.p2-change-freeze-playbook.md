# Change Freeze Playbook

A change freeze is a period of elevated production stability for protecting a critical business event, audit window, or major platform migration. It is not a policy of zero change — it is a policy of elevated scrutiny and compressed authority over what changes are permitted.

---

## Declaring a Change Freeze

**Who can declare:** CAB Chair or Director/VP. CISO can declare a security freeze independently.

**Trigger criteria:**

| Trigger | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Major business event | Peak traffic periods where a failed change would have outsized customer and revenue impact (holiday season, major carrier promotions, product launches) |
| Audit windows | SOC2, PCI-DSS, or FCC audit periods where production changes introduce audit complexity |
| Platform migrations | During the cutover window of a major platform migration where environmental stability is required |
| Post-major-incident | CAB Chair or Director can declare a 72-hour freeze after a P1 incident to allow the environment to stabilize before further changes |

**Notice requirement:** 2 weeks minimum for planned freezes. Immediate for security freezes and post-incident freezes.

---

## Communicating a Change Freeze

**Step 1: Freeze declaration document.** Contains:
- Freeze period (start date/time, end date/time, timezone)
- Scope (which services, which environments — is it all production or a subset?)
- Exception process (what changes can still proceed and who approves them?)
- Owner (who has authority to grant exceptions?)

**Step 2: Communicate to all teams via official channel.** Not just email — the freeze must be acknowledged by every team lead. Use Slack/Teams with required acknowledgment reaction or reply.

**Step 3: Update change calendar.** Mark the freeze period in the change management tool. Configure the tool to block Normal change approvals during the freeze.

**Step 4: Notify the CAB.** All RFCs already approved for the freeze window must either move before the freeze or defer until after. Change owners must be contacted individually.

---

## Exceptions During a Freeze

**What can proceed without exception approval:**
- Standard Changes that are safety-critical (certificate expiration that would cause outage)
- Emergency Changes — Category A and B only. Not Category C during a freeze.

**What requires exception approval:**
Any Normal change during a freeze window. Exception request goes to CAB Chair + Director. Decision within 4 hours. If approved, documentation must include:
- Business justification for why it cannot wait
- Additional review steps taken
- CAB Chair + Director names as approvers

**What cannot proceed even with exception:**
Major platform changes, database schema migrations, infrastructure topology changes during peak business freeze periods. These are not negotiable.

---

## Enforcing a Freeze

**Technical enforcement:** configure change management tool to block Normal change state transitions to "Approved" during freeze. This is not optional — manual enforcement fails.

**Process enforcement:** CAB Chair reviews the change calendar daily during freeze. Any approved change attempting to execute during freeze is blocked. Team lead contacted directly.

**Violation response:** a change executed during freeze without exception approval triggers mandatory PIR + Director conversation + escalation to VP if intentional.

---

## Lifting a Freeze

**Communication:** all-teams notification 24 hours before freeze lift.

**Backlog management:** the week after a long freeze typically has high change volume (pent-up demand). Increase CAB review capacity for the first two post-freeze weeks. Watch change calendar for conflicts.

**Post-freeze retrospective:** for freezes longer than 2 weeks, a brief retrospective on what tried to sneak through, what was deferred unnecessarily, and whether the freeze scope was calibrated correctly.

---

## Freeze Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | What Happens | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Scope too broad | Freezing all changes when only core payment services needed protecting. Teams work around the freeze via Emergency process. | Define service scope precisely. Use allowlists, not blocklists. |
| Scope too narrow | Allowing "low-risk" changes that cascade through shared infrastructure. | Blast radius mapping before scope is finalized. |
| Notice too short | Teams had changes in pipeline they cannot pull back. | 2-week minimum for planned freezes, no exceptions. |
| No technical enforcement | Relies on honor system. Does not work at scale. | Tool-enforced blocks on state transitions are mandatory. |
