# RFC Submission Playbook

An RFC is not paperwork. It is your proof that you have thought through what could go wrong and have a credible response. A well-written RFC does not just describe the change — it describes the failure modes and demonstrates you can handle them.

---

## When to Submit an RFC

- Any change to production that is not in the Standard Change catalog
- Any change that affects a shared service, shared infrastructure, or more than one team's systems
- Any database schema change (additive or breaking)
- Any change to authentication, authorization, network topology, or encryption
- Any change that requires a maintenance window

---

## RFC Submission Timeline

| Milestone | When |
|-----------|------|
| RFC submitted to change management tool | 5 business days before CAB meeting |
| CAB pre-reads RFCs and submits async questions | 3 business days before CAB |
| RFC reviewed, decision made | CAB meeting day |
| Change window executed | After CAB approval, during approved window |

---

## Step 1: Use the RFC Template

Use `templates/rfc-template.md`. Do not write from a blank document. Every section of the template is required.

"N/A" is only acceptable for the security review section if the change has no authentication, authorization, network, or customer data component — and you must state why.

---

## Step 2: Write the Change Description for a Non-Technical Reader

Your first paragraph should be understandable by your Director, your product manager, and your SRE colleague. If a non-technical person cannot understand what is changing and why, your RFC will generate more questions than answers.

---

## Step 3: Complete the Blast Radius Map

Before writing the risk assessment, draw your blast radius. Look up:
- What services call the component you are changing?
- What services does the changed component call?
- Which customer journeys traverse these services?

Go two hops. Write it down. This is your blast radius.

---

## Step 4: Complete the Risk Assessment

Use the change risk matrix (`reference/change-risk-matrix.md`). Score impact (P1/P2/P3/P4) and likelihood (High/Medium/Low). Identify your risk tier.

If your risk tier is **P1/High**: this change needs architecture review before CAB submission.

---

## Step 5: Write a Credible Rollback Plan

**The test:** can your on-call engineer execute this rollback plan at 3 AM without calling you? If no, write it more specifically.

Rollback plan must include:
- Specific trigger criteria — not "if something goes wrong." Name the metric and the threshold.
- Numbered steps with owner per step
- Estimated rollback duration
- Data integrity note: what is the data state after rollback?

---

## Step 6: Attach Test Evidence

- Staging test results
- Load test results (if applicable)
- Unit test coverage for changed code paths
- Link to CI/CD pipeline run

If your service does not have staging environment parity, note this explicitly and explain what compensating controls exist.

---

## Step 7: Get Peer Review

One engineer who did not write this change must review the RFC before submission. Their name goes on the RFC.

Peer review is a technical check. They are looking for:
- Steps that are missing
- Blast radius gaps
- Rollback plan credibility
- Test evidence gaps

Not a rubber stamp.

---

## Step 8: Submit and Track

Submit RFC in change management tool. Tag the CAB Chair. Confirm submission timestamp — if submitted less than 5 business days before CAB, it will be deferred without review.

---

## Pre-Submission Checklist

- [ ] Change description understandable by non-technical stakeholder
- [ ] Blast radius mapped (2 hops minimum)
- [ ] Risk score calculated using risk matrix
- [ ] Implementation steps: numbered, owner per step, time estimate per step
- [ ] Rollback trigger criteria: specific and observable
- [ ] Rollback steps: numbered, owner per step
- [ ] Test evidence attached or linked
- [ ] Maintenance window requested and justified
- [ ] Peer review documented (name of reviewer)
- [ ] Submitted 5+ business days before CAB meeting
