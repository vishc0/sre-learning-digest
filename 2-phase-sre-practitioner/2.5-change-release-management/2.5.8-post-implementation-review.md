# Chapter 8: Post-Implementation Review

**Audience**: Director/VP of Operations — PIR is your learning infrastructure. Without it, every change quality investment produces single-use knowledge. With it, every failure teaches the whole organization.

PIR is where change management earns its operational value. The RFC discipline and CAB process reduce risk before a change executes. The PIR captures what actually happened, determines whether the risk assessment was accurate, and feeds that learning back into the next change of the same type. A change management program without PIR rigor is a one-way ratchet — you put in governance but you do not get improvement out.

---

## 1. PIR Requirements: What Triggers a PIR

PIR requirements must be documented in policy, not left to judgment. "Use discretion" results in PIRs being skipped for exactly the changes where they matter most.

### Mandatory PIR Triggers

| Change Type | PIR Trigger | Deadline |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Normal change — P1 or P2 priority | All P1/P2 Normal changes, regardless of outcome | 5 business days post-implementation |
| Emergency change — Category A, B, or C | All emergency changes, regardless of outcome | 24 hours (Cat A/B), 5 business days (Cat C) |
| Normal change — P3, with deviation from plan | Any P3 change where execution deviated from the RFC | 10 business days |
| Standard Change — any failure | A Standard Change that fails is immediately escalated | 48 hours |

### What Does NOT Require a PIR

Standard Changes executed as documented, with no deviation, no rollback, and no post-implementation incidents, do not require a PIR. Standard Changes are pre-validated — the PIR was performed during the Standard Change creation process. If a Standard Change consistently performs within parameters, demanding a PIR for each instance adds overhead with no learning return.

The critical exception: any Standard Change that fails is no longer operating as a Standard Change. Treat it as a failed Normal change, escalate to Emergency process if live impact exists, and run a full PIR within 48 hours. The PIR should also assess whether the Standard Change should be retired or revised in the catalog.

### PIR Is a Leading Indicator of Team Maturity

> **Director Decision Point: PIR Completion Rate**
>
> Track PIR completion rate monthly: (PIRs completed by required deadline) / (PIRs required) × 100.
>
> Target: above 95%. If it is below 90%, PIRs are being skipped or late. That is not a scheduling problem — it is a culture signal. Teams skip PIRs when they feel the PIR will result in blame rather than learning, or when they do not see PIR findings driving actual change.
>
> The fix is not escalation. The fix is demonstrating, publicly and repeatedly, that PIR findings result in process improvements — not punitive consequences. If your teams believe PIR is a retrospective blame session, they will avoid it. Show them a PIR output that resulted in a better RFC template, a new Standard Change entry, or a tooling investment. Once they see that the feedback loop is real, participation follows.

## 2. PIR Timing and the 5-Business-Day Rule

The timing requirements are not arbitrary. They are tuned to the information half-life of a change event.

### Why 5 Business Days for Normal Changes

**Too fast (24 hours)**: you cannot distinguish a successful change from a change whose problems have not yet manifested. Memory leaks, slow query accumulation, latency drift, cache invalidation patterns — these take 2-3 days to surface under real production load. A PIR at 24 hours on a Normal change produces a false success signal.

**Too slow (2 weeks)**: beyond 10 business days, team members have moved to other work. The incident timeline is reconstructed from memory rather than recalled. Metric data requires a longer lookback query. The change implementer may not even be available. PIR quality degrades sharply after 10 business days.

**5 business days** is the window where both conditions are met: enough time to observe post-deployment behavior across a full weekly cycle, and close enough to the change that context is preserved.

### Emergency Change PIR Within 24 Hours

Emergency change PIR must happen within 24 hours for Category A and B changes. The reasons:

1. The incident or security context is still active in the team's working memory — you do not need to reconstruct it.
2. The urgency of the authorization must be matched by the urgency of the accountability. If you authorized a change in 15 minutes and documented it post-hoc, you owe the organization a rapid accounting of whether that was the right call.
3. If the emergency change did not fully resolve the issue, you need that finding within 24 hours while response options are still open.

### Combined PIR and Postmortem

For Category A (incident-driven) emergency changes, the change PIR and the incident postmortem should be combined if the postmortem is scheduled within the 24-hour window. These are related but distinct reviews:

- **Incident postmortem focus**: response effectiveness, detection latency, escalation path, recovery timeline. The "what happened and how did we respond" story.
- **Change PIR focus**: change quality, risk assessment accuracy, rollback plan validity, RFC completeness. The "was the change well-designed and well-executed" story.

Run them in the same session to avoid scheduling overhead, but address both sets of questions. Do not let the postmortem crowd out the change PIR questions — the change quality accountability is as important as the incident response accountability.

### PIR Facilitation and Objectivity

The change owner facilitates the PIR — they have the most context. But the change owner should not be the sole voice determining the quality score or the root cause. The CAB Chair or a neutral process owner should be present to ensure the questions are asked fully and the findings are documented accurately.

In practice, this is the most commonly violated PIR requirement. Change owners facilitating their own PIR without oversight produces optimistic quality scores, incomplete root cause analysis, and action items that are assigned to people who are not in the room. Build the CAB Chair PIR participation expectation into your operating model.

## 3. PIR Structure: Five Questions

PIR is not a free-form retrospective. It has a defined structure that produces comparable, trending data across all changes over time. Unstructured PIRs produce rich anecdotes and no metric.

### Q1: Did the change go as planned?

Compare the RFC implementation steps to actual execution. Every step in the RFC runbook is either:

- **On plan**: executed as written, on time, with expected outcome.
- **Deviated**: executed differently from what the RFC described — different steps, different sequence, different tooling used.
- **Extended**: executed as written but took longer than the RFC estimated.
- **Skipped**: not executed, and the reasoning was not in the RFC.

Document each deviation. The aggregate deviation pattern across 20 changes tells you whether your RFC templates produce accurate runbooks or aspirational ones.

### Q2: What deviated, and why?

For each deviation identified in Q1, categorize the root cause:

| Deviation Type | Cause Category | What to Fix |
|----------------|---------------|-------------|
| RFC said X would happen; Y happened instead | **Planning gap** — RFC was wrong about production behavior | RFC review process: was this difference detectable in staging? |
| RFC was correct but team executed differently | **Execution gap** — team diverged from documented plan | Training, runbook clarity, or execution verification step missing |
| Staging behavior differed from production | **Environment gap** — staging is not production-equivalent | Environment parity investment; staging fidelity assessment |
| External dependency behaved unexpectedly | **Dependency gap** — third-party or upstream service not modeled | Dependency documentation in RFC, blast radius assessment |

The cause category determines the corrective action. Conflating all deviations as "the plan was wrong" misses execution and environment problems that require different investments.

### Q3: Were the rollback criteria met? Was rollback triggered?

This question has two branches:

**If rollback was not triggered**: confirm that rollback criteria were monitored (not just documented). The RFC should have specified which monitoring signals or health checks would trigger rollback. Were those actively watched during the change window? If not, the rollback plan existed on paper but was not operationalized.

**If rollback was triggered**: walk through the rollback execution step by step against the RFC rollback procedure. Every deviation from the rollback plan is a finding — either the rollback procedure was wrong, the environment behaved unexpectedly, or the team was improvising. All three are correctable, but they require different corrections.

The most common rollback failure: the rollback procedure was never tested, and it failed or took 3x longer than expected when executed in a real event. Standard rollback testing in staging before every P1 change is the prevention.

### Q4: What was the customer impact vs. estimated?

The RFC required an estimated impact: X% of customers affected for Y minutes, or service latency increasing by Z ms for W minutes during the change window. Compare that estimate to what actually occurred.

**Actual < Estimated**: the risk assessment was conservative. Understand why — better tooling, faster execution, or was the estimate just padded? Consistently over-conservative estimates inflate the perceived risk of changes and slow CAB approval cycles.

**Actual = Estimated**: your risk assessment process is calibrated. Celebrate this.

**Actual > Estimated**: the risk assessment was wrong. This is the most important finding in the PIR. Why did actual impact exceed the estimate? Was the blast radius assessment wrong (more services were affected than expected)? Was the change duration wrong (it took twice as long)? Was the rollback slower than modeled? Each over-estimate failure teaches you something about where your change risk models are weak.

> **Director Decision Point: Impact Estimation Accuracy**
>
> Track impact estimation accuracy as a metric: (changes where actual impact exceeded estimated impact) / (total changes reviewed) × 100.
>
> If this is above 30%, your risk assessment process is systematically under-estimating change impact. That means your CAB is making authorization decisions on incomplete risk information.
>
> The investment: staging environment fidelity (if staging does not represent production load and topology, estimates will always be wrong), and historical change impact data in your RFC template (past similar changes took X minutes and affected Y users — use actual history as the estimate basis, not engineering intuition).

### Q5: What would we do differently?

This is the only forward-looking question. Every answer must be specific, actionable, and owner-assigned. Generic answers ("improve testing," "add more monitoring") are not actionable and will not be executed.

| Weak Answer | Strong Answer |
|------------|--------------|
| "Improve rollback documentation" | "Add a tested rollback verification step (service health check returning 200) to the RFC template for all database schema changes — owner: [name], due: [date]" |
| "Better staging" | "Provision a production-traffic replay in staging for the accounts service — owner: [name], due: [date], cost: [resource estimate]" |
| "More monitoring" | "Add deployment event webhook from CI/CD to Splunk for the Data Pipeline team so change-to-incident correlation is automatic — owner: [name], due: [date]" |

Q5 is where PIR value is created or destroyed. A PIR that ends with no actionable Q5 outputs is a documentation exercise, not a learning event.

## 4. Change Quality Scoring

Quality scores enable trending and comparison across teams, services, and change types. Without a scoring rubric, "success" and "failure" are subjective.

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Label | Criteria |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **1** | Success | Change implemented as planned. No unexpected deviations. Customer impact within estimated bounds. No rollback required. No post-change incidents attributed to the change within 72 hours. |
| **2** | Partial Success | Change implemented with deviations — extended maintenance window, additional steps required, minor sequence changes. Customer impact within estimated bounds. No rollback. Minor post-change issues resolved within 24 hours without incident escalation. |
| **3** | Failure | Rollback required. OR customer impact exceeded estimated bounds by more than 20%. OR a post-change incident (SEV1-3) directly attributed to the change within 72 hours. OR change could not be implemented in the maintenance window. |

**Partial Success is not a euphemism for failure.** A change that took 45 minutes longer than planned but achieved its goal without customer impact is genuinely a Partial Success. The value is in tracking the deviation — if Partial Success is consistently "extended maintenance window," you have a time-estimation problem, not a quality problem. They require different fixes.

### Quality Score Tracking by Team

| Team | Q1 | Q2 | Q3 | Q4 | Quarter Total | Success Rate |
|------|----|----|----|----|--------------|-------------|
| Platform API | 1 | 1 | 2 | 1 | Q1 | 75% Success, 25% Partial |
| Auth Services | 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 | Q1 | 100% Success |
| Data Pipeline | 3 | 2 | 3 | 3 | Q1 | 25% Success, 25% Partial, 50% Failure |
| Middleware | 1 | 2 | 1 | 2 | Q1 | 50% Success, 50% Partial |

> **Director Decision Point: Failure Pattern Response**
>
> Three consecutive failures for the same team → scheduled coaching session with the team lead. Not punitive — structured. Agenda: review the three failed PIRs together, identify the common root cause chain, agree on two specific process changes, and set a 30-day checkpoint.
>
> Three consecutive failures of the same change type across different teams (schema changes, infrastructure changes, Kubernetes deployments) → convene architecture review. The failure is in the change type itself — its complexity, the team's preparation for it, or the staging environment's ability to validate it. The fix is at the change type level, not the team level.

## 5. PIR-to-Problem Record Pipeline

Every change PIR must end with an explicit answer to: "Does this change failure create a Problem record?"

### When to Create a Problem Record

| Condition | Create Problem Record? |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Change failed with a one-time, non-systemic cause (misconfigured flag, typo in config value) | No — it is a change quality issue, not a systemic defect |
| Change failed due to a systemic defect in the application or infrastructure (root cause is a bug or design flaw) | Yes |
| Same failure type occurred in a prior change for the same team or service | Yes — recurrence signals a systemic issue |
| Change failure caused a production incident | Yes — the incident's Problem record links to the change PIR |
| Change failure revealed an environment gap (staging does not replicate production behavior) | Yes — the environment gap is a systemic defect that will cause future failures |

### Problem Record Content from PIR

The PIR owner provides the initial evidence for the Problem record. Required fields:

- Link to the failed change PIR (change number, PIR document)
- Root cause (preliminary — Problem Management owns full root cause analysis)
- Affected services and blast radius
- Assigned Problem owner (from Problem Management's on-call or team rotation)
- Target date for root cause confirmation

[Problem Management] owns the Problem record from the moment it is created. The PIR owner is a provider of evidence, not the investigator. This separation of concern matters — the team that caused the failure should not be the sole investigator of the failure.

> **Director Callout: The PIR-to-Problem Pipeline Is Where Change and Problem Management Connect**
>
> A failed change that does not create a Problem record is a missed learning. The PIR captures what happened. The Problem record drives to root cause and permanent fix. Without the Problem record, you have a documented failure and no accountability for preventing recurrence.
>
> In your monthly operational review, track: "Of all failed change PIRs this month, what percentage created a Problem record?" Target: 100% for systemic failures. If this is below 80%, teams are closing PIRs without making the Problem record determination — which means they are not asking the systemic question.
>
> The Problem record is also the mechanism for connecting repeated failures. Two failed changes from different teams, both caused by the same underlying infrastructure defect, will not be connected unless both PIRs create Problem records and Problem Management performs cross-linking. This is the operational value of ITSM integration between change and problem management.

## 6. Trends from PIR Data: What the Data Tells You

PIR data is worth mining quarterly. Not for individual change performance — for systemic patterns that no individual PIR would reveal.

### Team-Level Change Quality Trends

Pull the quality score distribution by team for the trailing 6 months. The trend is more informative than the absolute number.

**Improving trend** (Failure rate declining): the team is learning. Q5 action items are being executed. Standard Change candidates are being identified and promoted. This team is operating the PIR feedback loop correctly.

**Flat trend** (Failure rate stable but not improving): Q5 action items may not be getting executed, or the root causes being identified are not the actual root causes. Audit two recent PIRs — are the Q5 items done? If they are done and failure rate has not improved, the root cause diagnosis was wrong.

**Deteriorating trend** (Failure rate increasing): something changed in the team's environment, capacity, or service complexity. Increased deployment frequency without a corresponding increase in test coverage is the most common cause. Investigate before the trend produces a major incident.

### Service-Level Change Risk

Some services fail more often across all teams that touch them. That pattern tells you something about the service, not the teams. A service that consistently produces high-failure-rate changes may be:

- **Inherently fragile**: tightly coupled, poor observability, no canary capability, brittle dependency graph.
- **Inadequately owned**: the team that owns it does not have deep production familiarity. Ownership clarity issue.
- **Understaged**: the service's production behavior is not reproducible in staging. The service is changing in ways that are only validated in production.

Each root cause requires a different intervention. A Director who sees service-level failure clustering needs to ask: "Is this a team problem, a service architecture problem, or a tooling problem?"

### Change Type Failure Correlation

Calculate CFR by change type across your entire portfolio:

| Change Type | Deployments | Failures | CFR |
|-------------|-------------|---------|-----|
| Code deployment (containerized) | 280 | 12 | 4.3% |
| Database schema migration | 45 | 9 | 20.0% |
| Infrastructure change (Terraform) | 32 | 4 | 12.5% |
| Configuration update | 88 | 2 | 2.3% |
| Certificate/secret rotation | 22 | 0 | 0.0% |
| Kubernetes manifest update | 67 | 3 | 4.5% |

This table drives your training and tooling investment. Schema migrations at 20% CFR need attention — likely a combination of insufficient pre-migration validation (schema diff review, index impact analysis) and inadequate rollback procedures (forward-only migrations with no rollback path). The investment is a schema migration runbook, a mandatory pre-migration checklist, and a staging validation step.

### Deviation Pattern Analysis

Aggregate Q2 answers across all PIRs for a quarter. The most common deviation types tell you where your RFC process is systematically failing.

| Most Common Deviation | Root Cause | Fix |
|----------------------|------------|-----|
| Maintenance window exceeded (ran long) | Time estimates are wrong — RFC estimates are optimistic | Historical time data by change type as mandatory RFC input. Add 25% buffer to estimates. |
| Rollback triggered due to unexpected behavior in production | Staging is not production-equivalent | Staging fidelity investment for highest-risk services. Production traffic replay for validation. |
| Additional steps required not in RFC | RFC process is not capturing enough operational detail | RFC template revision; mandatory field for "known operational edge cases." |
| External dependency failure during change | Dependencies not modeled in blast radius | RFC template requires dependency map for P1/P2 changes. |

## 7. Using PIR Data to Expand the Standard Change Catalog

The Standard Change catalog is the structural output of a mature PIR program. Every Normal change that succeeds reliably is a Standard Change candidate. Without PIR data, you cannot make that judgment rigorously.

### The Standard Change Expansion Flywheel

1. A Normal change is executed and succeeds (quality score: Success).
2. It is executed again with the same result. And again. After 5 successful executions, the PIR data shows a pattern.
3. PIR data review at the monthly change review meeting surfaces this change type as a Standard Change candidate.
4. CAB reviews the candidate: procedure documented step-by-step? Rollback tested and documented? Success criteria measurable?
5. Change is added to the Standard Change catalog.
6. Future instances of this change type skip CAB review — they are executed against the documented procedure.
7. CAB bandwidth freed up for higher-complexity change reviews.

This is the flywheel: mature PIR process → Standard Change catalog growth → CAB burden reduction → CAB quality improvement on remaining reviews → better Normal change outcomes → more Standard Change candidates.

### Standard Change Promotion Criteria

All four criteria must be met:

| Criterion | Verification Method |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Executed at least 5 times as a Normal change with all Success quality scores | PIR records showing quality score = 1 for all 5+ instances |
| Procedure is documented with step-by-step detail executable by any qualified team member | Procedure walkthrough with a team member who did not author it |
| Rollback is tested and documented | Rollback drill in staging within the last 90 days, documented outcome |
| Success/failure criteria are defined and measurable | Named metrics or health checks that confirm success/failure within 5 minutes of completion |

### Standard Change Catalog Metrics

> **Director Decision Point: Catalog Growth as Maturity Signal**
>
> Track Standard Change catalog size quarter over quarter. A catalog that is not growing means your PIR data is not feeding back into change classification — PIRs are happening but the feedback loop is broken.
>
> A catalog that is shrinking (changes being demoted from Standard back to Normal after failures) is a quality signal worth understanding. A Standard Change that fails means the procedure was either wrong, the execution deviated, or the production environment changed since the procedure was validated. Each is a different root cause.

**Quarterly Standard Change review** — CAB Chair agenda item:

- **Remove**: Standard Changes that have failed in the last quarter. Investigate root cause. Either fix the procedure and re-promote after 5 successful Normal change instances, or retire the Standard Change category.
- **Add**: Normal change candidates identified by PIR review in the quarter.
- **Revise**: Standard Changes where PIR data shows consistent Partial Success (extended time, additional steps). The procedure needs updating.

The catalog review cadence operationalizes the learning loop. Without it, the catalog becomes stale — procedures that no longer match production reality, and changes that should be promoted but are not.

### Standard Change Coverage Target

| Maturity Level | Standard Change % of Total Volume |
|----------------|----------------------------------|
| Level 2 (Defined) | < 15% |
| Level 3 (Managed) | 15-35% |
| Level 4 (Optimizing) | 35-60% |
| Level 5 (Elite) | 60%+ |

At Level 5, the majority of your change volume executes without CAB review. CAB becomes a high-quality review body for genuinely complex, novel, or high-risk changes — which is what it should be. CAB reviewing 400 routine deployments per month is not governance; it is overhead.
