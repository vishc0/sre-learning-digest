# Chapter 2 — Change Types and Risk Classification

## Standard Change: Approval-Free, Pre-Validated, Repeatable

The Standard Change is the highest-leverage lever in your change program. It is the mechanism by which the CAB pre-approves an entire category of change rather than reviewing each instance. Done correctly, a growing Standard Change catalog simultaneously increases deployment frequency, reduces CAB load, and maintains governance — the only change management intervention that improves all three metrics at once.

### The Six Criteria for Standard Change Designation

Every criterion must be met. There is no partial credit.

**1. Risk assessment completed and documented.** The change type has been formally evaluated using the impact × likelihood framework. The assessment document names the impact tier (P1–P4), the likelihood tier (High/Medium/Low), the resulting risk tier, and the controls that reduce it to an acceptable level. The assessment is dated and reviewed within the last 12 months. An outdated risk assessment is not a risk assessment — it is a historical artifact that no longer reflects the current system.

**2. Procedure written and peer-reviewed.** A step-by-step execution procedure exists. It is written at a level where an engineer unfamiliar with this specific change type could execute it correctly. It has been reviewed by at least one SME from outside the authoring team. It is stored in a version-controlled location (Git repository, not a wiki that can be edited without audit trail). The version of the procedure that the CAB approved is explicitly referenced.

**3. Success and failure criteria defined.** There is a specific, observable, time-bounded definition of success and failure. Not "the service appears healthy." Rather: "Within 5 minutes of procedure completion, the certificate expiry date in `kubectl describe secret` shows a date more than 89 days in the future, and the HTTP readiness probe returns 200 on all pods." Failure criteria must be equally specific: "If the readiness probe fails on any pod within 10 minutes of execution, initiate rollback immediately."

**4. Rollback tested at least once in a production-equivalent environment.** The rollback procedure has been executed — not just written. Someone ran it against a staging environment that mirrors production topology and confirmed it restores the prior state within the specified time. The test date and the confirming engineer are documented. Rollbacks that have never been tested are plans, not procedures.

**5. Execution time is bounded and predictable.** The change completes within a known time range. The variance is small and explainable. A procedure that "usually takes 15 minutes but once took 4 hours" is not Standard — the 4-hour outlier indicates a failure mode that is not fully characterized. Standard Changes should execute within a ±20% time band consistently across the trailing 10 executions.

**6. Approved by CAB as a category.** An individual team cannot designate their own change as Standard. The CAB reviews the procedure, the risk assessment, and the rollback evidence, then formally adds it to the Standard Change catalog with a catalog ID, the owning team, the approval date, and the next review date. This gate exists to prevent teams from bypassing the approval process by self-labeling changes as Standard.

### Standard Change Catalog Governance

The CAB owns the catalog. It does not delegate this responsibility.

**Quarterly review agenda items:**

1. Candidate additions: Which new procedures have teams submitted? Does each meet all six criteria? Vote to add or return for additional evidence.
2. Candidate demotions: Which existing Standard Changes have had failures in the prior quarter? A single execution failure with unclear cause requires review. Two failures requires demotion to Normal until the root cause is understood and the procedure is updated.
3. Stale review: Which catalog entries have not been reviewed in 12+ months? Schedule review. A Standard Change that has not been examined in 18 months is an unmanaged risk — the system it touches has changed even if the procedure has not.

**Director metric: Standard Change percentage.** What fraction of all changes executed in the trailing quarter are Standard?

| Standard Change % | Interpretation | Intervention |
|------------------|----------------|-------------|
| 60–70%+ | Elite | Sustain; continue quarterly catalog reviews |
| 40–60% | Healthy | Identify the highest-volume Normal changes as catalog candidates |
| 30–40% | Developing | CAB is overloaded; audit which Normal changes repeat most frequently |
| Below 30% | Dysfunctional | CAB is a bottleneck; urgent catalog investment required |

> **What the VP Asks:** "What is our Standard Change percentage this quarter, and how does it compare to last quarter? What are the top five Normal changes by volume, and why are they not Standard yet?"

### Concrete Examples at Enterprise Scale

**Qualifies as Standard:**
- Automated certificate rotation for internal services where the rotation script is idempotent, self-verifying, and has been executed 50+ times with zero failures
- Kubernetes HPA min/max replica adjustment within a pre-approved band (e.g., min: 2–10, max: 10–50, CPU target: 60–80%) for stateless services only
- Redis cache TTL adjustment within ±20% of documented baseline TTL for a specific cache namespace
- DNS CNAME record addition for internal services following the documented naming pattern with TTL ≤ 300 seconds
- Splunk alert threshold adjustment within ±25% of current threshold for existing alert definitions

**Does not qualify as Standard:**
- Any change to a service or environment not previously in scope for this procedure, even if the procedure is otherwise identical
- Any schema change, including additive schema changes — schema changes have a special risk profile covered in a later section
- Any change requiring real-time coordination across three or more teams to execute safely
- Any change where the execution time has varied by more than 50% across recent executions
- Any change type that has had a failure in the prior 6 months where root cause is fully understood but the procedure has not been updated since

## Normal Change: RFC Process and CAB Review

Normal Change is the governance path for any planned change that has not been pre-approved as Standard and is not time-critical enough to trigger Emergency. It is the core of the CAB's weekly work.

### RFC Requirements

An RFC that is missing any of these elements should be returned to the submitter without review. A CAB that reviews incomplete RFCs trains submitters to submit incomplete RFCs.

| RFC Section | What Is Required |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Change description | What is changing, at what layer (code/config/schema/infrastructure), in what component |
| Business justification | Why this change is being made; what customer or operational outcome it enables |
| Scope | Specific services affected, environments affected, customer population at risk |
| Risk assessment | Impact tier (P1–P4), likelihood tier, resulting risk tier, risk matrix reference |
| Rollback plan | Specific steps, specific commands, specific owner, estimated time to restore |
| Test evidence | Results from staging/integration testing; automated test coverage of the changed code path |
| Implementation timeline | Proposed maintenance window; start time, end time, expected duration |
| Approval chain | Who must approve beyond CAB; application owner, data owner, security if required |

### CAB Review Focus

The CAB is a risk review panel, not an approval committee. The distinction matters operationally. An approval committee asks "should we allow this?" A risk review panel asks "has the submitter correctly identified and mitigated the risks?" The CAB's job is to find the risks the RFC submitter missed.

Four questions that drive effective CAB review:

1. **Does the risk assessment match the actual blast radius?** A team that rated their database migration as P3 impact (low customer impact) because it touches only one service may not have traced the downstream dependencies of that service. Pull the service dependency map. Count the downstream consumers.

2. **Is the rollback plan credible?** "Redeploy the previous version" is not a rollback plan for a database schema migration. A credible rollback plan names the specific steps, the estimated time, the person executing it, and — for schema changes — whether data written after the migration is recoverable under rollback.

3. **Is the maintenance window appropriate for the risk tier?** A P1-impact change scheduled in a 30-minute window at 2:00 AM Friday is a setup for an extended incident. The maintenance window must accommodate the expected execution time plus rollback time plus a buffer.

4. **Are the right SMEs involved?** A change to the messaging infrastructure (RabbitMQ cluster configuration, Kafka broker parameters) should have the middleware SME listed as a reviewer and on-call during implementation. A change to a service with database dependencies should have the DBA listed.

### Normal Change SLA

| Stage | SLA |
|-------|-----|
| RFC submission deadline | 5 business days before proposed implementation |
| CAB review | At weekly CAB meeting (or ad-hoc session for expedited cases) |
| CAB decision | Within 2 business days of CAB meeting |
| Submitter response to CAB questions | Within 1 business day |
| Implementation window | Within 30 days of approval; re-approval required if window expires |

> **What the VP Asks:** "What is our Normal Change average lead time from RFC submission to implementation? What is the distribution — median vs. 90th percentile? What are the top three causes of delay?"

If your Normal change lead time exceeds 10 business days on average, you have a CAB throughput problem, an RFC quality problem, or both. The solution is Standard Change catalog expansion (reduce CAB volume) and RFC quality gates (reject incomplete RFCs before review rather than during).

## Emergency Change: Declaration, Compressed Review, Post-Hoc PIR

The Emergency Change path exists for genuine crises. Its existence is a safety valve. Its abuse is a symptom of a broken Normal process.

### Declaration Criteria — At Least One Must Be Met

**Incident-driven mitigation:** An active production incident is in progress. The proposed change is the identified mitigation (not a speculative fix — the diagnosis has been confirmed to the extent possible given incident time pressure). Waiting for the next scheduled CAB meeting will prolong customer impact beyond what is operationally acceptable. The incident commander or on-call manager has declared this a production emergency.

**Security-critical response:** A CVE or active exploit has been confirmed for a component in your production environment. The vulnerability has a CVSS score of 9.0+ or active exploitation in the wild has been confirmed by your security team. The risk of delay (continued exposure) exceeds the risk of immediate change.

**Regulatory or compliance deadline:** A hard external deadline with legal consequence is imminent. A regulator has issued a directive. An audit finding requires remediation by a specific date. The deadline was not missed through negligence — this criterion is for genuinely hard deadlines, not internal milestones.

**Customer data integrity risk:** An ongoing data integrity issue (corruption, incorrect processing, incorrect billing) will worsen with time and requires an immediate operational change to halt the damage.

### What Is Not an Emergency

This list is not exhaustive but covers the most common abuses:

- A planned change where the team ran out of preparation time and the window is tonight
- A change to meet an internal sprint deadline that has no external commitment
- A fix for a non-production-impacting bug that a stakeholder has elevated to "urgent"
- A change that someone wants to deploy before the weekend to avoid doing it next week
- Any change that was Normal last week and has not changed in nature or urgency

The test: if the change could be delayed 5 business days with no production impact and no external consequence, it is not an emergency. Submit the RFC.

### Compressed Review Process

Emergency changes do not bypass review — they compress it.

| Step | Requirement | Time Target |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Emergency declaration | Incident commander or manager declares and documents justification | Within 15 minutes of change decision |
| Compressed review | CAB chair + one domain SME review verbally (phone/Teams) | Within 1 hour of declaration |
| Minimum approvals | CAB chair approval + domain SME sign-off | Before change execution |
| Abbreviated RFC | Completed by the change executor | Within 4 hours of change execution |
| Post-hoc PIR | Scheduled and completed | Within 24 hours of change completion |

The abbreviation is in the process, not in the thinking. The change executor must still answer: What are we changing? What is the expected impact? What is the rollback? The difference is that these answers are given verbally in a compressed window, documented afterward.

> **What the VP Asks in the monthly review:** "What was our Emergency Change count this month? What was our Emergency Change percentage? For any month where emergency % exceeded 15%, I want to see the root cause breakdown — how many were genuine incidents vs. other?"

### Emergency Change as a Leading Indicator

Track emergency change percentage on a monthly rolling basis. Plot it.

- Rising trend: your Normal process is getting slower or your teams are increasingly routing around it. Investigate both.
- Spike followed by return to baseline: likely a genuine cluster of incidents or a security event. Review the incident records.
- Sustained plateau above 20%: structural dysfunction. The Emergency path has become the de facto Normal path. This requires program intervention, not individual coaching.

## The Change Risk Matrix: Impact × Likelihood

Every Normal change requires a risk assessment before RFC submission. The risk matrix provides the framework. It is not a bureaucratic checkbox — it is the tool that determines CAB review depth and required controls.

### Impact Scoring (Customer/Revenue/Regulatory)

**P1 Impact:**
- More than 10% of production customer base affected (or: any customer-facing service with no degraded-mode fallback)
- Revenue impact exceeding $1M/hour at current transaction rates
- Regulatory violation: any change that could result in a reportable compliance breach (PCI, HIPAA, state regulatory)
- Complete loss of a core customer capability (voice, data, billing) for any customer segment

**P2 Impact:**
- 1–10% of production customer base affected
- Revenue impact $100K–$1M/hour
- SLA breach for a contractually committed service level
- Degraded customer experience for a non-trivial segment (a specific product line, a geographic region)

**P3 Impact:**
- Less than 1% of production customer base affected
- Revenue impact less than $100K/hour
- Internal SLA breach only (no customer commitment at risk)
- Degraded experience limited to internal tooling or back-office systems

**P4 Impact:**
- Zero production customer impact
- Development, test, or staging environments only
- No revenue at risk under any failure scenario

### Likelihood Scoring (Probability of Failure)

**High Likelihood:**
- New code path with no production exposure; deployed to production for the first time
- Change has not been tested in a production-equivalent environment
- Rollback is complex, multi-step, or has never been tested
- Change touches shared infrastructure (shared databases, shared message brokers, shared network components)
- The engineer executing has not done this change before in this environment

**Medium Likelihood:**
- Modification to an existing, tested code path
- Tested in a staging environment with production-equivalent data volume or topology
- Rollback has been tested at least once in staging
- Change is scoped to a specific service with defined dependencies

**Low Likelihood:**
- Configuration-only change with no code deployment
- Change has been tested in production via feature flag (only affects flagged users)
- Rollback is a single-command configuration revert with sub-5-minute restore time
- This exact procedure has been executed successfully in this environment at least 5 times in the trailing 12 months

### Risk Matrix: Impact × Likelihood → Risk Tier

| | **High Likelihood** | **Medium Likelihood** | **Low Likelihood** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P1 Impact** | CRITICAL — Emergency-level review even for planned changes; Executive notification required | HIGH — Extended CAB review; Director must approve; mandatory blast radius map | HIGH — Extended CAB review; mandatory blast radius map; canary deployment required |
| **P2 Impact** | HIGH — Extended CAB review; Senior SRE must be on-call during window | MEDIUM — Standard CAB review; rollback test required | LOW — Standard CAB review; Standard Change candidate if repeatable |
| **P3 Impact** | MEDIUM — Standard CAB review; rollback test required | LOW — Standard CAB review; Standard Change candidate | MINIMAL — Standard Change candidate; CAB review expedited |
| **P4 Impact** | LOW — Standard CAB review; testing evidence required | MINIMAL — Standard Change candidate | MINIMAL — Standard Change; automated approval eligible |

**Risk tier definitions:**

- **CRITICAL:** Requires executive notification before implementation. Director or VP must be briefed. Extended maintenance window (4+ hours). Dedicated rollback team on standby.
- **HIGH:** Extended CAB review (minimum 45 minutes; not a queue item). Director-level awareness. Canary or phased deployment required. Dedicated on-call during window.
- **MEDIUM:** Standard CAB review (15–20 minutes). Standard on-call rotation. Rollback test evidence required.
- **LOW:** Standard CAB review. Standard on-call. Expedited approval eligible (within 24 hours of submission).
- **MINIMAL:** Standard Change candidate. If repeatable with tested rollback, initiate catalog addition process.

> **What the VP Asks:** "Show me the distribution of our changes by risk tier this quarter. How many CRITICAL or HIGH changes did we have? What was the failure rate for each tier? Is our risk scoring calibrated — are CRITICAL changes actually failing more often than LOW changes, or is everything bunched in the middle because teams are sandbagging their scores?"

The last question exposes risk score inflation (teams rate everything LOW to avoid CAB scrutiny) and risk score deflation (teams rate everything HIGH to avoid accountability for failures). Both pathologies render the matrix useless. Calibrate by comparing declared risk tier to actual change failure rate by tier, quarterly.

## Configuration vs. Code vs. Infrastructure vs. Schema: Different Risk Profiles

Not all changes are the same even when they share a risk tier. The change type determines the rollback complexity, the blast radius shape, and the required testing depth.

### Configuration Changes

Configuration changes touch system parameters without deploying new code. Examples: feature flag state changes, environment variable updates, connection pool size, timeout values, log level adjustments.

**Blast radius shape:** Tightly scoped if the configuration is per-service. Potentially catastrophic if the configuration is shared — a global feature flag, a shared database connection string, a shared message broker parameter touches every consumer simultaneously.

**Rollback characteristics:** Fast. A configuration revert is typically a single command and takes effect in seconds to minutes, depending on how the configuration is consumed (environment variables require pod restart; feature flag systems typically propagate in under 60 seconds).

**Testing requirements:** Lower than code changes, but not zero. A timeout value change that is too aggressive will cause cascading failures under load that do not appear in low-traffic testing. Test under representative load.

**Director callout:** Configuration changes are the most underestimated source of catastrophic incidents. The blast radius is invisible until it is not. A shared configuration change that affects 30 services simultaneously has a P1 blast radius even if each individual service looks low-risk. Require blast radius assessment for any configuration change that touches shared infrastructure.

### Code Changes

Code changes deploy new logic, new dependencies, or new behavior. They are the most common change type and the one most teams have the most maturity around.

**Blast radius shape:** Depends on code path coverage. A change to a utility function called by 40 services has a broad blast radius. A change to a single API endpoint called only by the mobile app has a narrow blast radius.

**Rollback characteristics:** Requires redeployment of the prior artifact. Time to restore is the time to detect failure plus the time to re-deploy, which may include pipeline execution time. Plan for 15–45 minutes in most environments.

**Testing requirements:** Automated test coverage of the changed code path is required. Manual testing in staging for P1/P2 impact changes. Canary deployment for any change with a P1/P2 blast radius.

### Infrastructure Changes

Infrastructure changes touch the foundational systems that everything else runs on: VPC routing, security groups, subnet configurations, load balancer rules, DNS resolution, TLS termination, Kubernetes control plane configuration.

**Blast radius shape:** Potentially full-platform. A VPC routing change that introduces a routing loop does not affect one service — it affects every service in that VPC simultaneously.

**Rollback characteristics:** Often slow and sometimes impossible without data loss or service disruption. A subnet migration has no simple "undo." Infrastructure changes require rollback plans that are more conservative and more detailed than other change types.

**Testing requirements:** Highest of any change type. Staging validation is necessary but not sufficient — staging rarely mirrors production network topology exactly. Blast radius maps must be drawn and reviewed by a network or platform SME before CAB submission.

> **What the VP Asks before any infrastructure change:** "What is the rollback? How long does it take? Has anyone executed it outside of production? What happens to services that are mid-transaction when this change lands?" If the team cannot answer all four questions specifically, the RFC goes back.

### Schema Changes: The Special Category

Schema changes deserve explicit treatment as a separate category because they consistently carry more risk than their surface complexity suggests.

**Forward-compatible (additive) changes:** Adding a new column with a nullable type or a default value. Adding a new table. Adding an index. These do not break existing consumers immediately. Risk is Medium or lower depending on table size and lock behavior.

**Breaking changes:** Renaming a column. Changing a column type. Removing a column. Changing a constraint from nullable to non-null. These break existing consumers immediately or after the next deployment — whichever comes first. Breaking schema changes must be treated as P1-risk changes regardless of traffic impact.

**Migration irreversibility:** A schema migration that involves data transformation (converting varchar to UUID, splitting a column, merging two columns) cannot be rolled back without either restoring from backup (data loss) or writing a forward migration (time-consuming, not a fast rollback). Rollback time for a transformative schema migration may be hours or longer.

**Director callout:** Schema changes are the most underestimated change risk in every organization. Every database migration that cannot be rolled back without data loss is a P1 change, full stop. It does not matter how small the table is, how few services touch it, or how simple the migration looks. If rollback requires a backup restore, the blast radius in terms of data integrity is production-wide.

The correct pattern: expand/contract migrations. Phase 1 (additive): add the new column, deploy code that writes to both old and new columns. Phase 2 (transition): verify data integrity, migrate consumers to new column. Phase 3 (cleanup): remove old column. Each phase is a separate Normal change. The risk is distributed across three low-risk changes instead of concentrated in one high-risk change. This is the engineering discipline answer to schema migration risk.

## Blast Radius Assessment

Blast radius is the scope of impact if a change fails. It is the single most important dimension of risk assessment that teams consistently underestimate. Mapping blast radius before the RFC is not optional — it is the prerequisite for writing a credible rollback plan and a credible impact section.

### How to Map Blast Radius

Start with the component being changed. Answer four questions:

1. **What services call this component directly?** These are first-order dependencies. Pull your service dependency map — Splunk service map, APM topology view, architecture diagram, or (worst case) code review of what imports or calls this component.

2. **What services call those services?** These are second-order dependencies. The change you are making propagates one step further. Identify which downstream consumers have no fallback (synchronous callers with no circuit breaker) vs. which have graceful degradation (async callers, feature-flagged paths).

3. **Which customer journeys traverse these services?** This translates technical blast radius into customer impact. A change to the authentication service affects every customer journey that requires authentication — which is most of them.

4. **What is the data volume?** How many customers, transactions per second, or requests per minute flow through the affected services at peak? This translates customer journey exposure into revenue-at-risk and regulatory-at-risk calculations.

### Blast Radius Reduction Strategies

**Feature flags:** The change affects only customers for whom the flag is enabled. Blast radius = (flagged population / total population) × total blast radius. Roll out to 1% → 10% → 50% → 100% with validation gates at each step.

**Canary deployment:** The new version serves a specified percentage of traffic (typically 1–5% initially). Blast radius = canary percentage × total blast radius. Automatic rollback triggers if error rate or latency exceeds defined thresholds.

**Blue-green deployment:** The new version is deployed to a parallel environment and receives zero traffic until cutover. Blast radius at cutover is 100%, but rollback is near-instant (traffic routing switch). Reduces time-at-risk rather than customer scope.

**Maintenance window traffic reduction:** For changes to services where traffic volume can be artificially reduced (batch processing, non-critical async workflows), execute during periods of minimum traffic. Reduces absolute number of customers affected during the failure window.

At T-Mobile scale, a canary deployment of a notification service change that serves 1% of traffic affects approximately 250,000 customers in the failure scenario. The same change deployed fully affects 25 million customers. The blast radius reduction strategy is not an engineering nicety — it is the difference between a learning event and a regulatory notification.

> **What the VP Asks:** "What is the blast radius for this change, and what strategy are we using to reduce it? Have we drawn the dependency map? Who reviewed it?"

If the RFC's blast radius section says "only Service X is affected" for a change to a shared infrastructure component, the CAB should treat the blast radius as unknown — and unknown blast radius is P1 risk.

## Change Freeze Windows

A change freeze is a declared period during which no Normal or Emergency changes are permitted except for critical security patches (with CISO sign-off) or active incident mitigation (with CAB chair sign-off).

### When to Declare a Freeze

Freezes are appropriate for:

- **Peak business periods** where the cost of a failed change exceeds the cost of delayed delivery. In a telecom context: Super Bowl weekend, Black Friday and the surrounding week, major promotional launch weekends, back-to-school periods that drive device activation volume spikes.
- **Major platform migrations** where the change surface is already maximally elevated (e.g., a Kubernetes cluster upgrade, an AWS region migration). Adding discretionary changes on top of a major migration compounds risk exponentially.
- **Audit or compliance windows** where external auditors are reviewing production environments and unexpected changes create audit complications.
- **Holiday blackout periods** where staffing is reduced and incident response capability is degraded. Failed changes during reduced staffing have longer MTTR — the blast radius duration, not just scope, increases.

### Freeze Governance

**Declaration:** CAB chair declares the freeze with a minimum of 2 weeks advance notice. Earlier notice for predictable annual events (holiday blackouts should be declared by October). Notification goes to all engineering leads via the official change management channel.

**Scope of the freeze:** Normal changes are blocked. Emergency path remains available for genuine emergencies (per declaration criteria above). Security patch exception requires CAB chair + CISO written sign-off. Operational necessity exception requires Director + VP sign-off and is not granted for anything that could have been scheduled before the freeze.

**Enforcement:** Change management tooling (ServiceNow, Jira Service Management) is configured to block Normal change approval during the freeze window. The block is automated, not honor-system. Bypass requires manual override with named approvers.

**Duration governance:**
- Freeze windows under 2 weeks: CAB chair authority
- Freeze windows 2–4 weeks: Director approval required
- Freeze windows exceeding 4 weeks: VP approval required; justification reviewed quarterly

> **What the VP Asks when a long freeze is proposed:** "What is the change debt we are accumulating during this freeze, and what is our plan for the change avalanche when it lifts? Have we identified which changes are most time-sensitive and pre-scheduled them for the first week post-freeze?"

Change freezes create pressure. Changes that would have been spread across 4 weeks are queued for release in the first week after freeze lift. A 4-week freeze followed by an unmanaged thaw routinely generates more incidents in the first post-freeze week than the freeze prevented in four. Manage the thaw as deliberately as the freeze.

**Post-freeze management:** The week the freeze lifts, the CAB should hold a special session to sequence the queued changes in priority order. High-risk changes go first (when on-call staffing is at full strength). Low-risk Standard Changes can be batched and released. The first post-freeze week should be treated as a high-change-velocity period with elevated on-call readiness.

## Summary: Risk Classification Quick Reference

| Change Attribute | Standard | Normal | Emergency |
|-----------------|---------|--------|-----------|
| Approval required | CAB-approved catalog entry | RFC + CAB review | CAB chair + domain SME (compressed) |
| Lead time | None (per-instance) | 5 business days minimum | 1 hour from declaration |
| Risk assessment | Required (on file) | Required (in RFC) | Abbreviated; post-hoc full assessment |
| Rollback | Tested, documented | Documented (tested required for CRITICAL/HIGH) | Documented; rollback takes priority over process |
| PIR | Not required (track execution data) | Recommended for P1/P2 services | Mandatory, within 24 hours |
| Director visibility | Quarterly catalog review | Risk tier CRITICAL/HIGH | Every emergency change within 24 hours |

The risk matrix, the change type classification, and the blast radius assessment are not independent tools. They run in sequence: classify the change type → score impact × likelihood → map blast radius → determine required deployment strategy → write the RFC. A Director who builds this sequence into the RFC submission process — as a checklist, a template, a tooling gate — reduces CAB review time because the work has already been done before the RFC arrives.
