# Chapter 7: Change Metrics and Reporting

**Audience**: Director/VP of Operations — this chapter is about what to measure, how to calculate it correctly, and how to use it as a leadership instrument. Metrics that are not actionable are noise.

The right change metrics answer three questions: Are we improving? Where is the risk concentrated? What do we need to change about how we change? Everything else is dashboard decoration.

---

## 1. DORA Metrics: Calculation Methods and Data Sources

DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) metrics are the industry standard for software delivery performance. They were not designed for ITIL change management — they were designed for DevOps pipeline health. Understanding the gap between their original intent and your operational context is required before you instrument them.

### Deployment Frequency

**Definition**: count of successful production deployments per unit of time (day, week, or month depending on your maturity level).

**What to count**: deployments that completed successfully to the production environment and remained in place for at least 1 hour without rollback. Not deployments to staging. Not deployments to dev. Not failed deployments. Not hotfixes that were immediately reverted.

**Data source**: your CI/CD platform is the authoritative source — GitLab, GitHub Actions, Azure DevOps, ArgoCD, or Jenkins depending on your stack. Deployment events with outcome status. If your CI/CD platform does not emit success/failure events, instrument it before relying on the metric.

**Common calculation error**: counting pipeline runs instead of production deployments. A pipeline that runs 50 times to deploy 1 service version to production is 1 deployment, not 50. Your denominator is the number of distinct software versions that reached production in a given period.

**Benchmark targets** (DORA 2024 research):

| Tier | Deployment Frequency |
|------|---------------------|
| Elite | Multiple times per day |
| High | Once per day to once per week |
| Medium | Once per week to once per month |
| Low | Once per month or less |

At T-Mobile telecom infrastructure scale, "Elite" means your highest-velocity microservices — not your billing platform or core network functions. Benchmark by service category, not by org average.

### Lead Time for Changes

**Definition**: time elapsed from the first commit in a change set to successful production deployment.

**What to measure**: wall-clock time, not business hours. A change that waits over a weekend is a change with 48+ hours of lead time — do not normalize that out of the metric or you lose visibility into the real bottleneck.

**Data source**: this requires joining two data sources — git history (first commit timestamp) with CI/CD deployment completion timestamp. Most CI/CD tools measure "pipeline duration" starting at PR merge, which misses all pre-merge development time. If you start measurement at PR open rather than first commit, you are underreporting lead time but getting a more instrumentable proxy.

**Common instrumentation failure**: measuring from PR merge to deployment. This captures CI/CD pipeline efficiency, not the full lead time. A team with 2-hour pipelines but 5-day code review cycles has a 5+ day lead time that the pipeline metric does not reveal.

**Benchmark targets**:

| Tier | Lead Time |
|------|----------|
| Elite | Less than 1 hour |
| High | 1 day to 1 week |
| Medium | 1 week to 1 month |
| Low | 1 month to 6 months |

### Change Failure Rate (CFR)

**Definition**: percentage of deployments that cause a production failure or require rollback.

**Formula**: (deployments causing production failures or rollbacks) / (total deployments) × 100

**Data source**: this requires correlation between two systems — deployment records from your CI/CD platform and incident records from your ITSM or alerting tool (PagerDuty, xMatters, ServiceNow). The correlation is the hard part: matching a deployment event to a causally related incident within a defined lookback window (typically 24 hours for application changes, 48 hours for infrastructure changes).

**Benchmark targets**:

| Tier | CFR |
|------|-----|
| Elite | 0-5% |
| High | 5-10% |
| Medium | 10-15% |
| Low | Above 15% |

### MTTR (Mean Time to Recovery)

**Definition**: average time from incident detection to customer impact returning to zero.

**Data source**: incident management tool (PagerDuty, xMatters) correlated with monitoring/observability data. The detection timestamp is the alert time in your monitoring platform, not the time your on-call engineer acknowledged the page — monitoring-detected, not human-acknowledged.

**Benchmark targets**:

| Tier | MTTR |
|------|------|
| Elite | Less than 1 hour |
| High | Less than 1 day |
| Medium | Less than 1 week |
| Low | 1 week or more |

See [Incident Management] for the precise MTTR calculation methodology, especially the handling of multi-wave incidents and incidents with multiple contributing services.

## 2. Change Failure Rate Calculation: What Counts as a Failure

CFR is the metric most vulnerable to gaming and definitional drift. Establish the definition in policy and audit it quarterly.

### What Counts as a Failure

A deployment is a failure if ANY of the following are true:

- It directly causes a new incident record in your ITSM tool (SEV1, SEV2, or SEV3 attributed to the deployment).
- It is rolled back within 24 hours of deployment, for any reason (not just incidents — a "precautionary rollback" is still a rollback).
- It causes degradation beyond your established SLO thresholds within 1 hour of deployment — even if no incident was opened, if your error rate exceeded SLO for more than 5 minutes, the deployment caused a production failure.

### What Does NOT Count as a Failure

- Expected degradation during canary deployment that was contained within the canary percentage and recovered without incident escalation. A canary that catches a bad deployment is your deployment process working correctly.
- A deployment that exposed a pre-existing bug but did not introduce it. If the bug existed in production before the deployment, the deployment is not the failure — the testing process that missed the pre-existing bug is the failure.
- A deployment that required a post-deployment configuration adjustment (not a full rollback). If the application starts, serves traffic within SLO, and requires a config flag flip — that is a partial success, not a failure.

### The Gaming Risk and Director Controls

Teams under CFR pressure will find definitional gray areas. Common patterns:

- Classifying rollbacks as "configuration adjustments" to keep them out of the failure count.
- Opening no incident record for SLO breaches that were self-resolved within minutes, so the deployment-to-incident correlation produces no match.
- Attributing deployment-caused incidents to "pre-existing infrastructure issues" to remove the causal link.

**Director's control**: audit a random sample of rollback records quarterly — 10% of rollbacks, randomly selected. Review the rollback record, the deployment record, and the incident record if one exists. If you consistently find rollbacks that are not captured in CFR, the metric is wrong. Announce the audit; the announcement alone changes behavior.

### CFR by Team vs. Org Average

Org-level CFR is a lagging indicator you report to executives. Team-level CFR is the leading indicator you act on.

| Team | Deployments | Failures | CFR |
|------|-------------|---------|-----|
| Platform API | 45 | 1 | 2.2% |
| Auth Services | 28 | 0 | 0% |
| Data Pipeline | 12 | 4 | 33.3% |
| Core Network | 8 | 0 | 0% |
| **Org Total** | **93** | **5** | **5.4%** |

The org average of 5.4% looks healthy. The Data Pipeline team at 33.3% is a serious quality problem invisible in the aggregate. Team-level reporting is non-negotiable if you want CFR to drive improvement.

## 3. Change-to-Incident Correlation: % of Incidents Traced to Recent Changes

Industry research is consistent: 70-80% of production incidents are caused by changes. At platform scale — thousands of changes per month across hundreds of services — this is the single most important signal for understanding where your change risk lives.

### Calculation Method

For each incident in a given period:
1. Identify the service(s) affected.
2. Query your deployment records for any change deployed to those services within the lookback window (24 hours for application changes, 48 hours for infrastructure changes).
3. If a change is found: the incident is "change-correlated" — it may or may not have been caused by the change, but the temporal correlation flags it for review.
4. Change-correlated incidents / total incidents × 100 = your change-to-incident correlation rate.

Correlation is not causation — the PIR process ([Chapter 8]) is where causation is established. Correlation is the signal that triggers the review.

### Splunk Query Pattern

If you have CI/CD webhook events landing in Splunk (deployment events with service name, version, timestamp, status) and incident events from PagerDuty or xMatters also in Splunk (incident creation time, service name, severity):

```
index=deployments status=success
| eval deployment_time=_time
| join service_name [
    search index=incidents severity<=2
    | eval incident_time=_time
]
| where incident_time >= deployment_time AND incident_time <= deployment_time + 86400
| stats count as change_correlated_incidents
```

This is the query shape; your field names will differ. The join on service name within a 24-hour window surfaces the correlation. Build this as a scheduled report running weekly and trending month over month.

### Target and Trend

You do not want this correlation rate at zero — that would mean either you have no incidents (impossible at scale) or your correlation detection is broken. You want it measurable and declining quarter over quarter as your change quality improves.

**Detection time target**: less than 15 minutes from deployment to incident detection. If your monitoring is not alerting within 15 minutes of a bad deployment reaching production, your observability is insufficient for the deployment frequency you are running. A team deploying twice a day with 30-minute detection time has a cumulative daily exposure window larger than their deployment frequency justifies.

## 4. Lead Time Decomposition: Where the Delays Are

A single lead time number obscures the structure of the delay. Break it into stages to identify where your leverage is.

### Stage Breakdown

**Stage 1 — Commit to PR Open**: time between first commit and pull request creation. This is engineering work time — active development. Not a process bottleneck unless it is extremely long (days), which would indicate work-in-progress management issues.

**Stage 2 — PR Open to Merge**: code review cycle time. This is an engineering lead problem, not a VP problem.

| Tier | Stage 2 Target |
|------|---------------|
| Elite | Less than 1 day |
| High | 1-3 days |
| Medium | 3-7 days |
| Low | More than 7 days |

Root causes for long Stage 2: reviewer availability, large PR size (PRs over 400 lines take disproportionately longer), unclear review criteria, sequential approval requirements.

**Stage 3 — PR Merge to Pipeline Complete**: CI pipeline duration. This is a DevOps engineering problem.

| Tier | Stage 3 Target |
|------|---------------|
| Elite | Less than 15 minutes |
| High | Less than 30 minutes |
| Medium | Less than 1 hour |
| Low | More than 1 hour |

Root causes for long Stage 3: slow test suites, sequential test stages that could parallelize, flaky tests causing retries, large Docker build contexts.

**Stage 4 — Pipeline Complete to CAB Approval**: change governance wait time. For organizations with weekly CAB and a 5-day RFC submission deadline, this stage alone is 5-10 days. This is the Director's controllable variable.

> **What the VP Asks**
>
> Stage 4 is the leverage point for VP-level change governance decisions. Reducing Stage 4 does not require engineering investment — it requires process design: RFC submission tooling that reduces friction, a Standard Change catalog that removes Stage 4 entirely for routine changes, and a clear-path for low-risk changes to get async approval rather than waiting for the weekly CAB cycle.
>
> If your Stage 4 is 7 days and your total lead time target is 5 days, you have already failed before the change is submitted.

**Stage 5 — CAB Approval to Deployment**: maintenance window scheduling and deployment execution. This is the Release Manager's domain.

Root causes for long Stage 5: maintenance window availability (windows are overbooked), environment conflicts (multiple teams competing for the same window), manual deployment steps that should be automated.

## 5. CAB Efficiency Metrics

CAB efficiency metrics tell you whether your change review process is adding value or adding friction.

| Metric | Formula | Target | Interpretation |
|--------|---------|--------|---------------|
| RFC rejection rate | Rejected RFCs / submitted RFCs × 100 | 3-8% | Below 3%: rubber-stamping. Above 15%: RFC quality too low. |
| RFC deferral rate | Deferred RFCs / submitted RFCs × 100 | 5-10% | Track reason for deferral — patterns reveal training gaps. |
| Avg review time per RFC | Meeting minutes consumed / RFCs reviewed | < 10 min/RFC | Single RFC consuming 45 min = RFC not ready or change too complex to proceed. |
| RFC submission compliance | RFCs submitted by deadline / total RFCs × 100 | > 90% | Late submissions are a leading indicator of planning dysfunction. |
| Standard Change % | Standard changes / total changes × 100 | > 30% (mature) | A growing catalog reduces CAB burden without reducing governance. |

**Deferral rate root cause tracking**: every deferred RFC should have a coded reason. The top three reasons in your first 90 days will tell you what to fix. If "incomplete rollback plan" is the top reason for two consecutive quarters, you need a rollback plan template in your RFC form, not more CAB education sessions.

**The rejection rate paradox**: a CAB with 0% rejection rate is not more efficient — it is less valuable. CAB exists to catch quality and risk issues. A CAB that never rejects an RFC is either reviewing RFCs that are already perfect (unlikely) or not reviewing them critically (likely). The target rejection rate of 3-8% represents CAB adding real review value while not being so conservative that it becomes a bottleneck.

## 7. Maturity Scoring: Assessing Your Change Management Program

Use this model to assess where your organization is today and where it needs to be. Honest assessment is the only useful assessment.

| Level | Label | Characteristics |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| **1** | Ad Hoc | No standard process. Changes documented inconsistently or not at all. No change calendar. CFR unknown. Emergency % above 40%. Most organizations at Level 1 do not know they are at Level 1. |
| **2** | Defined | Standard RFC process exists. CAB meets regularly. Change calendar maintained. CFR measured but not acted on. Emergency % 20-40%. PIRs happen for major failures only. |
| **3** | Managed | All four DORA metrics tracked. Standard Change catalog exists (10-25 entries). CFR declining quarter over quarter. Emergency % below 20%. PIRs completed for most P1/P2 changes. |
| **4** | Optimizing | DORA metrics in High tier across most services. CFR < 10%. Standard Change covers 40-60% of volume. PIR data feeds back into risk classification. Change automation for standard patterns. Team-level CFR tracked and actioned. |
| **5** | Elite | Multiple deployments per day for high-velocity services. CFR < 5%. MTTR < 1 hour. Standard Change covers 65%+ of volume. Progressive delivery (canary, feature flags) is default. Change process is invisible — teams operate within guardrails, not around them. |

### Director Assessment Framework

Answering these three questions produces your maturity roadmap:

**Question 1**: Where is the organization today on this scale? Be honest — the answer is usually one level lower than your team believes it is.

**Question 2**: Where does the organization need to be in 12 months? This is a business-driven answer, not a technical one. If your organization is moving to daily deployments, you need Level 4. If you are in regulated telecom infrastructure with quarterly release cycles, Level 3 with strong audit trails may be the right target.

**Question 3**: What are the 3 investments that close the gap? Typical investments:
- **Level 1 → 2**: RFC template, weekly CAB, ITSM tool configuration. Primarily a process investment.
- **Level 2 → 3**: DORA instrumentation, Standard Change catalog initial build, PIR discipline. Process + tooling.
- **Level 3 → 4**: CI/CD pipeline investment, deployment automation, CAB process reform (reduce Stage 4 lead time). Engineering + process.
- **Level 4 → 5**: Progressive delivery tooling (feature flags, canary frameworks), test infrastructure. Engineering-heavy.

> **What the VP Asks**
>
> In an executive review: "We are currently at Level 3. Our 12-month target is Level 4. The three investments are: (1) Standard Change catalog expansion from 25 to 50 entries — 60 days, no new budget. (2) DORA dashboard in Splunk — 90 days, one engineer-quarter. (3) RFC submission tooling in ServiceNow — 120 days, ITSM team commitment."
>
> That is a maturity roadmap a VP can take to a Board. Not "we're improving our change process" — specific, bounded, measured.
