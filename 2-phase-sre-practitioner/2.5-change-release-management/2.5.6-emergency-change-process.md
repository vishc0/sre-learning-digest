# Chapter 6: Emergency Change Process

**Audience**: Director/VP of Operations — this chapter is about the governance decisions you own, not the execution steps your team follows.

Emergency change process is the pressure valve in your change management system. When it works correctly, it is used rarely, documented completely, and reviewed aggressively. When it fails, it becomes the back door that teams use to bypass governance — and your change failure rate and incident rate both climb as a consequence.

---

## 1. Emergency Change Criteria: What Qualifies

Not all urgency is emergency. The first thing a Director must establish — in writing, in policy — is the precise definition of an emergency change. Ambiguity here is expensive.

### Category A: Incident-Driven

An active production incident (SEV1 or SEV2) where a change is the required mitigation. Three conditions must all be true:

1. The incident is already declared and active in your Incident Management system ([Incident Management] for incident classification criteria).
2. The Incident Commander (IC) has determined that a change to production is the only path to recovery — not a configuration flip, not a cache clear, but an actual change to software, infrastructure, or platform configuration.
3. The change cannot wait for the next Normal change window without extending customer impact beyond acceptable SLO thresholds.

The IC does not self-authorize a Category A emergency change. The IC declares the need; the Emergency CAB process authorizes it. That distinction matters for audit trails.

### Category B: Security-Critical

A CVE with CVSS score ≥ 9.0 where both of the following are true:

1. A patch is available (not just a workaround or advisory).
2. There is evidence of active exploitation in the wild — published exploit code, observed scan traffic, or a confirmed security breach requiring immediate containment.

CVSS ≥ 9.0 alone is not sufficient. A critical CVE in a library you do not expose to the network does not meet the bar. Your CISO defines "active exploitation" — get that definition in writing before you need it.

### Category C: Regulatory/Compliance Deadline

A hard regulatory deadline — GDPR data deletion SLA, PCI-DSS remediation deadline, FCC compliance mandate — where the consequence of missing the deadline is a regulatory violation, not just an internal miss. Legal must confirm the deadline and the consequence in writing. "Our compliance team wants this done by Friday" is not a Category C emergency.

### What Is NOT an Emergency

This list is as important as the categories above. Write it into your policy:

- A planned change whose maintenance window was missed ("we forgot to schedule it").
- A feature that has a business deadline set by product management.
- A fix for a bug that is not impacting production customers.
- A change someone forgot to submit as Normal last week and now has a dependency on it.
- A change that is "urgent" because the team did not plan far enough ahead.

> **What the VP Asks**
>
> Every month in your change review: "What percentage of our changes went through Emergency process last month?"
>
> If it is above 15%, something is broken in the Normal process — not the Emergency process. Emergency overuse is a symptom. The root cause is either Normal process speed (it takes too long), Normal process friction (RFC submission is too hard), or planning dysfunction (teams don't plan changes far enough ahead). You fix the root cause, not the symptom.
>
> Target: Emergency changes should be 5-10% of total volume. Elite organizations run below 5%.

## 2. The Three Emergency Change Scenarios and Different Governance

Each category has a different authorization chain, documentation requirement, and PIR timeline. Governance is not one-size-fits-all in an emergency.

### Scenario A — Incident Mitigation

**Trigger**: IC declares emergency change needed during active incident war room.

**Authorization chain**:
- IC authorizes at the tactical level ("we need to make this change").
- CAB Chair is notified immediately — does not block the change but is informed.
- Domain SME provides technical approval. In a live war room, this is often the technical lead already in the room.
- Two people minimum: IC + domain SME. Both approvals recorded in the incident timeline.

**Documentation timing**: Abbreviated RFC completed during or immediately after the change, within 4 hours of change completion. During the incident, documentation follows the change — it does not precede it.

**PIR**: Within 24 hours. If the incident has its own postmortem scheduled within that window, combine them into a single session. The incident postmortem covers response and recovery; the emergency change PIR covers change quality specifically.

**Key operational reality**: Speed is the variable being maximized in Category A. Every minute of CAB approval delay is minutes of customer impact. Your governance design must make the authorization fast, not eliminate it. A verbal approval with a real-time timestamp in the incident timeline is a valid audit record.

### Scenario B — Security Patch

**Trigger**: CISO or Security team formally declares emergency patch requirement with CVE documentation and exploitation evidence.

**Authorization chain**:
- CAB Chair + CISO co-authorize. The security review IS the CAB review — you are not running two separate processes.
- Security advisory (CVE reference, exploitation evidence, patch source) is included as documentation in the RFC.
- The CISO's security review substitutes for the normal CAB risk assessment on security-specific risk dimensions.

**Documentation timing**: Abbreviated RFC required before the change, unlike Category A. Security patches typically have a few hours of preparation time — use that time for documentation. The "we need to move immediately with no documentation" pressure from security teams is real but manageable: a 30-minute RFC that covers what, why, blast radius, and rollback trigger is sufficient.

**Key operational reality**: Security patches often require broad, simultaneous deployment across all instances — bypassing your standard canary deployment pattern. The reason is sound: a canary deployment pattern leaves 95% of your instances unpatched while the canary runs, which is exactly the window threat actors are looking for. Your deployment runbook for security patches should default to all-at-once unless the security team explicitly authorizes a staged approach.

**PIR**: Within 48 hours. Slightly more latitude than Category A because the urgency is threat-driven rather than incident-active.

### Scenario C — Regulatory Deadline

**Trigger**: Legal or Compliance team confirms in writing that a hard regulatory deadline exists with a specific regulatory consequence for non-compliance.

**Authorization chain**:
- CAB Chair + Legal sign-off. This is the most formal of the three scenarios.
- Full RFC required. The change is "emergency" because of external deadline, not technical complexity — you have time to document properly.
- Same-day CAB convening is required if the timeline is within 48 hours. CAB Chair calls an ad-hoc meeting; full quorum preferred but compressed quorum acceptable.

**Key operational reality**: Category C is the scenario where "emergency" is most often misused. Regulatory deadlines are typically known weeks or months in advance. If you are declaring a Category C emergency 24 hours before a deadline, the problem is planning — not process. Your PIR for a Category C change should always include a root cause question: "When was this deadline first known, and why was the change not submitted as Normal process with adequate lead time?"

**PIR**: Within 5 business days — same timeline as a Normal change PIR. The urgency was external; the change quality review follows standard cadence.

## 3. Compressed CAB: Minimum Approvals, Timeboxed Review

Emergency CAB is not a smaller, slower version of your weekly CAB. It is a different process designed for a different constraint.

**Minimum quorum**: CAB Chair + one domain SME. If you cannot reach the CAB Chair, your policy must name a designated alternate. The alternate must have the same authority as the CAB Chair for emergency decisions — a policy that requires the CAB Chair personally for emergency authorization is a single point of failure.

**Time limit**: 15 minutes. This is a hard constraint, not a guideline. If the change cannot be explained and risk-assessed in 15 minutes, the RFC is not ready. Send the team back to complete the RFC; reconvene when it is ready.

**What gets reviewed in 15 minutes**:

| Question | What You Are Assessing |
|----------|----------------------|
| What is changing? | Scope clarity — do we know exactly what is being modified? |
| Why now? | Emergency justification — does this meet the criteria? |
| What is the blast radius? | Risk — how many services, customers, or systems are affected if this goes wrong? |
| What is the rollback trigger? | Pre-commitment to rollback — at what observable signal do we roll back, and who makes that call? |
| Who is executing the rollback? | Accountability — is there a named person ready to execute rollback immediately? |

**Async approval for remaining CAB members**: Notify within 1 hour of the emergency CAB decision. Allow 2 hours for objections. Silence = consent, for emergency changes only. This is explicitly not your normal change process; document that this consent mechanism exists only for emergency authorization.

**Audit trail requirement**: Every verbal approval must be recorded — name, timestamp, what was approved. In a war room, this means someone is explicitly documenting approvals in the incident record or change record in real time. If your incident command tool (PagerDuty, Slack channel, ServiceNow) has a timeline, use it. The audit trail is the accountability mechanism that makes the compressed process defensible to auditors.

> **Compressed CAB Decision Checklist**
>
> Before authorizing any emergency change, the CAB Chair must confirm:
> - [ ] Emergency category confirmed (A, B, or C) — not convenience or missed deadline
> - [ ] RFC present in abbreviated form (for Cat A, can be in-progress)
> - [ ] Blast radius assessed — scope is understood
> - [ ] Rollback trigger defined — specific observable condition named
> - [ ] Rollback executor named — a specific person, not "the on-call team"
> - [ ] Authorization recorded — names and timestamps in the change record
> - [ ] PIR scheduled — date, facilitator, and participants identified before change executes

## 4. Break-Glass Procedures: Bypassing Standard Controls

Break-glass is distinct from the emergency change process. The emergency change process governs changes to production systems. Break-glass governs access to production systems outside standard access controls — typically for incident recovery.

**Break-glass definition**: a pre-authorized bypass of a specific access control, with a defined scope, requiring a mandatory audit trail.

**Common break-glass scenarios in telecom/platform environments**:

- Production database access without a ticket — required when the incident involves data corruption or schema recovery.
- Deployment to production outside a maintenance window — when the incident requires an immediate hotfix before the next window.
- Service restart without a change ticket — when a service is actively failing and a restart is the fastest stabilization step.
- Elevated Kubernetes namespace permissions — when cluster state requires manual intervention beyond normal RBAC grants.

**Break-glass requirements** — all three are non-negotiable:

1. **Named authorization**: who authorized the break-glass action, in what context, at what time. "The IC authorized database access at 14:37 PST during the SEV1 incident" is sufficient. "I needed access" is not.

2. **Audit log**: every action taken during break-glass is logged with timestamp and actor. In Kubernetes environments, this means audit logging enabled at the API server level. In database environments, this means query logging active for the duration of the access. If the tooling does not produce audit logs, the break-glass procedure must include manual documentation of every action taken.

3. **Post-event review**: within 24 hours of break-glass use, a review documents what was done, why, and what process change prevents the need for break-glass next time. Break-glass for the same reason twice in 90 days is a process failure, not operational urgency.

> **Director Callout: Break-Glass Is Not a Failure Signal**
>
> Break-glass is not a sign of process failure. It is a sign that your emergency process is working correctly — you have a defined, authorized, audited bypass mechanism rather than ad-hoc access grants. What IS a failure:
> - Break-glass without an audit trail
> - Break-glass used to avoid Normal change process for convenience ("it was faster to just do it")
> - Break-glass for the same access type more than twice in a quarter without a process improvement
>
> When you see a spike in break-glass usage, investigate the access request process — not the people using break-glass. If standard access takes 3 business days to approve and break-glass takes 15 minutes, you have an access management bottleneck that will keep driving people to break-glass.

## 5. Post-Hoc PIR: Within 24 Hours, Mandatory Documentation

Emergency change PIR is not optional. It is the accountability mechanism that makes the compressed authorization process acceptable. No PIR = no justification for the compressed process.

**PIR trigger table**:

| Change Category | PIR Deadline | Facilitator | Combined With? |
|----------------|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| Category A (Incident) | 24 hours | Change owner (IC or delegate) | Incident postmortem if scheduled within window |
| Category B (Security) | 48 hours | Change owner | Security team debrief if applicable |
| Category C (Regulatory) | 5 business days | Change owner | Compliance review meeting if applicable |
| Standard Change failure | 48 hours | Change owner + CAB Chair | Standard Change catalog review |

**PIR questions for emergency changes** — these differ from Normal change PIR:

1. **Was the emergency declaration justified?** Which criteria were met? Was the change the only path to resolution, or were there alternatives that were not considered?

2. **Did the change achieve its goal?** Did it resolve the incident? Did the security patch deploy successfully to all instances? Did the regulatory requirement get met?

3. **What was the actual blast radius vs. assessed?** Emergency changes are authorized with compressed risk assessment. Did the actual impact match the estimate? If actual was worse, the risk assessment method needs calibration.

4. **Was the rollback plan needed? If yes, was it executed correctly?** Rollback plan quality is often degraded in emergency RFCs. This question tells you whether rollback documentation standards need to improve even in emergency scenarios.

5. **What process improvement would have prevented this emergency?** This is the most important question. Category A: could better monitoring have caught this earlier as a P3 so a Normal change could have addressed it? Category B: was this CVE on your patching radar before it reached critical exploitation? Category C: when was the regulatory deadline first known?

6. **Should this be added to the Standard Change catalog?** If this emergency was a recurring type of change (restarting a service, applying a known patch type, updating a configuration value), it may be a Standard Change candidate.

**PIR output artifacts**:
- Change quality score (Success / Partial Success / Failure)
- Process improvement action items with named owners and due dates
- Problem record trigger (yes/no — see [Problem Management])
- Standard Change candidate flag (yes/no)

> **Director Responsibility**
>
> Review all emergency change PIRs within the week they are completed. You are looking for patterns, not one-off events. A single Category A emergency is expected operations. Three Category A emergencies from the same team in one month is a signal about that service's stability or that team's change hygiene. The PIR is the data; pattern recognition is your job.

## 6. Emergency Change Metrics: How to Benchmark

| Metric | Formula | Target | Alert Threshold |
|--------|---------|--------|----------------|
| Emergency change frequency | Emergency changes / total changes × 100 | < 10% | > 20% |
| Category A lead time | Declaration to implementation complete | < 4 hours | > 8 hours |
| Category B lead time | CISO declaration to all-instances patched | < 8 hours | > 24 hours |
| Emergency CFR | Emergency rollbacks / emergency changes × 100 | Higher than Normal CFR is expected | Investigate if equal to or below Normal CFR |
| Emergency PIR completion | PIRs completed on time / emergency changes × 100 | 100% | Below 95% |

**The Emergency CFR expectation deserves explanation**: your Normal change failure rate should be lower than your Emergency change failure rate. In Emergency process, you are accepting more risk in exchange for speed. If your Emergency CFR is the same as or lower than your Normal CFR, one of two things is true: your Normal process is imposing unnecessary friction that does not improve quality, or your Emergency documentation is not capturing failures accurately. Investigate both.

**Trend by team, not just by month**: org-level emergency change frequency is a lagging indicator. Team-level frequency is the leading indicator. A team whose emergency rate moved from 8% to 30% over two quarters is the signal you need to act on before it becomes an org-level problem.

## 7. Anti-Pattern: Emergency Change Overuse as Process Bypass Signal

The Emergency process will be gamed. Not out of malice — out of rational self-interest. If Emergency is faster and lower-friction than Normal, and the consequences of using Emergency incorrectly are low, teams will use Emergency.

**How it happens**:

- Teams use Emergency to skip CAB when they miss the RFC submission deadline. ("We ran out of time to submit a Normal change" is a planning failure declared as an emergency.)
- Teams use Emergency to avoid scrutiny on a controversial or high-risk change — they know a Normal CAB review will generate hard questions, so they declare emergency to get compressed, 15-minute approval.
- Teams use Emergency because Normal process is genuinely too slow. This is the legitimate signal buried in the noise. Fix the Normal process; do not institutionalize Emergency as the workaround.

**Detection method**: trend emergency change rate by team, by quarter. Pull it in your change management tool monthly. A team at 35% emergency rate that was at 8% six months ago has either been through real incidents (check: were there incidents? were the emergencies categorized correctly?) or is gaming the process (check: do the PIRs show justified emergency declarations?).

**Director's response to abuse**: this is not a punitive conversation. It is diagnostic.

"Walk me through why this change went through Emergency. What would have needed to be different for it to go through Normal?"

The answer tells you:

- **Planning problem**: "We knew about this change two weeks ago but didn't submit the RFC in time." → process coaching on RFC submission discipline.
- **Process problem**: "The Normal RFC process takes 10 days and we had a 5-day window." → accelerated Normal review path may be needed.
- **Culture problem**: "The team lead thinks CAB review slows them down." → this is a trust and accountability conversation, not a process conversation.

The Emergency process is a controlled exception, not an alternative track. Treat it as such.
