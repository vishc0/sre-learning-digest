## Part 1: ICS Origins — From Wildfire to War Rooms

### The 1970 California Wildfires: A Command Failure Study

In September 1970, a series of wildfires burned across Southern California. Thirteen firefighters died, along with several civilians. The death toll was not caused by an absence of resources — there were sufficient personnel and equipment on the ground. The cause was command failure.

Multiple agencies responded: local fire departments, county agencies, the U.S. Forest Service, the National Guard. Each used different radio frequencies. Each used different terminology for the same objects — one agency's "tanker" was another's "engine." There was no agreed incident commander. Decisions made by one agency were unknown to another. Resources were deployed to the same location by different commanders who did not know about each other. Critical decisions were delayed because no one had clear authority to make them.

The post-event investigation produced a finding that became foundational to emergency management: **the disaster was organizational, not technical.** The engineering resources existed to fight the fires. What did not exist was a command structure capable of coordinating them under time pressure.

The investigation led directly to the development of the Incident Command System (ICS) by FIRESCOPE (Firefighting Resources of Southern California Organized for Potential Emergencies) in the early 1970s, later formalized by the National Incident Management System (NIMS) for nationwide adoption.

### ICS Design Principles

ICS was built on five structural principles, all of which translate directly to software incident response:

**1. Single command authority.** One person holds final authority for the incident. This does not mean one person does all the work — it means one person is accountable for all decisions. Distributed authority in a time-pressured situation produces coordination failure.

**2. Common vocabulary.** Every role, resource, and action has a single agreed name. In the 1970 California fires, agencies could not coordinate because they used different words for the same things. In software incidents, this means: every team uses the same severity definitions, the same role names, and the same status terminology. "The service is degraded" means the same thing to the on-call engineer, the IC, and the status page.

**3. Span of control: 3–7 direct reports.** Each commander or lead is responsible for no more than 7 people, ideally 5. Beyond 7 direct reports, communication quality degrades and a single supervisor becomes a bottleneck. For large SEV1 incidents with multiple investigation threads, this means the IC does not directly manage 12 individual engineers — they manage 3–4 Technical Leads, each of whom manages a small team.

**4. Modular scalability.** ICS activates only the roles needed for the incident. A small fire uses a minimal command structure. A multi-agency disaster activates the full hierarchy. The system scales up and down cleanly because every role is defined and every role has a named person or "not activated" status. Software incident response follows the same principle: a SEV3 needs an IC and maybe one TL; a SEV1 activates the full structure.

**5. Integrated communications.** All communication flows through the command structure. ICS eliminates side-channel decisions made in parallel without the IC's awareness. In software incidents: no one should implement a mitigation on production without the IC knowing.

### How ICS Arrived in Software Engineering

**Google (2003 onward):** Google SRE adopted ICS principles when designing their incident response framework, documented in the Site Reliability Engineering book (Beyer et al., 2016). Google's adaptation specifically defined the Incident Commander as separate from the technical responders, carried the span-of-control principle into multi-TL SEV1 structures, and formalized the Communications Lead as a distinct role.

**PagerDuty (2014 onward):** PagerDuty published their incident response documentation publicly and made it freely available (response.pagerduty.com). Their documentation is one of the most widely adopted open frameworks in the industry. PagerDuty's framework closely follows ICS structure adapted for software operations.

**Atlassian (2017 onward):** Atlassian standardized IM roles and vocabulary as part of their Jira Service Management and Opsgenie products. Their public-facing incident management guide defines roles, severity levels, and postmortem structure in explicit, adoptable terms.

## Part 2: Role-by-Role Deep Dive

### Role 1: Incident Commander (IC)

**What the IC does:**
- Holds authority over the incident lifecycle from declaration to closure
- Assigns roles at incident open
- Tracks the overall hypothesis and mitigation state
- Makes decisions with incomplete information — acts, does not wait for certainty
- Manages the cadence of the bridge: directs questions, calls pivots, sets check-in intervals
- Owns the decision to escalate to Executive Sponsor
- Declares mitigation when customer impact ends
- Schedules the postmortem before closing the bridge

**What the IC must NOT do:**
- Open a terminal, write a query, or execute a command
- Generate or investigate technical hypotheses
- Spend more than 60 seconds on any single conversation thread without maintaining situational awareness across the full incident
- Make unilateral business decisions that require stakeholder authorization (SLA credits, regulatory notifications)

**What good looks like:**
A well-functioning IC speaks frequently and briefly. They direct the bridge with questions — "TL, what's the current hypothesis?" — and synthesize input into decisions. They update the Scribe in real time. They manage silence by breaking it with directed questions, not by opening an investigation thread of their own. When the team reaches analysis paralysis, the IC imposes a time box: "Give me a recommendation in 3 minutes."

**What failure looks like:**
The IC opens their laptop and starts pulling logs. Within 90 seconds, they are deep in a Splunk query, the bridge has gone silent, three engineers have started separate investigation threads, and no one is driving the incident. This is the single most common IC failure mode. The moment the IC begins technical investigation, they have abandoned command.

**Concrete example:**
During a SEV1 at a major e-commerce platform (Black Friday, 2018), the assigned IC — a senior engineer with deep system knowledge — began investigating a database query spike directly after joining the bridge. Forty minutes later, they had confirmed that a query plan regression was the cause. But during those 40 minutes, no customer communication had gone out, two other engineers had started separate (incorrect) hypothesis threads that consumed additional engineers, and the mitigation (rollback the query plan change) had not been executed because no one had authority to call it. The IC had the right hypothesis. The incident management had failed.

### Role 2: Technical Lead (TL / Ops Lead)

**What the TL does:**
- Directs technical investigation: assigns hypothesis threads to SMEs, tracks their status
- Generates and prioritizes hypotheses based on available data
- Translates technical findings into IC-digestible summaries: "We've confirmed X is not the cause. Current hypothesis is Y. ETA to confirm: 10 minutes."
- Executes or authorizes mitigation actions on production systems
- Manages multiple SMEs simultaneously, applying span-of-control: if more than 5 SMEs are active, TL creates sub-leads

**What the TL must NOT do:**
- Make bridge-level decisions (escalation, communications, incident lifecycle) — those belong to the IC
- Work on a technical thread without reporting status to the IC every 10–15 minutes
- Suppress findings that contradict the working hypothesis

**What good looks like:**
The TL operates at the intersection of investigation and coordination. They run a mental scoreboard: "Thread A (database) — still investigating. Thread B (CDN) — ruled out. Thread C (deployment artifact) — confirmed partial match, 5 more minutes." They push the IC a status update every 10 minutes without being asked.

**What failure looks like:**
The TL gets absorbed in one investigation thread, stops directing others, and becomes an SME. The other SMEs are now without direction. The IC is asking for status updates and getting silence. The investigation has collapsed into one thread with one engineer, and the other resources are idle.

**Concrete example:**
During a SEV1 at a streaming platform, the TL maintained a live hypothesis board in the incident Slack channel: three numbered hypotheses, each with an assigned engineer and a status tag (investigating / confirmed / ruled out). When hypothesis 1 was ruled out at T+12, the TL reassigned that engineer to hypothesis 3 immediately. The IC could track the full investigation state from the Slack channel without interrupting anyone. The incident resolved in 22 minutes. The TL never opened a terminal — they directed the engineers who did.

### Role 3: Communications Lead (Comms Lead)

**What the Comms Lead does:**
- Posts first customer-facing status update within 10 minutes of incident declaration
- Manages status page updates on a defined cadence (typically every 30 minutes for active SEV1s)
- Handles executive escalation: writes the internal executive update (2–3 bullet points, no jargon, focused on customer impact and ETA)
- Shields the technical bridge from inbound pressure — customer success, account managers, executives all contact Comms, not the IC or TL
- Writes the "incident resolved" update when IC declares mitigation

**What the Comms Lead must NOT do:**
- Provide technical assessments without IC approval
- Promise specific ETAs without confirmation from the IC
- Allow executives or customer success to join the technical bridge

**What good looks like:**
The Comms Lead owns the information boundary between the technical response and the business. They translate: "We have identified a database query plan regression and are executing a rollback" becomes "We have identified the root cause and are applying a fix. We expect service to be restored within 15 minutes." They send this externally and update the IC that communication has gone out.

**What failure looks like:**
No Comms Lead is designated. Executives join the technical bridge. The IC is now managing two conversations simultaneously — the technical investigation and the executive pressure. The technical team, aware that executives are on the call, filters its communication. Hypothesis generation slows. The incident drags.

### Role 4: Scribe

**What the Scribe does:**
- Maintains a timestamped decision log: every action taken, every decision made, who made it
- Maintains a hypothesis log: hypotheses being investigated, their status (investigating / confirmed / ruled out)
- Documents timeline as it happens, including what information was available at each decision point
- Produces the raw material for the postmortem timeline

**What the Scribe must NOT do:**
- Interpret or analyze — the Scribe records, does not editorialize
- Delay logging to "understand" what's happening — log in real time, clarify later

**Why the Scribe is not optional:**
Every postmortem reconstruction of a large incident begins with a 60–90 minute session where participants try to remember what happened and in what order. Memory is poor and selective. The Scribe eliminates this by producing a real-time record. Without a Scribe, the postmortem timeline is a reconstruction; with a Scribe, it is a document.

At T+22 minutes into a SEV1, the IC calls a pivot from hypothesis A to hypothesis B. Without a Scribe, this decision evaporates — no one writes it down, because everyone is doing something else. Two hours later, in the postmortem, no one remembers exactly when the pivot happened or what data prompted it. With a Scribe, the postmortem timeline reads: "T+22: IC pivots from DB hypothesis (insufficient evidence after 15min investigation) to CDN hypothesis. Rationale: error pattern is geographically distributed, not query-type distributed."

**Concrete example:**
At a major financial services firm, a SEV1 that lasted 4.5 hours was reconstructed using only the Scribe's log. The postmortem timeline was generated in 20 minutes rather than the typical 2-hour reconstruction session. The log revealed a 17-minute gap between a mitigation action and a check for effectiveness — a gap no one remembered, that turned out to be the longest avoidable delay in the incident. That finding drove a new process rule: effectiveness checks within 5 minutes of any mitigation.

### Role 5: Subject Matter Expert (SME)

**What the SME does:**
- Accepts time-boxed investigation assignments from the TL
- Reports status at regular intervals: "confirmed," "ruled out," or explicit status with remaining ETA
- Brings deep system knowledge to bear on specific hypothesis threads
- Executes mitigation actions authorized by TL/IC

**What the SME must NOT do:**
- Expand scope of investigation without TL authorization
- Go silent for more than 10–15 minutes without a status update
- Represent partial findings as complete — "I think it might be the cache" is not a status; "cache miss rate is 40% above baseline, investigating whether this is cause or symptom" is a status

**The rabbit hole problem:**
SMEs are vulnerable to deep investigation spirals — they find something interesting and pursue it beyond the investigation time box. This is a natural consequence of expertise: the SME knows enough to see depth where others see surface. The TL's job is to enforce the time box and call the SME back if the thread is not confirming.

The report-back contract: every SME investigation thread ends with one of two outcomes:
1. "Confirmed: [specific finding]. Ready for mitigation recommendation."
2. "Ruled out: [what was checked and why it's not the cause]. Request re-assignment."

"Still investigating" without a time bound is not a status update — it is a black hole.

---

### Role 6: Executive Sponsor

**What the Executive Sponsor does:**
- Authorizes business-level decisions the IC cannot make alone: SLA credits, regulatory notifications, press statements, customer-specific outreach
- Unblocks organizational dependencies: if a mitigation requires an emergency change approval that only a VP can authorize, that is the Executive Sponsor's function
- Not on the technical bridge unless invited by the IC for a specific decision

**When the Executive Sponsor is NOT needed:**
SEV3 and SEV4 incidents do not require Executive Sponsor involvement. For SEV2 incidents, Comms Lead handles executive communication — the Sponsor does not need to join. The Executive Sponsor is activated for SEV1 incidents where business decisions are required, or whenever the IC identifies a decision outside their authority.

**The cost of premature executive involvement:**
When executives join the technical bridge without a specific role, they generate questions that the IC must answer, consuming IC bandwidth. They also change the behavior of the technical team — engineers under executive observation filter their communication, slow their hypothesis generation, and are reluctant to call out things they do not know. Executives should be kept in the Comms channel, not on the technical bridge.

## Part 3: Role Scaling by Severity

### Role Activation Table

| Role | SEV1 | SEV2 | SEV3 | SEV4 |
|------|------|------|------|------|
| Incident Commander | Required, dedicated | Required | Required (may be self-commanding) | Optional |
| Technical Lead | Required | Required | Combined with IC often | No |
| Communications Lead | Required | Required | Optional | No |
| Scribe | Required | Required | Recommended | No |
| SME(s) | As needed, multiple | As needed | As needed | No |
| Executive Sponsor | Activated as needed | Not on bridge | No | No |

### Google's Multi-TL Structure for SEV1

For large-scale SEV1 incidents, Google's model assigns one TL per major system layer, all reporting to a single IC. In a typical large-incident structure:

```
IC
├── TL: Application Layer
│   ├── SME: Service A
│   └── SME: Service B
├── TL: Infrastructure / Networking
│   ├── SME: Network
│   └── SME: Load Balancer
├── TL: Data Layer
│   ├── SME: Database
│   └── SME: Cache
└── Comms Lead
    └── Scribe
```

The IC receives status from three TLs, not from a dozen individual SMEs. Span of control is maintained. Each TL manages their investigation thread independently and reports to IC every 10–15 minutes.

### Self-Commanding Protocol for SEV3/SEV4

On small teams, a single engineer may be both IC and TL for lower-severity incidents. The self-commanding protocol:

1. Acknowledge the incident and begin IC log (even if it is just a Slack thread with timestamps).
2. Post initial hypothesis and begin investigation.
3. Update IC log every 10 minutes: "T+10: investigating X. Status: [finding]. Next: [action]."
4. If investigation exceeds 20 minutes without confirmation: escalate to SEV2 and activate a second engineer as TL so you can return to IC function.

The discipline of maintaining the IC log even when self-commanding is what separates structured response from unstructured heroism — and is what makes the postmortem timeline recoverable.

## Part 4: Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: The IC Who Debugs

The most common failure mode in software incident management. The IC — typically a senior engineer with deep system knowledge — cannot resist the pull of the investigation. They open a terminal. They start querying logs. They form a hypothesis and chase it.

The organizational cost: the bridge loses its conductor. Other engineers begin working independently without direction. Hypotheses multiply without prioritization. Communications are delayed because no one is tracking the customer-impact timeline. The incident that could have resolved in 20 minutes drags to 45 because the most experienced engineer is doing individual work rather than coordination work.

The structural fix: ICs who debug need coaching, not criticism. The IC role requires a genuine belief that coordination is the highest-leverage contribution in an incident — that the IC who directs five engineers is more valuable than the IC-turned-engineer who produces one thread of investigation. This is counterintuitive for senior engineers who have been rewarded throughout their careers for individual technical excellence. It requires explicit training and reinforcement.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Bridge Overcrowding

A SEV1 fires. Every senior engineer who might be relevant joins the bridge. The bridge has 18 participants. Three simultaneous hypothesis threads are happening in parallel across three Slack channels. The IC cannot track them all. No one is sure which threads are active and which are completed. Two engineers are unknowingly investigating the same thing.

The fix: the IC and TL are responsible for bridge membership. Engineers who are not assigned to an active hypothesis thread should leave the bridge and wait to be called on. Observers who joined "to stay informed" should follow the Comms lead's status channel. The technical bridge is for active responders, not for interested parties.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Silent Expert Syndrome

A database engineer joins the bridge 5 minutes into a SEV1. They have an immediate hypothesis based on a pattern they recognize from six months ago. They do not speak up because: the IC has not called on them, the working hypothesis is different from theirs, or they are not certain enough to contradict the current theory.

Thirty minutes later, when the original hypothesis is ruled out, the IC finally asks the database engineer directly. They share the hypothesis. It confirms in 8 minutes. The incident resolves. Thirty-two minutes of customer impact was silent expertise.

The IC's responsibility: never leave an SME silent for more than 10 minutes. Direct questions: "Database engineer — you've been on this call for 15 minutes. What are you seeing? What would you check first?"

The SME's responsibility: if you have a hypothesis, surface it immediately. "I want to flag an alternative: this pattern matches a sequence table exhaustion issue we saw in March. Can I have 5 minutes to check?" You do not need to be certain. You need to be heard.

### Anti-Pattern 4: No Scribe, Decisions Evaporate

Without a Scribe, every significant decision made during the incident exists only in the short-term memory of the participants. When the postmortem asks "why did we pivot from hypothesis A to hypothesis B at 35 minutes?" the answer is hazy reconstruction. When it asks "who authorized the database flush?" no one clearly remembers.

This is not a memory failure — it is a system design failure. Engineers on a high-pressure bridge call are cognitively occupied with their investigation threads. Expecting them to simultaneously maintain a decision log is expecting cognitive multitasking in a high-stress environment. It does not work. The Scribe role exists specifically to take that cognitive load off the responders.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Severity Inflation

When everything is a SEV1, SEV1 means nothing. Severity inflation happens when:
- Engineers declare SEV1 to guarantee attention and resources
- There is no clear, enforced severity definition
- The cultural norm is "better to overshoot than be blamed for undershooting"

The cost: senior ICs are burned out from high-frequency SEV1 activations. The urgency response to a real SEV1 is dulled — everyone has learned that "SEV1" does not reliably mean "major customer impact." Genuine SEV1 incidents receive slower response because the signal has been degraded.

The fix: enforced severity criteria with ownership. If a SEV3 is declared as SEV1, the IC should downgrade it during the bridge opening and document the downgrade. Track over-declared severity as a metric.

### Anti-Pattern 6: The Hero

One engineer knows every system. When anything breaks, they are called. They are the single person who can diagnose and fix the database, the message broker, the load balancer, and the deployment pipeline. They are celebrated for their knowledge and their willingness to respond.

They are also a single point of failure and a bottleneck for organizational learning.

The hero pattern produces: on-call burnout, knowledge that is never documented because the hero resolves incidents faster than anyone can learn from them, and a team that has not developed IM competency because the hero bypasses the process. When the hero leaves or is unavailable, the organization discovers it has been running on borrowed capability.

The structural fix: runbooks, knowledge transfer programs, and deliberate IC rotation that requires non-heroes to lead incidents with the hero in the SME role. This is uncomfortable. It produces slower incident resolution in the short term. It is the correct investment.

## Part 5: Running the Bridge Call

### Opening Protocol (First 2 Minutes, Every Time)

The IC opens every bridge with the same four statements, in under 2 minutes. This is not negotiable — it is a forcing function that ensures every participant begins with shared context:

1. **State the incident and symptoms:** "We have a SEV1. Payment processing is returning 503s at approximately 40% error rate. First alert fired at 14:22 UTC. Customer impact confirmed."

2. **Assign roles out loud:** "I'm [Name], acting as IC. [Name] is TL. [Name] is Comms Lead. [Name] is Scribe. Everyone else: please state your name and specialty so TL knows who's available."

3. **State the working hypothesis:** "Current hypothesis based on alert correlation: a deployment at 14:15 UTC may have introduced a configuration change affecting the payment gateway connection pool. TL, please start there."

4. **Set first check-in time:** "I want a status update in 10 minutes. TL, you're directing. Let's go."

This opening takes less than 90 seconds. It establishes command, context, and cadence. Every bridge call that does not open this way will drift — participants will begin working on different assumptions, with no shared understanding of who is driving.

### Silence Management

Silence on a bridge call is not neutral — it is information. It means either everyone is working (which is acceptable) or no one knows what to do next (which requires IC intervention).

The IC breaks silence every 5–7 minutes with a directed question — not an open question to the room.

**Open question (ineffective):** "Does anyone have an update?"
This produces: one person speaks, others stay silent, the IC gets partial information.

**Directed question (effective):** "[Name], what's the status on the database connection pool check?"
This produces: a specific update from a specific person. The IC can then ask the next person.

Directed questions maintain cadence and prevent the bridge from going silent for 15 minutes while everyone assumes someone else has the next action.

### Decision Forcing Functions

When the team reaches analysis paralysis — multiple hypotheses with insufficient evidence to choose — the IC imposes a time box on the decision.

"We've been evaluating these two hypotheses for 20 minutes. I need a recommendation in 3 minutes. TL: which hypothesis do we pursue first, and why? You don't need to be certain. Give me your best call."

This breaks the paralysis by making it clear that waiting for certainty is not an option. In incident response, a wrong action taken and quickly reversed is often better than no action while the system continues failing.

The time box also creates psychological cover: the TL's recommendation is explicitly made under uncertainty, on a tight timeline, at the IC's direction. This reduces the fear of being wrong — the decision is contextualized as a best-available call, not a certainty.

### Pivot Signals

A hypothesis should be pivoted away from when:
- Investigation has run for 15–20 minutes without confirming evidence
- Evidence found is consistent with the hypothesis but not exclusive to it (does not rule out other hypotheses)
- A new data point arrives that is inconsistent with the current hypothesis

When the IC calls a pivot: "The database hypothesis has been investigated for 18 minutes without confirmation. We're ruling it out and pivoting to the CDN distribution anomaly. TL, reassign resources."

The pivot call is the IC's most important active decision during the investigation phase. Pivoting too early abandons productive investigation. Pivoting too late wastes time on a dead hypothesis. The 15–20 minute rule is a heuristic, not a law — the IC adjusts based on signal strength.

### Bridge Hygiene

**Mute discipline:** All bridge participants who are not actively speaking should be muted. Background noise from open bridges has been documented as a significant cognitive load factor for ICs and TLs. The IC designates a bridge manager for large SEV1 bridges (>10 participants) whose job is to enforce mute compliance and manage bridge access.

**Bridge manager role:** For large SEV1 incidents, appoint a bridge manager who is not the IC. The bridge manager: controls bridge access (rejects observers), enforces mute discipline, manages the queue of people who want to speak (calls on them in order), and handles logistics so the IC can focus on the incident.

**Side channels:** During a large SEV1, multiple Slack channels or chat threads are often active simultaneously. The IC and TL should designate a single primary coordination thread and direct all status updates there. Side channels for sub-investigation are acceptable; they should report their conclusions to the primary thread.

### Closing the Bridge

The IC declares two events separately:

1. **Mitigation declared** when customer impact ends: "Payment processing error rate is below 0.1%. Customer impact is resolved. Mitigation is declared at [time]."

2. **Incident resolution scheduled:** postmortem is scheduled before the bridge closes. "PIR is scheduled for [date/time]. Owner is [Name]. All responders please add the meeting. Bridge is closed."

These two events are separate because system stability may require monitoring after mitigation, and because the postmortem scheduling is a non-negotiable closure step. An incident that closes without a scheduled PIR loses the learning.

## Part 6: On-Call Model Variations

### Team-Owned On-Call

**Model:** Each service team owns on-call for their own services. The team that builds the service is the team that responds to its alerts.

**Advantages:** Highest expertise at time of response. Strong incentive for reliability investment — the team that causes the incident also responds to it at 2 AM. Minimal knowledge transfer required.

**Disadvantages:** On-call load can be high for small teams. Teams with multiple services may have unreasonable rotation depth. Services that cross team boundaries create unclear ownership.

**Best for:** Organizations with strong service ownership boundaries, teams of sufficient size (>5 engineers), and clear service-to-team mapping.

**Example:** Netflix, Spotify, and most mature microservices organizations.

### Platform-Owned On-Call

**Model:** A central SRE or platform operations team acts as first responder for all production alerts. They triage, stabilize, and escalate to service teams when root cause requires service-level expertise.

**Advantages:** Consistent IM process across all services. Reduces on-call burden on individual product teams. Central team develops deep cross-service observability.

**Disadvantages:** Central team becomes a bottleneck. Knowledge transfer between SRE and service team is required for every escalation. Service teams may deprioritize reliability because they do not own on-call.

**Best for:** Organizations where product engineering teams are small or where service boundary ownership is unclear. Also appropriate for organizations in early SRE maturity (level 1–2 on the PagerDuty model).

**Example:** Many traditional enterprises adopting SRE practices, early-stage platform engineering organizations.

### Follow-the-Sun

**Model:** On-call responsibility rotates across global time zones so that all on-call coverage occurs during business hours in the on-call engineer's local zone.

**Advantages:** Eliminates the 2 AM wake-up. On-call engineers are rested, cognitively sharp, and supported by their teammates during their shift.

**Disadvantages:** Handoff complexity. Incidents that span handoff boundaries require explicit handoff protocols — the incoming on-call engineer must receive a full incident state brief. Knowledge gaps at handoff can cause regression.

**Handoff protocol for follow-the-sun:**
1. Outgoing on-call provides written handoff: incident state, current hypothesis, actions taken, outstanding questions, next check-in time.
2. Incoming on-call reads the handoff before joining the bridge.
3. Handoff call: 10 minutes, outgoing engineer briefs incoming, IC confirms the incoming engineer has sufficient context.
4. Outgoing engineer remains available for 30 minutes after handoff for questions.

**Example:** AWS, Google, and any organization with engineering presence across 3+ time zones.

### Hybrid Models

Many organizations combine elements of team-owned and platform-owned on-call:

- **First responder / escalation model:** SRE or NOC handles first response and initial triage; service teams are the second-tier escalation for root cause diagnosis.
- **Ownership by incident severity:** Platform SRE owns SEV1 coordination; service teams own SEV2 and below for their services.
- **Shared rotation:** Platform engineers are embedded in service team rotations; service engineers rotate through the platform on-call periodically.

**Trade-offs:**

| Model | Expertise at Response | On-call Load | IM Consistency | Reliability Incentive |
|-------|----------------------|--------------|----------------|-----------------------|
| Team-owned | High | High per team | Variable | High |
| Platform-owned | Medium | Centralized | High | Low for service teams |
| Follow-the-sun | High (in-hours) | Low per shift | High | High |
| Hybrid | Variable | Balanced | Medium | Medium |

### AIOps and First-Responder Automation

AI-assisted incident management is changing first-responder protocols in three areas:

**Automated triage.** AIOps platforms (PagerDuty AIOps, Moogsoft, Dynatrace Davis AI) correlate alert signals into incident groups, reducing alert noise by suppressing related sub-alerts and presenting a single incident context to the responder. The on-call engineer receives: "5 related alerts grouped into one incident. Probable cause cluster: database connectivity. 3 similar past incidents found."

**Runbook suggestion.** Based on incident signature (alert type, affected service, recent changes), AI systems surface relevant runbooks automatically. The responder does not need to search — the system identifies the most relevant past incidents and their resolutions.

**Noise reduction.** ML-based alert classifiers learn which alert combinations produce true incidents and which produce noise. Over time, transient alerts that self-resolve are suppressed automatically; only alert combinations that historically require human action are escalated.

**The current limit:** AIOps tools are highly effective at first-level triage for known failure patterns. They are poor at novel failures — new failure modes that have not been seen before. The on-call engineer who relies on AI triage for a truly novel incident may be given confidently wrong suggestions. The human remains the IC. AI is an SME with a large historical database, not a replacement for incident command.

**The emerging model:** AI handles noise reduction and alert grouping (reducing MTTA by surfacing the right signal). Human IC handles command, communication, and decision-making. AI SME surfaces historical pattern matches and runbooks. Human SMEs handle novel diagnosis and mitigation design.
