# IC Questions by Phase
**Purpose**: The questions an Incident Commander must ask during each phase — and what it means to have a good answer.  
**How to use**: These are not a script. They are decision gates. If you cannot answer a question, that gap *is* your next action.

---

## Phase 1 — Detection and Declaration (T+0 to T+5)

**What the IC is doing**: Deciding whether this is a real incident, what its severity is, and spinning up the response structure.

| # | Question | Why It Matters | What a Good Answer Looks Like | What a Bad Answer Looks Like |
|---|----------|----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 1 | **Is this alert real or noise?** | Flapping alerts train responders to ignore pages. If you treat noise as real, you waste the team. If you treat real as noise, you delay response. | "Error rate is 67% sustained for 4 minutes. Correlated PagerDuty from two teams. Not a maintenance window." | "The alert fired. I'm not sure yet." — this is an investigation, not an answer. |
| 2 | **What is actually broken vs. what is degraded?** | "Broken" means a customer journey is failing completely. "Degraded" means it is slower or partially failing. These are different severities. | "Checkout endpoint is returning HTTP 500 at 69% rate. Login is unaffected. Browse is slow but functional." | "The service is down." — which part? how much? which users? |
| 3 | **Which customer journeys are affected end-to-end?** | A broken component does not equal a broken journey. Customers care about journeys, not microservices. A broken recommendation service does not mean checkout is down. | "The checkout-to-payment journey is broken for 68% of users. Login and account management are working." | "The recommendations service is erroring." — that is a component, not a journey. |
| 4 | **What severity does this map to?** | Severity drives notification chains. Under-declaring hides the incident from people who could help. Over-declaring burns responders and credibility. | "BRI = 0.54 → SEV2 by BRI. Revenue rate = $2,040/min → SEV2 by revenue. Declare SEV2." | "I think it's a SEV2 but I'm not sure." — use the matrix. Gut feel is not classification. |
| 5 | **Who needs to be paged right now?** | Late notification = delayed mitigation. Every minute of ambiguity in "who owns this" adds to MTTR. | "TL for checkout-service, Comms Lead, Scribe. Payment team on standby — not yet confirmed as involved." | "Let me figure out the problem first, then I'll loop people in." — the TL starts the investigation. You start the structure. |

**Phase 1 gate**: You cannot open the bridge until you have answered questions 4 and 5. You cannot post the declaration message until you have answered question 3.

## Phase 2 — Triage (T+5 to T+30)

**What the IC is doing**: Driving the team toward a leading hypothesis and the first mitigation option. The IC is not investigating — the IC is watching the investigation and preventing dead ends.

| # | Question | Why It Matters | What a Good Answer Looks Like | What a Bad Answer Looks Like |
|---|----------|----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 6 | **What changed in the last 4 hours?** | 80%+ of incidents are caused by a recent change — deployment, config push, infrastructure event, third-party update. This is always the first diagnostic question. | "Checkout-service v2.3.4 deployed at 14:15. Payment-processor dependency updated at 13:50. No infrastructure changes." | "Nothing changed." — something always changed. Keep looking: feature flags, DNS TTLs, certificate expiry, upstream traffic patterns. |
| 7 | **Is the blast radius growing, stable, or shrinking?** | A growing blast radius means the incident is not contained. It may warrant escalation. A shrinking radius means the situation is improving without intervention — important to understand why. | "Error rate was 34% at 14:32, now 69% at 14:50. Blast radius is growing. Consider escalating to SEV1." | "It seems about the same." — get a number. "About the same" is not actionable. |
| 8 | **What is the leading hypothesis — and what would confirm or rule it out?** | The team needs ONE shared hypothesis at a time. Multiple parallel investigations without explicit tracking creates chaos and duplicated effort. | "Leading hypothesis: v2.3.4 deployment broke payment gateway auth token format. Evidence: error messages reference token validation. Test: check payment gateway logs for 400 responses. Rules it out if: gateway logs show no auth failures." | "We're looking at a few different things." — this is three investigations in progress with no shared prioritization. |
| 9 | **Is there a mitigation available without knowing the root cause?** | You do not need to know why something broke in order to stop customer impact. Rollback, traffic rerouting, or feature flagging can reduce impact while diagnosis continues. Google SRE principle: restore first, explain later. | "We can roll back v2.3.4 now. Risk is low — the previous version was stable. IC authorizes rollback." | "We need to understand the root cause before we roll back." — not during the incident. Root cause is for the PIR. |
| 10 | **Have we checked upstream dependencies?** | The most common investigative trap: the service team assumes they are the cause. Often the service is a victim — a shared database, upstream API, or infrastructure layer is failing. | "Checked: payment-processor API health page shows elevated latency from their side. This may not be our code." | "We're focused on our service right now." — you may spend 30 minutes diagnosing a problem that isn't yours. |
| 11 | **Who is affected and who is NOT affected?** | Scoping what is NOT broken is as diagnostic as what is. If SEV2 users are affected but enterprise accounts are not, that narrows the hypothesis significantly. | "Consumer web and mobile are affected. B2B uses a separate payment rail and is completely unaffected. This suggests the consumer payment path, not the shared infrastructure." | "Everyone is affected." — is that true? Segment it. |

**Phase 2 gate**: Before T+30, the IC must confirm: (a) leading hypothesis is named and being tested, (b) at least one mitigation option is identified, (c) impact trajectory is known.

## Phase 3 — Sustained Response / Mitigation (T+30 to T+120)

**What the IC is doing**: Managing a larger bridge, preventing hypothesis lock-in, ensuring mitigation and diagnosis run on parallel tracks, and keeping executives informed.

| # | Question | Why It Matters | What a Good Answer Looks Like | What a Bad Answer Looks Like |
|---|----------|----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 12 | **How long have we been on this hypothesis without confirmation?** | The sunk-cost trap. After 15–20 minutes with no confirming evidence, the hypothesis is likely wrong. The IC's job is to call the pivot before the team has spent 40 minutes on a dead end. | "We've been on the token validation hypothesis for 18 minutes. Logs are not showing auth failures. Call pivot: what is hypothesis B?" | "We're still investigating." — what does "investigating" mean? What would confirm it? How long have you been running? |
| 13 | **Are we running mitigation track and diagnosis track in parallel?** | If everyone collapses onto diagnosis, customer impact continues while the team chases root cause. If everyone collapses onto mitigation, you patch without understanding — the incident recurs. | "TL + 2 engineers on mitigation (rollback verification). 1 engineer on diagnosis (tracing root cause for PIR). Explicit split." | "We're all focused on figuring out what's wrong." — mitigation is being deprioritized. |
| 14 | **Is the mitigation actually working? What metric proves it?** | "I think the rollback helped" is not mitigation. The IC requires observable evidence before declaring improvement. | "Error rate dropped from 69% to 4% within 3 minutes of rollback. Synthetics are passing again. Revenue rate back to baseline." | "It looks better." — what metric? What baseline? What time did it change? |
| 15 | **What have we definitively ruled out?** | Negative findings are as valuable as positive ones. They prevent re-investigating the same dead ends if the incident recurs or if a new responder joins the bridge. | "Ruled out: v2.3.4 deployment (rollback did not resolve). Ruled out: payment processor outage (their health page is green). Current: database connection pool exhaustion." | "I don't know what we've ruled out." — the Scribe should have this. IC should check the decision log every 20 minutes. |
| 16 | **Do we have the right people on the bridge?** | If three hypotheses have been ruled out without a mitigation path, you likely need domain expertise you don't have. Asking for help early is faster than solving everything yourself. | "We've ruled out three hypotheses. Need the database team SME — can we page them? Specific ask: help diagnose connection pool exhaustion in the payments RDS cluster." | "We'll figure it out." — after how long? What's the cost of 30 more minutes of customer impact? |
| 17 | **What does the executive brief say right now?** | The IC owns the executive information loop. If an executive joins the bridge without a briefing, the IC loses 5 minutes explaining context that should have been in a SITREP already. | "SEV2 — Checkout down for 68% of consumer users. Revenue impact ~$2K/min. Investigating database connection pool. No ETA yet. Next update in 20 minutes." | "I haven't had time to update them." — the Comms Lead handles drafting; IC approves. It should take 2 minutes, not 20. |

**Phase 3 gate**: Every 20 minutes — is impact growing, stable, or shrinking? Every 30 minutes — has a SITREP gone out? Every hypothesis needs a time-box. Call pivot at 20 minutes without confirmation.

## Phase 4 — Mitigation and Resolution (T+60 onwards)

**What the IC is doing**: Confirming that customer impact is zero (not just that metrics look green), declaring mitigation, running a stability watch, and closing the incident cleanly.

| # | Question | Why It Matters | What a Good Answer Looks Like | What a Bad Answer Looks Like |
|---|----------|----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 18 | **Is customer impact actually zero — not just metrics?** | Dashboards can show green while customers are still experiencing problems. Synthetic monitors verify the journey works. Real user monitoring confirms no tail-end users are still hitting failures. | "Error rate is 0.4% (within SLO). Synthetics across all affected journeys are passing. No new customer complaints in support queue in the last 10 minutes." | "The dashboard is green." — that's necessary but not sufficient. Did the synthetic transactions pass? Did you check the support queue? |
| 19 | **Has stability held for 30 minutes?** | Premature resolution declarations are how incidents recur within hours. The fix may have worked for 5 minutes before the underlying issue resurfaces. 30 minutes of stability is the minimum hold. | "Metrics have been within SLO since 16:03. It is now 16:33. Stability confirmed." | "It's been stable for a few minutes, I think we can close this." — 30 minutes. Not a few minutes. |
| 20 | **What are the conditions that would trigger recurrence in the next 24 hours?** | The overnight watch requires someone to know exactly what to look for. "Watch the service" is not an instruction. Specific thresholds and escalation criteria are required. | "If error rate on /api/pay exceeds 5% for more than 2 minutes, page the IC. If connection pool utilization exceeds 80%, page the on-call. Otherwise, monitor every 30 minutes." | "Someone keep an eye on it tonight." — who? What threshold? What do they do when they see something? |
| 21 | **Is the PIR scheduled before the bridge closes?** | PIRs scheduled after the bridge closes often don't happen. Fatigue, competing priorities, and calendar conflicts all work against a post-incident retrospective. Booking it before you release the bridge is the single most effective way to ensure it happens. | "PIR on Thursday at 2pm, [Name] facilitating. Scribe will post draft timeline by Wednesday 5pm." | "We'll figure out the PIR timing later." — this is how PIRs don't happen. |

## Phase 5 — Learn / Post-Incident Review (T+3 to T+5 Business Days)

**What the IC is doing**: Facilitating a blameless, timeline-anchored review that produces systemic improvements — not a blame session or a checkbox exercise.

| # | Question | Why It Matters | What a Good Answer Looks Like | What a Bad Answer Looks Like |
|---|----------|----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 22 | **What actually happened — in timeline form?** | The PIR quality depends entirely on an accurate, timestamped timeline. Without it, the group argues about sequence and causality rather than learning from it. | A timestamped log from the Scribe, starting before the first alert fired, through detection, each hypothesis, mitigation, and resolution. | "Here's what happened: the deployment broke something and we fixed it." — no timestamps, no sequence, no causal chain. |
| 23 | **What is the root cause?** | "Root cause" in SRE is always a system property, not a person's action. "Human error" is never the root cause — it is a symptom of a system that allowed the error to have impact. | "Root cause: the deployment pipeline allowed v2.3.4 to reach production without validating the payment token format against the downstream API contract. No automated contract test existed." | "Root cause: the engineer deployed without testing." — this is blame, not root cause. What allowed the deployment to reach production without validation? |
| 24 | **What contributing factors made this worse?** | Root cause explains what broke. Contributing factors explain why it was hard to detect, slow to mitigate, or broader than expected. These are where the most actionable improvements live. | "Contributing: (1) No synthetic monitor on the payment journey meant detection was via customer complaints, not alerting. (2) Runbook for payment failures had not been updated in 8 months and referenced a decommissioned dashboard. (3) On-call responder had not been trained on the payment service architecture." | "I don't know. We just got unlucky." — luck is not a contributing factor. Every incident has contributing factors. |
| 25 | **What did our detection miss or catch late?** | MTTD is often the biggest driver of MTTR. If you cannot detect an incident until customers call support, you have a detection gap — not just a reliability problem. | "MTTD: 12 minutes. Alert threshold was set at 80% error rate; impact started at 34%. Alert fired late. Recommendation: lower threshold to 10% sustained for 2 minutes on P0 journeys." | "The alert fired eventually." — at what error rate? How many customers had already experienced failures before detection? |
| 26 | **What slowed our response?** | Post-incident is the only time you can honestly assess response friction. Runbook gaps, unclear severity criteria, missing contacts, tool access issues — these compound in every future incident. | "Two blockers: (1) Rollback procedure in the runbook was outdated — referenced v1 CLI flags. Responder spent 8 minutes discovering this. (2) Payment team SME's PagerDuty contact was not in the escalation path — we Slack-messaged instead, adding 12 minutes." | "The response went fine." — was MTTR within target? If not, something slowed it. |
| 27 | **What worked well?** | Blameless does not mean only identifying failures. Identifying what worked well prevents organizational overcorrection — "fixing" processes that actually performed correctly. | "The Scribe kept an excellent log — the timeline reconstruction took 20 minutes instead of the usual 2 hours. The IC called a hypothesis pivot at the right moment — we avoided a 30-minute dead end." | Skipping this question. Blameless culture requires acknowledging successes as explicitly as failures. |
| 28 | **What are the action items — and do they meet SMART+R criteria?** | Vague action items do not get done. "Improve monitoring" is not an action item. "Add a synthetic monitor on the /api/pay endpoint that pages PagerDuty if it fails for 2 consecutive minutes — Owner: [Name], Due: [Date], Review: next quarterly SRE review" is an action item. | Every item has: what exactly will be done, who owns it (a person, not a team), due date, and a review checkpoint. | "We'll improve the runbook. Engineering team owns it." — which runbook? Which section? What specifically will be added? Who is the named person? |

## Quick Reference: Questions by Phase

| Phase | Core IC Question |
|-------|-----------------|
| Detection | Is this real? What is broken? How severe? |
| Triage | What changed? What is the leading hypothesis? What can we mitigate now? |
| Mitigation | Is it working? Are we on parallel tracks? Are we pivoting on time? |
| Resolution | Is impact zero — confirmed? Is it stable for 30 min? Is PIR scheduled? |
| Learn (PIR) | What happened? Why? What slowed us? What systemic fix prevents recurrence? |
