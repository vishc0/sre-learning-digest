# Incident Management Metrics and KPIs
**Purpose**: Complete reference for SRE metrics — formulas, measurement methods, industry benchmarks, and improvement levers. Use for: PIR metric population, SLO design, reliability program health tracking, and leadership reporting.

## 1. MTTA — MEAN TIME TO ACKNOWLEDGE

**Definition**: Time from alert firing to a human confirming they have seen it and are working it.

**Formula**:
```
MTTA = Σ(acknowledge_time - alert_fire_time) / incident_count
```

**What it measures**: On-call responsiveness and escalation policy effectiveness. High MTTA means either the person didn't see the alert or the escalation chain took too long to reach someone who did.

**Measurement method**: Pull from PagerDuty / OpsGenie acknowledge timestamps vs. alert trigger timestamps. Exportable via API. Should be measured per rotation (not just average) to identify coverage gaps.

**Industry Benchmarks**:

| Percentile | MTTA         | Context                                  |
|------------|--------------|------------------------------------------|
| Top 25%    | <5 minutes   | Google SRE target; high-performing orgs  |
| Median     | 5–15 minutes | PagerDuty State of Digital Ops 2023      |
| Bottom 25% | >20 minutes  | Understaffed on-call or poor alert routing |
| SEV1 target| <5 minutes   | Industry standard for revenue-critical   |
| SEV2 target| <10 minutes  | Industry standard                        |

**How to improve**:
- Reduce alert noise (APR <0.80 → on-call ignores alerts → MTTA rises)
- Explicit escalation policy with sub-5-min auto-escalation for SEV1
- On-call scheduling that prevents coverage gaps between time zones
- Mobile app configuration (phone call, not just push notification) for P1 pages

## 2. MTTD — MEAN TIME TO DETECT

**Definition**: Time from when the failure begins causing customer impact to when the engineering team becomes aware of it.

**Formula**:
```
MTTD = Σ(detection_time - impact_start_time) / incident_count
```

**What it measures**: Observability coverage quality. A low MTTD means your monitoring is watching the right things at the right granularity. A high MTTD means you are learning about problems from customers, not your own systems.

**OCR (Observability Coverage Rate)**:
```
OCR = (incidents detected by monitoring) / (total incidents) × 100
```
Target: OCR > 95%. If OCR < 80%, you are finding out about most incidents from user complaints.

**Measurement method**: Compare PagerDuty alert timestamp vs. impact start time reconstructed from timeline in PIR. Requires accurate PIR timelines. Track trend, not just average.

**Industry Benchmarks**:

| MTTD              | Interpretation                                       |
|-------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| <1 minute         | Elite: real-time synthetic monitoring + SLO burn rate alerts |
| 1–5 minutes       | High-performing: good metric coverage; SLO burn alerts |
| 5–15 minutes      | Average: threshold-based alerts; symptom detection   |
| >15 minutes       | Below average: reactive; customers detect before monitoring |
| Customer-reported | Critical gap: no monitoring on this failure mode     |

**How to reduce MTTD**:
- SLO burn rate alerts (1h window): fires within 60–90 seconds of sustained error rate increase vs. 3–5 min for threshold alerts
- Synthetic monitoring: active user-path checks every 60 seconds from external vantage points
- Upstream metric alerting: alert on cause (connection pool 80%) before effect (error rate rising)
- Error budget burn alerting: fast-burn alert at 2% budget consumed in 1 hour

## 3. MTTR — MEAN TIME TO RESTORE

**Definition**: Time from customer impact beginning to customer impact reaching zero. NOT "when the dashboard goes green" or "when the alert clears" — it ends when users can successfully complete their journeys.

**Precise definition matters**: A service can appear healthy in metrics while a backlog of failed transactions is still processing. MTTR ends when:
1. Error rate is below baseline for at least 5 consecutive minutes
2. Synthetic monitor passes for at least 3 consecutive checks
3. Customer-visible success rate is at or above baseline

**Formula**:
```
MTTR = Σ(customer_impact_end_time - customer_impact_start_time) / incident_count
```

**What it measures**: Response process effectiveness. Combines detection lag, diagnosis time, decision speed, and execution speed.

**MTTR Decomposition**:
```
MTTR = MTTD + (time to diagnose) + (time to decide) + (time to execute) + (time to verify)
```
Understanding which component dominates tells you where to invest.

**Industry Benchmarks**:

| SEV Level | Elite (DORA 2023)  | High-Performing | Industry Median | Below Average |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| SEV1      | <30 minutes        | 30–60 minutes   | 1–4 hours       | >4 hours      |
| SEV2      | <60 minutes        | 1–2 hours       | 2–8 hours       | >8 hours      |
| SEV3      | <4 hours           | 4–24 hours      | 1–3 days        | >3 days       |

Source: DORA State of DevOps Report 2023; PagerDuty State of Digital Operations 2023.

**How to improve**:
- Runbooks: every P0 alert has a runbook; runbooks are tested; MTTR drops 40–60% (Atlassian research)
- Progressive delivery + auto-rollback: reduces diagnosis and execution time to near-zero for deploy-caused incidents
- Circuit breakers: shed load automatically during failure; buys time for diagnosis without user impact growing
- Game days: simulate the incident before it happens; teams who have practiced respond 2× faster (NIST research)

## 4. MTTF — MEAN TIME TO FAILURE

**Definition**: Average time between customer-visible failures for a given service or system. Inverse of failure rate.

**Formula**:
```
MTTF = Total operating time / Number of failures in that period

Example: 30 days × 1440 min/day = 43,200 min operating time
         3 SEV1/SEV2 incidents in 30 days
         MTTF = 43,200 / 3 = 14,400 min = ~10 days between failures
```

**Relationship to incident frequency**: MTTF is the operational metric that directly feeds SLO calculation. If MTTF is 10 days and MTTR is 2 hours, your availability is roughly 99.17% — which may be below your SLO.

**SLO calculation from MTTF and MTTR**:
```
Availability = MTTF / (MTTF + MTTR)

Example: MTTF = 14,400 min, MTTR = 120 min
Availability = 14,400 / (14,400 + 120) = 99.17%
```

**SLO Theater detection**: If your SLO claims 99.9% but your MTTF and MTTR math produces 99.2%, one of these is wrong:
1. Your SLO measurement excludes scheduled maintenance, partial degradations, or 5xx errors below a threshold
2. Your MTTF/MTTR numbers are wrong (incidents underreported or MTTR measured wrong)
Neither is acceptable.

## 5. ACHIEVABLE SLO

**Formula**:
```
Achievable_SLO = MTBI / (MTBI + MTTR)
Where MTBI = Mean Time Between Incidents (same as MTTF for this purpose)
```

**How to use this to surface SLO theater**:

1. Pull actual MTBI from incident data (last 90 days)
2. Pull actual MTTR from PIR timelines
3. Calculate Achievable_SLO
4. Compare to committed SLO

If committed SLO > achievable SLO: the commitment cannot be kept with current reliability. Either reduce MTTR, increase MTBI (reduce incident frequency), or renegotiate the SLO.

**Example**:
```
Last 90 days: 4 SEV1/SEV2 incidents
Total impact duration: 6 hours = 360 min
MTBI = (90 × 1440) / 4 = 32,400 min between incidents
Average MTTR = 360 / 4 = 90 min
Achievable SLO = 32,400 / (32,400 + 90) = 99.72%
Committed SLO = 99.9% ← CANNOT be met with current reliability
```

## 6. RECURRENCE RATE BY ROOT CAUSE CLUSTER

**Definition**: Percentage of incidents that are caused by the same root cause category within a 90-day window.

**Formula**:
```
Recurrence_Rate(cluster) = (incidents in cluster in period) / (total incidents in period) × 100
```

**How to trend it**: Track by root cause category (from Category taxonomy in industry-causes-and-mitigations.md) across rolling 90-day windows. A rising trend in a category means your action items for that category are not working.

**Reduction targets**:

| Category                  | Target Recurrence Rate (mature SRE org) |
|---------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Deployment failures       | <20% (progressive delivery gates most) |
| Capacity failures         | <10% (capacity planning catches most)  |
| Human error               | <15% (automation reduces, never eliminates) |
| Third-party dependency    | <10% (circuit breakers + multi-vendor) |
| Data / database           | <10% (migration patterns + backup)     |
| Network / infrastructure  | <5%  (multi-AZ + redundancy)           |
| Security-triggered        | <5%  (detect-and-respond posture)      |

**How to use**: Monthly recurrence rate review in SRE team meeting. If a category is above target, audit open action items in that category — they are likely stale or insufficient.

## 7. ALERT PRECISION RATE (APR)

**Definition**: Ratio of actionable alerts to total alerts. An alert is "actionable" if it fires for a genuine issue that requires human response.

**Formula**:
```
APR = actionable_alerts / total_alerts_fired

Where actionable = alert required human intervention AND the alert correctly identified the issue
Not actionable = alert fired but service was healthy (false positive) OR alert fired after the incident was already resolved
```

**Target**: APR > 0.80 (80% of alerts require action). Below 0.70, on-call engineers start to ignore alerts — MTTA rises, MTTD rises, and the monitoring system becomes theater.

**How to measure**:
1. Export all PagerDuty alert events for 30-day period
2. For each alert: was it acknowledged + an incident opened? (actionable) OR acknowledged + no incident? (false positive)
3. APR = incidents opened / total alerts acknowledged

**How to improve**:
- Increase alert thresholds: if an alert fires 30% of the time without action, raise the threshold
- Composite alerts: require 2 conditions to be true simultaneously before firing (reduces false positives)
- Alert-to-runbook mapping: every alert must have a runbook; if no runbook exists, the alert is not ready for production
- Monthly alert audit: review all alerts fired in the last 30 days; delete or tune any with APR < 0.60

**Google SRE benchmark**: Target APR > 0.85. Google internal data shows that APR below 0.70 correlates with 2.3× higher MTTD.

## 8. INCIDENT FREQUENCY TREND

**Definition**: Number of incidents per 30-day period by SEV level. The primary leading indicator of reliability program health.

**Formula**:
```
Incident_Frequency(SEV, period) = count of incidents at that SEV level in that 30-day period
```

**Why it's the primary leading indicator**: All other metrics (MTTD, MTTR, APR) measure response quality. Incident frequency measures how often things break. A program that responds fast to frequent incidents is better than one that responds slowly, but a program that prevents incidents is better than both.

**Target trajectory**: Declining trend in SEV1/SEV2 frequency quarter-over-quarter.

**Benchmark**:

| Org Maturity               | SEV1 incidents/month | SEV2 incidents/month |
|----------------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Elite (DORA top quartile)  | <1                   | <3                   |
| High-performing            | 1–2                  | 3–8                  |
| Average                    | 3–5                  | 8–15                 |
| Below average              | >5                   | >15                  |

Source: DORA State of DevOps 2023; Google SRE Book (Chapter 3).

**How to track**: Dashboard with 90-day rolling bar chart, SEV1 and SEV2 as separate series. Present at monthly engineering all-hands.

## 9. PIR QUALITY SCORE

**Definition**: 5-dimension quality score applied to every completed PIR. Measures whether the PIR will actually prevent recurrence.

**Dimensions and scoring** (1–5 each, total 25):

| Dimension                  | 1 (Poor)                              | 3 (Acceptable)                    | 5 (Excellent)                                   |
|----------------------------|---------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Timeline completeness      | <50% of events sourced                | >75% sourced, some unverified     | All events sourced from system logs; all verified |
| Root cause depth           | Stops at proximate cause              | Identifies contributing causes    | Reaches systemic/organizational root cause       |
| Action item specificity    | No owner or due date                  | Owner + date, vague verification  | Owner + date + specific measurable verification  |
| Blamelessness              | Individual named as cause             | Neutral language, some blame hints| Systems and processes named; no individual blame |
| Prevention effectiveness   | Action items address symptoms only    | Some actions address contributing causes | Actions directly close the root cause gap  |

**Score targets**:
- ≥ 20/25: Excellent — PIR will drive genuine improvement
- 15–19/25: Acceptable — PIR is complete; some gaps in depth or specificity
- < 15/25: Insufficient — PIR must be reworked before closing

**How to trend**: Track PIR quality score per incident per quarter. Rising average score means PIR culture is maturing. Declining score under high incident volume means triage is displacing quality.

## 10. ERROR BUDGET VELOCITY (EBV)

**Definition**: Rate at which the error budget is being consumed relative to baseline. EBV > 1.0 means you are consuming budget faster than expected.

**Formula**:
```
EBV = normalized_error_rate / baseline_error_rate

Where:
  normalized_error_rate = current error rate (rolling 7-day)
  baseline_error_rate   = error rate at 100% error budget consumed / total time

Example:
  SLO: 99.9% (0.1% error budget)
  Budget for 30 days: 0.001 × 43,200 min = 43.2 minutes of downtime
  Current error rate: 0.3% (3× baseline)
  EBV = 0.3% / 0.1% = 3.0 → consuming budget 3× faster than allowed
```

**How to use for change governance**:

| EBV Range  | Interpretation              | Change Policy                                          |
|------------|-----------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| < 0.5      | Budget healthy; accelerate  | All changes approved; accelerate feature delivery      |
| 0.5 – 1.0  | On track                    | Normal change velocity; standard review process        |
| 1.0 – 2.0  | Elevated consumption        | Increased scrutiny on changes; require SRE review      |
| > 2.0      | Budget at risk              | Feature freeze; only reliability work and critical bugs |
| Budget exhausted | SLO breached           | All non-emergency changes halted; incident program priority |

**Why this matters**: EBV makes the error budget concrete for engineering managers. "You're consuming your error budget at 3× the rate" is more actionable than "reliability is degrading."

## 11. TOIL RATIO

**Definition**: Percentage of SRE engineering time spent on toil (manual, repetitive, operational work that does not build lasting value) vs. engineering work (automation, reliability projects, improvements).

**Formula**:
```
Toil_Ratio = toil_hours / total_sre_hours × 100

Where toil = manual, repetitive, scalable-with-service-growth, interruptive operational work
      engineering = automation, reliability improvements, capacity planning, PIR action items
```

**Target**: Toil Ratio < 50%. Google SRE Book policy: if toil exceeds 50%, SRE team must stop accepting new operational work until toil is reduced.

**How to measure**:
- Weekly time-tracking by category (toil vs. engineering) — imperfect but necessary
- Proxy metrics: number of manual runbook executions per week; number of manual alerts per week that required human action
- On-call interrupt rate: pages per on-call rotation (target: <5 pages per week for a healthy service)

**Industry data**:

| Toil Ratio  | Org State                                     |
|-------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| < 30%       | Elite: high automation; SRE team scales freely |
| 30–50%      | Healthy: toil manageable; capacity to improve  |
| 50–70%      | Warning: reactive mode; improvement work crowded out |
| > 70%       | Critical: SRE team is an ops team; no reliability improvement occurring |

## METRICS DASHBOARD LAYOUT RECOMMENDATION

### Weekly SRE Dashboard (5-minute review)
1. Incident frequency this week vs. last week (SEV1/SEV2/SEV3)
2. MTTD and MTTR for incidents this week
3. Open P0 action items (count; names; age)
4. APR for the past 7 days
5. Error budget remaining for current month (all services)

### Monthly Leadership Dashboard (10-minute review)
1. SEV1/SEV2 frequency trend (90-day rolling bar chart)
2. Average MTTR vs. target (by SEV level)
3. Error budget consumption rate (EBV) by service
4. Open action item aging pyramid (0–7, 8–30, 31–60, 61–90, >90 days)
5. PIR quality score average (trailing 90 days)
6. Toil ratio trend (trailing 90 days)
7. OCR (% incidents detected by monitoring vs. customer reports)

### Quarterly Executive Summary (3-minute brief)
1. Incident frequency: Q/Q trend
2. MTTR: Q/Q trend
3. SLO performance: actual vs. committed (Achievable SLO comparison)
4. Top 3 root cause categories (by incident count)
5. Top 3 action items completed that reduced risk
6. Top 3 open risks requiring investment
