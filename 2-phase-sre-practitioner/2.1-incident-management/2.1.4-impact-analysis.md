## Part 1: Three Dimensions of Impact

### Why Three Separate Assessments

Every production incident generates impact across three simultaneous dimensions. These dimensions are related but they diverge — and when they diverge, conflating them produces communication errors and incorrect prioritization.

**The three dimensions**:
1. **Customer impact** — who is affected, how severely, what their experience is
2. **Financial impact** — revenue loss rate, SLA penalty exposure, recovery cost
3. **Business impact** — reputational, operational, compliance, regulatory

**Why they diverge**:

*Example A — Batch regulatory report failure*:  
A nightly job that generates SOX financial disclosures fails silently. Zero users are affected. The customer impact is zero. Financial impact (direct revenue) is zero. Business impact is potentially material — a missed SOX filing can trigger SEC scrutiny and officer liability. This incident scores low on two dimensions and high on one. Treating it as low-priority because "no users are affected" would be a significant error.

*Example B — Recommendation service degradation*:  
The recommendation engine is returning stale results. 35,000 active sessions are affected. Users are annoyed but not failing — they can still browse, search, and purchase. Customer impact (volume): high. Customer impact (severity): low. Financial impact: moderate (browse-to-purchase conversion drops ~12%). Business impact: minimal — no press sensitivity, no compliance exposure. This incident scores high on volume but low on severity — a nuanced picture that requires segmenting the customer impact dimension rather than reporting a single number.

**The discipline**: Always assess all three dimensions independently. State them separately in status updates and in post-incident reports. Never collapse them into a single "impact" number.

## Part 2: Customer Impact Quantification

### Deriving Affected User Count from Traffic Metrics

During an incident, exact user counts are unavailable in real time. You derive an estimate from three inputs available on any service dashboard:

**Inputs**:
- `error_rate` — fraction of requests returning errors (from APM or Prometheus)
- `RPS` — requests per second on the affected service
- `avg_session_duration` — average time a user session stays active (from analytics baseline)
- `avg_requests_per_session` — average requests a session generates during its lifetime (from analytics baseline)

**Formula**:

```
affected_RPS = RPS × error_rate
active_affected_sessions = affected_RPS × avg_session_duration_seconds / avg_requests_per_session
```

**Worked example**:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| RPS | 5,800 |
| Error rate | 22% |
| Avg session duration | 7 minutes = 420 seconds |
| Avg requests / session | 38 |

```
affected_RPS = 5,800 × 0.22 = 1,276 req/sec
active_affected_sessions = 1,276 × 420 / 38 = 14,105 sessions
```

Reported customer impact: **~14,000 active users** experiencing the failure at the time of measurement.

**Caveats to communicate**:
- This is a point-in-time estimate. Recalculate every 15 minutes.
- Sessions is not the same as unique users — a single user could have multiple sessions (multiple devices, tabs).
- The estimate uses baseline averages for session behavior. Atypical traffic patterns (flash sale, event) can make it inaccurate.

---

### Journey Criticality: Not All Affected Users Are Equal

A useful addition to the raw user count is a severity-weighted customer impact score that distinguishes between users who are completely blocked and users who are merely degraded.

**Journey impact classification**:

| Journey State | Description | Weight |
|--------------|-------------|--------|
| Hard failure | User cannot complete the journey. Gets an error, 500, timeout. | 1.0 |
| Soft failure | User gets a fallback, degraded result, or stale data. Can still transact but with friction. | 0.60 |
| Latency degradation | Journey completes but is significantly slower than SLO. | 0.35 |
| Quality degradation | Journey completes at normal speed but result quality is reduced (stale content, incomplete data). | 0.15 |

**Weighted customer impact**:

```
weighted_customer_impact = affected_users × journey_weight
```

**Example**: 14,000 users experiencing a soft failure on checkout = 14,000 × 0.60 = 8,400 weighted customer impact units. Report both numbers: "14,000 users affected; approximately 8,400 experiencing a materially degraded checkout experience."

---

### Customer Segmentation

Raw user count treats a free-tier user the same as an enterprise customer paying $2M ARR. This is wrong from a business perspective. Always segment the affected population when data is available:

**Segmentation dimensions**:

| Dimension | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------------|
| Customer tier (enterprise / SMB / free) | Revenue concentration; SLA obligations; relationship sensitivity |
| Geography | Regulatory exposure; localized vs. global; time zone for communication |
| Authentication state | Logged-in users are identifiable; anonymous users are not |
| Journey type | Payment path users vs. browse users — very different severity weights |

**Practical approach**: Your analytics or identity platform should have a session-to-tier mapping. During a SEV1/2, the IC should request this query within the first 15 minutes: "How many enterprise sessions are in the affected pool?"

If 500 of 14,000 affected sessions are enterprise tier customers, that changes the severity and communication calculus significantly, even though it is only 3.6% of the affected population.

---

### Customer Minutes of Downtime

**Customer Minutes of Downtime (CMD)** is the single aggregate metric that belongs in the executive report and in the post-incident record. It captures both breadth (user count) and duration (MTTR) in one number.

**Formula**:

```
CMD = affected_users × MTTR_minutes
```

**Example**:  
Affected users at peak: 14,000. MTTR: 47 minutes.  
CMD = 14,000 × 47 = **658,000 customer minutes of downtime**

For context: 658,000 customer minutes = 1,096 customer hours = 45.7 customer days of downtime in aggregate.

That framing — customer days — is the one that lands in an executive briefing. It makes the scale concrete.

**When to use CMD**:
- Final executive brief and post-incident report: always
- Mid-incident status updates: use instantaneous affected_users count instead (CMD is a post-fact metric)
- Year-over-year comparison across incidents: CMD normalized by total user-minutes provides a reliability index

## Part 3: Financial Impact Quantification

### Revenue-Per-Minute Models

The correct revenue model depends on how your system generates revenue. Three common models:

---

**Model 1: Checkout-Path Failure (E-commerce)**

```
revenue_per_minute = daily_GMV / (24 × 60)
effective_loss_per_minute = revenue_per_minute × affected_checkout_fraction × conversion_rate_of_affected_sessions
```

**Example**:  
Daily GMV: $3.2M. Checkout error rate: 35%. Normal checkout conversion rate: 4.2%.

```
revenue_per_minute = $3,200,000 / 1,440 = $2,222/min

effective_loss_per_minute = $2,222 × 0.35 × 1.0 = $778/min
```

Note: Use `× 1.0` for the conversion multiplier on hard failures (users cannot convert at all). Use `× 0.40` for soft failures (conversion drops but does not go to zero).

**At 47 minutes MTTR**:  
Revenue at risk: $778 × 47 = **$36,566** in direct GMV loss, plus indirect impact from abandoned cart recovery rate.

---

**Model 2: Payment-Path Failure (Transaction Processing)**

```
revenue_per_minute = transaction_volume_per_minute × avg_transaction_value × payment_processor_margin
effective_loss_per_minute = revenue_per_minute × affected_fraction
```

**Example**:  
Transaction volume: 420/min. Avg transaction value: $85. Payment processor margin: 1.8%. Affected fraction: 60%.

```
revenue_per_minute = 420 × $85 × 0.018 = $642/min
effective_loss_per_minute = $642 × 0.60 = $385/min
```

---

**Model 3: B2B SaaS (Subscription Revenue)**

```
revenue_at_risk_per_minute = (ARR / (365 × 24 × 60)) × affected_enterprise_fraction
```

Note: For SaaS, revenue is not "lost" per minute — it is contracted. The financial exposure is SLA credit liability, not GMV loss. Calculate that separately (see SLA section below).

**Example**:  
Total ARR: $48M. Affected enterprise customers: 15 of 120 enterprise accounts. Enterprise ARR fraction: 15/120 = 12.5%.

```
revenue_at_risk_per_minute = ($48,000,000 / 525,600) × 0.125 = $91.30/min
SLA_credit_exposure_per_minute = $91.30 × credit_rate_per_contract
```

This number feeds the SLA calculation, not the revenue loss calculation.

---

### SLA Penalty Exposure

SLA credits are the contractual financial penalty for breach. They are separate from revenue loss.

**SLA credit calculation**:

```
monthly_contract_value = ARR / 12
SLA_allowance_minutes = (1 - SLA_target) × 30 × 24 × 60
credit_rate = credit_schedule_from_contract (e.g., 10% of monthly contract for breach)
credit_exposure = monthly_contract_value × credit_rate × affected_customer_fraction
```

**Example**:  
Customer A: $10M ARR / 12 = $833,333/month. SLA: 99.95% = 21.9 minutes/month allowed downtime. Current month: 8 minutes consumed before this incident. Remaining budget: 13.9 minutes.

Incident MTTR: 47 minutes. Budget exhausted at minute 13.9 into the incident. SLA breach: 47 - 13.9 = **33.1 minutes of breach**.

Credit schedule (from contract): 10% of monthly value for any breach.  
Credit exposure: $833,333 × 0.10 = **$83,333 credit owed**.

This is not a trivial number. It belongs in the financial impact section of the post-incident report, and the Customer Success team needs to know before the customer contacts them.

### Regulatory Fine Exposure

For incidents involving regulated data or processes, financial exposure extends to regulatory fines. These are distinct from SLA credits — they are imposed by regulators, not negotiated with customers.

**Reference table** (values as of knowledge cutoff; verify against current regulatory guidance):

| Regulation | Trigger | Fine Structure |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| PCI-DSS | Cardholder data breach or unavailability affecting transaction integrity | $5,000–$100,000/month per violation; network may escalate to card removal |
| GDPR | Personal data breach with notification obligation | Up to 4% of global annual revenue OR €20M, whichever is higher |
| HIPAA | PHI unavailability or breach | $100–$50,000 per violation; max $1.9M/year per violation category |
| SOX | Material weakness in financial reporting controls | No direct fine; triggers restatement, SEC scrutiny, officer liability |
| CCPA | Consumer data breach | $100–$750 per consumer per incident (private right of action) |

**When to engage Legal / Compliance**: During triage, if your cascade check reveals that the failing service touches: cardholder data (PCI), protected health information (HIPAA), EU resident personal data (GDPR), or financial reporting systems (SOX) — notify the Compliance lead immediately. Do not wait for root cause confirmation.

---

### Recovery Cost

Recovery cost is often omitted from impact analysis because it is internal. It belongs in the PIR to build the true cost picture.

**Formula**:

```
recovery_cost = (SRE_hours × SRE_hourly_rate) + (contractor_hours × contractor_rate) + overhead_multiplier
```

**Example**:  
Incident duration: 47 minutes. SREs engaged: 4 (IC + 3 responders). Average loaded hourly rate (salary + benefits + overhead): $180/hr.

```
SRE_cost = 4 engineers × (47/60) hours × $180/hr = $564
```

Add post-incident activities (PIR, follow-up work): 12 person-hours × $180/hr = $2,160.  
Total recovery cost: **$2,724**.

In isolation, this is a rounding error against the revenue impact. But when aggregated across a quarter, recovery costs become a meaningful input to the ROI case for reliability investment.

## Part 4: Business Impact Assessment

### Reputational Impact

Reputational impact is harder to quantify than revenue or SLA exposure, but it is the dimension that executives most frequently ask about after a high-profile incident.

**Scoring framework**:

| Factor | Score (1–5) | Basis |
|--------|------------|-------|
| Social media velocity | 1 = no mentions; 5 = trending on Twitter/X | Monitor social listening tools |
| Press sensitivity | 1 = no press interest; 5 = reporters already covering | Check whether incident is in tech press or industry press scope |
| Customer tier affected | 1 = free users only; 5 = named enterprise, Fortune 500 | From segmentation data |
| Recency of prior incidents | 1 = no incidents in 90 days; 5 = third incident this month | From incident history |

**Composite reputational score** = average of four factors.  

Score 1–2: Low reputational risk — internal communication only.  
Score 3: Moderate — prepare a customer-facing statement; do not publish until confirmed resolved.  
Score 4–5: High — engage Communications immediately; draft customer statement in parallel with resolution.

---

### Operational Impact: Downstream Dependencies

The operational impact dimension asks: what other systems depend on the failing service, and what happens to those systems while this incident runs?

**Downstream dependency mapping** (should be pre-built, not done under incident pressure):

| Failing Service | Dependent Services | Impact If Unavailable |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Payment gateway | Order fulfillment, invoice generation, subscription billing | Orders cannot be fulfilled; invoices cannot be generated |
| Identity service | All authenticated endpoints | All logged-in user journeys fail |
| Inventory service | Checkout, product listing, search | Checkout cannot validate stock; listings show incorrect availability |

**During triage**: Pull the dependency map for the failing service. Identify which downstream systems are at risk. Check their dashboards for early-stage degradation. This is the operational impact assessment.

---

### Compliance Impact

Compliance impact is about which regulatory obligations are at risk during the incident window.

**Compliance risk matrix**:

| Regulatory Domain | Trigger Condition | Notification Obligation | Timeline |
|------------------|------------------|------------------------|----------|
| GDPR | Personal data breach or unavailability | Notify Data Protection Authority | 72 hours from breach awareness |
| HIPAA | PHI breach or unavailability > 4 hours | Notify HHS OCR; notify affected individuals | 60 days from discovery |
| PCI-DSS | Cardholder data environment affected | Notify acquiring bank and card brand | Immediately upon confirmation |
| SOX | Financial reporting system unavailable during close period | Notify Audit Committee | Before next filing deadline |

**Key rule**: Notification obligations are triggered by awareness, not by confirmed breach. If there is a reasonable possibility that regulated data is exposed or unavailable, the notification clock may have already started. Engage Legal immediately — do not make this determination yourself.

---

### The Impact Matrix

For rapid business impact classification, plot the incident on a 2×2 matrix:

```
                    HIGH SCOPE
                    (many users/systems)
         ┌──────────────┬──────────────┐
         │              │              │
  LOW    │   SEV3/4     │   SEV1/2     │
SEVERITY │  Operational │   All-hands  │
(user    │    ticket    │   response   │
 exp.)   ├──────────────┼──────────────┤
         │              │              │
  HIGH   │   SEV2       │    SEV1      │
SEVERITY │  Targeted    │  + Comms +   │
(user    │  response    │   Exec +     │
 exp.)   │              │   Compliance │
         └──────────────┴──────────────┘
                    LOW SCOPE
                    (few users/systems)
```

**Reading the matrix**:
- Top-right (high scope, high severity): Full response — IC, exec, comms, compliance check
- Bottom-right (low scope, high severity): Targeted response — right team, may not require all-hands
- Top-left (high scope, low severity): Operational ticket — prioritize based on trend (is severity rising?)
- Bottom-left (low scope, low severity): SEV4 — log and schedule

## Part 5: Real-Time Impact Communication

### The Executive Update Format

Executive updates during a live SEV1/SEV2 have a strict format: **three sentences maximum, no jargon, no speculation**.

**Template**:

```
Sentence 1 — Current state: What is broken and what is the status.
Sentence 2 — Customer count and experience: How many users affected and what they see.
Sentence 3 — ETA and next update: When we expect resolution and when we will update again.
```

**Example**:  
"Our payment processing service is experiencing elevated errors affecting approximately 14,000 users who cannot complete checkout. We have identified the root cause as a failed database connection pool and are executing a restart; we expect full recovery within 20 minutes. Next update in 15 minutes or immediately upon resolution."

**What not to include in the executive update**:
- Stack traces or log snippets
- Technology names (Kubernetes, Kafka) unless leadership is technical and has asked
- Speculation about root cause if unconfirmed
- Blame language ("the vendor failed", "a deployment broke")
- Confidence-hedged language ("we think", "possibly", "might be") — state what is known and what is unknown separately

---

### The Customer-Facing Impact Statement

Customer-facing communication during an active incident follows a different set of rules from internal communication.

**What to say**:
- What users are experiencing (the symptom, not the cause)
- Which services or features are affected
- That you are actively working on it
- When you will provide the next update

**What not to say**:
- Root cause (you don't know yet, and publishing an incorrect root cause creates more problems)
- How it happened (same reason)
- "Our systems" — prefer "some users may be experiencing" (scope uncertainty is expected)
- Apologies that over-promise (avoid "this will never happen again")

**Example customer-facing statement**:  
"We are currently investigating an issue affecting checkout for some users. Our team is actively working to resolve this. We will provide an update by [time]. Customers who have completed their orders are not affected."

---

### Financial Impact Disclosure

Financial impact numbers (revenue loss, SLA credit exposure) are internal until the incident is resolved and confirmed.

**Disclosure rules**:

| Audience | During Incident | Post-Incident |
|----------|----------------|---------------|
| Engineering / SRE team | Share working estimates freely | Share actuals |
| Engineering leadership | Share order-of-magnitude estimates | Share full report |
| Executive team | Share revenue exposure range | Share full financial impact |
| Customer Success | Notify if SLA breach is confirmed or likely | Provide credit calculation before customer asks |
| Legal / Compliance | Share if regulatory trigger is possible | Share full report |
| Customers | Do not share financial impact | Do not share; Customer Success manages credits |
| Public / press | Never share financial impact | Never share; refer to status page |

---

### The Business Impact Clock

As MTTR extends, different business impact thresholds are crossed. Track these proactively during a long incident:

**Example clock for a checkout service outage with 99.95% SLA and $10M ARR customer**:

| Time Elapsed | Threshold Crossed | Action Required |
|-------------|------------------|----------------|
| T+5 min | SEV1 declaration threshold | Open bridge, page IC |
| T+10 min | External status page update required | Post customer-facing statement |
| T+13.9 min | SLA monthly budget exhausted for $10M customer | Notify Customer Success |
| T+15 min | First executive update due | Send 3-sentence exec brief |
| T+30 min | Reputational risk window opens (press cycle) | Engage Communications |
| T+60 min | PCI-DSS escalation threshold (if applicable) | Notify Compliance |
| T+72 hr | GDPR notification window closes (if applicable) | Legal must have filed or confirmed no obligation |

The business impact clock should be pre-built for your most common incident types and attached to runbooks. Do not construct it from scratch during a live incident.

## Part 6: Post-Incident Impact Report

### Reconciling Estimates with Actuals

Real-time impact estimates are derived from current-state metrics using averages and approximations. Post-incident actuals are derived from complete traffic logs, transaction records, and identity data. They will differ. Reconcile them in the PIR.

**Common reconciliation findings**:

| Estimate Assumption | Actual Finding | Typical Direction |
|--------------------|--------------|--------------------|
| Avg session duration from baseline | Actual sessions were shorter (users abandoned quickly) | Estimate overstated affected users |
| Error rate from APM | Actual error rate from transaction logs was higher (APM sampling) | Estimate understated affected users |
| Revenue model used baseline conversion | Actual conversion for affected users was lower | Revenue impact understated |
| Affected user count from sessions | Actual unique users (deduplicated) was lower | Estimate overstated by session inflation |

**In the PIR**: State both the real-time estimate and the post-incident actual for every impact metric. Show the reconciliation delta and explain it. This builds credibility with stakeholders who received the real-time estimates.

---

### Final Impact Statement for the PIR

**Template**:

```
## Incident Impact Summary

**Incident**: [name/ID]  
**Duration**: [start time] to [end time] = [X] minutes MTTR

### Customer Impact
- Peak affected users: [N] (estimated real-time) / [N] (confirmed post-incident)
- Customer Minutes of Downtime: [CMD]
- Affected journeys: [list]
- Customer tier breakdown: [enterprise N / SMB N / free N]
- Journey impact classification: [hard failure / soft failure / degraded]

### Financial Impact
- Direct revenue at risk: $[amount] (estimated during incident)
- Confirmed revenue impact: $[amount] (from transaction reconciliation)
- SLA credit exposure: $[amount] for [customer name]
- Regulatory exposure: [none / potential GDPR / other]
- Recovery cost (SRE + post-incident): $[amount]
- Total financial impact: $[sum]

### Business Impact
- Reputational score: [1–5] — [rationale]
- Operational: [downstream services affected / not affected]
- Compliance: [obligations triggered / no obligations triggered]
```

### Customer Communication Post-Incident

The post-incident customer communication has a different goal from the real-time statement. It must accomplish three things:

1. **Acknowledge**: Confirm that the incident happened and that we are aware of the customer's experience
2. **Explain**: Provide a plain-language root cause explanation — not a technical deep-dive, but enough for the customer to understand what happened
3. **Commit**: State specific, time-bound actions that prevent recurrence — not "we will improve our processes"

**Example**:  
"On [date] between [time] and [time], some customers experienced errors when attempting to complete a purchase on our platform. This was caused by a failure in our payment gateway integration that prevented checkout completion for approximately 14,000 customers over 47 minutes. We have implemented [specific fix] and will complete [specific prevention work] by [date]. We are issuing service credits per our SLA terms, which your Customer Success Manager will apply to your next invoice."

### How Leading Companies Structure Public Incident Reports

Studying how mature organizations communicate about incidents provides a template for your own organization.

**Amazon / AWS model**:  
- Timeline first, then root cause, then corrective actions
- Specific about what failed and why (service names, region names)
- Quantified customer impact (number of customers affected, duration)
- Corrective action items are concrete and dated
- Tone: factual, no defensive language, no over-apology

**Google / GCP model**:  
- Root cause analysis in plain language
- Timeline of key events
- Lessons learned explicitly labeled
- "What went wrong" and "What went right" both addressed
- Recovery narrative explains the sequence of actions taken

**Stripe model**:  
- Customer impact quantified in business terms (failed payments, not error rates)
- Real-time communication quality acknowledged ("we could have communicated faster at T+12 min")
- Prevention commitments are engineering-specific ("we are adding circuit breakers to X")
- Transparent about what they don't yet know at the time of writing

**Common structure across all three**:

```
1. Summary (2–3 sentences: what happened, impact, duration)
2. Root cause (plain language, 1–2 paragraphs)
3. Customer impact (quantified)
4. Timeline of key events
5. What we are doing to prevent recurrence (specific, dated)
6. Apology (one sentence, sincere, not excessive)
```

**The transparency principle**: Public incident reports that are vague ("we experienced a system issue") generate more trust erosion than incidents that were severe but were communicated transparently. Customers understand that systems fail. What they do not forgive is being kept in the dark.

## Chapter Summary

### Impact Assessment Reference Card

| Dimension | Key Formula | Primary Metric | Report Vehicle |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| Customer — volume | `affected_RPS × session_duration / requests_per_session` | Affected sessions at peak | Status updates + PIR |
| Customer — aggregate | `affected_users × MTTR_minutes` | Customer Minutes of Downtime | PIR + executive report |
| Financial — e-commerce | `daily_GMV / 1440 × error_rate` | Revenue loss per minute | Internal only during incident |
| Financial — SaaS | `ARR / 525600 × affected_fraction` | SLA credit exposure | Customer Success notification |
| Financial — regulatory | Per regulation table | Fine exposure | Legal + Compliance |
| Business — reputation | Average of 4 scored factors | Reputational score 1–5 | Comms engagement trigger |
| Business — compliance | Dependency × regulation matrix | Obligations triggered | Legal notification trigger |

### The Three Commandments of Impact Communication

1. **Separate the three dimensions**: Never report a single "impact" number. State customer impact, financial impact, and business impact separately in every update.

2. **Distinguish estimates from actuals**: During the incident, everything is an estimate. After the incident, reconcile with actual data. State which is which in every communication.

3. **Match the format to the audience**: Engineering gets raw metrics and formulas. Executives get three-sentence summaries with dollar figures. Customers get plain-language descriptions of their experience. Legal gets regulatory trigger analysis. Never send the wrong format to the wrong audience.
