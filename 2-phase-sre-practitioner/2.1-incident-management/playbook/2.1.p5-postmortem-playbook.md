# Postmortem (PIR) Playbook
**Audience**: PIR facilitator, IC, and SRE team leads  
**Purpose**: Run postmortems that produce learning and action — not blame and busywork  
**Usage**: Facilitator uses this to prepare, run, and close out every PIR. SRE manager uses the scoring section for quality review.

---

## Why This Exists

A postmortem is worth running only if it produces two things: a shared understanding of what actually happened (not the simplified narrative), and action items that change the system's failure mode. PIRs that produce neither are not postmortems — they are accountability theater.

The standard for this process: every SEV1 and SEV2 produces a PIR document that a new SRE joining six months from now can read and understand exactly what failed, why the system allowed it, and what was done to close the gap.

---

## Pre-PIR Preparation (Facilitator's Job)

### Timeline: What to Gather Before the PIR

This work happens before the meeting — not in it. If the facilitator arrives at the PIR without a draft timeline, the first 30 minutes of the meeting will be reconstruction instead of analysis.

**Gather these artifacts**:
- Scribe's incident channel log (export to document — don't use Slack as the live source during PIR)
- Alert history: first fire time, acknowledgment time, escalation timeline
- Change log for the 24 hours preceding the incident (deploys, config changes, infra changes)
- Relevant dashboard exports — screenshot or export to static at the timestamp of the incident. Do not use live dashboards in the PIR; metrics shift.
- On-call roster for the incident window
- Customer impact metrics: affected users, error counts, transaction failures, duration per affected journey
- SLA breach data if applicable

**Build the draft timeline** before the PIR:
- Pull from the incident channel log — every timestamped post
- Fill gaps where you know what happened even if it wasn't posted
- Flag gaps where you do NOT know what happened — these become discussion points in the meeting
- Minimum granularity: 5-minute intervals for the core incident window; 15-minute intervals for surrounding context

### Circulate Timeline 24 Hours Before the PIR

Send the draft timeline to all participants with this note:
> "Please review and add any corrections, gaps, or context before we meet. We want to spend our time in the PIR on analysis, not reconstruction."

Participants should annotate the timeline before the meeting. The facilitator incorporates corrections into a clean version before the session starts.

### Setting the Blameless Norm

Put this in the calendar invite — not just said at the start of the meeting:
> "This PIR is a learning exercise, not an accountability review. We are not looking for who made a mistake. We are looking for where the system failed to prevent or limit the impact. Individual actions are examined to understand system design, not to assign blame."

Why the invite matters: people who expect a blame session show up defensively. People who expect a learning session show up with information.

### Attendance: Keep It Small

A PIR with 15 people produces worse outcomes than one with 7. The large meeting creates:
- Social pressure to not say uncomfortable things in front of management
- Significant time spent on background for people who weren't involved
- Diffusion of accountability on action items

**Invite criteria** (max 10-12 people):
- IC from the incident
- TL and active SMEs from the investigation
- Comms Lead if communication gaps are expected to be discussed
- Owners of systems that contributed to the failure mode (even if they weren't on the bridge)
- SRE manager if they were involved or if the PIR is for a high-profile incident

**Do not invite**: people who only want to observe, people whose systems were unaffected, anyone who will need a 15-minute briefing before they can contribute. Send them the PIR document afterward.

## PIR Structure (60–90 Minutes)

### 1. Opening (5 Minutes)

Facilitator runs this — not IC, not SRE manager.

- Restate the blameless norm out loud even though it's in the invite: "We are here to find system gaps, not individual fault."
- Confirm timeline accuracy: "Has everyone had a chance to review the draft timeline? Any corrections before we start?"
- Incorporate any last corrections into the working document
- State the session goal: "By the end of this session, we will have a confirmed timeline, a root cause with contributing factors, and action items with owners and due dates. That is what done looks like."

### 2. Timeline Review (15 Minutes)

Walk the timeline chronologically. Do not skip entries; do not rush.

Facilitator's job during timeline review:
- Ask for clarification on any entry that is ambiguous: "What was the decision-making process here?"
- Surface gaps: "We have nothing logged between 14:30 and 14:55. What happened in that window?"
- Note entries that reveal interesting system behavior — circle back to these in the RCA session
- Do not let timeline review become RCA. When causality analysis starts, say: "Let's park that and come back to it in the RCA section."

Output: a verified timeline with no unexplained gaps >15 minutes for the core incident window.

### 3. Impact Quantification (10 Minutes)

Confirm the actual numbers. These will differ from estimates made during the incident — use actual data.

- **Customer impact**: number of affected users (if measurable), transactions failed, journeys broken
- **Duration**: time from first customer impact to mitigation (use the actual metric, not declaration time)
- **Financial impact**: revenue impact estimate (involve Finance if needed — do not estimate in the PIR)
- **SLA impact**: was an SLA breached? Which one? What is the contract implication?
- **Repeat occurrence**: has this failure mode occurred before? Check the PIR archive.

Why this matters: accurate impact numbers inform priority of action items. Teams fight harder to fix something that cost $200K than something that cost "some customers some friction."

### 4. Root Cause Analysis Session (20–30 Minutes)

**Do not just discuss.** Run a framework. Facilitators who let RCA sessions become open discussion produce narratives, not root causes.

**Choose one framework per PIR — don't mix them**:

#### Option A: 5 Whys
Best for: incidents with a clear chain of causality (one thing led to another led to another).

Start with the symptom. Ask "why" at each level until you reach a systemic gap that could be changed by a policy, tool, process, or architectural decision.

Example progression:
- Symptom: Checkout failed for 40% of users for 45 minutes
- Why 1: The payment service returned 503 errors
- Why 2: The database connection pool was exhausted
- Why 3: A query introduced in the 14:15 deploy was not indexed and caused full table scans
- Why 4: The query was not caught in performance testing
- Why 5: The performance test environment uses a 1% production dataset — the unindexed query was fast on small data

**Root cause**: Performance test environment is not representative of production query patterns. This is a systemic gap — it will produce another incident with a different query.

**Facilitator discipline**: stop at the level where the answer is a system change. "Human error" is not a root cause. "The engineer didn't check performance" is why 3 — keep asking why until you reach the gap that allowed the error to have production impact.

#### Option B: Fishbone (Ishikawa)
Best for: incidents with multiple contributing factors that don't form a clean chain.

Draw the fishbone. Main spine = the effect. Branches = contributing factor categories:
- People (knowledge gaps, unclear ownership, communication failures)
- Process (missing steps, poorly defined steps, steps bypassed)
- Technology (system design, monitoring gaps, deployment tooling)
- Environment (load patterns, external dependencies, infrastructure configuration)

Place each contributing factor on the appropriate branch. The bones with the most factors are where action items should concentrate.

#### Option C: Apollo (Causal Factor Charting)
Best for: complex incidents with multiple simultaneous failure paths, especially where a cascade produced the impact.

Build a causal factor chart: start from each contributing event on the timeline and trace its causal chain forward to the impact. Identify which causal chains were necessary conditions for the incident (removing them would have prevented it) vs. which were contributing but not necessary.

Action items should target necessary condition chains first.

### 5. Detection and Response Gap Analysis (10–15 Minutes)

This section is separate from RCA because the failure mode and the response failure mode are often different problems with different owners and different fixes.

**Detection gap questions**:
- When did the system actually start failing? (From metrics)
- When did we first receive an alert? (From alert history)
- The gap between these two is the detection lag. What caused it?
  - Missing alert coverage?
  - Alert threshold too conservative?
  - Alert fired but was ignored or buried in noise?
  - Alert fired on the wrong signal (symptom instead of cause)?

**Response gap questions**:
- When was the alert acknowledged?
- When was the incident declared?
- When was the first mitigation attempt made?
- When was mitigation achieved?
- Where in the response timeline did we lose the most time?
  - Investigation of a wrong hypothesis?
  - Waiting for an SME who was hard to reach?
  - Missing runbook guidance?
  - Authorization delays?
  - Communication overhead?

For each gap identified: the action item must directly address that specific gap, not a generalization of it. "Improve monitoring" is not an action item. "Add an alert on database connection pool utilization at 70% threshold" is.

### 6. Action Items (15 Minutes)

Every action item defined in this session must leave the room with an owner, a due date, and a verification criterion. If any of these three are missing, it is not an action item — it is a wish.

**Action item quality gate (SMART+R)**:
- **Specific**: what exactly will be built, changed, or implemented?
- **Measurable**: how will we know it is done?
- **Achievable**: is this within the owner's authority and capability?
- **Relevant**: does this directly address a gap identified in the PIR?
- **Time-bound**: due date, not "next quarter"
- **+Responsible**: one named owner, not a team

**Action item categories to cover**:
1. Detection improvement (closes the detection gap)
2. Mitigation speed improvement (closes the response gap)
3. Root cause fix (prevents recurrence)
4. Documentation/runbook update (prevents repeat investigation time)
5. Process change (if a process gap was identified)

**Prioritization within the meeting**: categorize each action item as:
- P1: prevents recurrence of this specific failure mode
- P2: reduces impact or improves detection if it recurs
- P3: general improvement, valid but not urgent

Leave the meeting with P1 items fully formed. P2 and P3 can be refined post-meeting but must have an owner.

## PIR Quality Scoring

Run this scoring after every PIR. The facilitator scores it. The SRE manager reviews scores monthly.

Score each dimension 1–5. Maximum score: 25.

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Scoring Criteria |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Timeline completeness | | 5 = no gaps >15 min; 3 = 1-2 gaps with noted explanation; 1 = significant unexplained gaps |
| Root cause specificity | | 5 = systemic gap identified, not human error; 3 = contributing factor identified, root cause inferred; 1 = "human error" or "process failure" with no specificity |
| Action item quality | | 5 = all items are SMART+R; 3 = some items missing owner or due date; 1 = vague or unassigned |
| Detection gap addressed | | 5 = specific alert/monitoring change proposed with owner; 3 = gap identified, no specific fix yet; 1 = detection gap not discussed |
| Response gap addressed | | 5 = specific MTTR improvement proposed with owner; 3 = gap identified, no specific fix; 1 = response gap not discussed |

**Scoring thresholds**:
- 20–25: PIR meets quality standard
- 15–19: PIR is acceptable but facilitator should revisit open gaps async
- < 15: Facilitator must reconvene a 30-minute follow-up session within 5 business days to close the gaps. This is not optional.

## Action Item Tracking Protocol

### Entry into Tracking System (24 Hours)

All action items from the PIR enter Jira (or equivalent) within 24 hours of the PIR. The IC or PIR facilitator is the DRI (Directly Responsible Individual) for this — not the facilitator's assistant, not "the team."

Each Jira ticket must include:
- PIR reference (incident ID and PIR date)
- Which gap it addresses (detection / response / root cause / documentation / process)
- Owner (a person, not a team)
- Due date
- Verification criterion (how will we know this is done? what test or review confirms it?)

### Weekly Review

Any action item open more than 2 weeks gets IC review. At the 2-week review:
- Is it in progress? Confirm ETA.
- Is it blocked? Identify blocker and escalate if the blocker is organizational (team capacity, prioritization conflict, dependency on another team).
- Is it no longer relevant? Document why and close with a reason.

Items cannot be closed as "won't do" without IC sign-off and a documented rationale in the Jira ticket.

### Monthly PIR Quality Review (SRE Manager)

Each month, SRE manager reviews:
1. Quality scores for all PIRs in the month — trend up or down?
2. Action item closure rate — what percentage are closed on time?
3. Any PIR that scored <15 — did the follow-up session happen?
4. Any action items open >30 days — what is blocking them?

Output: a brief written summary posted to the SRE team channel. Not a meeting — a post.

### Quarterly Recurrence Review

Every quarter, SRE manager (or designated analyst) reviews:
- Which failure modes appeared in more than one incident? (Cluster by root cause, not by service name)
- Which action items from previous PIRs were closed but did not prevent recurrence?
- Which detection gaps appear repeatedly across different services? (Suggests a systemic monitoring gap)

Output: a quarterly failure mode cluster report. This drives program-level investment (e.g., if connection pool exhaustion appears in 4 PIRs across 4 different services, the fix is a platform-level connection pool configuration standard — not 4 separate runbook updates).
