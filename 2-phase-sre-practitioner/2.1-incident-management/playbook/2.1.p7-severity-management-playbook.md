# Severity Management Playbook

**Scope**: Governs severity assignment, escalation, and downgrade during active incidents.  
**Authority**: Incident Commander (IC) has final authority on severity classification at all times.  
**Version**: 1.0  
**Owner**: SRE Manager  

---

## Severity Reference

| Severity | BRI Threshold | Revenue Impact | Customer Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SEV1** | > 0.70 | > $10K/min | Complete failure of checkout, auth, or payment; major enterprise customer confirmed down; data exposure |
| **SEV2** | 0.30 – 0.70 | $1K – $10K/min | Significant degradation affecting a broad user segment; partial failure of critical path |
| **SEV3** | 0.10 – 0.30 | $500 – $1K/min | Localized impact; workaround available; single non-critical feature degraded |
| **SEV4** | < 0.10 | < $500/min | Minimal user impact; cosmetic or edge-case failure; monitoring alert with no customer reports |

**Classification rule**: Classify by the **highest dimension**. If revenue impact says SEV2 but customer impact says SEV1, the incident is SEV1.

**Default bias**: When in doubt, declare higher and downgrade when conditions are verified. A false SEV2 costs 30 minutes of mobilized response. A missed SEV1 costs customers, SLA credits, and executive trust.

## Section 1 — Severity Escalation (Upgrade)

### 1.1 Automatic Triggers — No IC Judgment Required

The IC **must** escalate immediately upon confirmation of any of the following. These are not judgment calls.

**Immediate upgrade to SEV1**:

- [ ] Customer data exposed or potentially exposed (any volume, any type)
- [ ] Complete failure of the checkout flow — users cannot complete purchases
- [ ] Complete failure of authentication — users cannot log in
- [ ] Complete failure of payment processing — transactions not going through
- [ ] A named enterprise customer (tier-1 contract) confirms they are fully down
- [ ] A confirmed security incident involving active exfiltration or unauthorized access

**Immediate upgrade to SEV2**:

- [ ] Error rate on a primary API endpoint exceeds 10% for 5+ consecutive minutes
- [ ] P99 latency on a critical path exceeds 3x baseline for 5+ consecutive minutes
- [ ] A payment or auth failure rate that is non-zero and accelerating

### 1.2 Judgment Triggers — IC Decision Required

These conditions warrant escalation but require the IC to assess context. Document the decision in the incident channel.

**Consider upgrading one severity level when**:

- MTTR is tracking > 30 minutes with no confirmed mitigation path and impact is not contained
- Impact scope is actively spreading beyond the initially identified blast radius
- Revenue loss rate has crossed the threshold for the next severity level
- Two or more independent mitigation attempts have failed
- On-call responders are unable to identify the fault domain after 20 minutes

### 1.3 SEV-to-SEV Escalation Criteria

#### SEV3 → SEV2

Escalate when **any one** of the following is true:

| Condition | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Error rate (primary path) | > 10% sustained for 5 min |
| P99 latency (critical path) | > 3x baseline for 5 min |
| Revenue rate | Crosses $1K/min |
| User-visible failures | Customer reports arriving and accelerating |
| Blast radius | Impact spreading to second service |

#### SEV2 → SEV1

Escalate when **any one** of the following is true:

| Condition | Threshold |
|---|---|
| BRI score | > 0.70 |
| Revenue rate | Crosses $10K/min |
| Critical path availability | Complete failure of checkout / auth / payment |
| Enterprise customer | Tier-1 customer confirmed fully down |
| Data exposure | Any confirmed or suspected data exposure |
| Mitigation | No mitigation path after 30 min active response |

### 1.4 Escalation Notification Chain

When upgrading severity, page the following within **5 minutes** of the upgrade decision.

| Upgrade To | Who Gets Paged | Channel | Method |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SEV2** | On-call SRE + Engineering Manager | `#incident-active` | PagerDuty |
| **SEV1** | On-call SRE + EM + VP Engineering + SRE Manager | `#incident-active` + `#executive-war-room` | PagerDuty + phone bridge |

The IC posts the severity change notification (see Section 5) **before** placing the escalation page.

## Section 2 — Severity Downgrade

Downgrade is **not the reverse of escalation**. Escalation is triggered by impact indicators. Downgrade requires verified evidence that the impact has resolved and has remained resolved.

### 2.1 Downgrade Gate Checklist

The IC must verify **all items** in the relevant checklist before downgrading. Partial verification is not sufficient.

#### SEV1 → SEV2

- [ ] Error rate on all affected endpoints has returned to baseline (< 0.1% or pre-incident P99, whichever is tighter)
- [ ] Synthetic monitors (external probes) are passing for at least 15 consecutive minutes
- [ ] Payment, auth, and checkout flows confirmed operational via synthetic transaction
- [ ] No new customer-impact reports arriving in the last 15 minutes
- [ ] Revenue rate confirmed below $10K/min
- [ ] Data exposure either ruled out or contained and handed off to security team
- [ ] Enterprise customer (if applicable) has confirmed their service is restored
- [ ] **Stability hold**: conditions above have been true for at least **15 minutes**
- [ ] **Approval**: IC and Technical Lead both agree on the downgrade (both must confirm in the incident channel)

#### SEV2 → SEV3

- [ ] Error rate on primary endpoints at or below pre-incident baseline
- [ ] Synthetics passing for at least 15 consecutive minutes
- [ ] Revenue rate confirmed below $1K/min
- [ ] Customer report volume is flat or declining (not zero required, but not accelerating)
- [ ] Root cause is identified and a mitigation or workaround is confirmed active
- [ ] **Stability hold**: conditions above have been true for at least **15 minutes**
- [ ] **Approval**: IC decision only; log in incident channel

#### SEV3 → SEV4

- [ ] User-visible error rate is below 1% and trending to baseline
- [ ] No customer reports arriving in the last 30 minutes
- [ ] Revenue rate below $500/min
- [ ] Impact is confirmed isolated to a non-critical path
- [ ] **Stability hold**: conditions above have been true for at least **15 minutes**
- [ ] **Approval**: IC decision only; log in incident channel

### 2.2 Required Evidence Before Downgrading

The IC must post the following evidence in the incident channel as part of the downgrade decision.

| Evidence Type | Source | Required For |
|---|---|---|
| Error rate graph (last 30 min) | Grafana / Datadog screenshot or link | All downgrades |
| Synthetic monitor status | Monitoring platform link | SEV1→SEV2, SEV2→SEV3 |
| Customer feedback sample | Support ticket count or CX Slack report | SEV1→SEV2 |
| Revenue rate estimate | Finance dashboard or proxy metric | SEV1→SEV2 |
| Root cause statement | One sentence minimum | SEV2→SEV3, SEV3→SEV4 |

### 2.3 Stability Hold Rule

**15-minute hold is mandatory before any downgrade.**

The clock starts when all checklist conditions are first met — not when the fix is deployed. Metrics can look recovered while customer-side caches, retry storms, or upstream backlogs are still resolving. The stability hold catches this.

The IC sets a 15-minute timer in the incident channel at the moment conditions are met. Downgrade does not happen until the timer fires and conditions are still met.

### 2.4 Downgrade Approval Authority

| Transition | Approval Required |
|---|---|
| SEV1 → SEV2 | IC **and** Technical Lead must both confirm in the incident channel |
| SEV2 → SEV3 | IC only |
| SEV3 → SEV4 | IC only |

## Section 3 — Severity Disagreement Protocol

### 3.1 When IC and Technical Lead Disagree

1. IC states their proposed severity and rationale in the incident channel (one sentence each)
2. TL states their proposed severity and rationale (one sentence each)
3. Apply the higher of the two severities immediately — do not deliberate during active impact
4. If the TL's rationale reveals information the IC did not have, the IC updates their assessment
5. Revisit classification at the next status update cycle (15 minutes)

The goal is not consensus — it is speed. Default to higher, re-evaluate quickly.

### 3.2 When an Executive or Stakeholder Pushes for a Different Severity

Executives and stakeholders may request a severity change. This is common in both directions — "make it a SEV1 so we get resources" and "downgrade it, I'm getting too many pages."

The IC's response in either case:

> "Severity is a technical and business risk classification based on [BRI / revenue rate / customer impact]. The current classification is [SEV-X] because [specific reason]. I'll reassess when [specific condition changes]. I'm not able to change the classification based on resource or notification preferences."

**The IC has final authority on severity classification.** No executive may override a severity classification during an active incident. Severity determines the SLA clock, notification obligations, and response commitments — changing it to satisfy a stakeholder preference corrupts the incident record and may create compliance exposure.

If an executive believes the IC is wrong, they may escalate to the SRE Manager. The SRE Manager may override the IC only if the IC has made a factual error in applying the severity criteria.

### 3.3 Escalating a Disagreement

If IC and TL cannot resolve a disagreement within 5 minutes:

1. Escalate to SRE Manager for a fast adjudication call (< 5 min)
2. Continue incident response at the higher severity while adjudication happens
3. SRE Manager documents the adjudication outcome in the incident channel

## Section 4 — Common Mistakes and Anti-Patterns

### 4.1 Downgrading Before the Stability Hold Completes

**Pattern**: The fix is deployed. Metrics look clean. IC declares SEV2 before the 15-minute hold timer fires.

**What happens**: Retry storms, caches warming up, or downstream backlogs cause a secondary spike 8 minutes later. The incident is back at SEV1 but the executive bridge has been stood down and responders have been released.

**Rule**: The fix being deployed does not start the downgrade process. Metrics returning to baseline starts the downgrade process. The stability hold is the gate.

### 4.2 Refusing to Escalate to SEV1 Because "We've Never Had a SEV1 Before"

**Pattern**: Error rates, revenue impact, and customer reports all point to SEV1 criteria. The IC hesitates because declaring SEV1 feels like admitting a major failure, or "we just need another 10 minutes to fix this."

**What happens**: The SLA clock runs without being acknowledged. Executive notifications happen late. The post-incident record shows the team underclassified a major incident.

**Rule**: SEV1 is a factual classification, not a judgment of team performance. If the criteria are met, declare it. The cost of a false SEV1 is a 30-minute mobilization. The cost of a missed SEV1 is SLA credits and eroded executive trust.

### 4.3 Using Severity to Communicate Urgency to Management

**Pattern**: The team doesn't have enough help on the incident. An engineer suggests "let's call it SEV1 so we get more people."

**What happens**: Severity is inflated above what the customer impact warrants. The post-incident record is wrong. Future severity calibrations are anchored on incorrect data. Executives stop trusting the classification system.

**Rule**: If you need more resources, ask for them directly: "I need two more engineers on this bridge." Do not use severity as a resource-request mechanism.

### 4.4 Keeping Severity High After the Crisis Has Passed to Justify Resource Allocation

**Pattern**: The active impact has resolved. The IC keeps the incident at SEV1 or SEV2 because "there's still cleanup to do" or because it helps justify keeping the incident open for the post-incident review.

**What happens**: SLA timers continue to run incorrectly. Responders stay mobilized unnecessarily. The incident record misrepresents when the customer impact ended.

**Rule**: Severity reflects current customer impact, not the complexity of cleanup or the importance of the investigation. Downgrade when impact is resolved. Track follow-up work in a separate ticket.

## Section 5 — Severity Change Communication Templates

### 5.1 Incident Channel — Severity Change Notification

Post this message in the incident channel (`#incident-[ID]`) every time severity changes.

```
**SEVERITY CHANGE**

Previous severity: SEV[X]
New severity:      SEV[Y]
Direction:         [ESCALATION / DOWNGRADE]
Time (UTC):        [HH:MM]
IC:                [@name]

Reason:
[One to two sentences. State the specific condition that triggered the change.
For escalations: name the threshold crossed. For downgrades: name the evidence
reviewed and confirm the 15-min stability hold completed.]

Current status:
[One sentence on what is happening right now — mitigation in progress, monitoring
stability, root cause identified, etc.]

Next update in: [15 / 30] minutes
```

### 5.2 Executive Loop — Severity Change Notification

Send this to the executive Slack channel (`#executive-war-room`) or email distribution for SEV1/SEV2 changes.

```
**Incident Update — Severity Change**

Incident:    [Short title, e.g., "Payment API elevated error rate"]
Incident ID: INC-[XXXXX]
Time (UTC):  [HH:MM]

Severity changed from SEV[X] to SEV[Y].

Customer impact:
[One sentence on what customers are experiencing. Quantify if possible:
"~12% of payment transactions are failing" or "Checkout is fully unavailable."]

Current status:
[One sentence — are we mitigating, monitoring, investigating root cause?]

[ESCALATION ONLY] Immediate actions taken:
- [e.g., VP Engineering paged]
- [e.g., Payment team and SRE on bridge]
- [e.g., Traffic shifted to secondary region]

[DOWNGRADE ONLY] Basis for downgrade:
- Error rate returned to baseline at [HH:MM]
- Synthetics passing for 15 minutes
- Revenue rate below $[X]K/min

Next update: [HH:MM UTC] or sooner if status changes.

IC: [Name] | TL: [Name] | Comms: [Name]
```
