# On-Call Responder Rulebook
**Audience**: On-call SRE receiving a page  
**Purpose**: Your operating rules from page receipt to handoff — what to do, when to do it, and what not to do  
**Usage**: Know Phases 1 and 2 cold before your rotation starts. The rest you can reference in-flight.

---

## Phase 1: Alert Receipt (0–2 Minutes)

### Acknowledge Immediately

Acknowledge the PagerDuty alert as your first action — even if you are mid-task. Acknowledging stops the MTTA clock and signals to the rotation that you are handling it. Acknowledging is not a commitment to investigate alone; it is a commitment to own the next decision.

**MTTA targets**:
- SEV1: acknowledge within 5 minutes
- SEV2: acknowledge within 10 minutes
- SEV3/4: acknowledge within 30 minutes

If you cannot respond substantively within these windows (you are driving, on another call, or otherwise blocked), acknowledge and immediately escalate to your backup. Do not acknowledge and then go silent for 20 minutes.

### 30-Second Reality Check Before Paging Anyone

Confirm the alert is a real signal before pulling other people out of sleep or meetings.

Check these three things only — do not investigate:
1. Is the alert a first-fire or a flapper? (PagerDuty alert history — did this fire 40 times last week?)
2. Are correlated signals present? (Error rate + latency + second team's alert = real signal. Isolated metric blip = noise candidate.)
3. Does this correlate to a known change? (Recent deploy, scheduled batch job, announced maintenance window?)

**Decision gate**:
- Confirmed real: proceed to Phase 2
- Suspected noise: acknowledge, post note in team channel, monitor for 5 minutes — if it resolves, add to noise review queue
- Uncertain: treat as real, proceed to Phase 2 — you can downgrade later; you cannot un-miss a real incident

### Find the Runbook

Every alert should link to a runbook. If the PagerDuty alert doesn't link to one:
- Check the runbook repo by service name
- Check the team wiki
- If no runbook exists: this is an action item for after the incident

### Determine Command Situation

- **Is an IC already assigned?** Check the incident channel or PagerDuty escalation. If yes, join the bridge and report to IC.
- **No IC assigned + SEV1/2**: You must either declare and command (if you have IC authority) or escalate immediately to someone who does.
- **No IC assigned + SEV3/4**: Self-command is appropriate. Work the issue, document in the service's Slack channel, escalate if it grows.

## Phase 2: Initial Assessment (2–10 Minutes)

### The 5-Question Triage Protocol

Work through these in order. Stop when you have enough to post a useful status to the incident channel — you don't need all five answered perfectly before posting.

1. **What is broken?** (Symptom — observable behavior, not a hypothesis)
   - Example: "Checkout API returning 503 on 40% of requests"
   - Not: "I think the database is down"

2. **Who is affected?** (User scope — percentage, segment, region)
   - Check: traffic volume by region, error rate by user tier, customer-facing vs. internal systems

3. **When did it start?** (Timestamp — correlate to change log)
   - Check: alert fire time vs. deploy timestamps in the last 4 hours

4. **What changed recently?** (Causal candidate — don't assume, but note)
   - Check: deploy history, config change log, infrastructure changes, upstream dependency alerts

5. **What is the blast radius?** (Cascade risk — are downstream services showing correlated errors?)
   - Check: APM service dependency map, error rates on services downstream of the broken component

### Cascade Risk Check

Before you decide this is a single-service problem, verify:
- Are there alerts on services that call the broken service?
- Are there alerts on services that share infrastructure with the broken service?
- Is the broken service in the critical path for a high-traffic customer journey (auth, checkout, search)?

If cascade is confirmed or likely: escalate severity and say so explicitly in your channel post.

### Post Your Initial Findings

Within 10 minutes of the page, post this to the incident channel — even if incomplete:

```
INITIAL ASSESSMENT — [HH:MM]
Symptom: [What is broken, observable]
Affected: [Who and what scope — estimate is fine]
Started: [Timestamp — correlate to deploy if possible]
Recent changes: [Deploys/configs in last 4 hours — list them]
Cascade risk: [Yes / No / Unknown — explain briefly]
Current hypothesis: [One sentence — leading theory]
Runbook: [Link if applicable]
Next action: [What I'm doing right now]
```

Do not post this as a DM to the IC. Post it in the incident channel. It feeds the Scribe's timeline and the PIR.

## Phase 3: Active Response (10+ Minutes)

### Operating Under IC Direction

When an IC is assigned, your role is to investigate and report — not to make autonomous decisions.

**Your obligations**:
- Report to IC at the cadence IC sets, not whenever you feel like it
- Do not take mitigation actions without IC authorization. This includes rollbacks, config changes, traffic routing, and restarts.
- If you identify a mitigation action and IC is slow to respond: say on the bridge "I recommend [action] — requesting authorization." Do not act first and tell them after.
- If you are given an action to take: confirm understanding, execute, report outcome in the channel with a timestamp.

**Investigation discipline**:
- Test one hypothesis at a time. Do not run parallel investigations without IC awareness — it creates conflicting actions and unclear causality.
- Each hypothesis you test: state it before testing, then report the outcome. Do not simply report "I checked a bunch of things."
  - Format: "Hypothesis: Redis cache exhaustion. Testing: checking memory and eviction metrics. Result: Redis at 45% memory, no eviction — hypothesis ruled out."
- Time-box your investigation: set an internal alarm for 15 minutes. If you have not confirmed or ruled out your hypothesis in 15 minutes, report back to IC and ask whether to continue or pivot.

### Avoid Side Conversations

All significant findings go to the incident channel, not to the IC via DM. This is not about preference — it is operational discipline:
- The Scribe cannot log what they can't see
- Other responders cannot build on findings they don't know about
- The postmortem timeline depends on the channel record

**Acceptable side channel use**: coordinating logistics (meeting link, quick clarification on a specific tool). Not acceptable: sharing investigation results, hypotheses, or mitigation recommendations outside the main channel.

### Documentation Standard

Every action you take during the incident, post to the channel:
```
[HH:MM] ACTION: Restarted payment-service pod in us-east-1a
RESULT: Error rate dropped from 40% to 8% — partial recovery
NEXT: Monitoring for 5 minutes to confirm trend
```

"I tried some things" is not a log entry. Future-you and the PIR facilitator need specifics.

## Phase 4: After-Hours Escalation

### When to Wake Someone Up

This is a real decision with real consequences — respect the threshold, but don't hide behind it to avoid a hard call:

| Severity | Escalation Rule |
|----------|----------------|
| SEV1 | Always wake the appropriate SME. No exceptions. |
| SEV2 | Wake up if MTTR is tracking >30 minutes and you need domain expertise you don't have. |
| SEV3 | Only if you need a specific person's access or knowledge and no daytime alternative exists. |
| SEV4 | Do not wake anyone. Handle async or defer to business hours. |

**Domain-specific triggers** (always escalate, regardless of SEV):
- Any suspected data breach or security event: wake Security on-call immediately
- Any confirmed financial system impact: wake Compliance lead
- Any customer data exposure: wake IC and Legal simultaneously

### How to Escalate After Hours

Escalation method priority:
1. Voice call (mobile phone)
2. SMS if call is not answered within 60 seconds
3. Push notification via PagerDuty
4. Slack DM is NOT acceptable for SEV1 escalation — people sleep with notifications off

When they answer, you have 15 seconds to give them enough to decide whether to join:
```
"[Their name], this is [your name]. [Service name] is down. [One-sentence symptom].
We've been investigating for [X] minutes. We've ruled out [hypotheses]. 
I need your help with [specific thing — not 'I need you to look at it'].
Bridge is open now. Can you join?"
```

Do not lead with apologies. Do not give them a 5-minute background. They need: service, symptom, duration, what you need from them.

### Escalate Up, Not Around

Escalate to the next level in the rotation chain, not to whoever you know best on the team. Ad-hoc escalation to friendly colleagues:
- Creates scheduling resentment
- Bypasses people who may have context
- Leaves gaps in the on-call structure

If the rotation chain is broken (no one answering), escalate to the IC and let IC make the call on who to pull in.

## Phase 5: Handoff Protocol

### When Handoff Is Required

- **Shift change**: rotation schedule triggers a natural handoff
- **Responder fatigue**: you have been actively engaged on a complex incident for >4 hours
- **Scheduled overlap**: your backup comes online and you are handing primary responsibility

### What a Complete Handoff Contains

Never hand off a live incident with "you're up, good luck." The incoming responder must understand:

1. **Current state**: is customer impact active, mitigated, or resolved?
2. **Working hypothesis**: what does the team currently believe is causing the issue?
3. **Ruled-out hypotheses**: what has been investigated and eliminated? (Saves the incoming responder from repeating work)
4. **Open actions**: what is currently in-flight? Who owns each action?
5. **Next check-in time**: when does IC expect the next status update?
6. **Key contacts**: who are the active SMEs on the bridge right now?

Do not rely on "they can read the channel." Walk them through it verbally. The channel is a log — the handoff is a briefing.

### Minimum Handoff Time: 5 Minutes

Before releasing yourself from the incident:
- Spend at least 5 minutes briefing the incoming responder
- Confirm they have bridge access, channel access, and tool access
- Confirm they understand what they own from this point forward

### Written Handoff Summary (Required for Shifts Crossing Hours)

Post to the incident channel:
```
HANDOFF — [HH:MM]
Outgoing: [Your name] ([IC/TL/Responder role])
Incoming: [Their name] ([same role])

Current status: [one sentence — customer impact state]
Working hypothesis: [current leading theory]
Ruled out: [list — brief]
Open actions: [list with owners]
Next IC check-in: [HH:MM]
Notes: [anything the incoming responder must know that isn't obvious from the channel]
```
