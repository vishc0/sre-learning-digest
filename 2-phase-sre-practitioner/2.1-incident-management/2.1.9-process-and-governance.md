# Chapter 9 — Post-Incident Process and Governance

**Series**: SRE/DevSecOps Interview Preparation — Incident Management  
**Audience**: Principal SRE / SRE Manager candidates targeting Director/VP roles

## Introduction

Running an incident well is table stakes. What separates reactive SRE teams from engineering organizations that improve over time is what happens after the incident closes. Post-incident process is where learning either crystallizes into systemic improvement or evaporates into a shared Slack channel that nobody rereads.

This chapter covers three distinct scopes. First, the mechanics of what must come out of every incident — the three mandatory outputs. Second, how to run a post-incident review that actually produces learning rather than theater. Third, the governance structures that turn individual PIR action items into measurable reliability improvement over time.

For candidates interviewing at Director/VP level: most organizations can describe their postmortem process. Fewer can show that their PIRs produce completed action items, reduced recurrence rates, and measurable MTTR improvement. The governance half of this chapter is what distinguishes a functional SRE program from a documentation exercise.

## Part 1 — The Three Mandatory Outputs of Every Incident

Every incident, regardless of severity, must produce exactly three artifacts before it is considered closed. Not a template for potential future use — actual, completed, reviewed outputs.

### Output 1: The Verified Timeline

The timeline is not a narrative. It is a sequence of timestamped, verified events drawn from system logs, monitoring data, alert history, and corroborated human observations. Every entry must have a source.

**What belongs in a timeline:**
- First anomaly detectable in telemetry (often precedes detection by minutes or hours — this gap is your MTTD problem)
- Alert fired / first human awareness
- Incident declared
- Each hypothesis formed and tested
- Each action taken (with actor and outcome)
- Each escalation
- Mitigation applied
- Customer impact began (often this is before detection — reconstruct from logs)
- Customer impact ended
- Incident closed

**What does not belong in a timeline:**
- "Team investigated." This is a 45-minute gap disguised as an entry. Every investigation has steps. List them.
- Vague entries like "discussed options." What were the options? What was decided?

The timeline serves two purposes: it is the foundation for RCA (you cannot identify cause without sequence), and it is the primary defense against memory distortion. Human memory begins degrading within hours of a high-stress event. The timeline anchors the PIR to what actually happened rather than what participants believe happened.

### Output 2: The Impact Statement

The impact statement quantifies what the incident cost. Vague descriptions ("some users were affected") are not acceptable. Quantify across four dimensions:

**Customer impact**: How many users? What percentage of traffic? Which segments (premium vs. free, geography, device type)? What was the user experience (timeout, error, degraded performance, data loss)?

**Financial impact**: Revenue lost during the outage window. Potential SLA credits owed. Support cost spike (ticket volume × average handle time × cost per agent hour). These numbers exist — find them.

**Business impact**: Which downstream processes were affected? Did the outage affect a deployment, a marketing campaign launch, a compliance deadline? Some incidents have a $50K direct revenue impact and a $500K indirect impact through a disrupted enterprise deal.

**Reputational impact**: Social media volume, press mentions, executive escalations. These are leading indicators of brand erosion that finance cannot easily model but that VPs track.

If you cannot quantify an impact dimension, say so explicitly and state why — not because it is zero, but because the data is unavailable. That data unavailability is itself a finding: add an action item to instrument the missing dimension.

### Output 3: Action Items

Action items are addressed at length in Part 3. The key constraint here: action items are not closed with the incident. They are the incident's outputs that survive into engineering planning. An incident with zero action items is either a false positive or an incident where nobody looked hard enough.

## Part 2 — PIR vs. Postmortem: Why Language Matters

"Postmortem" is the dominant industry term. It is also a poor choice of words for the culture you are trying to build.

The word postmortem implies that something died. In ordinary usage — medical examinations, legal proceedings — a postmortem investigates what killed something. The implicit question is: who or what is responsible for the death? In an incident context, this framing trains everyone in the room to think about responsibility before they think about systems.

"Post-Incident Review" makes no such implication. The service did not die. The incident closed. Now we review what happened and what to improve.

This is not semantics. Word choice shapes the mental frame participants bring into the room. PIR participants who walk in expecting a review think about systems. Postmortem participants who walk in expecting accountability think about people. The observable outcome: PIRs in blameless cultures produce more root causes per incident and more action items per root cause than equivalent sessions framed as postmortems.

For Director/VP candidates: your ability to articulate this distinction — and to have actually changed your organization's language and seen the effect — is a differentiator in interviews. Most organizations have adopted blameless postmortem language; fewer have measured whether the culture actually shifted.

### PIR Timing Standards

| Severity | PIR Must Be Held Within | Rationale |
|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| SEV1 | 24–48 hours | Memory degrades fastest for high-stress events; blast radius was large enough to warrant immediate learning |
| SEV2 | 72 hours | Significant customer impact; systemic root causes likely |
| SEV3 | 7 days | Lower urgency but still requires documented learning |
| SEV4/SEV5 | Optional; trend-review monthly | Individual events are low signal; watch for recurrence clustering |

Timing matters for two reasons beyond memory degradation. First, the longer the delay between incident and PIR, the stronger the signal to the engineering organization that learning is not actually prioritized. A SEV1 PIR that happens three weeks later tells your engineers that incidents are resolved, not learned from. Second, delayed PIRs allow informal narratives to calcify. By the time a PIR happens two weeks late, everyone has already agreed on a story — often one that protects the team from scrutiny. The PIR becomes a documentation exercise for a conclusion already reached.

### Who Facilitates

The Incident Commander should not facilitate their own PIR.

The IC was the decision-maker during the incident. They have opinions about what happened, why decisions were made, and what should have been done differently. They are not neutral. More importantly, they were in command — which means participants may self-censor to avoid disagreeing with the IC's narrative.

A separate facilitator — ideally trained in the role, not just assigned by default — produces better outcomes. Google formalized this with a dedicated "PIR Facilitator" role outside the on-call rotation. This person's job is to ask the questions, protect the blameless norm, and keep the session producing findings rather than drifting into defense or storytelling.

The IC attends as a participant and domain expert, not as the person running the room.

## Part 3 — Running a High-Quality PIR

### Preparation (Before the Meeting)

A PIR that starts from a blank page inside the meeting is a PIR that will produce shallow findings. Preparation is not optional.

**The scribe's timeline as foundation.** The real-time scribe produced a rough timeline during the incident. This is the starting point — not the final product. Before the PIR, someone (usually the IC or a designated preparer) converts the scribe's notes into a clean, timestamped sequence and verifies each entry against logs, alerts, and monitoring data. Entries that cannot be verified are flagged as "unverified — reconstruct from system data."

**Pre-gather all relevant signals.** Before anyone is in the room: pull the alert history, the deployment log for the affected services, the traffic and latency graphs, the error rate dashboards, any relevant Splunk queries. These should be accessible during the PIR, not hunted for mid-session. Hunting for data during the PIR kills momentum and trains people to accept unverified answers.

**Pre-circulate the draft.** Send the draft timeline and known facts to all attendees 24 hours before the PIR. This serves two purposes. Participants arrive with corrections and additions rather than spending the first 20 minutes reading. And participants who have factual corrections have time to raise them before the session, reducing the chance that corrections happen in a charged group setting.

### Opening Ritual: The Blameless Commitment

Begin every PIR the same way. Not as a legal disclaimer, not as a bullet on the agenda — as a facilitated commitment that the group makes together.

The facilitator says something like:

> "Before we start, I want to name the norm we operate under in this room. We are here to understand what happened — not to assign fault. Every decision made during this incident was made by a person doing their best with the information they had at the time. Our job today is to identify what the system — the technology, the processes, the tools — made harder than it should have been. When we find a gap, we ask what in the system allowed it. We do not ask who failed. Let's begin."

This is not a speech to be skipped. It sets the cognitive frame for the session. Facilitators who skip it because "everyone already knows this" are wrong — people know the stated norm, but they do not enter a charged technical room in blameless mode automatically. The ritual activates the frame.

### The Five PIR Questions

Replace the standard "timeline, contributing factors, action items" agenda with five specific questions. These questions are harder to answer with surface-level responses.

**Question 1: What happened and when?**

This is timeline validation. The goal is not to tell the story — it is to verify the sequence of events against the prepared timeline. Participants correct timestamps, add missing entries, and flag entries that contradict their memory or the data. The output is a timeline the group agrees is accurate.

Common finding: the official customer impact window (from the alert) is shorter than the actual customer impact window (from reconstructed traffic data). That gap is an MTTD problem.

**Question 2: Why did it happen?**

This is RCA. It requires a framework — not a guess. Using the 5 Whys or a fishbone diagram, trace the proximate cause back to contributing factors and systemic root causes. (RCA frameworks are covered in depth in Ch05.) The key discipline: do not stop at "why did the system fail?" Ask also "why did the system allow this failure mode to exist?"

**Question 3: Why didn't we catch it sooner?**

This is MTTD analysis. Compare the first detectable signal in telemetry against the time the alert fired and the time a human became aware. The gaps are your detection investments. A 45-minute gap between first anomaly and alert firing is an instrumentation or alerting gap. A 20-minute gap between alert firing and human awareness is an escalation or routing gap.

**Question 4: Why did it take as long to fix?**

This is MTTR analysis. Walk the response timeline: time from awareness to diagnosis, from diagnosis to first remediation attempt, from first attempt to mitigation. Where did time go? Likely candidates: diagnosing the wrong hypothesis, waiting for access or approval, runbook was wrong, coordination overhead between teams, the right person was not reachable.

Each time sink is an action item candidate.

**Question 5: What prevents recurrence?**

This produces the action items. For each root cause and contributing factor identified in questions 2–4, the group proposes a specific, testable change that addresses the gap. Action item quality standards are in Part 4.

### Handling Blame Instincts

Blame instincts surface in PIRs as person-level observations: "the engineer deployed without approval," "the on-call didn't follow the runbook," "the developer wrote a bad query."

The facilitator's job is to translate these immediately into system observations without dismissing the observation.

Person-level: "The engineer deployed without approval."  
System-level: "The deployment system did not enforce the approval gate. What made it possible to bypass it?"

Person-level: "The on-call didn't follow the runbook."  
System-level: "The runbook was not followed. Was it accessible? Was it correct? Was it clear enough that following it was easier than improvising?"

Person-level: "The developer wrote a bad query that caused full-table scans."  
System-level: "The database accepted a query pattern that degraded production performance. Why didn't code review, CI, or pre-production load testing catch this?"

This translation is not about protecting people from consequences. It is about finding the systemic conditions that made the person-level failure possible — because those conditions will produce the same failure with a different person in the future if not addressed.

### PIR Quality Anti-Patterns

**Vague root cause.** "Human error," "insufficient monitoring," "lack of testing" are stopping points, not root causes. If your PIR ends at one of these, you have identified a category of problem, not a cause you can act on. "Human error" does not produce an action item. "The deployment system allowed a destructive operation without a confirmation step or a dry-run preview" produces an action item.

**Action items without owners.** "Improve observability" assigned to the platform team is not an action item. It is an aspiration. Every action item needs a single named person. That person may delegate, but they are accountable for completion.

**Action items without verification criteria.** How do you know when "add more monitoring" is done? You do not, which is why it stays in the backlog indefinitely. Every action item must specify the observable state that proves it is complete.

**Blame laundering.** The PIR is blameless. The follow-up Slack messages from leadership are not. This happens when an organization adopts the blameless norm in the document but not in behavior. The signal: after a PIR, participants fear that what was said in the room will be used in performance reviews. The fix is leadership behavior, not process changes.

**Timeline gaps.** If the timeline has entries like "team investigated (40 minutes)" with nothing more specific, the timeline is incomplete. Every investigative period has steps. What hypotheses were formed? What was tested? What was ruled out? Vague investigation entries mean the MTTR analysis cannot identify where time was lost.

## Part 4 — Action Item Quality Standards

Most PIR action items die in the backlog. The primary cause is not that engineering teams are negligent — it is that the action items as written are not closeable. A vague action item cannot be completed because no one can tell when it is done.

### Good Action Item Anatomy

A closeable action item has six components:

**What**: A specific, testable technical change. Not "improve observability of the Auth service." Instead: "Add p99 latency alert for Auth DB connection pool with threshold set to 80% saturation, linked to the Auth DB runbook, with PagerDuty routing to the auth-on-call rotation."

**Why**: Explicit link to the incident and the specific gap. "During the 2026-06-15 SEV1, Auth DB saturation reached 97% before any alert fired. The 23-minute detection gap accounts for 40% of total MTTR."

**Who**: Single named person. Not "the Auth team." One human being is accountable. They may involve others in the work, but accountability is not shared.

**When**: A specific date. Not "next sprint" — a date. Sprint assignments move. Dates do not.

**How to verify**: What observable state confirms the action item is complete? "Alert exists in PagerDuty with correct threshold, linked runbook URL returns 200, and alert has fired correctly in staging under synthetic load."

**Recurrence prevention**: What does this change do to the failure mode's likelihood or severity? "Reduces detection lag for Auth DB saturation from ~23 minutes to <2 minutes, eliminating the primary MTTR contributor from this incident class."

### The SMART+R Standard

Apply SMART criteria plus a reliability-specific extension:

| Criterion | Incident-Specific Application |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Specific** | Exact technical change, exact service, exact threshold — not a category |
| **Measurable** | Verification criteria defined before the item is created, not after |
| **Achievable** | Within the owner's authority. If it requires another team's approval, that is a separate action item |
| **Relevant** | Directly addresses an identified gap from this specific incident |
| **Time-bound** | A specific date, not a sprint name |
| **Reversible risk** | Note if the fix could introduce new failure modes. New alerts can cause alert fatigue. New circuit breakers can cause unexpected fallback behavior. This risk should be planned for, not discovered post-implementation |

## Part 5 — Governance Frameworks for Action Item Completion

Individual PIR quality is necessary but not sufficient. The question is not whether your team produces good PIRs — the question is whether your PIRs reduce incident frequency and severity over time. That requires governance.

### The Action Item Graveyard

In most engineering organizations, postmortem action items have a predictable lifecycle: created with good intentions in the PIR, added to a "reliability backlog," deprioritized in the next sprint planning in favor of feature work, forgotten by month two, rediscovered when the same incident recurs six months later.

The graveyard is not caused by bad intentions. It is caused by structural incentives. Feature work is visible to product management and to customers. Reliability work prevents something invisible from happening. Organizations reward what is visible.

The governance structures below exist to make reliability work visible and to create forcing functions that compete with feature prioritization.

### Governance Structures That Work

**Weekly Incident Review.** The SRE Lead or IC reviews all open action items older than two weeks. Not to apply pressure — to identify blockers. Is the item blocked waiting for access? Waiting for another team? Was it descoped without an updated due date? This meeting exists to surface blockers before they produce a missed due date, not after.

**Monthly PIR Quality Review.** The SRE Manager reviews PIR quality scores and trends. What is the average number of action items per PIR? How many are being completed by their due date? Are root causes getting to systemic level or stopping at "human error"? Trending these metrics tells you whether your PIR process is improving or stagnating.

**Quarterly Recurrence Review.** This is the most important governance meeting most organizations do not hold. The question is not "did we close all action items?" The question is "are the same root cause clusters appearing in multiple incidents?" If the same architectural weakness underlies three incidents per quarter, the individual PIR action items are not solving the systemic problem. The quarterly review identifies which root cause clusters require an engineering investment that individual PIRs cannot authorize.

**Error Budget Tie-In.** Incidents that consume more than 20% of monthly error budget trigger mandatory engineering sprint allocation. This is a mechanism, not a request. When the error budget trigger fires, the product team's sprint plan must include reliability items. The SRE Manager and Engineering Director agree on this trigger in advance — the conversation about whether reliability work belongs in the sprint is not re-litigated after every incident.

### Ownership Escalation

Action items that are not closed by their due date follow a defined escalation path — not an ad hoc one. The path should be defined before it is needed:

1. Owner notified 3 days before due date (automated reminder from tracking system)
2. On due date, SRE Lead is notified if incomplete
3. At 7 days overdue, Engineering Manager is notified
4. At 14 days overdue, Engineering Director is notified with business impact context from the original PIR

This escalation path is not punitive. Its purpose is to surface organizational blockers before they become a reliability pattern. If action items consistently miss due dates, either the items are being written with unrealistic timelines, or the organizational prioritization is structurally broken. Both are Director-level problems.

### The SRE Tax Model

Google's model is directionally correct and worth understanding for senior interviews. When a service exhausts its error budget, engineering teams must fix reliability issues before shipping new features. The reliability "tax" is paid before new feature investment is authorized.

The critical design requirement: this must be agreed as a contract before budget exhaustion, not negotiated after. An SRE team that tries to impose a feature freeze post-incident without a prior agreement will lose the political fight every time. An SRE team operating under a pre-agreed error budget contract has the mechanism in writing.

Implementing the SRE tax at an organization that does not currently practice error budget management is a multi-quarter change program. For Director/VP candidates: your ability to describe how you built organizational alignment around this model — not just that you are familiar with it — is the interview differentiator.

## Part 6 — Governance Gaps That Incidents Reveal

Incidents are diagnostic instruments for organizational health. The technical root cause is usually the smaller story. The more important story is what the incident reveals about governance gaps that created the conditions for the root cause to have impact.

### Pattern 1: Change Freeze Exception Culture

**What it looks like.** Every change gets approved during freeze if someone pushes hard enough. Incidents during "frozen" periods reveal that the freeze was not enforced.

**How to detect it.** Query your change management system for all approvals during the last freeze period. Calculate the approval rate. If it is above 30%, your freeze is theater.

**Remediation.** Change freeze approval requires explicit sign-off from VP or above, not manager. Document who approved, who was overruled, and what the business justification was. Make this visible in the change record. Visibility alone reduces exception requests by 40–60% in most organizations.

**Governance change.** Quarterly audit of exception rates published to engineering leadership. Freeze policy reviewed annually to ensure it balances deployment velocity with stability.

### Pattern 2: Manual Override Normalization

**What it looks like.** Runbooks say "get approval from X." Everyone knows the shortcut — skip the approval when X is unavailable. The shortcut is so common it is practically the default process. Incidents happen on the shortcut.

**How to detect it.** Compare runbook-prescribed steps with actual steps taken during incidents over the last six months. How often is the prescribed approval step skipped?

**Remediation.** For high-risk operations, make the approval technically enforced rather than procedurally required. If the runbook says "get approval before running this command," the command should require an approval token to execute. Procedure that requires willful bypass is stronger than procedure that can be skipped accidentally.

**Governance change.** Runbook audit: identify all procedures with approval requirements. Tier them by risk. Highest risk: technical enforcement. Medium risk: dual-person verification (two people must confirm in the ticket before execution). Lower risk: documented exception process with post-hoc review.

### Pattern 3: Ownership Vacuum

**What it looks like.** The service has no clear owner. Nobody acts until the IC tracks someone down through three layers of Slack escalation. A 30-minute MTTR becomes 90 minutes because 60 minutes were spent finding the person with the right access.

**How to detect it.** Run a service ownership audit. For every service in production: who is the primary owner? Who has deployment access? Who is on the on-call rotation? If any of these answers is "the team" without a specific name, you have an ownership vacuum.

**Remediation.** Service ownership registry with a named primary owner and a named secondary for every service. On-call rotation tied to the service, not the team. Access provisioned to named individuals with annual review.

**Governance change.** New service onboarding checklist requires ownership declaration before the service goes to production. Quarterly review of ownership registry for accuracy.

### Pattern 4: Escalation Path Rot

**What it looks like.** The phone tree was built two years ago. Half the contacts have left the company. The IC escalates to a person who is on leave, then to a person who moved to a different team, before finding someone with actual authority at minute 45.

**How to detect it.** Test your escalation paths before an incident requires them. Quarterly: send a test notification through the escalation chain. Verify each link responds.

**Remediation.** Escalation path owned by the SRE Lead with quarterly review cadence. Every departure triggers an immediate update. New hires in relevant roles trigger an addition.

**Governance change.** Escalation path staleness is a SEV3 finding during PIRs. If an incident reveals a broken escalation path, the remediation action item has a 2-week deadline, not a quarterly one.

### Pattern 5: Runbook Staleness

**What it looks like.** The runbook references a load balancer that was decommissioned eight months ago. The on-call follows the runbook for 20 minutes before discovering it is wrong. Those 20 minutes are wasted MTTR.

**How to detect it.** Every runbook must have a "last validated" date and a named owner. Runbooks with no validation in the last 90 days are stale by definition. Audit quarterly.

**Remediation.** Runbooks have owners. Owners are responsible for validation when underlying infrastructure, configurations, or dependencies change. Change management process triggers runbook review for any change that affects documented procedures.

**Governance change.** Runbook staleness is a blocker for on-call rotation assignment. Engineers cannot be added to the on-call rotation for a service whose runbooks are stale. This creates a self-enforcing incentive.

### Pattern 6: Alert Routing Gaps

**What it looks like.** The alert fires. It routes to a channel that is high-volume and unmanned at 2:00 AM. Nobody pages until a customer calls in.

**How to detect it.** Audit alert routing for all P1 and P2 alerts. For each: who is paged? Is there a human in the on-call rotation for that routing target at all times of day? Is there a backup if the primary does not acknowledge?

**Remediation.** All P1 and P2 alerts route to PagerDuty (or equivalent) with escalating notification, not to a Slack channel. P3 alerts can route to Slack during business hours with PagerDuty escalation for weekends and nights.

**Governance change.** Alert routing audit is part of the quarterly SRE review. Any alert without a 24x7 on-call coverage path is remediated before the next review.

### Pattern 7: Change Authority Ambiguity

**What it looks like.** A P0 incident requires rolling back a service. Nobody is sure if the on-call can execute the rollback without manager approval. Fifteen minutes of Slack messages to find the answer. The manager approves. The rollback takes two minutes.

**How to detect it.** During your next GameDay or tabletop exercise, pose the question: "The on-call needs to roll back service X at 3:00 AM. Who can authorize that?" If the answer is not immediate and unambiguous, you have authority ambiguity.

**Remediation.** Define a clear "emergency authority" framework. On-call engineers have pre-authorized authority to execute rollbacks, scale-downs, and circuit-breaker activations for their owned services without manager approval. Post-action notification is required within 30 minutes. Authority boundaries are documented in the runbook.

**Governance change.** Emergency authority framework reviewed annually and when organizational structure changes. Every on-call engineer must confirm they have read and understand their authority before joining the rotation.

## Part 7 — Process Changes Post-Incident

Not every incident produces a process change. Not every process change is the right fix. The hardest judgment in post-incident governance is distinguishing between a process gap that a new procedure will fix and a systemic problem that a new procedure will paper over.

**When process change is the right answer.** The gap is behavioral: a step that was skipped because nobody knew it was required, or because the process was unclear. The fix is clarity — a better runbook, a clearer escalation path, a more explicit change management step.

**When process change is a band-aid.** The gap is architectural or incentive-based. If the system allows destructive operations without confirmation, adding a runbook step that says "confirm before executing" does not fix the gap — it creates a procedural dependency that will eventually be skipped. The right fix is technical enforcement. If engineers skip the change approval process because it is too slow, adding a "please follow the process" reminder does not fix the gap — the process needs to be faster, or the consequences of skipping it need to be visible.

### Runbook Staleness Prevention

Runbooks must have:
1. A named owner (a person, not a team)
2. A last-validated date
3. A review trigger (what changes to the system should prompt a review)
4. A scheduled review date (at most 90 days out, or after any significant infrastructure change)

Runbook staleness discovered during an incident is a PIR finding and produces an action item. Runbook staleness discovered before an incident — during a quarterly audit — is an opportunity to prevent the incident.

### PIR Quality Metrics

A PIR quality score gives SRE Managers a lever for improving the process over time. Score each PIR on:

| Dimension | Scoring |
|-----------|---------|
| Timeline completeness | No gaps > 15 minutes unexplained (0–25 points) |
| Root cause depth | Reached systemic level, not stopped at "human error" (0–25 points) |
| Action item quality | All items meet SMART+R standard (0–25 points) |
| Action item closure rate | % of items closed by due date, tracked at 30/60/90 days (0–25 points) |

Trend PIR quality scores quarterly. A declining score is a signal that either the process is degrading or the incidents are becoming more complex. A score that is flat and mediocre is a signal that the improvement infrastructure is not working.

### The Reliability Maturity Ladder

Organizations move through predictable stages of incident management maturity:

**Stage 1 — Reactive.** Incidents are handled ad hoc. No consistent process. MTTR is variable and unpredictable. PIRs are optional or nonexistent. The same incidents recur. This is where most organizations start.

**Stage 2 — Process-Defined.** Consistent incident response process. PIRs are held after SEV1/SEV2. Action items are captured. Runbooks exist. MTTR is measurable. However, action items still have high dropout rates and recurrence is common.

**Stage 3 — Managed.** PIR quality is tracked. Action items have governance. Error budget is used as an investment signal. Incident frequency is declining. MTTR is trending down. Recurrence by root cause cluster is tracked and addressed.

**Stage 4 — Proactive.** GameDays identify failure modes before incidents occur. Chaos engineering is routine. Architecture reviews include reliability criteria. Incident frequency is low and declining. Most incidents are detected before customer impact.

**Stage 5 — Predictive.** Anomaly detection and trend analysis surface likely failure modes before they manifest as incidents. Risk-weighted debt backlog drives reliability investment. The SRE team spends more time preventing incidents than responding to them.

Most large enterprise organizations are at Stage 2 when an SRE program arrives. The transition from Stage 2 to Stage 3 requires governance — the structures described in Part 5. The transition from Stage 3 to Stage 4 requires engineering investment — the hardening investments described in Chapter 10.

## Summary

The three mandatory outputs — timeline, impact statement, action items — are the minimum viable artifacts of every incident. PIR quality determines whether those artifacts produce learning. Governance determines whether that learning produces measurable improvement.

For Director/VP candidates: your ability to describe not just the PIR process but the governance architecture that makes PIR outputs durable — the weekly reviews, the escalation paths, the error budget tie-in, the quarterly recurrence analysis — is what signals operational maturity at the organizational level, not just the technical one.

The goal is not a better postmortem template. The goal is an engineering organization where the same incident does not happen twice.
