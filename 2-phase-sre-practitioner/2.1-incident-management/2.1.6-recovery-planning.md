# Chapter 6: Recovery Planning

**Audience**: Principal SRE / SRE Manager with production ops experience.

## Part 1: Mitigation vs. Resolution

### The Precise Distinction

These two words are not synonyms. Treating them as synonyms causes premature incident closure, which causes recurrence.

**Mitigation**: Customer impact has stopped. By any means. The system may still be in an abnormal state — circuit breakers tripped, traffic shifted, features disabled, a service running on degraded mode — but customers are no longer experiencing errors. The SLA clock stops. The on-call engineer can breathe.

**Resolution**: The root cause has been identified and eliminated. The system is operating in its intended state with all mechanisms functioning normally. Circuit breakers are closed. Traffic is nominal. Features are re-enabled. A postmortem is now appropriate.

### Why the Distinction Matters

If you close an incident when mitigation is achieved (customer impact stopped) but before resolution is complete (root cause eliminated), you have set a timer on recurrence. The next time the same failure condition occurs — and it will — you will have the same incident without the institutional memory of having fixed it.

The other failure mode: spending 45 minutes diagnosing root cause while customers are actively experiencing errors because "we want to understand what happened before we start changing things." This is diagnostic curiosity overriding operational obligation. The Incident Commander must prevent this.

### Restore First, Explain Later

This is the foundational principle of incident management. It is not a platitude. It is a decision rule.

During active customer impact:
1. Mitigation actions take priority over diagnostic actions
2. Diagnosis happens in parallel, not instead of mitigation
3. Root cause that cannot be established quickly should be deferred
4. The IC must enforce this — engineers left to their own instincts will over-diagnose

The one exception: when the fastest path to mitigation *is* diagnosis. If you suspect a specific config change caused the failure, and reverting that config change is a 30-second fix, then diagnosing to confirm takes 2 minutes and saves you from a wrong mitigation action. This is the exception that requires IC judgment, not the default stance.

### ITIL Formalization: Incident vs. Problem Management

ITIL drew a formal process boundary here for good reason.

**Incident Management**: Restore service. Focus is speed of recovery, not depth of understanding. Artifacts: incident ticket, timeline, impact statement, mitigation record.

**Problem Management**: Eliminate root cause. Focus is systemic understanding and prevention. Triggered after incident closure. Artifacts: postmortem, RCA document, corrective action tickets.

These are separate queues, separate owners, separate timelines. The problem management process starts *after* the incident is closed. The incident is closed when customer impact is zero and the system is stable (even if on a degraded configuration). Problem management then owns root cause analysis at whatever pace is appropriate.

This separation is why postmortems have a scheduled time slot after the incident — not a frantic real-time analysis session during customer impact.

## Part 2: RTO/RPO Framework for Recovery Strategy Selection

Before you can select a recovery strategy, you need to know what constraints you are operating under. RTO and RPO are those constraints. They are defined before incidents, not during them.

### RTO: Recovery Time Objective

**Definition**: The maximum tolerable duration of downtime. If RTO is 15 minutes, you must restore service within 15 minutes of failure onset or you are in SLA breach.

RTO drives **how fast** you must act. A 1-minute RTO means you cannot be manually sshing into servers. A 15-minute RTO means you have time for a careful rollback. A 4-hour RTO means you can do database restoration from snapshot.

### RPO: Recovery Point Objective

**Definition**: The maximum tolerable data loss, expressed as time. If RPO is 5 minutes, you must be able to recover to a state no older than 5 minutes before the failure.

RPO drives **how frequently** you must checkpoint state. RPO of 0 (zero data loss) requires synchronous replication. RPO of 1 hour requires hourly snapshots minimum. RPO of 24 hours requires daily backups minimum.

### Strategy Selection Matrix

| RTO | RPO | Strategy | Typical Use |
|-----|-----|----------|-------------|
| < 1 minute | ~0 (zero data loss) | Hot standby / Active-Active | Payment processing, authentication, order write path |
| 1–15 minutes | < 5 minutes | Warm standby (failover) | Core APIs, order management, session stores |
| 15 min – 4 hours | < 1 hour | Pilot light (minimal running) | Non-critical internal services, reporting APIs |
| 4–24 hours | < 24 hours | Backup / Restore | Batch systems, analytics, audit logs |

**Active-Active**: All instances actively serve traffic. Failure of one is transparent to customers. Requires data synchronization, conflict resolution, and adds latency from consistency protocols. The most expensive but only option at <1 minute RTO.

**Warm Standby**: A replica is running and ready, but not serving traffic. Failover takes 1–15 minutes because DNS propagation, health check convergence, and connection pool warm-up all take time even if the instance is ready.

**Pilot Light**: Minimal infrastructure is running (just enough to boot the service quickly). Data is replicated. The service is not running but can be started in 15 minutes to 4 hours.

**Backup/Restore**: No live replica. Data is in backups. Full restore required. Only acceptable for services where 4–24 hours of downtime is business-tolerable.

### Applying the Matrix During an Incident

If your service's RTO is 5 minutes and you are at T+10 minutes with no mitigation, you are already past your RTO. The recovery strategy you should be executing is the one designed for 1–15 minute RTO — failover to warm standby, not debugging the primary.

Know your services' RTO/RPO before incidents. Document them in your runbook. The incident is not the time to discover that you have no warm standby for a service with a 5-minute RTO.

## Part 3: Rollback vs. Fix-Forward Decision Matrix

This is the most frequent IC decision during a post-deployment incident. Getting it wrong costs time, data integrity, or both.

### The Decision Framework

Work through each signal in order. The first signal that gives a clear answer wins.

| Signal | Rollback Indicated | Fix-Forward Indicated |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Deployment age | < 30 minutes | > 60 minutes |
| Root cause known? | No — rollback is safer | Yes, with fix ready |
| Data mutations | None or fully reversible | Schema applied — rollback not viable |
| Blast radius | Broad / still expanding | Narrow / stable / known |
| Rollback artifact clean? | Verified as clean | Unverified or known bad |
| Stateful coupling | Stateless service | Database schema version advanced |
| Traffic already shifted? | No | Yes — shift back may re-expose |

**If multiple signals conflict**: weight the data mutation signal highest. A schema migration makes rollback structurally unsafe — fix-forward is mandatory regardless of other signals.

**If rollback artifact is unverified**: do not roll back blindly. A rollback to a bad artifact is a second incident layered on the first. Verify the artifact is clean before executing.

### The Schema Migration Trap

This is the single most common reason engineers choose the wrong recovery strategy.

When a deployment includes a database schema migration:
- The migration runs during or before the new code deployment
- The new schema may be backward-incompatible with the old code
- If you roll back the code to the previous version, the old code now runs against the new schema

Outcome: the old code may fail to start, or may start and corrupt data by writing to schema columns it does not understand.

**Pre-incident architecture fix**: use backward-compatible migrations. The migration adds a new column that old code ignores. Old code still works. New code uses the new column. After the new code is stable, a second migration removes the old column. This is the expand-contract pattern.

**During the incident**: if a schema migration has run and is non-reversible, fix-forward is your only viable path. Accept this constraint, focus on what needs to change in the code to work with the current schema, and ship the fix.

### Verifying Rollback Artifact Safety

Before executing a rollback:

1. Identify the previous artifact version (container image tag, package version, config commit)
2. Check CI/CD records — was that version's pipeline green?
3. Check incident history — was there a separate incident on that version?
4. Check schema compatibility — does the previous code version work with the current schema?
5. Check configuration compatibility — were any config values changed that the old code depends on?

If all five checks pass: rollback is safe to execute. If any check fails: do not roll back to that version. Either find an earlier clean version or fix-forward.

## Part 4: Recovery Strategy Taxonomy

Eight strategies. Each is the right answer in specific conditions and the wrong answer in others.

### 1. Code Rollback

**What**: Revert the running code to the previous artifact version. In Kubernetes: update the container image tag in the Deployment spec. Kubernetes rolls out the previous image, old pods start, new pods drain.

**When to use**: The incident was caused by a code change deployed in the last 30–60 minutes. Schema is unchanged. Rollback artifact is verified clean.

**How**: 
```bash
# Kubernetes rollback
kubectl rollout undo deployment/<name> -n <namespace>

# Or to a specific revision
kubectl rollout undo deployment/<name> --to-revision=<N> -n <namespace>

# Verify
kubectl rollout status deployment/<name> -n <namespace>
```

**Time to mitigation**: 3–10 minutes depending on pod startup time, health check convergence, and traffic shifting.

**Risk**: Rolling back to a version that has a different failure mode. Verify the artifact before executing.

### 2. Configuration Rollback

**What**: Revert a configuration value — a Kubernetes ConfigMap, a feature flag, a parameter in Parameter Store or Secrets Manager.

**When to use**: The incident correlates with a config change, not a code change. Or a feature flag enable/disable caused the incident.

**How**: Revert the ConfigMap and restart affected pods, or toggle the feature flag back in your flag management system. Config-driven incidents are the fastest to mitigate — no code deploy required.

**Time to mitigation**: 1–5 minutes if using a feature flag system. 5–10 minutes for a ConfigMap rollout with pod restart.

**Risk**: Config values may have dependencies — reverting one value without reverting a correlated value leaves the system inconsistent.

### 3. Traffic Shift

**What**: Move a percentage of traffic away from the failing endpoint, cluster, or region to healthy infrastructure.

**When to use**: A specific region or cluster is degraded. Healthy capacity exists elsewhere. The issue is not in shared infrastructure (databases, queues) that the healthy region also uses.

**How**: 
- Istio VirtualService weight adjustment (instant, no DNS propagation)
- Route 53 weighted routing (DNS TTL latency before effective)
- ALB target group weight adjustment (fast, takes effect within seconds)

**Time to mitigation**: Seconds to minutes depending on routing mechanism and DNS TTL.

**Risk**: Traffic shift overloads the destination if capacity is not pre-validated. Always check destination headroom before shifting.

### 4. Geographic Failover

**What**: DNS-level redirect of all traffic from primary region to secondary region.

**When to use**: Primary region has a broad, unrecoverable failure. Secondary region is warm and has current data (RPO met). The RTO allows for DNS propagation delay.

**Time to mitigation**: 5–30 minutes depending on DNS TTL, data replication lag, and warm-up time.

**Risk**: Data written to primary after the last replication sync is lost (RPO exposure). Secondary region may have stale cached data. Reverse failover (back to primary) requires careful planning.

### 5. Circuit Breaker Trip

**What**: Stop calling the failing dependency. Return a fallback response — cached data, a default, or an explicit clean error — instead of waiting for the downstream.

**When to use**: A downstream service is failing or degraded and is causing connection pool exhaustion upstream. Covered in detail in ch05, Part 6.

**Time to mitigation**: Immediate. Thread pools release as soon as the circuit opens.

**Risk**: If the fallback is not well-implemented, the degraded response may be worse than the original error. A fallback that returns stale data without surfacing that it is stale misleads customers.

### 6. Load Shedding

**What**: Selectively reject or queue non-critical requests to protect critical request paths.

**When to use**: The service is overloaded. You cannot scale fast enough. You must choose which requests to serve and which to fail.

**Priority framework**:
- Netflix Hystrix: bulkhead thread pools per priority tier, low-priority pools shed first
- Amazon shuffle sharding: partition customer load so one customer's traffic pattern does not affect others
- Google Borg: priority classes — when capacity is scarce, lower-priority work is preempted

**Customer experience during load shedding**:
- Best: a clean error with a retry-after header (customer knows to retry)
- Acceptable: a queue-and-notify pattern (submit your request, we will fulfill it later)
- Worst: silent timeout (customer does not know if the request was received)

**Time to mitigation**: 1–3 minutes to configure and propagate load shedding rules.

**Risk**: Incorrect priority classification sheds critical traffic. Validate priority tiers before incidents.

### 7. Scale Out

**What**: Increase pod replica count or add capacity to handle load that is causing the failure.

**When to use**: The failure is a capacity issue — the service is healthy but overloaded. Not a code bug, not a downstream failure.

**How**: In Kubernetes, update the Deployment replicas or trigger an HPA scale-up:
```bash
kubectl scale deployment/<name> --replicas=<N> -n <namespace>
```

**Time to mitigation**: 2–8 minutes for pod startup, health check pass, and readiness to serve traffic.

**Risk**: If the failure is not a capacity issue, adding replicas does not help. Adding replicas to a service that is failing because its downstream is failing only increases the load on the failing downstream. Diagnose before scaling.

### 8. Data Recovery

**What**: Restore data to a known good state using PITR (Point-In-Time Recovery), snapshot restore, or event replay.

**When to use**: Data corruption or loss has occurred. The service may be technically healthy, but the data it is serving is wrong.

**Options**:
- **PITR**: restore database to exact timestamp before corruption. Requires PITR to be enabled and tested. AWS RDS, Aurora, and CloudSQL support PITR out of box.
- **Snapshot restore**: restore to the most recent clean snapshot. Faster than PITR for large datasets but coarser-grained.
- **Event replay**: if events were persisted to Kafka or an event store, replay from the last clean offset. Requires idempotent consumers.

**Time to mitigation**: 15 minutes (event replay from offset) to several hours (full snapshot restore for large databases).

**Risk**: Data written between the recovery point and now is lost (RPO exposure). Replay may reprocess events against a different system state than when they were first processed — validate idempotency before replay.

## Part 5: Parallel vs. Serial Recovery

Not all recovery tracks can run simultaneously. Getting the sequencing wrong creates new failures on top of the existing one.

### When to Run Parallel Tracks

Parallel tracks are safe when:
- The failure domains are **independent** — Track A's actions do not affect Track B's system
- You have **adequate staffing** — each track has its own lead engineer; tracks do not share people
- The tracks have **independent validation criteria** — Track A can declare success without Track B being complete

Example: a regional outage where the web tier is failing in us-east-1 (Track A: failover to us-west-2) and the background job service is backlogged (Track B: controlled queue drain after queue pause). These are independent — Track A's failover does not affect Track B's queue backlog.

### When Serial Recovery Is Mandatory

Serial sequencing is required when there is a **hard dependency** between recovery steps. Violating the sequence creates a new failure.

Common mandatory sequences:
1. **Database before application tier**: restore the database to a consistent state before restarting application servers. Application servers that start before the database is ready will fail their health checks, cycle, and produce misleading logs.
2. **Auth before downstream services**: if the auth service is part of the failure, bring it back before restarting services that depend on it for token validation.
3. **Message broker before consumers**: restart the broker before starting consumers, not simultaneously. Consumers that start before the broker is ready create connection retry storms.

### IC Coordination in Parallel Tracks

When running parallel tracks, the IC must:
- Assign a **Track Lead** to each track — one engineer owns decisions for that track
- Establish a **shared timeline** (single incident ticket or collaboration channel) — all actions logged centrally with timestamps
- Enforce **no-conflict rule**: any action that touches shared infrastructure (load balancer, DNS, database) must be coordinated across tracks before execution
- Mandate **sync every 10 minutes** — short status check across all tracks to surface conflicts before they happen

### The Parallel Investigation, Serial Action Principle

Investigate in parallel. Execute actions serially.

Multiple engineers can simultaneously gather data — one reads logs, another checks metrics, another reads the service dependency graph. No conflicts arise from simultaneous reads.

But execution actions — config changes, pod restarts, traffic shifts, circuit breaker trips — must be serialized. Two engineers simultaneously restarting pods and shifting traffic in conflicting directions can create a failure state that neither intended and both find inexplicable. The IC controls the execution queue. Engineers propose actions; the IC approves and sequences them.

## Part 6: Traffic Management During Recovery

### Load Shedding Patterns in Practice

**Netflix Hystrix bulkheads**: separate thread pools per command group. When one group's pool is exhausted, other groups are unaffected. During recovery, the most critical command groups are given larger thread pools; less critical groups are reduced. This is runtime-configurable via Hystrix Dashboard.

**Amazon shuffle sharding**: partition customers into shards. Each shard uses a different subset of workers. A failure in one shard affects only that shard's customers — typically 1/N of total. This limits the blast radius of any single failure to 1/N users, regardless of how many replicas fail.

**Google Borg priority classes**: batch jobs run at lower priority than serving jobs. When cluster capacity is constrained, Borg preempts batch jobs first. Serving jobs are protected. The recovery implication: during an incident caused by resource exhaustion, you can increase headroom by evicting batch workloads without stopping customer-facing traffic.

### Graceful Degradation vs. Total Degradation

**Graceful degradation**: the customer journey continues, but with reduced fidelity. Product recommendations are static (cached from yesterday). Account balance is read from a 5-minute cache, not real-time. Order status shows last known state.

Design for graceful degradation before incidents. Ask for every service: "What does this feature look like if its downstream dependency is not available?" If the answer is "it breaks completely," you have a degradation gap.

**Total degradation (hard error)**: customer sees an error page or a 503. Journey stops. This is only appropriate when partial data is worse than no data — for example, a payment confirmation that cannot actually confirm whether the payment succeeded. In that case, a clean error with a "try again" CTA is better than a degraded response that creates false confidence.

### Queue Backlog Management During Recovery

A queue that filled during the outage is not automatically safe to drain at full speed post-recovery. The recovering service's capacity at T+15 minutes is not the same as its steady-state capacity at T-60 minutes. The recovering service is likely running with cold caches, warming connection pools, and potentially reduced replica count.

Controlled drain procedure:
1. Pause the consumer before beginning any recovery actions (prevent new messages from being processed mid-recovery)
2. Assess queue depth — know the volume before you start
3. Set prefetch limit to a conservative value (20–30% of steady-state throughput)
4. Resume consumers at reduced concurrency
5. Monitor consumer error rate and latency as the backlog clears
6. Increase concurrency progressively as the service stabilizes

If messages have a TTL and significant messages have expired: do not drain blindly. Inspect a sample of the dead-letter queue before deciding to reprocess, discard, or manually handle.

## Part 7: Recovery Validation

### Confirming Customer Impact Is Actually Zero

Dashboard green does not mean customer impact zero. This gap has caused premature incident closure more times than it should have.

The verification gap: your service metrics show error rate < 0.1%. But:
- CDN cache is still serving stale error pages to some users
- Sessions that were dropped during the incident have not been re-established
- Retry backoff means some clients are still in their backoff window and have not tried again
- Downstream systems that received corrupt data during the cascade are still serving that corrupt data

Full recovery validation requires checking the customer journey end-to-end, not just the service dashboard.

### Verification Checklist Before Declaring Resolution

- [ ] Service error rate < threshold for at least 5 minutes (not just 30 seconds)
- [ ] Latency within normal range (p50, p95, p99)
- [ ] All circuit breakers in closed state (no edges still tripped)
- [ ] Traffic shift or failover reversed (traffic back to primary, if applicable)
- [ ] Feature flags re-enabled (if any were disabled as mitigation)
- [ ] Queue depth returned to near-zero (backlog cleared)
- [ ] Downstream systems that were affected have also returned to normal
- [ ] Synthetic transaction successfully completed end-to-end (see below)
- [ ] On-call engineer has verbally confirmed from customer data (support queue, real-time session data) that customer experience is normal

### Synthetic Transaction Testing Post-Recovery

A synthetic transaction is a scripted customer journey executed against production infrastructure but isolated from real customer data. It validates the end-to-end path, not just individual service health.

Minimum post-recovery synthetic transactions to run:
1. Login / authentication flow
2. The specific journey that was impacted during the incident (checkout, search, data fetch, etc.)
3. Any journey that traversed the cascade origin service

If any synthetic fails, the incident is not resolved — it is mitigated at best. Do not declare resolution.

In Kubernetes environments, synthetic transactions can be run as one-off Jobs:
```bash
kubectl create job --from=cronjob/synthetic-checkout synthetic-checkout-postrecovery -n synthetic
kubectl logs job/synthetic-checkout-postrecovery -n synthetic --follow
```

### Chaos Engineering as Recovery Validation

The highest form of recovery validation is to test your recovery procedures before you need them.

**Netflix Simian Army model**:
- **Chaos Monkey**: randomly terminates instances in production
- **Chaos Gorilla**: removes an entire availability zone
- **Latency Monkey**: injects artificial latency into service calls

The outcome: recovery procedures are exercised continuously. When a real incident occurs, the steps are familiar and the runbooks are current.

**Google DiRT (Disaster Recovery Testing)**: annual exercises where Google intentionally fails systems and practices recovery. Recovery procedures that have not been practiced are unreliable — the people who wrote them may have left, the systems may have changed, the procedures may not work anymore.

**GameDay structure for your environment**:
1. Define the failure scenario (specific service failure, zone loss, dependency outage)
2. Set a start time and duration (45–90 minutes)
3. Assign IC and engineering roles
4. Execute the failure injection in a controlled environment (staging that mirrors production topology)
5. Run through the detection-decomposition-mitigation-validation cycle
6. Debrief: what worked, what was slower than expected, what was missing from the runbook

GameDay findings should generate backlog items with P1 priority if they reveal a gap in your RTO/RPO capability.

## Best Practices and Guardrails

### Pre-Incident (Build Into Process)

- **Pre-test rollback procedures quarterly**. Do not assume a rollback works. Run it in staging against a production-mirror database. Time it. Confirm the result.
- **Validate rollback artifacts before every major incident response**. An unvalidated rollback artifact is a loaded risk, not a safety net.
- **Define RTO/RPO for every Tier 1 and Tier 2 service** before they are needed. If you do not know your RTO, you cannot choose the right recovery strategy.
- **Design graceful degradation modes into every service that calls external dependencies**. If there is no degraded mode, there is no graceful degradation option during an incident.
- **Use backward-compatible schema migrations (expand-contract)**. This eliminates the schema migration trap and preserves rollback viability.
- **Jitter all client retry logic**. Fixed-interval retries without jitter are a thundering herd waiting to happen on recovery.

### During Incident (IC Enforcement)

- **Enforce 15/30/60 minute mitigation checkpoints**. If not mitigated at 15 minutes: reassess strategy. If not mitigated at 30 minutes: escalate or change approach. If not mitigated at 60 minutes: executive notification, strategy overhaul.
- **Serialize execution actions**. Parallel investigation is encouraged; parallel execution is dangerous.
- **Do not declare resolution until synthetic validation passes**. Dashboard green is necessary but not sufficient.
- **Keep the mitigation vs. resolution distinction explicit in the incident ticket**. Stamp: "Mitigation achieved at T+22 min. Resolution in progress."

### Post-Incident (Structural Improvement)

- **CC audit**: compute CC for every Tier 1 service after every significant incident. Add circuit breakers wherever CC > 2.0.
- **FLI baseline**: track FLI per incident. A downward trend means your architecture is becoming less isolated over time — a warning signal.
- **DSA reduction**: reduce transitive synchronous dependencies. Every synchronous transitive hop is a cascade vector.
- **Runbook currency**: update runbooks within 48 hours of an incident. Stale runbooks are worse than no runbooks — they give false confidence.

### Risk Matrix for Recovery Decisions

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation | Effectiveness | Residual |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Rollback to bad artifact | M | H | Validate artifact pre-rollback | H | L |
| Traffic shift overloads destination | M | H | Verify destination headroom first | H | L |
| Queue drain overwhelms recovering service | H | M | Set prefetch limit, drain at 20-30% | M | L |
| Schema mismatch after code rollback | L | H | Use expand-contract migrations | H | L |
| Premature incident closure before validation | H | M | Require synthetic validation gate | H | L |
| Retry storm re-triggers after recovery | M | H | Rate limit at origin, add jitter | M | L |

## Summary: Recovery Planning Checklist

### Mitigation Phase (T+0 to mitigation declared)

- [ ] RTO for affected service confirmed — know your time budget
- [ ] Rollback or fix-forward decision made using the decision matrix
- [ ] Recovery strategy selected from the taxonomy (code rollback, traffic shift, etc.)
- [ ] Rollback artifact verified clean (if rollback selected)
- [ ] Execution actions serialized — no conflicting parallel changes
- [ ] 15-minute checkpoint: is customer impact stopping? If not, reassess.
- [ ] 30-minute checkpoint: if not mitigated, escalate or change strategy
- [ ] Queue paused if applicable — backlog will drain post-recovery, not during

### Validation Phase (mitigation declared to resolution declared)

- [ ] Service error rate below threshold for 5+ consecutive minutes
- [ ] All circuit breakers closed
- [ ] Traffic shifts and failovers reversed
- [ ] Feature flags re-enabled
- [ ] Synthetic transaction passed end-to-end
- [ ] Downstream systems confirmed healthy
- [ ] Customer support data confirms experience normal
- [ ] Incident ticket stamped: "Resolution declared at [timestamp]"

### Post-Incident (48 hours after resolution)

- [ ] Postmortem scheduled within 5 business days
- [ ] Runbook updated with any gaps found during the incident
- [ ] CC audit run for services involved in the cascade
- [ ] Corrective action tickets created and prioritized in backlog
- [ ] GameDay scenario created if a recovery procedure was exercised for the first time during this incident
