## Part 1: What Triage Actually Is

### Triage Is Not Diagnosis

This distinction is the most important thing in this chapter. Triage and diagnosis are different jobs, done at different times, with different goals.

**Medical analogy**: When a patient arrives in the ER with chest pain, the triage nurse does not determine whether it is a STEMI, a pulmonary embolism, or costochondritis. The triage nurse determines: *Does this person need a surgeon in the next ten minutes, or can they wait?* That question can be answered in 90 seconds. The diagnosis happens later, by someone else, with more time and more information.

In production systems, the equivalent is:
- **Triage**: Is this breaking users right now? How many? Is it getting worse? What do I need to do in the next five minutes?
- **Diagnosis**: What is the root cause? Why did this code path fail? What changed at 14:32?

**The failure mode**: Engineers who are strong diagnosticians often collapse triage into diagnosis. They start reading logs at 02:00 when they should be assessing scope. The result is that five minutes pass, the incident is spreading, and no one has declared a severity or opened a bridge. The incident grows unchecked while the engineer is reading stack traces.

**The mental model**: Triage is answering five questions in two minutes. Diagnosis is answering one question over twenty minutes. Never start the second job until you have finished the first.

---

### The Triage Question Set

These five questions, answered in order, constitute a complete triage:

**Q1: Is anything broken right now?**  
Not "was something broken" — right now. Validate the alert is live, not stale. Check the current error rate, not the spike that triggered the alert three minutes ago.

**Q2: How many users and transactions are affected?**  
Not exact — approximate. Is it 10 users or 10,000? Is it 2% of checkout traffic or 80%? Order-of-magnitude accuracy is sufficient for triage. Exactness is a job for the impact analysis phase.

**Q3: Is it spreading?**  
Is the error rate climbing? Are adjacent services showing correlated spikes? A contained failure that is stable at 5% error rate is a different problem from a failure that was 5% three minutes ago and is now 15%.

**Q4: Do we have a mitigation path?**  
Is there a known runbook? Is there a feature flag that can disable the failing component? Is there a rollback available? You do not need to execute the mitigation during triage — you need to know whether one exists, because that affects time-to-resolution estimates and communication.

**Q5: How fast is the budget burning?**  
Two sub-questions: (a) Revenue — what is the per-minute revenue exposure? (b) Error budget — are we inside or outside the monthly SLA allocation? A failure that burns through the remaining error budget in two hours is a different severity than one that consumes 10% of the remaining budget.

---

### The Cost of Bad Triage

**Undertriage** occurs when you assign a severity lower than the actual impact warrants.

Consequences:
- IC is not engaged. No one is coordinating.
- Senior engineers are not paged. The right people are not on the call.
- Exec notification does not happen. Leadership is surprised.
- External communication is delayed. Customers are in the dark.
- The incident runs longer because response is under-resourced.

**Pattern**: Checkout is down for enterprise customers only. The alert fires on a service that shows 3% overall error rate — within normal variation for that service. An engineer sees 3% and calls it SEV3. But enterprise customers represent 40% of revenue and 100% of them are hitting the broken path. This is a SEV1 being triaged as a SEV3.

**Overtriage** occurs when you assign a severity higher than the actual impact warrants.

Consequences:
- Executives are woken at 02:00 for a non-emergency.
- Senior SRE bandwidth is consumed on minor events.
- Teams become desensitized. The next real SEV1 is treated with skepticism.
- Alert fatigue sets in at the leadership level — they stop trusting the paging system.

**Pattern**: A monitoring agent on a single non-production host starts failing. Someone pages the VP of Engineering. This is a SEV4 being escalated as a SEV1.

**The calibration target**: Triage severity should match reality within one level, 95% of the time. Systematic bias in either direction is a process failure.

## Part 2: Severity Classification Framework

### The Four-Level Model

The industry standard is four severity levels. Some organizations use five (adding SEV0 for catastrophic/existential events). The four-level model is sufficient for most production systems.

---

| Severity | Name | Plain Language |
|----------|------|----------------|
| SEV1 | Critical | The house is on fire. Everyone stops what they are doing. |
| SEV2 | Major | A room is on fire. Significant response, but not all-hands. |
| SEV3 | Degraded | The smoke detector is beeping. Address it during business hours with urgency. |
| SEV4 | Minor | A lightbulb is out. Log it, schedule it, no paging. |

---

### SEV1 — Critical

**Definition**: A failure that causes complete or near-complete loss of a core user journey, affects a significant user population or high-value customer segment, and is actively causing revenue loss or regulatory exposure.

**Classification criteria**:

| Dimension | SEV1 Threshold |
|-----------|---------------|
| Customer impact | >20% of users on a critical journey OR any outage of an enterprise/premium tier |
| Revenue exposure | >$10,000/minute OR >5% of daily revenue |
| SLA breach risk | Will exhaust monthly error budget within 60 minutes at current rate |
| Spread risk | Active cascade detected — 2+ services showing correlated degradation |
| Regulatory | Any confirmed PCI, HIPAA, or SOX data integrity failure |

**Response requirements**:
- MTTA target: < 5 minutes
- IC: Required immediately — senior SRE or SRE Manager
- Exec notification: Within 15 minutes of declaration
- Bridge: Open immediately, all-hands join
- External status page: Update within 10 minutes of declaration
- Cadence: Internal updates every 15 minutes

**Real example — Checkout Outage**:  
At 14:47, the payment gateway begins returning HTTP 502 for all requests. The checkout service error rate climbs from 0.1% to 94% in 90 seconds. The service processes $2.3M/day in GMV. Revenue exposure: $2.3M / (24×60) = $1,597/minute. At 94% error rate on checkout, effective exposure is ~$1,502/minute. The failure is not spreading — payment gateway is isolated. But revenue exposure alone clears the SEV1 bar in 90 seconds. BRI = (0.94) × (1.0 journey criticality for checkout) × (1.0 tier weight for payment gateway) = 0.94. Immediate SEV1 declaration.

---

### SEV2 — Major

**Definition**: A failure that significantly degrades a user journey for a meaningful portion of the user base, causes measurable revenue impact, or risks becoming a SEV1 if not contained within the next 30-60 minutes.

**Classification criteria**:

| Dimension | SEV2 Threshold |
|-----------|---------------|
| Customer impact | 5–20% of users on a critical journey OR >50% on a non-critical journey |
| Revenue exposure | $1,000–$10,000/minute |
| SLA breach risk | Will exhaust monthly error budget within 4 hours |
| Spread risk | Cascade risk elevated but not confirmed — one upstream dependency showing stress |
| Regulatory | Potential exposure; not yet confirmed |

**Response requirements**:
- MTTA target: < 10 minutes
- IC: Required — senior SRE or on-call lead
- Exec notification: Notify within 30 minutes; wake overnight only if escalation path clear
- Bridge: Open; targeted responders (not all-hands)
- External status page: Update if user-visible; optional if backend-only
- Cadence: Internal updates every 30 minutes

**Real example — Recommendation Service Degradation**:  
At 09:15, the recommendation engine begins returning stale results with 8-second latency (P95 normally 200ms). Error rate is 0%. Users are not failing — they are experiencing slow, degraded recommendations. Traffic to recommendation-driven pages drops 22% as users abandon. Revenue impact: recommendation-driven pages contribute 18% of GMV. Effective impact: 22% abandonment × 18% GMV share = ~4% GMV loss = ~$1,380/min. User count: ~35,000 active sessions affected. BRI = (0.35 session fraction) × (0.60 journey criticality for browse/discovery) × (0.50 tier weight for P2 recommendation service) = 0.105. By BRI alone this is borderline SEV3. Revenue exposure at $1,380/min clears the SEV2 threshold ($1,000–$10,000/min). Multi-dimension rule: classify by the highest dimension. **Declare SEV2.** Requires IC and active response, but not all-hands emergency.

---

### SEV3 — Degraded

**Definition**: A failure that affects a small fraction of users, causes minor or indirect revenue impact, and is unlikely to cascade. Addressable with normal work-hours resources.

**Classification criteria**:

| Dimension | SEV3 Threshold |
|-----------|---------------|
| Customer impact | <5% of users on any journey OR non-critical journey, any fraction |
| Revenue exposure | <$1,000/minute; indirect impact only |
| SLA breach risk | Will exhaust monthly error budget within 24 hours |
| Spread risk | Low — isolated to single service, no upstream dependencies stressed |
| Regulatory | No exposure |

**Response requirements**:
- MTTA target: < 30 minutes (business hours); < 60 minutes (off-hours)
- IC: Optional — engineer resolving is self-managing
- Exec notification: None
- Bridge: Optional; Slack thread sufficient
- External status page: No update unless user-facing and visible
- Cadence: Update at resolution

**Real example — Search Autocomplete Latency**:  
Search autocomplete P99 latency is 1.4s (SLO: < 800ms). P50 is normal at 180ms. Only users with slow connections or 4G experience noticeable degradation. Autocomplete is a convenience feature; search still functions. Affected user count: ~2,400 sessions/hour. No direct revenue path from autocomplete. This is SEV3. An engineer investigates during their shift. No bridge, no paging.

---

### SEV4 — Minor / Informational

**Definition**: A failure that causes no user-facing impact or affects only internal tooling, with zero revenue exposure and no SLA risk.

**Classification criteria**:

| Dimension | SEV4 Threshold |
|-----------|---------------|
| Customer impact | Zero user-facing impact |
| Revenue exposure | Zero |
| SLA breach risk | None — internal systems only, or well within budget |
| Spread risk | None |
| Regulatory | None |

**Response requirements**:
- MTTA target: Next business day
- IC: None
- Exec notification: None
- Bridge: None
- Cadence: Ticket update at resolution

**Real example — Internal Dashboard Timeout**:  
The internal analytics dashboard times out for 3 of 12 engineers. The underlying data warehouse query is unoptimized after a schema change. Users are internal only. No customer or revenue impact. Ticket created. Assigned to on-call engineer for next business day.

## Part 3: The Triage Protocol — First 5 Minutes

The following is a prescriptive, step-by-step protocol. Each step has a time budget. The total budget is five minutes. After five minutes, you should have a declared severity and an active incident channel.

---

**Step 1 — Acknowledge the Alert (0:00–0:30)**

Action: Acknowledge in PagerDuty / alerting platform immediately.  
Purpose: Stops MTTA clock. Signals to the system that a human is aware.  
Do NOT: Start reading logs. Do NOT open dashboards yet.  
Output: Alert acknowledged. Clock stopped.

---

**Step 2 — Validate the Alert Is Real (0:30–1:30)**

Action: Open the primary dashboard for the alerting service. Look at the current metric — not the history.  
Check: Is the error rate still elevated? Is the alert still firing?  
If no: The spike resolved. Investigate why the alert fired, but this may be a false positive. Apply false positive classification (see Alert Architecture chapter).  
If yes: Proceed. The signal is real.  
Time limit: 60 seconds. You are not debugging. You are confirming existence.

---

**Step 3 — Assess Scope (1:30–3:00)**

Action: Answer Q2 from the triage question set. Use the fastest available proxy — do not run a full query.  

**Fast proxy method** (90 seconds):
1. Open service dashboard — what is the current RPS and error rate?
2. Multiply: `affected_RPS = total_RPS × error_rate`
3. Estimate user count: `affected_users ≈ affected_RPS × avg_session_duration_seconds / avg_requests_per_session`
4. Identify which user journey is affected — is this checkout, login, search, browse?

Example calculation:  
Total RPS: 4,200. Error rate: 18%. Affected RPS: 756/sec.  
Avg session duration: 8 minutes = 480 seconds. Avg requests/session: 45.  
Affected sessions active: 756 × 480 / 45 = 8,064 sessions ≈ 8,000 users affected right now.  
Journey: Checkout path (confirmed from service name in alert).

Output: "~8,000 users on checkout path, 18% error rate." That is your scope statement.

---

**Step 4 — Assess Spread Risk (3:00–3:45)**

Action: The 5-second cascade check — open the dependency map or service mesh dashboard. Look for correlated error rate spikes on services downstream or upstream of the failing service.

Check two things:
1. Are services that call the failing service also showing elevated error rates? (upstream impact)
2. Are services the failing service calls also showing elevated error rates? (downstream cause or effect)

**If both upstream and downstream are clean**: Failure is isolated. Low spread risk.  
**If upstream is also degraded**: The failing service may be a symptom, not the cause. Escalate triage scope.  
**If downstream is also degraded**: Cascade is in progress. Isolate first.

Time limit: 45 seconds. You are not debugging the dependency — you are confirming whether it is moving.

---

**Step 5 — Declare Severity and Assign IC (3:45–4:15)**

Action: Apply the classification framework from Part 2. Make a decision.  
Post to incident channel: "Declaring SEV[X] — [one sentence description]. IC: [name]."  
If SEV3/4 and you are the on-call: You are the IC. State that explicitly.  
If SEV1/2: Page the SRE Manager or senior on-call lead immediately.

Rule: Never leave the severity undeclared. "Unknown" is not a severity. If you have insufficient information, default one level higher and de-escalate when evidence is available.

---

**Step 6 — Open the Bridge / Incident Channel (4:15–4:30)**

Action: Create the incident in your incident management platform (PagerDuty, Firehydrant, Incident.io). This auto-creates a Slack channel or opens a Zoom bridge, depending on your tooling.  
First message in channel: Copy your scope statement from Step 3 and cascade assessment from Step 4.

---

**Step 7 — Page Additional Responders Based on Severity (4:30–4:45)**

| Severity | Page Who |
|----------|----------|
| SEV1 | IC + relevant service team lead + SRE Manager + Comms lead |
| SEV2 | IC + relevant service team lead |
| SEV3 | Assign to self or specific on-call; no additional paging |
| SEV4 | Create ticket; no paging |

---

**Step 8 — Post First Status Update (4:45–5:00)**

Internal update (all severities): Post in incident channel — current state, scope, next action, 15-minute update commitment.  
External update (SEV1/SEV2 with user-visible impact): Update status page — "We are investigating reports of [symptom]. [X] users may be affected. We will update in 15 minutes."

**Never**: Post "we are investigating" without a time commitment for the next update. That sentence by itself is noise.

## Part 4: Triage Decision Matrix

### Triage Is Not One-Time

The severity you declare at T+5 minutes is a hypothesis, not a verdict. It must be revisited every 15-30 minutes throughout the incident. New information changes the picture.

**Escalation triggers** — upgrade severity when:
- Affected user count increases by more than 2x from initial estimate
- A new user journey is confirmed affected
- A dependency is confirmed in cascade
- Revenue exposure crosses the next threshold
- A regulatory trigger is confirmed
- MTTR estimate exceeds what the current severity's error budget allows

**De-escalation triggers** — downgrade severity when:
- Error rate drops below the lower threshold for current severity
- Blast radius is confirmed smaller than initially estimated (e.g., the affected population was internal users, not external)
- A mitigation is applied that contains the impact even before root cause is found
- Cascade risk is eliminated (circuit breaker engaged, traffic cut)

---

### The Blast Radius Index (BRI)

BRI is a single normalized score that quantifies the customer impact of an incident. It is designed to be calculated in under two minutes during active triage.

**Formula**:

```
BRI = (affected_users / total_active_users) × journey_criticality × tier_weight
```

**Parameters**:

| Parameter | Description | Range |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| `affected_users / total_active_users` | Fraction of current active user base experiencing the failure | 0.0 – 1.0 |
| `journey_criticality` | How critical is the affected journey to user value? | 0.0 – 1.0 |
| `tier_weight` | What tier of service is failing? | 0.0 – 1.0 |

**Journey criticality reference values**:

| Journey | Criticality Score |
|---------|------------------|
| Payment / Checkout | 1.00 |
| Account login / authentication | 0.95 |
| Order management / fulfillment | 0.85 |
| Core product feature (e.g., primary feed) | 0.75 |
| Secondary feature (e.g., recommendations) | 0.60 |
| Convenience feature (e.g., autocomplete) | 0.35 |
| Internal tooling | 0.10 |

**Tier weight reference values**:

| Service Tier | Weight |
|-------------|--------|
| Tier 0 — Revenue-critical, external | 1.00 |
| Tier 1 — Core product, external | 0.85 |
| Tier 2 — Supporting, external | 0.65 |
| Tier 3 — Internal, operational | 0.35 |
| Tier 4 — Internal, non-operational | 0.15 |

**BRI thresholds**:

| BRI Score | Severity |
|-----------|----------|
| > 0.70 | SEV1 |
| 0.30 – 0.70 | SEV2 |
| 0.10 – 0.30 | SEV3 |
| < 0.10 | SEV4 |

---

### 3-Question Proxy for BRI Under Time Pressure

When you cannot run the full calculation, answer these three questions and use the table to approximate:

**Q1**: What fraction of active users are affected? (< 5%, 5–20%, 20–60%, > 60%)  
**Q2**: Is the affected journey revenue-critical? (Yes / Partial / No)  
**Q3**: Is this cascading or isolated? (Cascading / Isolated)

| Q1 | Q2 | Q3 | Approximate BRI | Severity |
|----|----|----|-----------------|----------|
| > 60% | Yes | Cascading | 0.85+ | SEV1 |
| > 60% | Yes | Isolated | 0.65–0.80 | SEV1–SEV2 |
| 20–60% | Yes | Any | 0.50–0.75 | SEV2 |
| 5–20% | Yes | Cascading | 0.40–0.60 | SEV2 |
| 5–20% | Partial | Isolated | 0.20–0.35 | SEV3 |
| < 5% | Any | Isolated | < 0.15 | SEV4 |

## Part 5: Cascade Detection During Triage

### The Cascade Coefficient (CC) — Real-Time Detection Ratio

During triage, before you have time to compute the full weighted CC formula, use this ratio as a fast detection signal. It answers: "Is this failure propagating right now?"

**Triage-time ratio**:

```
CC_observed = (services showing correlated error spike) / (direct dependencies of the failing service)
```

This is a real-time observation ratio (0.0–1.0), not the canonical weighted CC formula. Use it in the first five minutes to decide whether cascade isolation is needed. The canonical CC formula — used for pre-incident risk scoring — is in ch05 (Part 4, Step 5).

**Triage thresholds**:

| CC_observed | Interpretation | Action |
|-------------|---------------|--------|
| 0.0 | No correlated spikes — failure isolated | Normal triage; monitor |
| 0.1 – 0.3 | Low cascade risk — one dependency stressed | Watch; do not escalate on CC alone |
| 0.3 – 0.6 | Moderate cascade risk | Escalate severity one level; prepare circuit breaker |
| > 0.6 | Active cascade — majority of dependencies affected | Isolate immediately; escalate to SEV1 |

---

### The 5-Second Cascade Check

Open your service mesh or APM dashboard. Look for this pattern: **a second service, not the alerting service, showing an error rate spike that began within 60 seconds of the primary failure.**

Two things to verify:
1. Is the second service a dependency of the first? Check the dependency map.
2. Did the spike on the second service start after the primary failure? Check the time axis.

If both are true: you have cascade evidence. Act.

---

### Cascade vs. Coincidence

Two services failing simultaneously is not always cascade. Consider:

**Same root cause, different failure path**: A DNS resolution failure can cause multiple services to fail at the same time without one causing the other. They share a dependency (DNS) but are not directly coupled.

**Maintenance window collision**: Two teams deployed at the same time. Coincident failures from independent causes.

**How to distinguish**: Cascade shows a time delay and a dependency relationship. Coincidence shows simultaneous onset and no dependency relationship. Check the dependency graph first.

---

### When Cascade Is Confirmed

The action sequence is: **Isolate → Stabilize → Investigate**. Never reverse this order.

1. **Isolate**: Engage circuit breakers on the failing service. Cut traffic to the failing path if a degraded fallback exists. Do not wait for root cause.
2. **Stabilize**: Confirm dependent services are recovering after isolation. Watch error rates for 2-3 minutes.
3. **Investigate**: Only after the blast radius is contained, begin root cause analysis.

**The mistake**: Investigating while the cascade is still spreading. Every minute spent on root cause while traffic continues to flow through a broken path is a minute of additional user impact.

## Part 6: Alert-to-Triage Integration

### Why Alert Design Determines Triage Quality

An alert that fires without context forces the on-call engineer to do triage from scratch. They have to open dashboards, find the right service, identify the affected journey, and estimate scope — all under time pressure, all at 02:00. This adds 3-5 minutes to MTTA and increases triage errors.

An alert that fires with context pre-computes the first three steps of triage. The engineer reads the alert body and knows the scope, the journey, and the suggested severity before they open a single dashboard.

**Triage quality is an artifact of alert design.**

---

### Alert Anatomy — Required Fields

Every alert should contain:

| Field | Example | Why It Matters |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Service name | `payment-gateway` | Identifies scope immediately |
| Metric and current value | `error_rate: 18.4%` | Validates alert is real without opening a dashboard |
| Affected journey | `checkout path` | Drives severity classification |
| Affected percentage | `~8,200 of 45,000 active sessions` | Enables BRI estimation |
| Trend | `↑ from 3.2% over last 5 min` | Enables CC assessment |
| Runbook link | `https://runbooks.internal/payment-gateway/high-error-rate` | Provides mitigation path immediately |
| Suggested severity | `Suggested: SEV2` | Pre-computes the classification decision |
| Environment | `production / us-east-1` | Prevents wasted response to non-production alert |

---

### Alert Precision Rate (APR) and Triage Behavior

**APR** = actionable alerts / total alerts fired, over a rolling 30-day period.

When APR drops below 0.50, a systematic behavior change occurs in the on-call rotation: engineers begin investigating alerts before deciding whether to act on them. This is the reverse of the correct triage order.

The correct sequence: **Assess → Decide → Investigate**  
The APR-degraded sequence: **Investigate → Assess → Decide** (if ever)

In the degraded sequence, the engineer reads 20 log lines, determines the alert is a false positive, and acknowledges. Response time to real incidents increases by 4-8 minutes on average because the engineer is conditioned to assume false positive.

**Target APR**: > 0.85. Below 0.70, audit and prune the alert catalog before addressing response time improvements.

---

### The "Who Cares at 3am" Test

For every alert in your catalog, ask: "If this fires at 03:00, would the on-call engineer take a meaningful action within the next 10 minutes?"

If the answer is no — for any reason — the alert should not page at 03:00. It should:
- Be downgraded to a Slack notification
- Be batched into a daily digest
- Be moved to business-hours-only paging
- Be deleted if it has never resulted in a meaningful action in the past 90 days

This is not about reducing alert volume. It is about ensuring that every page at 03:00 is worth waking a human for. When that contract is maintained, engineers trust the paging system. When it is violated repeatedly, engineers stop trusting it — and the next real SEV1 gets a slow response.
