# Chapter 03 — Root Cause Analysis at Program Scale

**Audience**: Director / VP of Operations candidates  
**Context**: T-Mobile-scale SRE environment — millions of active subscribers, 3,000+ CI/CD changes/month, 50+ SEV2s/month across platform services  
**Prerequisite**: Chapter 02 — Problem Management Lifecycle

---

## 1. RCA at Program Level — Portfolio View

Single-incident RCA is a tactical discipline. A Duty Manager runs one. A Director owns the program that produces fifty of them per month and extracts systemic signal from the aggregate.

At T-Mobile scale, you will generate 600+ RCA-eligible incidents per year across platform, network, and application tiers. Full 5-Why on every one of them is operationally impossible and strategically wrong. The incidents that warrant deep investigation are the ones generating repeat business impact — not the noisiest ones, not the ones with the most political attention.

**The portfolio question is**: which problems, across all active problem records, are consuming the most incident volume and have the highest MTBI (Mean Time Between Incidents) improvement potential if fixed?

A Director running a healthy RCA program answers four portfolio-level questions every month:

1. **Volume concentration**: Which five problem root causes account for the highest incident recurrence? (Pareto principle applies — in a mature environment, 20% of problems drive 80% of incident volume.)
2. **Fix progress**: Of active RCAs, which corrective actions are past their committed due date? Who owns the remediation backlog?
3. **Systemic vs. symptomatic fixes**: Are corrective actions modifying the underlying system, or are they adding more runbook steps and alert thresholds on top of a broken architecture?
4. **Escape rate**: How many incidents this month were caused by problems with an existing open RCA? That number should trend toward zero as fixes land.

> **What the VP Asks**
>
> "Show me the top 5 problems by incident recurrence this quarter. For each one: what is the root cause, what is the corrective action, who owns it, and what is the fix ETA? If the ETA has slipped, tell me why and what you're doing about it."
>
> If you cannot answer this question in under three minutes from a live dashboard or report, your RCA program is producing documentation, not outcomes. The VP is not asking for RCA documents — they are asking for evidence that the program is reducing incident recurrence over time.

**Director responsibility** here is not to facilitate RCAs — that is the Problem Manager's job. Your responsibility is governance: ensuring the program has teeth. That means:

- RCA corrective actions are tracked in the same system as engineering work (Jira, not a spreadsheet)
- Fix completion is a metric in your QBR, not an afterthought
- Teams cannot close a Problem record until corrective actions are verified as implemented, not just approved
- You escalate to engineering leadership when a fix has slipped twice — the third slip means it needs to be prioritized in the next PI planning cycle

## 2. Deciding Which Problems Get Full RCA vs. Expedited vs. Workaround-Only

Not all incidents are equal. Not all problems warrant the same RCA investment. Misapplying this is one of the most common program-level failures — teams either over-RCA (burning engineer time on P4 incidents) or under-RCA (running workaround-only on P1 recurrences because the fix is hard).

The decision framework is a two-axis assessment: **Impact** (customer reach, revenue risk, regulatory exposure) versus **Recurrence Rate** (how often this problem is generating incidents).

### RCA Depth Decision Matrix

| | **Low Recurrence** (≤2 incidents/quarter) | **High Recurrence** (≥3 incidents/quarter) |
|---|---|---|
| **High Impact** (P1/P2, customer-visible, revenue or compliance risk) | **Expedited RCA** — root cause likely known; document, fix, verify; timebox to 5 business days | **Full RCA** — mandatory; causal chain must be fully mapped; fix must be systemic; executive visibility required |
| **Low Impact** (P3/P4, internal-only, minimal customer reach) | **Accepted Risk** — formally document known root cause; PM + Service Owner sign off; revisit quarterly | **Workaround-Only or Expedited RCA** — cost-of-fix vs. cost-of-failure analysis; if fix cost exceeds impact cost, document and monitor; if trend worsens, escalate to full RCA |

**Definitions for this matrix**:

- **Full RCA**: Structured investigation with facilitated session, blameless postmortem, formal root cause statement, causal chain documentation, and verified corrective actions. Owner: Problem Manager + Engineering Lead. Timeline: 5–10 business days.
- **Expedited RCA**: Abbreviated investigation where root cause is suspected from prior knowledge. Confirm hypothesis, document concisely, assign fix. Timeline: 2–3 business days.
- **Workaround-Only**: Permanent workaround implemented and documented. Root cause recorded but fix deprioritized. Risk formally accepted and signed. Requires re-evaluation if recurrence increases.
- **Accepted Risk**: Root cause known. Fix technically possible but not cost-justified. Business owner and Service Owner sign a formal risk acceptance record. Time-bounded — must be revisited at next quarterly review.

**Director callout**: Accepted Risk is not "we gave up." It is a deliberate, documented business decision. Without formal sign-off, it becomes technical debt that nobody owns, which means the next incident will surprise everyone. Make every Accepted Risk visible and owned.

## 3. RCA Method Selection

Method choice is not a philosophical preference — it is driven by the failure type, evidence availability, and how much time you have before the root cause evidence degrades.

### Method Selector Table

| Failure Type | Recommended Method | Time Required | Output Format |
|---|---|---|---|
| Linear causal chain, single failure mode, human-process interaction | **5 Whys** | 2–4 hours | Causal chain diagram + corrective action table |
| Multi-factor failure, unclear which category the root cause lives in, team alignment needed | **Fishbone (Ishikawa)** | 4–8 hours | Fishbone diagram + ranked contributing factors |
| Distributed system failure, multiple contributing paths, AND/OR causality | **Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** | 1–3 days | FTA diagram + minimal cut sets + corrective actions per path |
| Pre-deployment risk analysis on new systems or major architecture changes | **FMEA** | 2–5 days | Failure mode register with RPN scores + mitigation plan |

**5 Whys — operational reality**

Works well when the causal chain is linear and evidence is still warm (within 24–48 hours). Best for human-process failures: a deployment step was skipped, a runbook had an error, an alert was suppressed during a maintenance window that overran. Fast, low overhead, accessible to non-engineers in a joint session.

Breaks down in distributed systems. "Why did the API gateway return 503s?" → "Why was the upstream service slow?" → "Why was the JVM under pressure?" — the chain branches into five parallel paths and the 5-Why structure cannot hold it. Forcing it produces a single root cause where there are actually three, and all three corrective actions are needed for the fix to hold.

Also tends toward blame. Structured facilitation must explicitly steer away from "Why did the engineer not check X" — that line ends at "human error," which is never an acceptable root cause.

**Fishbone (Ishikawa) — operational reality**

Useful when your team cannot agree on what category the root cause lives in. The six bones (People, Process, Technology, Environment, Measurement, Materials — adapt for software: People, Process, Platform, Code, Observability, External Dependencies) force structured brainstorming. A team of eight will surface causes they would not have found in free-form discussion.

Weakness: the fishbone shows *what* contributed but not *how strongly* or *whether it would have happened anyway*. Two bones can both be true but only one is the load-bearing cause. You need a prioritization step after the fishbone to identify which contributing factor, if removed, would have prevented the incident.

**Fault Tree Analysis — operational reality**

The right tool for complex distributed system failures — a Kafka consumer lag event that cascades into an API timeout that cascades into a mobile checkout failure affecting 800,000 subscribers during peak traffic. FTA maps the AND/OR logic: the checkout failure required *both* the API timeout *and* the circuit breaker not triggering. That AND gate tells you two things must be fixed, not one.

Requires a facilitator who understands the system architecture. At T-Mobile scale, this means having your platform architect in the room. The output directly maps to your system dependency diagram, which makes it auditable and useful for future failure modeling.

Time-intensive. Do not use it for P3 incidents or incidents with a single obvious cause.

**FMEA — operational reality**

FMEA is a pre-launch tool. Using it reactively after an incident is like reading the fire safety manual after the building burned. Its value is in the risk priority number (RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detectability) calculated *before* a system goes live. When your platform team is launching a new microservice that will handle payment authorization for 40 million T-Mobile customers, FMEA run during design review catches failure modes that would otherwise become your next SEV1.

Build this into your engineering readiness gate — not as optional documentation, but as a signed artifact that must exist before a new service reaches production at scale.

## 4. Running a High-Quality RCA

### Evidence Collection — Do This First, Before the Session

Evidence degrades. Logs rotate. Engineers' memories shift toward what makes sense rather than what happened. Start collecting before you schedule the RCA session.

**Evidence collection checklist**:
- [ ] Incident timeline from on-call tool (PagerDuty, ServiceNow) — exact alert timestamps
- [ ] Splunk logs: service logs, infrastructure logs, security events — pull the full window from T-30min to T+60min relative to incident start
- [ ] APM traces: AppDynamics or equivalent — transaction traces showing latency and error rates at the time of failure
- [ ] Grafana / CloudWatch metrics: CPU, memory, network, queue depth — pull the 4-hour window
- [ ] Change management records: all changes deployed in the 72 hours prior to incident — code, config, infrastructure, database
- [ ] On-call logs: engineer notes, commands run, decisions made during the incident
- [ ] Runbook adherence: was the runbook followed? If deviated from, where and why?
- [ ] Customer impact data: number of users affected, transaction failure rate, revenue exposure window

Missing evidence is itself a finding. If you cannot determine what happened between 02:14 and 02:31 because there is no log coverage for that service at that layer, that gap becomes a corrective action: instrument that service.

### Facilitation Rules

The RCA session is blameless by design, not by hope. Set the rules at the opening of every session:

1. We are investigating the system, not the people. Systems allow human errors to cause customer impact. The system is the root cause.
2. "I should have" and "they should have" are prohibited phrases. "The runbook lacked a step for X" is the correct framing.
3. The facilitator manages time — evidence timeline first, hypotheses second, root cause last. Do not let the group jump to solutions before the cause is agreed.
4. Silence at the table means the evidence is being read, not that agreement has been reached. Explicitly poll.

**Timeline reconstruction**: Build the event timeline event-by-event, with timestamps, before anyone offers a root cause hypothesis. The timeline reveals detection lag (time between failure start and first alert) and response lag (time between first alert and first respective action). Both are findings. At T-Mobile, a 12-minute detection lag on a payment service failure affecting millions of checkout attempts is not acceptable — and it shows up only when you reconstruct the timeline.

### Root Cause Statement Format

Every RCA must contain exactly one root cause statement in this structure:

> **Due to** [structural condition — the underlying architectural or process weakness], **when** [triggering event — the specific thing that happened], **[failure mode] resulted in [customer impact].**

Example: *Due to the Cassandra cluster having no automated capacity enforcement and JVM heap alerts configured at 85% with a 1-minute window, when a batch reconciliation job executed during peak transaction load, heap exhaustion caused write latency to spike above 10 seconds, resulting in 340,000 failed payment authorizations over a 22-minute window.*

This statement forces three things: it names the structural condition (the real root cause, not the trigger), it separates the trigger from the cause, and it quantifies customer impact. If your root cause statement does not contain all three elements, it is not complete.

### Corrective Actions — The Standard

Every corrective action must be:
- **Specific**: Names the exact system, configuration, threshold, process, or code being changed
- **Assigned**: One named owner. Not "the platform team." One engineer's name.
- **Time-bound**: Hard date. Not "next sprint." A date.
- **Verifiable**: Defines how completion will be confirmed — PR merged, alert fired in test environment, monitoring dashboard updated

**Unacceptable**: "Improve monitoring on the Cassandra cluster."  
**Acceptable**: "Update Cassandra JVM heap alert threshold from 85% to 70% with a 5-minute sustained breach window, tested in staging before production rollout, owned by [Engineer Name], completed by [Date]. Verified by on-call confirming alert fires in load test."

The difference is accountability. Vague corrective actions are written to close the document, not to fix the system.

## 5. Common RCA Anti-Patterns

These patterns appear at every scale. At T-Mobile scale, they compound — one bad RCA teaches a team how to write bad RCAs, and the pattern replicates across fifty incidents per month.

**Stopping at proximate cause**

"The server ran out of disk space." That is an observation, not a root cause. Why did it run out? Was there a log rotation misconfiguration? Was a deployment job writing uncompressed artifacts to a data volume? Was there no disk capacity alert? What was the architectural decision that put application logs and data artifacts on the same volume? Stopping at "disk full" generates the corrective action "add more disk," which means this incident will repeat the next time a deployment job runs, just with a larger disk.

**Human error as root cause**

Never acceptable. Period. If your RCA concludes "the engineer did not follow the runbook," the correct question is: why did the runbook allow an engineer to skip that step and cause customer impact? The corrective action is automation, enforcement, or a gate — not retraining the engineer. Engineers will make errors at 3 AM during a P1. The system must be designed to survive those errors.

**Vague corrective actions**

Described above. The organizational symptom of vague CAs is an RCA program with 90% on-time document approval and 40% corrective action completion. The documents are closed; the system is not fixed. A Director who accepts vague CAs is accepting documentation theater.

**RCA without fix verification**

An RCA is not complete when the document is approved. It is complete when the corrective action is implemented, the fix is validated (either in test or confirmed in production via the next incident window without recurrence), and the Problem record is closed with evidence of verification. Skipping verification means you will see the same incident six months later and reopen the same RCA.

**Timeline gaps presented as acceptable**

"We could not determine what happened between 02:14 and 02:31" is not a dead end — it is a corrective action. The gap exists because a service is not instrumented, because log retention is too short, or because trace context is not propagated across service boundaries. Name the gap, own the gap, fix the gap. The next incident in that service will thank you.

## 6. Quality Gates — What Makes an RCA Acceptable to Close

An RCA document that passes a quality gate review is not just complete — it is defensible. It must be defensible to your VP in a QBR, to a regulatory auditor, and to an SRE engineer who inherits the system six months from now and needs to understand why a particular design decision was made.

### RCA Quality Gate Checklist

- [ ] **Root cause statement** is present in the required format: structural condition + triggering event + failure mode + customer impact
- [ ] **Root cause is systemic** — does not name a human as the root cause; does not stop at a proximate symptom
- [ ] **Timeline** is complete with timestamps, no unexplained gaps (gaps are acknowledged as findings with CAs)
- [ ] **Evidence sources** are listed: Splunk query strings or saved searches referenced, metric dashboard links, change records cited
- [ ] **Contributing factors** are documented separately from the root cause — multiple factors acknowledged where they exist
- [ ] **Corrective actions** are specific, assigned to named owners, time-bound, and include a verification method
- [ ] **Customer impact** is quantified — number of users, transaction volume, revenue exposure, or service degradation duration
- [ ] **Detection and response lag** are both calculated and assessed — is the lag acceptable? If not, there must be a CA addressing it
- [ ] **Corrective actions are tracked** in the engineering work system (Jira or equivalent), not only in the RCA document
- [ ] **Fix completion is not marked closed** on the corrective action until the implementing engineer has confirmed the change is in production and verified

**Minimum approval required to close a Problem record**: Problem Manager sign-off + Service Owner sign-off. For P1 incidents or incidents with regulatory exposure (payment, authentication, data privacy), Director sign-off is required before closure.

> **Director Callout**
>
> An RCA document that names a human as the root cause fails the quality gate. Return it to the Problem Manager with this note: "A human made an error. Why did the system allow that error to cause customer impact? That answer is the root cause. Resubmit."
>
> An RCA document with corrective actions that say "review," "improve," or "assess" without a specific deliverable fails the quality gate. Return it. These words are not actions — they are intentions. Intentions do not reduce MTBI.
>
> Your signature on a Problem closure means you have verified these two things. If you are signing RCAs you have not reviewed, you are signing documentation, not outcomes. At the Director level, that distinction is the job.

## Summary Reference

| RCA Depth | When to Use | Timeline | Approval |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full RCA | P1/P2, customer-visible, recurrence ≥3, unclear root cause | 5–10 business days | Problem Manager + Service Owner + Director (P1) |
| Expedited RCA | P2/P3, known failure mode, workaround available | 2–3 business days | Problem Manager + Service Owner |
| Workaround-Only | P3/P4, fix cost > failure cost, root cause suspected | 1 business day | Problem Manager |
| Accepted Risk | Root cause known, fix not justified | 1 business day | Service Owner + Business Owner; quarterly review |

| Method | Best For | Avoid When |
|---|---|---|
| 5 Whys | Linear chains, human-process failures, warm evidence | Distributed systems, multi-path failures |
| Fishbone | Multi-factor, unclear category, team alignment needed | Time-critical; produces inventory not causality |
| FTA | Distributed system failures, AND/OR causality | Simple single-cause failures; no architecture model available |
| FMEA | Pre-launch risk analysis, new systems | Post-incident reactive use (limited value) |
