# Chapter 07: Problem Management Governance and Reporting

> **Director/VP Lens:** Governance without authority is theater. A problem management program only works when roles have clear accountability, escalation paths have teeth, and the monthly report can drive a funding conversation. If your governance model cannot answer "who is accountable for this problem not being fixed," it is not governance — it is a filing system.

---

## 1. Problem Management Governance Model

At T-Mobile scale — processing millions of API transactions per day, running thousands of CI changes per month — problem management governance cannot be a rotating SRE hat or a committee-by-availability. It requires a dedicated Problem Manager role, clear service owner accountability, and a Director who is willing to break resource deadlocks.

### Roles and Governance Responsibilities

**Problem Manager**
- Owns the process end-to-end: problem record quality, SLA compliance, KEDB maintenance
- Chairs the weekly problem review meeting — non-negotiable, not delegatable
- Reports directly to the Director of Operations on program health
- Escalates to Director when fix commitments are missed beyond threshold
- Does NOT do the RCA work — manages the process that ensures RCA gets done

**Service Owner**
- Accountable for accepting risk on problems within their service
- Must formally sign off on any "accepted risk" disposition (see escalation section)
- Provides fix ETAs and escalates internally when engineering capacity is insufficient
- Cannot close a problem record without Problem Manager concurrence

**CAB (Change Advisory Board) Liaison**
- Ensures problem-driven changes are fast-tracked through change governance
- Reviews problem records that link to change events — feeds findings into CAB lessons learned
- Ensures emergency change process is invoked when P1 problem fixes conflict with change freeze

**Engineering Lead**
- Accountable for fix delivery timeline — owns the engineering commitment, not just the plan
- Attends weekly problem review to report on fix progress for assigned problems
- Escalates to Director of Operations when fix delivery is blocked by resource or dependency

**Director of Operations**
- Receives monthly problem management report
- Approves escalations beyond Problem Manager authority
- Breaks resource deadlocks — provides funding, capacity reallocation, or executive timeline pressure
- Formally accepts risk on problems where fix is deferred by organizational decision (not technical inability)

### RACI: Problem Management Governance Decisions

| Decision | Problem Manager | Service Owner | Engineering Lead | CAB Liaison | Director of Operations |
|----------|----------------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Open new problem record | R | C | I | — | I (P1 only) |
| Approve problem priority (P1/P2) | R | A | C | — | — |
| Accept risk (defer fix indefinitely) | C | A | C | — | A (requires Director sign-off) |
| Escalate to Director | R | C | C | — | A |
| Escalate to VP | I | C | — | — | R/A |
| Approve fix plan and ETA | R | A | R | — | — |
| Archive to KEDB | R | C | I | — | — |
| Approve emergency change for P1 fix | I | C | I | R | A (if CAB deadlock) |
| Close problem record | R | A | C | — | — |

R = Responsible | A = Accountable | C = Consulted | I = Informed

### Dedicated Role vs. Rotating Hat

At T-Mobile scale, a rotating Problem Manager hat (e.g., SRE on-call rotation) fails for three reasons:

1. **Context discontinuity**: Problem records span weeks to months. A rotating role loses institutional knowledge on handoff.
2. **Authority gap**: SREs on rotation do not have the organizational authority to hold Service Owners accountable for missed fix ETAs.
3. **Process drift**: A role with competing priorities (incident response, project work) will deprioritize problem management during busy periods — precisely when it matters most.

The dedicated Problem Manager does not need to be a deeply technical role. It needs strong process discipline, organizational credibility, and the ability to manage up when engineering leads miss commitments.

## 2. Weekly Problem Review Meeting

The weekly problem review is the operational heartbeat of problem management. Everything that happens during the week — incident correlation, RCA work, fix delivery — feeds into this meeting. The output of this meeting drives the next week's work.

### Meeting Structure

**Cadence:** Every week, same day, same time. This is not negotiable. Variability in cadence creates information gaps and reduces accountability pressure.

**Duration:** 50 minutes. If it runs over consistently, the root cause is either RCA being done in real time (wrong venue) or no decision-making authority in the room (wrong attendees).

**Participants:**
- Problem Manager (chair) — mandatory
- Service Owners for active P1/P2 problems — mandatory
- Engineering Leads for problems with active fix plans — mandatory
- KEDB Owner — mandatory
- SRE Team Lead — standing attendee

**Inputs (distributed 24 hours before meeting):**
- Updated problem records with current status for all P1/P2 problems
- Incident-to-problem correlation report (trailing 7 days)
- SLA breach report: which problems have missed or are approaching fix commitment deadlines
- New problem candidates identified during the week (from Splunk queries, incident reviews, or SRE observation)

### Agenda

**Minutes 0–10: SLA Breach Review**
- Which P1 problems have missed their fix commitment date?
- Which P2 problems are within 5 business days of their commitment date with no fix deployed?
- Decision required: extend commitment (with new date and justification), escalate to Director, or accept risk
- No deep-dive into why the fix is late — that is an offline conversation. The meeting decision is: what is the new commitment?

**Minutes 10–25: RCA Status Review**
- Problems in "Investigating" status that have exceeded their RCA SLA (P1: 5 days, P2: 10 days)
- Engineering Lead provides status: what has been found, what is blocking completion, when will RCA be done?
- Decision required: is the RCA blocked by something the Problem Manager can remove? Or does this require Director escalation?
- Output: updated RCA due dates, escalation decisions, any new findings that change problem priority

**Minutes 25–40: Fix Delivery Review**
- Problems in "Fix in Progress" status — are they on track for committed delivery date?
- Engineering Lead provides a traffic light: Green (on track), Amber (risk to delivery, mitigated), Red (delivery at risk, help needed)
- For Red items: what is needed? Problem Manager owns follow-up within 24 hours.
- This is NOT a detailed technical status update — it is a governance check on delivery commitment

**Minutes 40–50: New Problem Candidates**
- Problem Manager presents candidates identified during the week from Splunk queries, incident reviews, and proactive signals
- Service Owner for each candidate is present — they state whether they concur with the characterization
- Decision: approve as problem (assign priority and owner), reject with documented rationale, or defer to "Suspected Problem" status pending further investigation
- Output: new problem records opened with initial priority, owner, and RCA due date

### Outputs

After every meeting, Problem Manager publishes within 4 hours:
- Updated problem records reflecting meeting decisions
- Action items with named owners and due dates
- Escalation notifications to Director (same day, if any escalations were triggered)
- New problem records with assignments

### What NOT to Do in This Meeting

Do not conduct RCA in the weekly review. If an Engineering Lead starts walking through their investigation findings in detail, the Problem Manager stops them: "Send that to the problem record. What I need from you today is: when will the RCA be complete, and what do you need to get there?"

> **Director Callout #1:** "If your problem review meeting is running 2+ hours, it is either trying to do RCA in real time or has no decision-making authority. Both are governance failures. RCA is an offline analytical activity. The review meeting is a governance and accountability checkpoint. Conflating them means neither works."

## 3. Monthly Reporting

Monthly reporting answers three questions for the Director: Is the program working? Where are the risks? What decisions do I need to make?

### Monthly Report Template

| Metric | Current Month | Prior Month | 3-Month Trend | Target | RAG |
|--------|--------------|-------------|--------------|--------|-----|
| Open P1 problems | 3 | 4 | ↓ | <5 | 🟢 |
| Open P2 problems | 14 | 11 | ↑ | <12 | 🔴 |
| Avg age P1 problems (days) | 18 | 22 | ↓ | <30 days | 🟢 |
| Avg age P2 problems (days) | 67 | 61 | ↑ | <60 days | 🟡 |
| P1 fix delivery rate (on-time %) | 94% | 91% | ↑ | >90% | 🟢 |
| P2 fix delivery rate (on-time %) | 74% | 79% | ↓ | >80% | 🔴 |
| New problems opened | 17 | 14 | ↑ | Stable or decreasing | 🟡 |
| Problems closed | 12 | 10 | ↑ | ≥ New opened | 🟡 |
| Active workarounds (KEDB) | 28 | 31 | ↓ | Trending down | 🟢 |
| Problems with no RCA after SLA | 2 | 3 | ↓ | 0 | 🟡 |
| Proactive problems (% of new) | 24% | 21% | ↑ | >30% | 🟡 |
| Problems escalated to Director | 1 | 2 | ↓ | <2/month | 🟢 |

### RAG Threshold Definitions

| Metric | Green | Amber | Red |
|--------|-------|-------|-----|
| Avg P1 problem age | <30 days | 30–45 days | >45 days |
| Avg P2 problem age | <60 days | 60–90 days | >90 days |
| P1 fix delivery rate | >90% | 80–90% | <80% |
| P2 fix delivery rate | >80% | 70–80% | <70% |
| New vs. closed ratio | Closed ≥ New | Closed 80–100% of New | Closed <80% of New |
| Active workarounds | Declining MoM | Stable MoM | Increasing MoM |
| RCA SLA breaches | 0 | 1–2 | >2 |

### Interpreting the Report: Decisions It Should Drive

**P2 fix delivery rate at 74% (Red):** This is not an SRE execution problem — it is an engineering capacity problem or a prioritization problem. The Director's question to Engineering VP: "Why are 26% of P2 problem fix commitments not being met? Is this a resourcing issue or a commitment credibility issue?"

**New problems increasing while closed remains flat:** The backlog is growing. At some point, this reaches critical mass. The Director conversation is about whether the organization is generating technical debt faster than it is paying it down.

**Active workarounds declining:** This is a positive signal — workarounds in the KEDB represent known reliability debt. Declining count means permanent fixes are being delivered. If this number is stable or growing while incident volume is also stable, the program is managing symptoms rather than eliminating causes.

## 4. Escalation Thresholds: When a Problem Gets Director/VP Attention

Escalation is not failure — it is the governance system working. The Problem Manager escalates to Director when the problem has exceeded the thresholds that normal process can handle. The Director escalates to VP when the resolution requires executive resource allocation or organizational priority decisions.

### Escalation Trigger Table

| Trigger | Threshold | Notification Target | Expected Response Time |
|---------|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| P1 problem — no approved fix plan | 10 business days from problem open | Director of Operations | Same business day acknowledgment; fix plan committed within 2 business days |
| P2 problem — no RCA complete | 30 calendar days from problem open | Director of Operations | 5 business day response; resource assignment or accepted risk decision |
| High-cost problem (annual incident cost) | >$100K estimated annual incident cost | Director of Operations → VP if no fix plan in 5 days | Director: 2 business days. VP: 5 business days |
| Fix date slippage | >30 days from original committed fix date | Director of Operations | 3 business day response; new commitment or escalation to VP |
| Incident frequency surge | >5 incidents from same problem in any 30-day window | Director of Operations (immediate) | Same business day; emergency fix timeline or temporary workaround mandate |
| Vendor SLA breach on problem | Vendor misses committed resolution date by >5 business days | Director of Operations → vendor account exec | Director: 2 business days. Vendor exec: 3 business days |

### What Director/VP Does With an Escalation

The Director is not there to do the technical work. The Director breaks resource deadlocks — the engineering team says they cannot prioritize the fix because of competing project work, and the Director decides which work yields.

When a VP receives an escalation from a Director:
- **Resource deadlock**: VP provides organizational priority directive — this fix moves above competing project
- **Risk acceptance**: VP formally accepts the residual risk of deferring a fix (signed, documented, time-bounded)
- **Timeline commitment**: VP sets a fix delivery timeline that engineering cannot negotiate around — this is the "drop everything" signal

Every escalation that reaches VP level must have a documented outcome within 5 business days: either a fix timeline commitment, a formal risk acceptance, or an emergency fix approval. An escalation that disappears into a VP inbox without a decision is a governance failure — and the Problem Manager surfaces it back to the Director within 5 days if no response.

> **Director Callout #2:** "When an escalation reaches your desk, the decision you are making is not technical — it is organizational priority. You are deciding whether this problem is more important than the project work competing for the same engineering capacity. Make that decision explicitly and document it. Letting an escalation sit without a decision is itself a decision — and it is the wrong one."

## 5. Integration with Change Management

Every permanent fix for a problem requires a change record. This is not bureaucracy — it is the mechanism that ensures the fix itself does not cause a new incident and that the CAB has visibility into problem-driven changes.

### Problem-Driven Change Fast-Track

Problem-driven changes carry less risk in the CAB review process because the problem record provides:
- Evidence of the failure mode (RCA documentation)
- Justification for why the change is needed (incident history)
- Description of what the fix changes and why it addresses root cause

This evidence replaces the standard risk justification documentation required for routine changes. CAB should have a fast-track lane for problem-driven changes that:
1. Have a linked problem record with completed RCA
2. Have a tested fix plan (dev/staging validated)
3. Have a documented rollback procedure

**Standard process** (no fast-track): scheduled CAB review, typically 5–7 business days
**Problem-driven fast-track**: Problem Manager requests expedited review; CAB reviews within 2 business days

### Change Freeze Conflicts

When a change freeze is in effect (typically around major business events or holiday periods), P1 problem fixes require the emergency change process. The Problem Manager owns the request; the CAB Liaison facilitates. Key requirement: emergency change for a problem fix must demonstrate that deferral until freeze end creates higher risk than the change itself. The problem record's incident cost data is the evidence.

### Reverse Integration: Change-Caused Problems

When a change causes an incident and subsequently a problem record, the problem record must link back to the originating change record. This bidirectional linkage enables:
- CAB lessons learned review: did the change approval process miss something?
- Change template improvement: if the same change category repeatedly causes problems, the template is updated
- Problem Manager reporting: tracking what percentage of problems are change-induced vs. organic

In ServiceNow: enforce this linkage at the problem record creation step — the Problem Manager cannot save a new record without confirming whether a change event is associated. In Jira-based workflows, this is a required field with a "no associated change" explicit selection to force the decision.

### The Full Lifecycle Loop

```
Incident occurs
     ↓
Post-incident review identifies root cause candidate
     ↓
Problem record opened (links to incident record)
     ↓
RCA completed → Root cause identified
     ↓
Fix plan approved → Change record created (links to problem record)
     ↓
Change approved and implemented
     ↓
Problem resolved → Problem record closed with resolution evidence
     ↓
KEDB updated with workaround archive (if workaround was applied during the problem lifecycle)
     ↓
CAB lessons learned updated if problem was change-induced
```

## 6. Audit Readiness: SOC2/ISO27001 Evidence Requirements

Problem management is an auditable process. SOC2 Type II and ISO27001 auditors will look at your problem records as evidence that the organization has a systematic approach to identifying and managing recurring deficiencies.

### Relevant Controls

**SOC2 CC7.3**: The entity evaluates security events to determine whether they could or have resulted in a failure to meet its objectives. Problem management is part of this control — recurring incidents that are not addressed represent a systemic evaluation failure.

**SOC2 CC7.4**: The entity responds to identified security incidents by executing a defined incident-response program. Problem management demonstrates sustained response beyond the immediate incident.

**ISO27001 A.12.6.1**: Management of technical vulnerabilities. Known errors in the KEDB that carry customer risk must be tracked with remediation plans. A KEDB entry without a fix timeline is an audit finding.

### Evidence Package for Problem Management Audit

| Evidence | What It Demonstrates | Where It Lives | Audit Red Flag |
|----------|---------------------|---------------|----------------|
| Problem record with full status history and timestamps | Process is followed consistently | ServiceNow / Jira | Records with no status update for >60 days |
| RCA documentation with approvals | Root cause analysis is systematic and reviewed | Problem record attachment | RCA missing or unapproved |
| KEDB entries with last review date | Known errors are actively maintained | KEDB tool or ServiceNow | Entries not reviewed in >90 days |
| Fix delivery evidence (change record → problem record linkage) | Permanent fixes are actually implemented | Change record cross-reference | Problem "resolved" without a linked change record |
| Risk acceptance sign-offs | Risk is formally accepted, not ignored | Problem record field + email trail | Accepted-risk problems with no named individual sign-off |

### Common Audit Traps

**Problem records stuck in "Identified" for months**: An auditor sees a problem opened in January with no status change in four months. Their question: "Where is the accountability for this?" The answer must be a named individual with a documented reason for the delay and an approved timeline. "The team is busy" is not an acceptable audit response.

**KEDB entries without review dates**: Stale KEDB entries suggest the organization is not actively managing known errors. Each KEDB entry should have a review date — if the workaround is still valid and the permanent fix is still in progress, the KEDB entry is reviewed and timestamp updated. This takes 30 seconds per entry and is the difference between a clean audit and a finding.

**Risk acceptance without a named individual**: "Risk accepted by team" is not an audit-acceptable sign-off. Risk acceptance on a known problem must be signed by a named individual with the organizational authority to accept that risk — typically Service Owner for service-scope risk, Director for cross-service or customer-facing risk.

> **Director Callout #3:** "Auditors will look at problem records with no status change in 60+ days and ask who is accountable. The answer needs to be a named individual, not a team. 'The SRE team is aware' is not accountability — it is diffusion of responsibility. Every open problem record must have one human being's name attached to it who can explain the current status in a 2-minute conversation."

## Summary

Problem management governance is not about process compliance — it is about creating the organizational conditions where problems actually get fixed. A Problem Manager with clear authority and a Director willing to enforce escalation consequences is more effective than any tool or template. The weekly meeting keeps the program honest. The monthly report drives resource decisions. The escalation triggers ensure that problems do not sit unaddressed because everyone assumed someone else would act.

**Governance standards to hold:**
- Problem Manager: dedicated role, not rotated
- Weekly review: 50 minutes, fixed agenda, decision authority in the room
- P1 fix delivery rate: >90% — below this triggers a Director conversation with Engineering
- Escalation response: Director acknowledges within 1 business day, resolves within 5
- Audit evidence: named individuals on every open record, every risk acceptance, every status change
