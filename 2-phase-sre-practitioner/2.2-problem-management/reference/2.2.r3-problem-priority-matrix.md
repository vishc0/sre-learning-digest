# Problem Priority Matrix

> **Purpose**: Standardize how problems are scored and classified so that priority decisions are defensible, consistent across teams, and auditable. A priority decision made by intuition is a liability. A priority decision backed by a documented score is a program.

---

## SECTION 1: PRIORITY SCORING MODEL

### Composite Score Formula

```
Priority Score = Frequency Score × Impact Score × Complexity Score

Range: 1 (trivial, rare, simple) → 125 (constant, catastrophic, complex)
```

Score is calculated at problem creation and recalculated if any dimension changes materially (e.g., impact grows after additional incident correlation).

---

### Frequency Score

> Measures how often this failure pattern occurs. Higher frequency = higher urgency to fix because each occurrence costs engineering time, damages customer trust, and increases SLA exposure.

| Score | Definition | Incident Rate Example | Confidence Guidance |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Isolated — occurred once; no evidence of pattern | Single incident; no prior history in 12 months | Use only if ITSM search confirms no prior occurrences |
| 2 | Rare — 2–3 occurrences in 12 months | Quarterly-ish; low operational burden | Requires confirmed incident correlation; not just a guess |
| 3 | Occasional — monthly occurrences | 4–12 times per year; predictable pattern | Document the months it occurred |
| 4 | Frequent — weekly occurrences | 13–52 times per year; measurable operational drag | Quantify engineer-hours burned per month |
| 5 | Constant — multiple times per week or daily | >52 occurrences per year; chronic condition | At this frequency, customer trust is actively eroding |

**Scoring note**: If historical incident data is incomplete (e.g., incidents were not linked to a problem), score conservatively (higher) and note the data gap.

---

### Impact Score

> Measures the severity of the failure's effect on customers, revenue, and compliance. Higher impact = higher urgency because each occurrence carries greater cost.

| Score | Definition | Users Affected | Revenue Exposure | Compliance Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Operational impact only | Internal teams only; zero customer-facing symptoms | Negligible | None |
| 2 | Minor customer impact | <1,000 customers; degraded experience, not unavailability | Minor — measurable but not material | None |
| 3 | Moderate customer impact | 1,000–100,000 customers affected per occurrence; service degradation | Moderate — quantifiable per occurrence | Low — minor reporting obligation possible |
| 4 | Significant customer impact | 100,000–1M customers; service unavailability or data risk | Significant — SLA penalty exposure, NPS impact | Medium — regulatory notification may apply |
| 5 | Severe / enterprise-wide | >1M customers; complete service unavailability or data breach risk | Severe — major revenue loss, executive escalation | High — regulatory breach notification required |

**Impact scope guidance**: Assess impact at the time of worst occurrence, not average occurrence. If the failure has caused a Score 5 event once and Score 2 events five times, score it 5 — you cannot predict when the next occurrence will be severe.

---

### Complexity Score

> Measures how difficult the fix is to design, implement, test, and deploy. Higher complexity = higher scheduling and delivery risk.

| Score | Definition | Engineer-Hours | Dependencies | Deployment Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Trivial — config change or simple patch | <4 hours | No dependencies | Standard deployment; low change risk |
| 2 | Minor — small code change or runbook update | 4–16 hours | 1 team; no external dependency | Normal change; tested in staging |
| 3 | Moderate — multi-component change or process redesign | 2–10 engineer-days | 2–3 teams; possible vendor dependency | Requires coordination; staging test required |
| 4 | Complex — architecture change or multi-system integration | 2–8 engineer-weeks | 3+ teams; vendor dependencies; compliance review | High change risk; requires rollback plan |
| 5 | Very complex — platform-level or vendor-driven change | >8 engineer-weeks | Cross-organizational; regulatory approval may apply | Major release required; prolonged test cycle |

**Complexity caveat**: Complexity score is an input to priority, not a ceiling on priority. A P1 problem with a Complexity 5 fix is still a P1 — the fix timeline is longer, but the urgency of the workaround and close monitoring is unchanged.

## SECTION 2: PRIORITY CLASSIFICATION TABLE

### P1 — Critical

| Dimension | Value |
|---|---|
| **Score Threshold** | Score ≥ 75 OR any override condition applies |
| **Classification Criteria** | Frequent or high-impact failure with significant customer, revenue, or compliance exposure. Recurrence is unacceptable at current frequency or impact level. |
| **Fix Plan SLA** | Root cause identified within 5 business days of problem opening. Fix delivery committed within 30 calendar days unless Complexity 5 (then 60 days with Service Owner approval). |
| **Escalation Owner** | Director of Engineering / VP of Operations — briefed weekly. |
| **Meeting Cadence** | Reviewed at every weekly Problem Review. Problem Manager provides ad-hoc status updates to Service Owner if material change occurs between meetings. |
| **Workaround Requirement** | Verified workaround required within 48 hours or risk acceptance documented with Service Owner sign-off. |
| **KEDB Requirement** | Active KEDB entry required within 48 hours of workaround verification. |

**P1 examples at enterprise scale**:
- Notification platform with 25% message drop rate affecting 1M+ customers; root cause in Kafka consumer configuration
- Authentication service returning 401 errors for all new sessions; 500K login attempts failing per hour
- Data pipeline producing incorrect calculations for customer billing; financial and compliance exposure

---

### P2 — High

| Dimension | Value |
|---|---|
| **Score Threshold** | Score 40–74, OR Score ≥ 75 with a verified workaround reducing customer impact to <Score 3 level |
| **Classification Criteria** | Recurring impact on a meaningful customer segment. A workaround may exist but is not acceptable as a permanent state. Each occurrence carries measurable cost. |
| **Fix Plan SLA** | Root cause identified within 10 business days. Fix delivery committed within 60 calendar days. |
| **Escalation Owner** | SRE Manager / Engineering Manager — briefed weekly. Escalate to Director if fix delivery slips >2 weeks. |
| **Meeting Cadence** | Reviewed weekly. Full status expected every 2 weeks from Problem Owner. |
| **Workaround Requirement** | Workaround expected but not mandatory if fix delivery is <30 days. If fix >30 days, workaround required. |
| **KEDB Requirement** | KEDB entry required within 72 hours if workaround exists. |

**P2 examples**:
- Recurring authentication timeout affecting 50K customers per week; retry configuration is a viable workaround; fix requires library upgrade with 3-team coordination
- Mobile app login failures during network handoff events; affects 20K customers monthly; workaround is manual app restart guidance in support script
- Payment notification delays of 5–15 minutes during batch windows; customers receive confirmations late but receive them; processing integrity is intact

---

### P3 — Medium

| Dimension | Value |
|---|---|
| **Score Threshold** | Score 15–39 |
| **Classification Criteria** | Noticeable failure with limited scope or existing workaround that substantially neutralizes customer impact. Operational friction but not customer-facing urgency. |
| **Fix Plan SLA** | Root cause investigation within 20 business days if warranted. Fix delivery within 90 calendar days. RCA may be waived with Problem Manager approval for clear-cause problems. |
| **Escalation Owner** | Team Lead / SRE Manager — no executive briefing required unless problem ages past 90 days without progress. |
| **Meeting Cadence** | Reviewed monthly in dedicated P3/P4 slot. Problem Owner updates problem record bi-weekly. |
| **Workaround Requirement** | Workaround encouraged but not mandatory. |
| **KEDB Requirement** | KEDB entry encouraged if workaround exists; not mandatory. |

**P3 examples**:
- Internal monitoring dashboard rendering incorrectly after DST timezone changes; no customer impact; SRE team has manual query workaround; fix is timezone normalization in dashboard config
- Splunk saved search returning stale results due to index rotation misconfiguration; affects 2 internal teams; manual re-run is 2-minute workaround
- Load balancer health check producing false-positive alerts 3× per month; engineers ignore them; fix requires health check endpoint update

---

### P4 — Low

| Dimension | Value |
|---|---|
| **Score Threshold** | Score 1–14 |
| **Classification Criteria** | Cosmetic, negligible, or pre-impact condition. Caught before customer impact. Low recurrence risk. Fix is either trivial or not warranted. |
| **Fix Plan SLA** | Fix within 180 calendar days or documented as accepted risk. No RCA required. |
| **Escalation Owner** | None — Problem Owner manages autonomously. |
| **Meeting Cadence** | Reviewed quarterly. |
| **Workaround Requirement** | Not applicable in most cases. |
| **KEDB Requirement** | Not required. |

**P4 examples**:
- Log rotation misconfiguration causing slow disk growth on monitoring host; caught before impact; one-time cleanup + cron fix resolves it
- Stale session token in non-critical internal tool causing weekly re-authentication prompt for 5 internal users; cosmetic friction only
- Deprecated API endpoint responding with deprecation warning headers; no functional impact; tracked for cleanup in next quarterly release

## SECTION 3: PRIORITY OVERRIDE RULES

> Override rules exist because some failures carry risk beyond what frequency × impact × complexity captures. When an override applies, the problem is automatically assigned the override priority regardless of composite score. Document the override reason explicitly in the Priority Score table of the Problem Record.

### Override 1: Compliance / Regulatory Impact → Auto-P1

Applies when:
- The failure creates exposure to a regulatory reporting obligation (GDPR, CCPA, PCI-DSS, HIPAA, FCC, FTC)
- The failure involves incorrect data in a regulated dataset
- A regulatory audit has identified this as a control gap

Action: Problem Manager notifies Legal/Compliance within 24 hours of override classification.

---

### Override 2: Safety Impact → Auto-P1

Applies when:
- The failure affects a system where service unavailability creates physical safety risk (emergency services integration, network-based safety features, 911/E911 routing)

Action: Immediate escalation to VP level. SLA clocks start immediately.

---

### Override 3: Vendor-Caused Problems

Scoring matrix applies identically — vendor causation does not reduce urgency from the customer's perspective.

**Differences in management**:
- Fix ETA is governed by vendor SLA; document vendor ticket ID and committed SLA in the Fix Plan section
- Escalation path includes Vendor Account Manager and internal Vendor Management team
- If vendor SLA is insufficient given impact, escalate through contract management for SLA enforcement
- Problem Manager maintains the record and owns internal communication even when fix is vendor-dependent

## SECTION 4: SCORING WORKED EXAMPLES

### Example A: Kafka Consumer on Payment-Events Queue

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency | 4 | Occurs 2–3× per week during peak processing windows; 52+ annual occurrences |
| Impact | 5 | 1.2M customers affected per occurrence; 47-minute payment notification delays; revenue and NPS exposure |
| Complexity | 3 | Fix requires consumer group configuration change + load testing across payment and notification services; 2 teams; 1 week estimate |
| **Composite** | **4 × 5 × 3 = 60** | |
| **Priority** | **P1** | Score 60 ≥ threshold 40 for P2, but frequency × impact pattern warrants P1; also review for override (customer financial impact may carry regulatory exposure) |

---

### Example B: Authentication Timeout with Retry Workaround

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency | 3 | Occurs 8–10× per month; consistent pattern during authentication token refresh cycles |
| Impact | 3 | 50K customers per week experience retry; degraded experience, not unavailability; no data risk |
| Complexity | 4 | Fix requires library upgrade with 3-team coordination + compliance sign-off on new auth library |
| **Composite** | **3 × 3 × 4 = 36** | |
| **Priority** | **P2** | Score 36 falls in P2 range; workaround (retry configured at app level) reduces immediate pressure |

---

### Example C: Monitoring Dashboard Timezone Rendering

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency | 2 | Occurs 2× per year (DST transitions); predictable and bounded |
| Impact | 1 | Internal operational impact only; no customer-facing symptoms; 3 SRE engineers affected |
| Complexity | 2 | Fix is dashboard config change; 4–8 hours; 1 team |
| **Composite** | **2 × 1 × 2 = 4** | |
| **Priority** | **P4** | Score 4; trivial fix, minimal recurrence, no customer impact |

---

### Example D: Log Rotation Misconfiguration

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency | 1 | Single occurrence; caught before impact |
| Impact | 2 | Potential disk exhaustion on monitoring host; would cause monitoring gaps, not customer impact |
| Complexity | 1 | Config fix + cron job; < 2 hours |
| **Composite** | **1 × 2 × 1 = 2** | |
| **Priority** | **P4** | Score 2; fix immediately and close; no ongoing monitoring needed |
