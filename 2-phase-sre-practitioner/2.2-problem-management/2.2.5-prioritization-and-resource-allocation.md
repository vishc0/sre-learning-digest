# Chapter 05 — Prioritization and Resource Allocation

**Audience**: SRE Director / VP of Operations  
**Prerequisite**: Ch04 — Known Error Management  
**Outcome**: You can score, prioritize, fund, and defend a problem backlog at the board level.

---

## 1. Problem Scoring Model

A problem backlog without a scoring model is just a list of grievances. Scoring converts intuition into a defensible, repeatable prioritization framework that survives cross-team negotiation and executive scrutiny.

### The Three-Dimensional Model

**Priority Score = (Frequency Score × Impact Score) / Complexity Score**

- **High score** = fix it now. High recurrence, high customer impact, low engineering effort. The ROI is immediate.
- **Low score** = monitor or defer. Rare, contained, expensive to fix. Workaround may be the right long-term answer.
- **Never let Complexity alone kill a P1.** If something hits millions of customers weekly, complexity is a resourcing problem, not a deferral argument.

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Frequency Definition | Impact Definition | Complexity Definition |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| **1** | Once per year or fewer | Internal systems only; zero customer visibility | Config change or flag flip; < 1 engineer-hour |
| **2** | Quarterly (2–4 per year) | < 10,000 customers affected; degraded but functional | Single-service code fix; 1–3 days of engineering |
| **3** | Monthly (5–12 per year) | 10K–500K customers; noticeable degradation, no outage | Multi-service change; 1–2 week sprint |
| **4** | Bi-weekly (13–26 per year) | 500K–1M customers; service disruption, SLA at risk | Cross-team refactor; 2–4 weeks, coordination required |
| **5** | Weekly or more | > 1M customers; SEV1-equivalent, SLA breach, compliance risk | Architecture rework; multiple sprints, funding required |

**T-Mobile context**: At 100M+ subscribers, a score-4 impact event (500K–1M customers) is still less than 1% of the customer base — but it appears in CEO dashboards and FCC reporting. Score your problems against absolute user counts, not percentages.

### Scoring in Practice

A RabbitMQ consumer backlog failure that causes payment processing delays:
- Frequency: 3 per year → Score 2
- Impact: 800K customers affected, payments queued 15 min → Score 4  
- Complexity: Queue depth alerting + auto-scaling consumer fix → Score 2

**Priority Score = (2 × 4) / 2 = 4.0**

That score competes with a Kafka partition leader re-election issue:
- Frequency: Weekly during peak traffic → Score 5
- Impact: 200K customers, 2-min latency spike → Score 3  
- Complexity: Broker config tuning + client retry logic → Score 2

**Priority Score = (5 × 3) / 2 = 7.5** — Kafka issue wins the next sprint.

## 2. The Problem Priority Matrix (P1–P4)

The scoring model gives you a number. The priority matrix gives that number organizational meaning — who owns it, when a fix plan is required, and when it escalates.

### Priority Thresholds

| Priority | Score Range | Fix Plan SLA | Review Cadence | Escalation Owner |
|----------|-------------|--------------|----------------|------------------|
| **P1** | ≥ 8.0 | Within 10 business days | Weekly until closed | VP of Engineering / CTO |
| **P2** | 5.0–7.9 | Within 30 days | Bi-weekly | Director of SRE / Platform |
| **P3** | 2.5–4.9 | Within 90 days | Monthly | SRE Manager |
| **P4** | < 2.5 | Quarterly review | Quarterly | Team Lead |

### Priority Definitions and T-Mobile Examples

**P1 — Fix plan required within 10 business days**

Customer-visible and actively breaching or approaching SLA breach. Compliance or safety exposure. No acceptable workaround — or workaround itself requires manual intervention at scale.

*Example*: DNS resolution failures in the EKS service mesh are causing 0.3% of authentication requests to drop. At 50M daily auth events, that is 150,000 failed logins per day. The workaround is a retry loop in the client — but retries are increasing cluster load by 8%, compressing error budget. Fix plan: CoreDNS cache tuning + Kubernetes node-local DNS, targeted for completion in Sprint N+1.

**P2 — Fix plan within 30 days**

Recurring customer-visible impact at rate of more than 3 incidents per quarter. A workaround exists but must be manually triggered each time. Engineering and on-call teams know the exact steps — which means the organization has institutionalized the failure.

*Example*: A known Splunk Heavy Forwarder pipeline saturation event occurs every 3–4 weeks during month-end billing cycles. On-call SRE manually increases TCP receive queue depth and restarts the pipeline. The workaround takes 45 minutes. Four engineers are paged each time. The root cause is a misconfigured props.conf transform that fails to trim high-cardinality billing fields before indexing. Fix: update the transform and add a pipeline pressure alert with auto-remediation. One sprint of work.

**P3 — Fix plan within 90 days**

Recurring operational burden — the on-call team is absorbing cost, but customers are not yet visibly affected. Risk profile: if the workaround fails, customers are next.

*Example*: A TIBCO EMS topic subscriber occasionally falls behind during traffic surges, accumulating a message backlog. The backlog resolves itself within 30 minutes. No customer-facing SLA impact yet. But on-call SRE gets paged, wastes 20 minutes validating, and closes the alert. Happens 6 times per quarter. Fix: subscriber thread pool tuning and a proper backlog depth alert with Pagerduty suppression for sub-threshold events.

**P4 — Quarterly review**

Known issue, stable workaround, low recurrence, no customer risk in the near term. Document it, revisit quarterly, and do not let it rot silently.

*Example*: A legacy TIBCO BusinessWorks adapter occasionally drops a connection to a decommissioned legacy CRM endpoint. It self-heals in under 60 seconds. The CRM system is scheduled for retirement in Q3. Fix plan: decommission alongside the source system. Review quarterly to confirm retirement timeline has not slipped.

## 3. Resource Allocation: Engineering Time to Fix vs. Maintain Workaround

### The False Economy of Workarounds

A workaround is not free. Every workaround has a recurring operational cost: engineer hours paged, time to diagnose, time to execute the workaround, recovery validation, post-incident documentation. That cost compounds every time the problem recurs.

Organizations systematically undercount workaround cost because it is distributed across on-call rotations, incident reports, and sprint velocity degradation. It never appears as a line item. Your job as Director is to make it visible.

### Making the Business Case

The frame that lands with engineering leadership:

> "This problem costs us 12 engineer-hours per month in incident response. The permanent fix is estimated at 80 engineer-hours. ROI break-even is 6.7 months. After break-even we reclaim 12 hours per month indefinitely — which is 144 engineer-hours per year returned to feature delivery."

Break-even calculation: **Fix Cost / Monthly Ops Cost = Months to Break-Even**  
80 hours / 12 hours per month = **6.7 months**

After 12 months: net savings = (12 hours × 12 months) − 80 hours = **64 engineer-hours ahead**.

That is not SRE math. That is CFO math. Present it that way.

### Competing Priorities: Problem Fix vs. Feature Development

The PM/Product Director will always argue for features. Features have revenue attribution. Problem fixes have cost avoidance attribution — which is harder to defend in a roadmap negotiation.

The error budget is your weapon:

> "This problem is consuming 20% of our error budget every month. At current consumption rate, we will exhaust our error budget in 5 months. When error budget is gone, the SRE team will invoke the reliability policy and freeze non-critical feature deployments. You can either fund the fix now, or I will be back in 5 months recommending a feature freeze."

That conversation reframes the problem fix from "SRE tax" to "cost of continued deployment velocity." Every PM understands what a feature freeze costs.

### The Resource Conversation Framework

1. **State the recurrence rate**: "This has triggered 24 times in the last 12 months."
2. **Quantify the ops cost**: "Each incident consumes 4 engineer-hours and pages 3 people."
3. **Calculate annual ops cost**: "24 × 4 = 96 engineer-hours/year at $150/hr fully burdened = $14,400/year in direct ops cost."
4. **State the fix cost**: "Engineering estimates 120 hours across 2 engineers and one sprint."
5. **Calculate break-even**: "120 hours / 8 hours per month = 15 months to break-even."
6. **State the ask**: "Fund 2 engineers for one sprint in Q3. Problem is permanently closed by Q4."

## 4. Cost of Workaround Model

### The Formula

**Annual Cost of Workaround = (Incidents/Year × Mean Response Time × Engineer Hourly Rate × Engineers Per Incident) + (Customer Impact Cost Per Incident × Incidents/Year)**

### Cost Model Table

| Variable | Description | Example Value |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| `Incidents/Year` | How many times this problem triggered in the trailing 12 months | 24 |
| `Mean Response Time (hrs)` | Average time from page to resolution, including bridge, diagnosis, fix, validation | 2.0 hours |
| `Engineers Per Incident` | Average headcount paged and actively engaged | 4 |
| `Engineer Hourly Rate (fully-burdened)` | Salary + benefits + overhead. T-Mobile SRE range: $140–$160/hr | $150/hr |
| `Direct Ops Cost` | Incidents × Response Time × Engineers × Rate | 24 × 2 × 4 × $150 = **$28,800/year** |
| `Customer Impact Cost Per Incident` | SLA credit exposure + customer care escalations + brand cost. Estimate conservatively. | $500/incident |
| `Annual Customer Impact Cost` | Customer impact per incident × incidents | 24 × $500 = **$12,000/year** |
| **Total Annual Cost of Workaround** | Direct ops cost + customer impact cost | **$40,800/year** |
| `Fix Cost (one-time)` | Engineering hours × rate + any infrastructure cost | 80 hrs × $150 = **$12,000** |
| `Break-Even` | Fix cost / monthly ops cost | $1,000/month → **12 months** |

### The Hidden Costs That Don't Appear in That Table

- **On-call fatigue**: Engineers paged 24 times per year for a known problem start treating pages as noise. Alert fatigue is how real incidents get missed. This is a reliability culture cost, not just an ops cost.
- **Alert noise saturation**: Every known error that pages generates false urgency. At T-Mobile scale, processing 3,000+ CI changes per month means the signal-to-noise ratio in your observability platform directly controls MTTD. Known errors burning alert capacity is not a minor inconvenience — it is a detection risk.
- **Sprint velocity drag**: On-call engineers who spent their weekend on a known workaround are not operating at full capacity Monday morning. Untracked, this explains 10–15% velocity degradation on teams with chronic known-error load.

### Board-Level Language

> "This known error is costing the organization $40,800 per year in direct operations expense and customer SLA exposure. The engineering investment to permanently resolve it is $12,000 — a one-time spend. We are currently spending $3.40 for every dollar it would cost to fix it, every year, indefinitely. I am recommending we fund the fix in Q3 and close this item from the problem backlog."

That is a sentence a CFO can act on.

## 5. Board-Level Reporting: Converting Problem Backlog to Business Risk Language

### Three Metrics That Matter to VP/Director Audiences

**Metric 1 — Problem Backlog Size by Priority**  
How many P1 and P2 problems are currently open, and how long have they been open? Age matters. A P1 open for 45 days with no approved fix plan is a governance failure, not an engineering problem.

**Metric 2 — Fix Delivery Rate**  
Of problems with committed fix dates, what percentage were delivered on time? Below 70% on-time delivery means your fix commitments are not credible. That destroys the funding case for future fix investment.

**Metric 3 — Recurrence Cost**  
Annual operations cost of all unfixed known errors combined. This is the reliability debt payment the organization makes every year, invisibly, in engineering hours and customer impact. Make it visible.

### RAG Thresholds

| Metric | Green | Amber | Red |
|--------|-------|-------|-----|
| P1 Open Problems | 0 open > 10 days | 1–2 open > 10 days | ≥ 3 open > 10 days, or any open > 30 days |
| P2 Open Problems | All ≤ 30 days | 1–3 open > 30 days | > 3 open > 30 days |
| Fix Delivery Rate (trailing quarter) | ≥ 85% on time | 70–84% on time | < 70% on time |
| Recurrence Cost (annual) | < $100K | $100K–$500K | > $500K |

### Sample Executive Dashboard

| Metric | Current | RAG | Trend | Owner | Action Required |
|--------|---------|-----|-------|-------|-----------------|
| P1 Open Problems | 1 (age: 8 days) | Amber | Stable | VP Engineering | Fix plan approved, sprint N+1 |
| P2 Open Problems | 4 (2 > 30 days) | Red | Worsening | Director SRE | Escalate 2 to P1 review |
| Fix Delivery Rate | 72% (Q2) | Amber | Down from 81% | SRE Manager | Root cause sprint planning gaps |
| Recurrence Cost (annual) | $312K | Amber | Up 18% YoY | Director SRE | 3 P2 fixes would reduce by $180K |
| Error Budget Consumed (MTD) | 61% of monthly budget | Red | Accelerating | Platform SRE Lead | Feature freeze threshold at 80% |

> **What the VP asks in the review meeting:**
>
> "What is our current reliability debt? How much is it costing the business? When does it get paid down?"
>
> Those three questions must have specific, numerical answers ready before you walk into the room. "We're working on it" is not an answer. "$312K per year in direct ops cost, declining to $132K by Q4 if we fund the three approved fixes, paid down fully within 18 months" — that is an answer.

### What This Dashboard Signals to Leadership

A VP reading this dashboard is not reading individual problem records. They are reading risk posture. Two P2 problems aged over 30 days without a fix plan tells them: the organization cannot fund reliability work against feature pressure. The fix delivery rate at 72% tells them: commitments made are not being kept. The error budget at 61% tells them: we have 39% runway left this month and are accelerating consumption.

If you cannot articulate that narrative in 90 seconds, you are not ready to present to a VP audience.

## 6. Trade-off: Fix the Symptom vs. Invest in the Systemic Fix

### Two Different Decisions

**Symptom fix**: Restore functionality without addressing root cause. Fast, cheap, creates or extends a known error. Appropriate when the system needs to be back online and you do not have the context or resources to fix properly yet.

**Systemic fix**: Eliminate the root cause. Expensive, time-consuming. Permanently closes the problem record and eliminates recurring ops cost. Requires funded engineering capacity and cross-team coordination at T-Mobile scale.

The mistake organizations make is treating symptom fixes as a valid permanent strategy. They are not. A symptom fix is a bridge — it has an expiration date that must be set at the moment of application.

### When to Choose the Symptom Fix

- Fix cost exceeds annual workaround cost multiplied by 3 years (the system may evolve faster than the fix pays off)
- Problem frequency is clearly declining (traffic pattern change, partial mitigation already working)
- The system or component is on a confirmed deprecation timeline within 18 months
- The root cause requires architectural change that is already on the roadmap for another reason — wait for natural alignment

### When to Invest in the Systemic Fix

- Same failure mode has recurred more than 3 times in a single quarter
- Customer impact is growing with each recurrence (blast radius expanding)
- The workaround requires increasing engineering sophistication to execute (complexity drift)
- Team reliability goals — error budget targets, SLO commitments — are at measurable risk if this continues
- The problem is consuming on-call bandwidth that is suppressing detection of other incidents

### The Kafka Example at T-Mobile Scale

A Kafka partition leader re-election storm occurs during broker rolling restarts. The workaround: pre-stage consumers to tolerate partition unavailability by increasing `max.poll.interval.ms` and reducing `session.timeout.ms` before each restart window. It works. It has worked 18 times. It requires a platform SRE to manually execute a pre-restart checklist every deployment cycle.

Symptom fix analysis: 18 executions × 1.5 hours × $150/hr = $4,050/year in direct ops cost. Not alarming.

But: the underlying cause is that the broker cluster is undersized for the rebalance load during peak traffic. As CI deployment frequency increases from 2,000 changes/month to 3,500 changes/month — a realistic T-Mobile trajectory — broker restart frequency doubles. The $4,050 ops cost doubles to $8,100, then to $16,200 as the platform scales. The workaround complexity grows because the consumer groups multiply as more services onboard Kafka.

The systemic fix: broker cluster right-sizing + automated partition reassignment tooling. One-time cost: 160 engineer-hours. At $150/hr: $24,000. Break-even against current ops cost: 5.9 years. Sounds like a symptom-fix argument.

But the break-even against projected ops cost at full scale (3 years out): 14 months. And the systemic fix eliminates a human step from every deployment cycle, which reduces MTTR and lowers compliance audit exposure for change management.

> **Director decision point:**
>
> "The organization's cultural problem with 'fix the symptom' is that it feels efficient in the moment and catastrophic over time. Every workaround institutionalized is a capability the team must maintain, document, train, and execute under pressure. At T-Mobile scale — thousands of CI changes per month, hundreds of services, a rotating on-call pool — the cognitive load of maintaining 30 known workarounds is itself a reliability risk.
>
> Your job as Director is to hold the long view. Set a policy: any workaround in place for more than two consecutive quarters without a funded fix plan escalates automatically to your desk for a resource decision. Do not let the backlog age silently. Aging known errors are not a sign that things are stable — they are a sign that the organization has learned to live with failure."

### Systemic Fix Decision Checklist

Before approving a systemic fix investment, confirm:

- [ ] Root cause is confirmed and documented (not suspected)
- [ ] Fix approach has been reviewed by platform architecture
- [ ] Cost estimate includes integration testing and rollout risk
- [ ] Break-even analysis uses projected scale, not current scale
- [ ] Engineering capacity has been identified and committed
- [ ] Stakeholder notification plan exists for any customer-visible change window
- [ ] Success criteria are defined: what proves the problem is permanently closed?
