## 1. KEDB Purpose

The Known Error Database is the organization's institutional memory of confirmed failures. Not suspected failures. Not incidents under investigation. Confirmed failures — where root cause is known and a workaround exists, even when the permanent fix has not yet been deployed.

**Without KEDB**: every recurrence of a known failure starts from zero. An engineer gets paged at 2am, pulls up a blank terminal, and begins re-diagnosing a failure the team has seen — and solved — four times in the past eighteen months. That engineer does not know it is a known error. The RCA lives in a Confluence page no one links to during active incidents. The fix is buried in a postmortem three scrolls down. They find it in 90 minutes, or they call in three more people to find it faster.

**With KEDB**: the incident responder opens the incident management system, searches by CI name or alert name, and surfaces KEDB entry KE-0117 in under three minutes. The workaround is numbered, actionable, and tested. Customer impact closes in under 15 minutes.

The KEDB is to incident response what a Splunk saved search is to alert triage — the documented pattern that shortcuts investigation. When your on-call engineer fires up Splunk during a SEV2, they are not typing a raw SPL query from memory. They are running a saved search built by someone who already solved that problem. KEDB is the same concept applied to failure patterns: codified institutional knowledge that eliminates rediscovery under pressure.

**Critical distinction**: KEDB is not a postmortem database. Postmortems are retrospective artifacts — written after the fact, read occasionally, rarely consulted at 2am. KEDB is a live operational tool. It is designed to be searched, referenced, and applied during an active incident. The moment a KEDB entry becomes hard to find or too long to scan in three minutes, it has failed its purpose.

> **Director/VP Lens — KEDB as Incident Cost Reduction**
>
> Consider a T-Mobile-scale notification platform processing 25 million messages per day. A known RabbitMQ queue saturation pattern recurs on Saturday night — peak traffic, skeleton crew on-call. Without KEDB: 90-minute rediscovery, two escalations, full SEV1 declaration. Customer-visible impact window: 90+ minutes. With KEDB and a tested workaround: 15-minute resolution, SEV2 contained without escalation.
>
> The math is not abstract. At T-Mobile scale, a 75-minute customer impact reduction on a notification platform translates to millions of failed message deliveries avoided, potential regulatory notification thresholds not crossed, and one less executive bridge call on a weekend. The cost to build and maintain a KEDB entry: roughly two engineer-hours. The cost of a single recurring SEV1 rediscovery: four to eight engineer-hours plus reputational cost. KEDB investment pays back on the first recurrence.

## 2. Structure of a KEDB Entry

A KEDB entry must be scannable in under 90 seconds and actionable in under 5 minutes. Every field has a purpose. None are optional once a problem record reaches "Known Error" status.

| Field | Purpose | Example Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Known Error ID** | Unique identifier referenced in incident tickets and runbooks | `KE-0042` |
| **Problem Record ID** | Links this entry to its parent problem investigation | `PRB-2024-0119` |
| **Title** | Symptom description written as the responder would recognize it at 2am — not root cause jargon | `API Gateway latency spike >2s — downstream Kafka consumer lag on orders topic` |
| **Symptoms** | Observable indicators: alert names, log patterns, metric thresholds. Copy-pasteable into Splunk or Datadog. | Alert: `kafka_consumer_lag_critical`; Log: `ConsumerFetchManagerMetrics lag > 500K messages`; Metric: `p99 latency > 2000ms on /api/v2/orders` |
| **Root Cause Summary** | Confirmed root cause from completed RCA — not suspected. One paragraph maximum. | `Kafka consumer thread pool exhaustion on orders-consumer-v2 when partition count exceeds 48. Thread pool fixed at 32 threads; added partitions during June capacity event not matched by thread pool config.` |
| **Workaround Steps** | Numbered, actionable, testable. Each step includes expected output so responder knows it worked. | `1. kubectl scale deployment orders-consumer-v2 --replicas=6 (expected: lag drops below 50K within 4 min) 2. Verify: kubectl get hpa orders-consumer-v2 — READY column matches 6` |
| **Workaround Risk** | What can go wrong when applying the workaround | `Scaling to 6 replicas increases Kafka broker load by ~18%. Monitor broker CPU. If broker CPU >80%, halt scale-out and escalate to Platform Engineering.` |
| **Permanent Fix Status** | Current fix state: `In Progress` / `Not Started` / `Scheduled` / `Deployed — Pending Verification` | `In Progress — JIRA PLAT-4421` |
| **Fix ETA** | Committed delivery date, not aspirational | `2024-09-15 (Sprint 24.09 commit)` |
| **Review Date** | Staleness governance trigger — entry must be reviewed by this date | `2024-10-01` |
| **Owner** | Individual accountable for technical accuracy of this entry. Not a team. | `Priya Nair (Staff SRE, Platform Engineering)` |
| **Last Validated** | Date the entry was last tested against real system behavior | `2024-07-22` |
| **Related Incident Count** | How many incidents have referenced this KEDB entry since creation | `7 incidents — last: INC-2024-3841` |

The "Related Incident Count" field is often omitted in immature KEDB implementations and is one of the most valuable. It is your signal for priority: a KEDB entry referenced in 12 incidents in six months is a permanent fix that belongs at the top of your engineering backlog, not at the bottom.

## 3. How KEDB Accelerates Future Incident Response

The value of KEDB is measurable. These are not qualitative benefits — they are KPIs that belong in your SRE quarterly review.

**MTTD Reduction — Eliminating the Investigation Phase**

Mean Time to Detect is typically measured as the gap between failure onset and alert firing. But in practice, there is a second MTTD: the time from alert to confirmed understanding of what is wrong. KEDB collapses this second phase. When the incident responder finds a matching KEDB entry within the first five minutes, the investigation phase is complete before it starts. Symptom pattern matching in KEDB replaces 30–90 minutes of log diving, hypothesis testing, and SME consultation.

**MTTR Reduction — Eliminating the "What Do We Try First" Loop**

The single most expensive phase of a well-instrumented incident is not detection — it is the workaround decision loop. Engineers know something is wrong. They have a hypothesis. They debate whether to try fix A or fix B. They try A, observe for 5 minutes, conclude it did not work, move to B. KEDB eliminates this loop entirely for known failures. The workaround steps are not opinions. They are tested procedures derived from confirmed root cause. The responder executes, observes the expected output at each step, and confirms resolution.

**Integration Point: On-Call Runbooks**

Every runbook section that covers a "known failure mode" must reference the KEDB entry, not repeat it. The runbook says: "If you observe Kafka consumer lag >500K messages on the orders topic, refer to KE-0042." The runbook does not duplicate the workaround steps. Duplication creates drift — the KEDB entry gets updated, the runbook does not, and the responder follows stale guidance. Link, do not copy.

**Integration Point: Incident Management System**

ServiceNow, PagerDuty, and Jira Service Management all support surfacing KEDB matches during incident creation. When an incident is opened against the `orders-consumer-v2` CI, the system should automatically query the KEDB for entries associated with that CI and surface matches in the incident interface. This is not a nice-to-have — it is the primary mechanism that gets KEDB into the responder's workflow without requiring them to remember to search.

**Concrete Example — Kubernetes OOMKilled Pattern**

A Kubernetes pod running the order-processing service is repeatedly OOMKilled across three deployments in six weeks. The first occurrence: 75-minute investigation, involves three engineers, two escalations. Root cause confirmed: JVM heap not set explicitly — container inherits node default, which is insufficient for peak order volume. Problem record created, KEDB entry KE-0055 created.

Second occurrence, six weeks later, different on-call engineer: opens incident against `order-processing` CI, KEDB match surfaces automatically. KE-0055: "OOMKilled on order-processing — JVM heap unset." Workaround step 1: `kubectl set env deployment/order-processing JAVA_OPTS="-Xmx2g -Xms1g"`. Expected output: pods restart, memory stabilizes below 80% of container limit within 3 minutes. Resolved in 12 minutes. No escalation. No SME consultation. No re-investigation.

**KEDB Match Rate as a KPI**

Track this metric monthly: the percentage of incidents where a KEDB entry was found and applied. Typical progression in a maturing organization:

- Month 1–3 post-KEDB launch: 15–25% match rate (KEDB is sparse)
- Month 4–9: 40–60% match rate (entries accumulate from each RCA)
- Month 12+: 65–80% match rate for recurring infrastructure failure categories

A match rate below 30% after 6 months signals one of three problems: KEDB entries are not being created from RCAs, entries exist but are not searchable, or the integration with the incident management system is broken. Each is a solvable operational problem, but you have to measure it to find it.

## 4. KEDB Governance

A KEDB that is not governed becomes shelfware within 12 months. Entries accumulate, stop being reviewed, diverge from system reality, and responders learn through experience that the KEDB cannot be trusted. Once trust is lost, rebuilding it requires the same effort as building it from scratch — plus you are fighting organizational skepticism.

**Staleness — The 90-Day Rule**

Any KEDB entry not reviewed in 90 days must be flagged. This does not mean it is wrong — it means no one has validated that it remains accurate. Systems change. Deployments happen. A workaround that worked in Q1 may not work in Q3 because the underlying component was upgraded. The 90-day flag is a forcing function for review, not a judgment on quality.

**Archival Criteria**

An entry is archived — not deleted — when all three conditions are met:
1. The permanent fix has been deployed
2. The fix has been verified in production (not just in staging)
3. Zero recurrences have been observed for 60 consecutive days post-fix

Archived entries remain searchable. If a regression reintroduces a known failure pattern six months after archival, the entry is restored and updated — it is not recreated from scratch. Institutional memory is preserved even in the archive.

**Governance Cadence**

| Entry Age | Review Action | Owner | Escalation Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0–30 days | Initial validation — confirm workaround matches current system state | Problem Owner (technical) | None — standard creation window |
| 31–90 days | Confirm no system changes that affect workaround validity | Problem Owner | Flag in monthly Problem Review if not completed |
| 91–180 days | Full re-validation — run workaround against current system state (or staging equivalent) | Problem Owner + Problem Manager | Escalate to SRE Manager if owner unresponsive for 14 days |
| 180+ days active | Review for archival eligibility OR re-commit permanent fix timeline | Problem Manager | Escalate to Director if permanent fix timeline has slipped >90 days with no re-commit |
| Post-permanent fix | Initiate 60-day recurrence watch period | Problem Owner | Reopen problem record if recurrence detected during watch period |

**Who Owns KEDB Governance**

Two roles, two scopes:

- **Problem Manager** (process ownership): Ensures the KEDB as a whole is healthy — review cadence is met, match rate is tracked, archival process is followed. Reports KEDB health metrics in quarterly SRE reviews.
- **Problem Owner** (entry ownership): Ensures the specific entry they own remains technically accurate. They are the SME for that failure domain. If the system changes in a way that invalidates the workaround, they update the entry before the next incident.

**Governance Failure Mode**

The most common KEDB failure pattern: entries are created diligently for the first three months. No one assigns owners. No one schedules reviews. Twelve months later, the KEDB has 200 entries, 40% of which reference components that have been refactored or replaced. Responders try two KEDB entries, find them both stale, and stop using it. The KEDB continues to exist as an artifact. It ceases to function as a tool.

> **Director/VP Lens — KEDB Governance as an SRE Culture Signal**
>
> The discipline with which an SRE team maintains its KEDB is a direct proxy for how that team treats institutional knowledge under operational pressure. Teams that let the KEDB go stale are the same teams that have 200 open problem records, runbooks no one trusts, and postmortems that recycle the same action items quarterly. A Director walk-through of KEDB health metrics — entry count by age, match rate trend, average time to permanent fix — reveals more about SRE program maturity than any incident count dashboard.
>
> In a Director/VP interview, framing KEDB governance as "operational debt management" lands at the right altitude. Every unarchived, unreviewed KEDB entry is operational debt. It was cheap to create. It costs nothing to ignore today. It creates compounding cost at 2am when a responder cannot distinguish trustworthy entries from stale ones.

## 5. Integration with On-Call Runbooks and Incident Management

**Runbook → KEDB Linkage Pattern**

Runbooks operate at the level of a CI or service. A runbook for the `orders-api` service covers standard operating procedures, common alert responses, escalation paths, and known failure modes. The "known failure modes" section is where KEDB integration lives.

Correct pattern:
```
### Known Failure: Kafka Consumer Lag Spike on orders-topic
Alert: kafka_consumer_lag_critical
See: KE-0042 for confirmed root cause and tested workaround.
```

Incorrect pattern (do not do this):
```
### Known Failure: Kafka Consumer Lag Spike on orders-topic
Steps: 1. Scale consumer replicas... 2. Check broker CPU... [full workaround duplicated here]
```

The incorrect pattern creates two sources of truth. When the KEDB entry is updated — because the system changed, the workaround evolved, or the risk profile shifted — the runbook is not updated. The responder finds conflicting guidance. In a high-pressure incident, conflicting guidance between a runbook and a KEDB entry is worse than having no KEDB entry at all.

**Incident Management System Integration**

Configure your incident management system to query KEDB on incident creation based on:
- **CI match**: incident opened against `orders-consumer-v2` surfaces all KEDB entries for that CI
- **Alert name match**: `kafka_consumer_lag_critical` maps directly to KE-0042
- **Error message pattern**: Splunk alert payload containing `ConsumerFetchManagerMetrics lag > 500K` triggers KEDB lookup

ServiceNow supports this via the Problem module and KEDB integration out of the box. PagerDuty supports it via incident note injection at alert trigger. Jira Service Management requires a custom automation rule but is achievable in under a day of configuration. The implementation complexity is low. The resistance is organizational — teams that do not trust the KEDB will disable or ignore automated surfacing. Fix the KEDB quality first; then push the automation.

**Search Design — KEDB Must Be Found Under Pressure**

A KEDB that requires knowing the entry ID to find is not a KEDB — it is a document archive. Search must work across:
- CI name (exact and fuzzy)
- Alert name
- Error message keyword (the text the engineer is literally looking at on their screen)
- Symptom keyword
- Service name or component name

If your KEDB lives in Confluence, verify that Confluence search returns the right entry when someone types the exact error message they are seeing. Test this quarterly. If the entry does not surface in the top 3 results, the search configuration is wrong, not the content.

**First 5 Minutes of Investigation — IC Protocol**

The Incident Commander's standard protocol in the first five minutes of a declared incident should include an explicit KEDB search step. Not optional, not conditional — standard. If your IC runbook does not include "search KEDB by CI name and alert name" as step 2 or 3, you are relying on individual engineers to remember to check. Under pressure, they will not.

## 6. KEDB vs Runbook Distinction

The most common confusion in organizations new to formal problem management: teams either conflate runbooks and KEDB, or they build one and ignore the other. Both are necessary. They solve different problems.

**Rule of thumb**: A runbook is written before the failure occurs. A KEDB entry is written after the RCA is complete. Runbooks cover anticipated operational scenarios. KEDB entries document confirmed failures that have already happened and will happen again.

| Dimension | Runbook | KEDB Entry |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Operational guide for the service — normal operations, common alerts, escalation paths, known response procedures | Documented record of a confirmed failure — root cause known, workaround tested, permanent fix tracked |
| **When Written** | Before failure — at service launch or when new failure modes are anticipated | After failure — created only when problem root cause is confirmed via RCA |
| **Root Cause** | May not be known — covers "if you see X, try Y" without necessarily explaining why Y works | Root cause is required — entry cannot be created without confirmed RCA output |
| **Scope** | Covers entire service or CI — broad operational coverage | Covers one specific failure pattern — narrow, deep, precise |
| **Update Trigger** | System architecture changes, new alert thresholds, service evolution | Workaround becomes invalid, system changes affect applicability, permanent fix deployed |
| **Primary Consumer** | On-call engineer during any incident involving the service | On-call engineer during an incident that matches the specific known failure pattern |

The overlap is intentional and valuable: the runbook for a service references the KEDB entries for that service's known failures. The KEDB entry contains the deep detail. The runbook contains the pointer. Neither duplicates the other.

> **Director/VP Lens — The Runbook/KEDB Maturity Ladder**
>
> An organization that has runbooks but no KEDB is operating on institutional knowledge that lives in individual engineers' heads, transcribed into runbooks based on experience. That knowledge is undifferentiated — the runbook cannot tell you whether a procedure is based on confirmed root cause analysis or on "this worked once, let's add it." KEDB forces the distinction. Every KEDB entry is backed by a confirmed RCA. It is not folklore — it is evidence-based operational knowledge.
>
> In a T-Mobile-scale SRE organization processing thousands of CI changes per month, the ratio of known failures to novel failures increases over time. A mature platform has been through enough failure modes that the majority of 2am incidents are recurrences of known patterns. At that scale, KEDB match rate is not a nice-to-have metric — it is the primary lever on MTTR. An organization targeting sub-15-minute MTTR for recurrent failures cannot get there on runbooks alone.

## Summary: KEDB Operational Health Checklist

This is not a launch checklist — it is an ongoing operational health assessment. Run it quarterly.

| Health Indicator | Healthy Signal | Unhealthy Signal |
|---|---|---|
| KEDB match rate | >60% for recurring infrastructure failure categories | <30% after 6 months of operation |
| Entry staleness | <5% of entries flagged past 90-day review threshold | >20% of entries overdue for review |
| Owner assignment | 100% of active entries have a named individual owner | Team-owned or unowned entries exist |
| Permanent fix tracking | Fix ETA present and current for all In Progress entries | Fix ETAs older than 90 days without re-commit |
| Archival rate | Entries are archived within 90 days of verified permanent fix | KEDB grows monotonically with no archival activity |
| Runbook linkage | All active KEDB entries referenced from corresponding runbooks | KEDB entries exist in isolation, not linked from runbooks |
| IC protocol | KEDB search is a documented step in incident commander protocol | KEDB search is ad hoc, dependent on individual engineer memory |
