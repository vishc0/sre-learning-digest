# Chapter 06: Trending and Pattern Recognition in Problem Management

> **Director/VP Lens:** Pattern recognition is the difference between a problem management program that generates paperwork and one that actually bends the reliability curve. Your job is to build the analytical discipline that sees the incident before it happens — not the one that explains it afterward.

---

## 1. Leading Indicators of Emerging Problems

Most organizations declare problems reactively — after the third or fourth incident on the same CI, someone finally opens a problem record. By then, customers have already felt three incidents. Leading indicator analysis moves the detection point upstream.

At T-Mobile scale — thousands of configuration items, hundreds of services, tens of thousands of CI change events per month — you cannot manually review every CI's incident history. You need automated signal detection that flags CIs for problem review before the next incident.

### Signal Detection Framework

| Signal | Threshold | Detection Method | Action |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|--------|
| CI recurring incidents | 3+ incidents involving same CI within 30 days, no problem record | Splunk CI correlation query | Auto-flag for Problem Manager review within 24 hours |
| Aggregate team service error rate | Team's combined service error rate increases >15% week-over-week | Grafana team dashboard | Problem Manager notified; team lead investigation within 48 hours |
| Alert frequency acceleration | Alert volume for a service increases >20% week-over-week without corresponding incident declaration | Splunk alert volume trend | Problem candidate review next weekly meeting |
| Change correlation | >40% of incidents in a service correlate with a change event within 24 hours | Change-incident correlation report | Open problem on change process for that service |
| Dependency graph degradation | Upstream service p99 latency increases >50% — downstream services not yet showing symptoms | Service mesh metrics (Istio/Linkerd) | Proactive problem declaration if upstream has no fix ETA |
| SLO burn rate acceleration | 7-day burn rate > 2x expected rate | SLO dashboard (Grafana/Nobl9) | Evaluate for proactive problem declaration |

### Splunk SPL: Automated CI Problem Flagging

This query runs as a scheduled search (daily) and identifies CIs that have accumulated enough incident history to warrant problem review but do not yet have an open problem record:

```spl
index=incidents earliest=-30d
| stats count as incident_count by affected_ci
| where incident_count > 3
| join type=left affected_ci [
    search index=problems status!="resolved" status!="closed"
    | stats count as open_problems by affected_ci
]
| where isnull(open_problems) OR open_problems = 0
| sort -incident_count
| table affected_ci, incident_count, open_problems
| rename affected_ci as "CI Requiring Problem Review", incident_count as "Incidents (30d)", open_problems as "Open Problem Records"
```

This produces a daily list of CIs that have recurring incidents with no active problem record. The Problem Manager reviews this list every morning. If a CI appears on this list for two consecutive days and the incident count is still climbing, a problem record opens the same day — no meeting required.

The threshold of 3 is not arbitrary. At T-Mobile scale, single incidents can be transient; two incidents can be coincidence; three incidents on the same CI within 30 days is a pattern. Adjust the threshold based on the criticality of the CI — for P1-impact CIs, the threshold should be 2.

### Change Correlation at Scale

The 40% correlation threshold for change-driven incidents is a yellow flag, not a root cause. It means the change process for that service warrants investigation as a problem. The question to answer: is the service change-sensitive due to deployment quality issues, insufficient testing coverage, or architectural fragility that means any change carries high failure risk?

Splunk query for change-incident correlation per service:

```spl
index=changes earliest=-30d
| eval change_window_end=relative_time(change_start_time, "+24h")
| join service [
    search index=incidents earliest=-30d
    | where incident_time >= change_start_time AND incident_time <= change_window_end
    | stats count as post_change_incidents by service
]
| stats sum(post_change_incidents) as post_change_count, count as total_changes by service
| eval correlation_pct=round((post_change_count / total_changes) * 100, 1)
| where correlation_pct > 40
| sort -correlation_pct
| table service, total_changes, post_change_count, correlation_pct
```

Any service with >40% correlation appearing on this report is a problem candidate. The problem record is about the change process, not the service itself — this distinction matters for assignment.

## 2. Proactive Problem Identification

Proactive problems are declared without an active incident to point to. This is the hardest discipline to establish because it requires analytical judgment rather than a trigger event.

### SLO Burn Rate as a Problem Signal

SLO burn rate measures how quickly you are consuming your error budget. A 30-day burn rate greater than 3x expected means you are on track to exhaust your error budget within 10 days at current rates. This is a proactive problem signal because the customer impact threshold has not been crossed yet — but it will be.

**Burn rate calculation:**

```
Expected 30-day budget consumption = 100% / (30 days / SLO measurement period)
Current burn rate = (1 - current_SLO_achievement) / (1 - SLO_target)
Ratio = current_burn_rate / expected_burn_rate
```

If your SLO target is 99.9% (43.8 minutes downtime/month allowance) and your trailing 7-day SLO achievement is 99.5%, you are burning budget at roughly 5x the expected rate. At that rate, the budget is gone in 6 days. Open a problem record now, not after the budget is exhausted.

### Change Correlation Analysis Leading to Process Problem

When Splunk shows that 60%+ of P2 incidents in a specific service correlate with change events, the evidence threshold for a process problem is met. The problem record is not against the service — it is against the change and release management process for that service.

The RCA question becomes: is this a test coverage gap, a deployment pipeline deficiency, or a fragile service architecture that cannot absorb change without failure? Each answer leads to a different fix, but all three are identified through the same pattern analysis.

### Capacity Trajectory Signals

CPU/memory/disk trending toward saturation on a 90-day trajectory is a proactive problem before the capacity incident occurs. At T-Mobile scale, with Kubernetes clusters running thousands of pods, per-node resource trending is not sufficient. You need aggregate cluster resource trending, namespace-level trending, and per-service resource consumption growth modeling.

The operational standard: any resource metric on a 90-day trajectory to >85% utilization without a scaling plan triggers a problem record. The problem owner is the capacity planning team, and the fix plan includes either a scaling timeline or a demand reduction plan.

### Key Principle: Proactive Problems Need Evidence, Not Incidents

Opening a proactive problem without sufficient evidence creates noise and erodes trust in the problem management process. The evidence threshold:

- **Sufficient to open**: 2+ signals from the leading indicator table above pointing at the same CI or service, OR 1 signal where the customer impact potential is P1-equivalent
- **Insufficient to open**: a single team member's intuition without supporting data, or a single data point that could be explained by a transient event

Some organizations use a "Suspected Problem" status for pattern-based problems under investigation. This allows the problem to be tracked before root cause evidence is complete, without formally committing to a problem record. Use this status with a 5-business-day investigation window — if the investigation yields sufficient evidence, transition to "Identified." If not, close with findings documented.

## 3. Clustering Techniques

Clustering groups problems by a shared dimension to reveal systemic patterns that are invisible when problems are reviewed in isolation.

### Clustering Analysis by Dimension

| Dimension | How to Cluster | What the Pattern Means | Action |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|--------|
| Service | All problems affecting Service A | Common architectural weakness — design, dependency, or debt | Architecture review for that service; engage engineering lead |
| Team | Which team's services generate the most problem records | Coaching signal — capability gap, tech debt accumulation, or understaffing | Director conversation with engineering manager; SRE embedding |
| Failure mode | All problems caused by "dependency timeout" | Systemic timeout configuration gap across services | Platform-level fix — timeout standards, circuit breaker adoption |
| Time-of-day | Problems clustering at batch job windows or peak hours | Resource contention or inadequate isolation between batch and real-time | Architecture or scheduling change; problem against infrastructure design |
| Change category | Problems following infrastructure patching or certificate rotation | Change process quality issue for that change category | Process improvement problem; update change templates |
| Dependency | Problems in downstream services following upstream degradation | Missing resilience patterns (retries, circuit breakers, fallback) | Platform resilience standard; problem against architecture |

**Splunk clustering by failure mode:**

```spl
index=problems status!="closed" earliest=-90d
| stats count as problem_count by root_cause_category
| sort -problem_count
| head 10
| table root_cause_category, problem_count
```

**By team (coaching, not blame):**

```spl
index=problems status!="closed" earliest=-90d
| stats count as open_problems, avg(age_days) as avg_age by owning_team
| sort -open_problems
| table owning_team, open_problems, avg_age
```

The team clustering result is a conversation starter, not an accusation. The questions to answer: does the team have the staffing to address the backlog? Is the backlog a product of inherited technical debt? Is there an architectural pattern creating recurring failures across the team's services? The Director takes this data into the engineering manager 1:1, not into a public forum.

## 4. MTBI as a Problem Management KPI

**Mean Time Between Incidents (MTBI)** for a specific problem or CI measures how frequently the same failure recurs. Unlike MTTR (which measures recovery speed), MTBI measures whether the underlying problem is getting better or worse.

### Formula

```
MTBI = Total observation period / Number of incident occurrences

Example:
  CI: Payment Gateway Auth Service
  Observation period: 90 days
  Incident count involving this CI: 6
  MTBI = 90 / 6 = 15 days
```

### Interpreting MTBI Trends

| MTBI Trend | Interpretation | Action |
|------------|---------------|--------|
| MTBI decreasing (incidents more frequent) | Problem is worsening despite open record | Escalate; current workaround ineffective; urgent permanent fix |
| MTBI increasing after workaround deployment | Workaround is working; recurrence slowing | Continue monitoring; permanent fix still required on schedule |
| MTBI stable despite workaround | Workaround is ineffective | Remove workaround from KEDB; escalate; revise fix plan |
| MTBI improving quarter-over-quarter >25% | Problem management is working | Maintain cadence; document pattern for KEDB |

**Target**: P1/P2 problems should show >25% MTBI improvement quarter-over-quarter after the problem record is opened and a workaround is in place. If a problem shows no MTBI improvement after one quarter, the workaround is either not being applied consistently or is not effective.

> **Director Callout:** "MTBI improvement rate is the metric that tells you whether your problem management program is actually reducing reliability risk, or just generating paperwork. If your problem backlog is growing but MTBI is not improving, your team is documenting problems, not solving them. Those are very different activities."

## 5. Declaring a Problem from a Pattern vs. Waiting for Recurrence

The most common governance failure in problem management: "We'll open a problem when it happens again." This is reactive management masquerading as caution. It guarantees at least one more customer-impacting incident.

### Evidence Threshold for Proactive Problem Declaration

**Evidence sufficient to declare a problem:**
- 2+ signals from the leading indicator table pointing at the same CI or service
- 1 signal with P1-equivalent customer impact potential (payments, auth, core connectivity)
- Chaos engineering finding that reveals an untested failure mode
- Change correlation report showing >60% incidents correlate with changes for that service

**Evidence insufficient to declare a problem:**
- Single transient incident with no recurrence
- Team member intuition without supporting data
- Monitoring noise that does not persist across alert evaluation windows

### Decision Flowchart: Proactive Problem Declaration

```
Is there an active incident?
├── YES → Follow Incident Management process. Flag for post-incident problem review.
└── NO → Continue ↓

Do you have 2+ leading indicator signals for the same CI or service?
├── NO → Single signal?
│         ├── YES → Is customer impact potential P1-equivalent?
│         │         ├── YES → Declare problem. Route to Problem Manager.
│         │         └── NO → Log observation. Monitor for 5 business days.
│         │                   Escalate if second signal appears.
│         └── NO → No action. Re-evaluate next weekly problem review.
└── YES → Continue ↓

Is the CI or service P1/P2-impact classified?
├── YES → Declare problem immediately. Assign to Problem Manager same business day.
└── NO → Continue ↓

Does the trend show acceleration (worsening over time)?
├── YES → Declare problem. Open as P2 proactive.
└── NO → Log as "Suspected Problem." Investigate for 5 business days.
          Confirm or close based on findings.
```

**Who declares proactive problems:** SRE Team Lead or Problem Manager, with mandatory Service Owner notification within 4 business hours of the record opening.

## 6. Executive Reporting: Reliability Trends

Executive reporting on problem management serves two purposes: demonstrating that the program is functioning, and providing the data that drives resource allocation decisions. A VP approving headcount for SRE should be able to point to problem trend data showing why the investment is needed.

### Three-Metric Executive View

1. **Incident volume by service (trailing 90 days, trending)** — is the incident rate going up or down?
2. **MTBI by top-10 problem** — are known problems getting better or worse?
3. **Problem backlog by age and priority** — how long are problems sitting without resolution?

### Monthly Executive Report Template

| Metric | Current Value | Prior Month | Trend | Target | Status |
|--------|--------------|-------------|-------|--------|--------|
| Total open problems (P1) | 3 | 4 | ↓ Improving | <5 | 🟢 Green |
| Total open problems (P2) | 14 | 11 | ↑ Worsening | <12 | 🔴 Red |
| Avg age of open P1 problems (days) | 18 | 22 | ↓ Improving | <30 | 🟢 Green |
| Avg age of open P2 problems (days) | 67 | 61 | ↑ Worsening | <60 | 🟡 Amber |
| P1 MTBI improvement (QoQ) | +31% | +28% | ↑ Improving | >25% | 🟢 Green |
| P2 MTBI improvement (QoQ) | +8% | +12% | ↓ Worsening | >15% | 🔴 Red |
| Fix delivery rate P1 | 94% | 91% | ↑ Improving | >90% | 🟢 Green |
| Fix delivery rate P2 | 74% | 79% | ↓ Worsening | >80% | 🔴 Red |
| Active workarounds (KEDB) | 28 | 31 | ↓ Improving | Trending down | 🟢 Green |
| Problems opened proactively | 4 of 17 (24%) | 3 of 14 (21%) | ↑ Improving | >30% | 🟡 Amber |

### RAG Thresholds

| Metric | Green | Amber | Red |
|--------|-------|-------|-----|
| Avg P1 problem age | <30 days | 30–45 days | >45 days |
| Avg P2 problem age | <60 days | 60–90 days | >90 days |
| P1 fix delivery rate | >90% | 80–90% | <80% |
| P2 fix delivery rate | >80% | 70–80% | <70% |
| MTBI improvement (QoQ) | >25% | 10–25% | <10% |
| Proactive problem % | >30% | 20–30% | <20% |

### Healthy vs. Unhealthy Trend Interpretation

**Healthy:** Declining incident volume, MTBI improving quarter-over-quarter, backlog age shrinking. This means problem management is translating into fewer incidents for customers.

**Unhealthy:** Stable incident volume despite an active problem backlog. This is the most dangerous pattern — it means problems are being documented but fixes are not being delivered. The VP question is: are resource constraints preventing fix delivery, or is the problem record not driving any engineering action?

**Report cadence:** Monthly to Director of Operations. Quarterly summary (trend line, not monthly snapshot) to VP of Engineering or VP of Operations.

## Summary

Pattern recognition in problem management requires three capabilities working together: automated signal detection (Splunk queries running daily), analytical discipline to cluster and interpret signals, and governance authority to declare proactive problems before incidents recur. MTBI is the North Star metric — it tells you whether the program is actually bending the reliability curve or generating documentation without operational impact. The executive reporting framework translates that operational data into the resource allocation language that Directors and VPs use to make investment decisions.

**Key thresholds to memorize:**
- 3+ incidents on same CI within 30 days → auto-flag for problem review
- >40% change-incident correlation → process problem candidate
- 30-day burn rate >3x expected → proactive problem signal
- MTBI improvement target: >25% quarter-over-quarter per tracked problem
- Proactive problem target: >30% of all new problems opened without a preceding incident
