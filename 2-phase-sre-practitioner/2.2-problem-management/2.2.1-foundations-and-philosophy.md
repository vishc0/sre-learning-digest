# Chapter 1: Foundations and Philosophy of Problem Management

---

## 1. Problem vs. Incident: Two Different Disciplines

An incident is the fire. A problem is the broken fire suppression system that guaranteed the fire would spread.

These two disciplines look related because they share a trigger — something breaks in production. But the moment you understand what each one is optimizing for, you realize they are fundamentally different jobs that cannot be owned by the same people using the same processes and expecting different outcomes.

**Incident management** is time-bounded, command-driven, and customer-impact-focused. The IC declares an incident, assembles a response team, drives toward mitigation, and closes the incident when customer impact reaches zero. The entire discipline is organized around speed: MTTA, MTTR, time-to-first-response. The clock is running. The customer is affected. The job is to stop the bleeding.

**Problem management** is open-ended, analytically-driven, and systemic-fix-focused. There is no command structure because there is no burning production environment. The job is to understand why the fire was able to start and spread — and to change the system so that class of fire becomes structurally impossible, or at least structurally harder. The timescale is weeks to months, not minutes to hours.

The confusion runs deep in most organizations because the people who respond to incidents are the same people who are asked to "follow up" on the problem. They bring incident habits to a problem discipline: they want to close it fast, they want to identify a discrete cause, they want to move on to the next alert. Problem management requires the opposite posture — extended inquiry, systemic thinking, tolerance for ambiguity while the root cause is still being mapped.

**The 50-incident pattern**: The same underlying fault can generate 50 incidents before it is recognized as one problem. This is not hypothetical. In practice, it looks like this: an on-call rotation restarts a service twelve times over six months. Each time, the incident is classified as "service instability" and resolved with a restart. No problem record is opened because the resolution is fast, the SLA is met, and the pressure is always to move on. No one notices that 12 incidents share a root cause — because no one is looking across incidents for patterns. The on-call team is optimizing for time-to-close. Problem management requires someone who is optimizing for recurrence elimination.

**The RabbitMQ analogy**: In RabbitMQ operations, an incident is "the queue is backing up and consumers can't keep pace — we're seeing 50,000 unacknowledged messages and growing." The response is command-driven: scale consumers, check prefetch configuration, identify the stuck message, restore consumer throughput. Incident closed in 40 minutes.

The problem is something different: "The retry storm pattern on circuit failure causes guaranteed queue saturation under sustained load. When the upstream payment service goes into circuit-open state, the notification retry logic does not honor backoff — it requeues at the original rate. Under the current consumer capacity, any circuit-open event lasting more than 8 minutes will saturate the queue and cascade into consumer connection exhaustion." That is a structural condition. Fixing it requires a code change to the retry logic, load testing to validate the fix, and potentially a capacity conversation. Different timescale. Different owner. Different outcome metric.

| Dimension | Incident Management | Problem Management |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Trigger** | Customer impact detected | Incident cluster / postmortem / proactive signal |
| **Timescale** | Minutes to hours | Days to months |
| **Success metric** | MTTR — time to restore service | MTBI — time between incident recurrences |
| **Ownership model** | Incident Commander (command authority) | Problem Manager + Problem Owner (distributed accountability) |
| **Decision style** | Command-driven, consensus optional | Analytically-driven, evidence-required |
| **Primary output** | Service restored; PIR written | Root cause identified; permanent fix delivered |
| **Failure state** | Incident drags; MTTR degrades | Problem recurs; MTBI does not improve |

## 2. ITIL Problem Management in Modern SRE Context

ITIL v4 defines problem management across three activities: reactive problem management (after incidents), proactive problem management (before incidents), and error control (managing known errors from identification through permanent fix or risk acceptance). This is the right model. The SRE community arrived at the same structure through a different path — error budget economics — and operationalizes it with different language and measurement tooling.

Understanding how they fit together is a Director-level competency because you will be in organizations that use both vocabularies. Your team's SREs will talk in error budgets and burn rates. Your ITSM partners will talk in problem records and KEDB entries. You need to translate fluently in both directions.

| ITIL Construct | SRE Equivalent | Key Difference |
|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| Problem Record | Postmortem action item tracked to completion | ITIL tracks the structural condition; SRE often tracks the remediation action |
| Known Error | Documented failure mode with active SLO impact | ITIL explicitly tracks workarounds; SRE tends to rely on runbooks |
| KEDB | On-call runbook library | KEDB is formal governance; runbooks are operational procedures |
| Reactive Problem Mgmt | Post-incident review → engineering backlog | ITIL has explicit lifecycle stages; SRE often stops at the postmortem |
| Proactive Problem Mgmt | Error budget burn rate analysis → preemptive work | SRE has more sophisticated measurement; ITIL has more explicit process governance |
| Error Control | Known error governance, risk acceptance process | ITIL requires formal risk acceptance sign-off; SRE may accept implicitly |

**The ITIL-SRE integration model**:

```
Incident Declared
      ↓
   [ITIL: Incident Management]
   [SRE: On-call response, IC command structure]
      ↓
   Customer Impact = Zero
      ↓
   PIR Completed
   [ITIL: Problem identified from PIR]
   [SRE: Postmortem identifies SLO impact + error budget consumed]
      ↓
   Problem Record Opened
      ↓
   RCA Performed
   [ITIL: Root cause analysis, workaround documented]
   [SRE: Permanent fix prioritized against error budget burn rate]
      ↓
   Known Error (if fix pending) OR Permanent Fix (if fix deployed)
   [ITIL: KEDB entry created]
   [SRE: Runbook updated, burn rate monitoring adjusted]
      ↓
   Problem Closed
   [ITIL: Problem record closed with outcome documented]
   [SRE: Error budget stop-drain verified; MTBI tracking reset]
```

**The key tension**: ITIL is process-heavy — it specifies lifecycle stages, required fields, and governance checkpoints but is agnostic about how you measure success. SRE is measurement-heavy — it defines quantitative thresholds (error budget burn rates, SLO targets, MTBI trends) but often leaves process governance implicit. Mature orgs use both. The process governance of ITIL prevents problem records from languishing without accountability. The measurement rigor of SRE prevents problem management from becoming a documentation exercise that produces no change in reliability outcomes.

At T-Mobile scale — thousands of change records per month, a notification platform processing 25M messages per day, multiple engineering teams with competing sprint priorities — you cannot run problem management on informal processes or implicit measurement. Both dimensions are required.

## 3. Reactive vs. Proactive Problem Management

Most organizations are 90% reactive. A Director/VP goal for the first 12 months in a role should be to shift the balance to at least 40% proactive — not because proactive is ideologically superior, but because proactive problem management catches failure conditions before they produce incidents, which means lower customer impact, lower error budget consumption, and lower on-call burden.

**Reactive problem management** is triggered by things that have already happened:
- An incident cluster: the same failure mode appearing 3 or more times in 30 days
- A PIR finding: the postmortem identifies a structural root cause that requires engineering investment beyond the immediate fix
- A repeat SEV1 or SEV2: the same service fails in the same way within a 90-day window
- A change-correlated incident: the same change window consistently produces incidents

The reactive trigger is unambiguous and easy to automate: if the conditions are met, open the problem record. The challenge is organizational — ensuring that the record is opened, that it is owned, and that it does not age out without an outcome.

**Proactive problem management** is triggered by signals that predict future incidents before they occur:
- SLO burn rate acceleration: the error budget is being consumed faster than historical baseline, indicating a degradation trend that will produce an SLO breach before the measurement window closes
- Capacity signals: resource utilization trending toward saturation thresholds based on growth projections
- Change failure rate increase: a service's deployment pipeline is producing failed changes at a higher rate than baseline, indicating a quality problem that will produce incidents
- Dependency aging: a third-party library or platform component approaching end-of-support, creating a future operational risk

**Proactive detection example at T-Mobile scale**: A Splunk query runs on a 24-hour schedule across the change management and incident data. The query identifies any Configuration Item (CI) that has generated 3 or more SEV2 incidents in the last 30 days. The query output automatically creates a Problem record in ServiceNow with priority P2, linked to the three incidents, with the Problem Manager assigned based on the CI's service ownership table. No human decision required to open the record — the human decision is required to run the RCA and fund the fix.

This is the architecture of mature proactive problem management: automated detection, automated record creation, human judgment applied where it adds value (analysis and decision-making), not where it can be automated (pattern recognition and record housekeeping).

## 4. Why Most Organizations Fail: Treating Problems as Slow Incidents

The most common failure mode in problem management is the category error described in the README: treating problems as slow incidents with longer SLAs. This shows up in specific organizational patterns that are recognizable and preventable.

**Pattern 1: The incident team closes the ticket; the problem record never opens.**
The PIR is written. The action items include "investigate root cause of service instability." That action item is assigned to the on-call engineer. Three weeks later, the engineer has responded to four more incidents. The problem action item is 30 days old and no one has followed up. No problem record exists in the ITSM platform. The next incident of the same class opens fresh, with no documented history.

Fix: Problem records must open automatically as a post-PIR step for any incident above a defined severity threshold. The Problem Manager, not the incident team, owns opening the record.

**Pattern 2: Problem records are opened but owned by no one with authority to fund fixes.**
A problem record exists. It has a root cause. The root cause requires 3 sprints of engineering work. The problem record is assigned to a mid-level engineer. That engineer's squad lead says the work will be prioritized in Q3. Q3 arrives. The squad has 12 other priorities. The problem record is updated with a new ETA of Q4. This cycle repeats until the record is more than a year old.

Fix: Problem Owner must be at a level that can commit sprint capacity. For P1 and P2 problems, that means a senior technical lead or architect with explicit agreement from their engineering manager that the work is funded. Without that agreement, the problem record should not leave "Investigating" status.

**Pattern 3: Problem status never changes because engineering is always on the next sprint.**
The problem record reflects an honest status: "Root Cause Known." It has been "Root Cause Known" for 4 months. The fix plan exists but was never scoped into a sprint. No one escalates because the problem is not causing a current incident. The record is stale but formally compliant — it has a status, it has an owner, it has a root cause. It will remain in this state until the next incident recurs.

Fix: SLA enforcement. Every status has a maximum time. "Root Cause Known" with no fix plan approved within 20 days for a P2 problem triggers automatic escalation to the Problem Manager's director. This is not a punitive mechanism — it is a signal that the fix needs to be funded or the risk needs to be formally accepted.

**Pattern 4: "We'll fix it in Q3."**
This is the most insidious pattern because it feels like accountability. A commitment is made. A date is set. It is tracked. But it is tracked at the problem record level, not at the sprint level. "Fix it in Q3" is not a commitment until the engineering work is in the sprint backlog with story points, an owner, and a dependency map. Until that happens, "Q3" is a polite way of saying "not now." Directors who accept "Q3" as a fix commitment without verifying sprint-level planning are accepting a fiction.

**The result of all four patterns**: MTBI for known problems does not improve. The on-call team carries the cost of organizational failure in the form of repeat pages, repeat incidents, and repeat stress. The reliability program shows competent incident management metrics — improving MTTR, growing runbook library — but the underlying incident rate for known problem classes does not decline. This is the signature of Failure Mode 1 and 2 combined.

## 5. Three Outcomes of a Problem Record

A problem record has exactly three valid terminal states. Understanding them is a Director-level decision point because two of the three require active organizational commitment, and one requires explicit authority to accept.

**Outcome 1: Permanent Fix**
An engineering change eliminates the root cause permanently. The same failure class cannot recur under the same conditions. This is the preferred outcome. The problem record closes with the fix change record linked and the MTBI clock reset. Going forward, the fix is validated through production monitoring: if the same incident class recurs, the fix either was insufficient or a new variant of the problem has emerged.

**Outcome 2: Known Error (workaround available, permanent fix pending)**
The root cause is identified. A workaround exists that allows operators to restore service faster than without it. The permanent fix is on the engineering roadmap but not yet delivered. The problem record moves to the KEDB as a Known Error entry. The KEDB entry is the operational artifact; the problem record remains open. This is a legitimate interim state — the key word is "interim." A Known Error without a committed fix ETA and sprint-level planning is not a legitimate known error: it is an accepted dysfunction with documentation.

**Outcome 3: Accepted Risk**
The root cause is known. A permanent fix exists in concept. But the fix is not economically justified given the probability of recurrence, the cost of impact, the engineering cost of the fix, and the organizational priorities. The risk is formally accepted with compensating controls documented. The problem record closes with risk acceptance notation.

**Director decision point**: Who has authority to accept a problem as "Accepted Risk"? This must be defined, documented, and reviewed annually. Accepted risk is not free. The compensating controls must be maintained. The risk must be re-evaluated when the business context changes (higher user volume, new regulatory requirements, new dependencies). A risk accepted in 2023 may not be acceptable in 2025 with 40% user growth. The authority to accept risk and the obligation to re-evaluate it must rest with the same person at the appropriate organizational level.

| Outcome | Problem Record State | KEDB Entry | Fix Plan Required | Authority Level |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| Permanent Fix | Closed (Resolved) | Not required (or retired) | Delivered | Problem Owner |
| Known Error | Open (Fix in Progress) | Required | Required + ETA | Problem Owner + EM |
| Accepted Risk | Closed (Risk Accepted) | Optional (for ops awareness) | Not required | Director minimum |

## 6. Cultural Requirements for Problem Management to Work

Technical process is necessary but not sufficient. Problem management fails in organizations with technically correct processes and culturally broken execution. Three cultural conditions are required.

**Blameless environment**: RCAs that name humans as root cause are invalid — not because humans are never involved, but because humans are always the symptom, and the system is the cause. The engineer who pushed the breaking change is not the root cause. The deployment pipeline that lacked pre-production validation is the root cause. The on-call engineer who applied the wrong workaround is not the root cause. The runbook that was ambiguous under the observed conditions is the root cause. When humans are identified as root cause, the fix is always a training or supervision intervention — which has a short half-life and doesn't change the system. When systems are identified as root cause, the fix is an engineering change — which has a lasting effect.

**Psychological safety**: Engineers must be able to surface problem signals — recurring failures, known design weaknesses, capacity concerns — without fear that surfacing the problem will lead to blame, reduced performance ratings, or increased scrutiny of their team. In organizations where surfacing problems creates personal risk, problems are hidden until they produce incidents. This is not a policy failure. It is a leadership failure. Psychological safety is created and destroyed by individual leadership behavior, not by policy documents.

**Engineering investment**: Leadership must fund problem resolution, not just postmortem writing. A program that produces excellent RCAs and funds zero engineering remediation has spent money on analysis and produced no reliability improvement. The decision to fund engineering work on problem resolution is a leadership decision about priorities. Directors and VPs who do not create the organizational conditions for that investment — sprint capacity allocation, technical debt budgets, explicit headcount for reliability work — are choosing to accept the cost of recurring incidents instead.

> **Director/VP Callout — The Question That Reveals Everything**
>
> In any problem management review, ask: "Show me the last 10 problems we closed. How many resulted in a permanent fix versus a workaround that was accepted?" If the answer is 3 permanent fixes and 7 workarounds, you have a funding problem. If the answer is 0 permanent fixes and 10 workarounds, you do not have a problem management program — you have a documentation program that produces no reliability outcomes. The ratio of permanent fixes to accepted workarounds is the single most revealing metric in the problem management portfolio. It tells you whether the organization is investing in reliability or managing appearances.
