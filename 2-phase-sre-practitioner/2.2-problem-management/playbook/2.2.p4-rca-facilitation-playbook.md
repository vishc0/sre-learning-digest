# RCA Facilitation Playbook
## Step-by-Step Guide for the RCA Facilitator — Director/VP of Operations Lens

---

## Facilitator Role Clarity

You are running the meeting, not contributing to the analysis. Your job is to:
- Hold the room to the process
- Protect the timeline from derailment
- Ensure every finding is evidence-based, not opinion-based
- Redirect "human error" findings to systemic conditions
- Ensure corrective actions are specific, owned, and time-bound

If you find yourself contributing technical analysis, you have left the facilitator role. Hand facilitation to someone else or stop contributing.

---

## Phase 0 — Pre-Session Preparation
### 72 Hours Before the Session

Preparation determines quality. A poorly prepared RCA session produces low-quality findings regardless of who is in the room.

**Evidence Collection Checklist**:

| Evidence Type | Source | Who Collects | Must-Have? |
|--------------|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Incident timeline (preliminary) | Incident record, war room notes | Problem Owner | Yes |
| Splunk/log excerpts (error window ± 30 min) | Observability platform | On-call SRE | Yes |
| Alert history (what fired, when, in what order) | PagerDuty / Opsgenie | On-call SRE | Yes |
| Change records (72 hours prior to incident) | Change Management system | Change Manager | Yes |
| Runbook adherence log (was runbook followed?) | Incident Manager review | Incident Manager | Yes |
| Dependency map (what calls what) | CMDB / architecture diagram | System SME | Yes |
| Prior incident history for this CI (90 days) | Incident Management | Problem Manager | Yes |
| Vendor support ticket (if third-party involved) | Vendor portal | Problem Owner | If applicable |
| Metric snapshots (CPU, latency, queue depth at failure time) | Monitoring platform | On-call SRE | Yes |

> **Watch for**: Log retention gaps. If logs for the critical window are gone (retention policy, system failure), document this as a finding before the session. "We don't have evidence for X" is a legitimate gap and often points to a monitoring corrective action.

**Participant Identification**:

- **Incident Responders** — eyewitness value. They know what they observed vs. what the system reported. Do not substitute with someone who "read the ticket."
- **System SMEs** — architecture knowledge. They know what the system does under failure conditions. One per major affected system.
- **Problem Owner** — accountable for the problem record; owns corrective action delivery.
- **Process Observer** (optional) — someone from SRE practice or QA to assess process adherence findings.
- **Scribe** — dedicated note-taker. Not the facilitator. Not an active participant.

Cap attendance at 8. More people does not mean better RCA — it means more noise and less psychological safety for direct reporting.

**Pre-Read Package** (send 48 hours before):

```
Subject: RCA Pre-Read — [Incident ID] — [Short Title] — Session [Date/Time]

Team,

We have an RCA session scheduled for [date/time] for incident [ID].

WHAT WE KNOW:
[3–5 bullet preliminary summary — symptoms, impact, timeline anchor points]

PRELIMINARY TIMELINE:
[Attach or inline the preliminary timeline with evidence sources noted]

WHAT WE DO NOT KNOW YET:
[List explicit gaps — these are what we are investigating]

QUESTIONS TO THINK ABOUT BEFORE THE SESSION:
1. [targeted question based on known gap]
2. [targeted question based on known gap]
3. [targeted question based on known gap]

Please come ready to contribute to the timeline and challenge assumptions — not to defend your team's actions.

Session rules: no blame, no solutions yet, facts first.

[Facilitator Name]
```

**Logistics**:

- Book 90 minutes for P2 RCA; 2 hours for P1
- Shared document prepared (timeline table, fishbone/5 Whys template) — accessible to all participants before the session starts
- Screen share ready with preliminary timeline visible
- Whiteboard (physical or digital) for real-time timeline updates

## Phase 1 — Opening the Session
### First 10 Minutes

**0:00 — Ground Rules (3 min)**

State explicitly at the start of every session. Do not assume people remember from last time.

> "Before we start, three ground rules.
>
> One: no blame. We are here to understand how our systems and processes failed, not who made a mistake. If someone made a decision that looks wrong in hindsight, our question is: what information did they have, what options did they see, and what did our systems allow them to do? Human error is never a root cause — it is a symptom.
>
> Two: no solutions yet. We are spending the next 60 minutes understanding the problem. Solutions come at the end. If you think of a fix during the timeline or analysis phase, write it down — we will get to it.
>
> Three: evidence over opinion. Every claim needs a source. 'I think the latency spiked at 2:15' is not evidence. 'The APM dashboard shows p99 latency exceeding 5 seconds at 02:17:43' is evidence. Source everything."

**0:03 — Scope Confirmation (3 min)**

Read the scope statement aloud. Get verbal confirmation from the group.

> "We are investigating incident [ID], which caused [customer-visible impact — be specific: transactions failed, API unavailable, data delayed]. The scope of this investigation is [time window: from first anomaly to service restoration]. We are not investigating [explicitly out of scope: e.g., the downstream impact on system Y which has a separate RCA, the business decision to deploy without change freeze].
>
> Does anyone have a scope question before we start?"

**0:06 — Role Assignments (2 min)**

- Confirm the scribe: "Who is taking notes?"
- Confirm the SME roles: "When we get to the message broker behavior, [name], you're our resource. For the database layer, [name]."

**0:08 — Timeline Review (2 min)**

- Display the preliminary timeline
- "Before we start reconstructing, let's look at this together. This is a draft — it will change. Please do not defend or challenge it yet. We will work through it chronologically."

## Phase 2 — Timeline Reconstruction
### 0:10 – 0:40 (30 Minutes)

The timeline is the foundation. Everything else — analysis, corrective actions — is only as good as the timeline.

**Working Method**:

Work through events in strict chronological order. For each event, ask:

1. **What happened?** — observable, specific, measurable. Not "the system got slow." Specify: "Message queue depth on [queue name] began rising above baseline at 02:14. By 02:19 it was at 47,000 messages."
2. **How do we know?** — evidence source. Metric name, log line, alert name, eyewitness. If the evidence source is "someone told me," that is a gap.
3. **What triggered it?** — what was the prior state that made this event possible? This is where causal chains begin to emerge.

**Key Markers to Identify**:

- **Failure start**: when did the condition that caused the incident first exist? This is almost always before the first alert.
- **Detection point**: when did monitoring or a human first identify something was wrong?
- **Detection lag** = Detection point − Failure start. This is a metric. It tells you about observability gaps.
- **Response start**: when did the first operational response action occur?
- **Response lag** = Response start − Detection point. This is a metric. It tells you about paging, alerting, and on-call effectiveness.
- **Recovery start**: when did the first action that contributed to recovery occur?
- **Service restoration**: when did normal service resume?

**Evidence Gap Protocol**:

When the group does not know what happened during a time window:

> "We have a gap from [time] to [time]. We don't know what happened during this window. That gap is a finding — it tells us something about our observability. Let's mark it on the timeline and continue."

Do not let the group spend more than 3 minutes trying to reconstruct an unknown window from memory. If evidence doesn't exist, the gap is the finding.

> **Watch for**: Participants filling gaps with plausible narratives. "It was probably the deployment." Redirect: "What evidence do we have for that?" If none, it goes on the hypothesis list, not the timeline.

> **Watch for**: Hindsight bias — "Obviously the cache was the problem." Redirect to what was known at each point in time. The question is not "what do we know now?" but "what did the team know at 02:19?"

## Phase 3 — Root Cause Analysis
### 0:40 – 1:20 (40 Minutes)

**Method Selection**:

Refer to `rca-method-selector.md` for the full decision tree. Quick guide:

| Failure Type | Primary Method | Secondary |
|-------------|----------------|----------|
| Single clear causal chain | 5 Whys | — |
| Multiple contributing factors | Fishbone | 5 Whys per branch |
| Cascading system failure | Fault Tree Analysis | Fishbone for human factors |
| Recurring failure, different triggers | Fishbone | FMEA review |

**5 Whys — Facilitation Rules**:

- Start with the customer-visible symptom: "Why did customers experience [impact]?"
- Each "why" should advance toward a structural condition
- Stop when you reach a condition that cannot be made worse by another "why" without changing systems, processes, or organizational decisions
- Target depth: 4–6 Whys. Fewer than 4 usually means you stopped at symptoms. More than 6 usually means you are generating hypotheticals.

> **Watch for**: Stopping at human action. "The engineer did not restart the service." Why was a manual restart required? Why was there no automated recovery? Why was the alert threshold set too high to catch this? Keep going.

> **Watch for**: "Why" answers that are opinions: "Because the team didn't prioritize it." Redirect: "What specifically was not prioritized? What process or decision led to that deprioritization?"

**Fishbone — Facilitation Rules**:

Categories to populate (do not skip any — absence is a finding):

| Category | RCA Translation |
|----------|----------------|
| People | Knowledge gaps, staffing, decision-making constraints |
| Process | Runbook gaps, change process failures, on-call handoff failures |
| Technology | Architecture weaknesses, configuration drift, capacity limits |
| Environment | Infrastructure dependencies, network, vendor components |
| Measurement | Alerting gaps, missing metrics, logging gaps |
| Management | Prioritization decisions, accepted risks, resource constraints |

For each category: "Is there a contributing factor here?" Do not accept "no" without the group explicitly confirming they have checked.

**Root Cause Statement Format**:

Once analysis is complete, facilitate the group to produce one root cause statement in this structure:

> "Due to [structural condition — the systemic weakness], when [triggering event] occurred, [failure propagation mechanism] resulted in [customer impact]."

Example:
> "Due to the absence of circuit breaker logic in the payment service's dependency on the fraud-scoring API, when the fraud-scoring API response time exceeded 30 seconds, payment transactions queued until thread pool exhaustion caused the payment service to stop accepting requests, resulting in 100% transaction failure for 23 minutes."

Read this statement back to the group. Get explicit confirmation that this matches what they investigated. If there is disagreement, surface it — a disputed root cause statement means the analysis is not complete.

> **Watch for**: Groups that want to list 8 root causes. A long root cause list usually means contributing factors are being elevated to root cause. There is typically one root cause and multiple contributing factors. Be specific about the distinction.

> **Watch for**: Root cause statements that blame a person. Redirect: "What organizational or systemic condition allowed this action to have this impact?"

## Phase 4 — Corrective Actions
### 1:20 – 1:30 (10 Minutes)

**Action Classification**:

For each root cause and contributing factor:

| Class | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Prevention | Eliminates the root cause — this failure mode cannot occur again | Circuit breaker added; configuration validated by deployment pipeline |
| Detection | Finds the failure faster next time — does not prevent but reduces impact | Alert added for queue depth > 5,000 messages; dashboard panel created |
| Response | Limits the blast radius when it does occur | Runbook updated with manual circuit-break procedure; escalation path clarified |

Aim for at least one prevention action per root cause. Detection and response actions without prevention mean the problem will recur — you will just know about it faster.

**Action Quality Gates**:

Reject any corrective action that fails these tests:

| Rejected Form | Why Rejected | Require Instead |
|--------------|--------------|----------------|
| "Improve monitoring" | No specificity — what metric, what threshold, what alert | "Add alert: fraud-scoring API p99 latency > 5s for 3 consecutive minutes, severity P2" |
| "Review process" | Review is not action — review produces a decision, not a change | "Update runbook section 3.2 to include circuit-break procedure by [date]" |
| "Train the team" | Training is not a systemic fix and degrades over time | "Implement automated validation for [specific configuration] in CI pipeline" |
| "Increase awareness" | Awareness is not accountable — no one can verify it | Not acceptable — return to root cause and identify structural control |

**Required Fields for Each Corrective Action**:

- Description (specific, verifiable)
- Owner (named individual — not a team, not a role)
- Due date (not "next quarter" — a calendar date)
- Verification method (how will Problem Manager confirm this is done and effective?)

> **Watch for**: Participants volunteering corrective actions without ownership confirmation from the named person. Confirm: "[Name], you're the owner on this — is that confirmed?"

## Phase 5 — Closing the Session

**Root Cause Confirmation (2 min)**:

Read the root cause statement aloud. Ask: "Does everyone agree this accurately describes what we found today? Any objections or additions?"

If anyone objects: surface the disagreement, note it, and either resolve it in the room or flag it for the written RCA review process. Do not suppress dissent.

**Corrective Action Confirmation (3 min)**:

Read back each corrective action with owner and due date. For each: "Confirmed?" Get verbal confirmation from the named owner — not from the group on their behalf.

**Next Steps**:

- Written RCA document: who drafts, when is it due (target: 24 hours), who reviews, who approves
- RCA document circulation: all participants + Service Owner + Problem Manager
- Problem record update: Problem Manager updates status to "Root Cause Known" after RCA is approved

---

## Phase 6 — Post-Session Documentation
### Within 24 Hours of Session

1. Write the RCA report using `rca-report-template.md`
2. Include the confirmed timeline, root cause statement, contributing factors, and all corrective actions with owners and dates
3. Update the problem record:
   - Status: Root Cause Known
   - Root cause field: copy the confirmed root cause statement
   - RCA completion date: today
4. Create KEDB entry if a workaround exists (even a partial one) — see `kedb-governance-playbook.md`
5. Send RCA draft to all session participants for review: 48-hour review window
6. Send final approved RCA to: Problem Owner, Service Owner, Director of Operations (for P1), KEDB Owner

**Quality Gate — RCA Is Complete When**:

- All corrective actions are verified as implemented (not just committed to)
- No corrective action is more than 90 days overdue without an accepted deferral
- KEDB entry is published or archived
- Problem record status is "Fix Verified" or "Closed"

Signing the RCA document is not completion. Completion is when the actions are done and verified.

## Common Derailment Patterns and Redirects

| Derailment | Signal | Facilitator Redirect |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Technical debate breaks out | Two engineers debating root cause interpretation | "This is a useful debate — let's capture both hypotheses and note what evidence would resolve it. Can you two take this offline and send findings to [Problem Owner] by [date]?" |
| Solutions dominate analysis | "We should just add a retry" before root cause is found | "Let's add that to the solutions parking lot. We need to finish understanding the problem first." |
| Blame creeps in | "The DBA should have caught this" | "What process or tooling should have caught this? Let's stay on systems." |
| Participant gets defensive | Responder justifying their actions | "I want to be clear — we're not evaluating your decisions. We're asking what information you had and what the system allowed. What did the runbook say to do at that point?" |
| RCA stops at human error | "The engineer didn't follow the runbook" | "What made it possible for skipping the runbook step to cause this outcome? What should have prevented that?" |
| Scope creep | Discussion shifts to related but separate system | "That's important but it's out of scope for this RCA. Let's open a separate problem record for [related issue]." |
| Session runs long | 80 minutes in, still in analysis | "We have 10 minutes left. Let's confirm the root cause statement and corrective actions, even if not all details are final. We can complete nuance in the written document." |
