# Vendor Problem Escalation Playbook
## Third-Party and Vendor Problem Management — Director/VP of Operations Lens

---

## Why Vendor Problem Management Is Different

Vendor problems operate under a different accountability structure than internal problems. You cannot directly investigate the root cause, you cannot direct engineers to fix anything, and the fix timeline is outside your control. What you can control is:

- **Evidence quality** — the case you build determines vendor response speed
- **Escalation velocity** — knowing when to escalate and to what level
- **Contractual leverage** — SLA breach documentation is evidence for credit claims and contract renegotiation
- **Internal workaround** — what you do while waiting, so customers are not waiting with you

A vendor problem record that is not actively tracked is a liability. If the vendor misses their fix date and you have no documentation trail, you have no leverage.

---

## Section 1 — When to Open a Vendor Problem Record

### Mandatory Triggers

Open a vendor problem record immediately when any of the following occur:

| Trigger | Criteria |
|---------|----------|
| Recurring vendor-caused incidents | 2+ incidents in 30 days where vendor component is confirmed root cause |
| Vendor SLA breach requiring operational workaround | Any single incident where your team implemented a workaround because vendor failed to resolve within contracted SLA |
| Vendor RCA does not match observed behavior | Vendor provides root cause explanation that contradicts your log evidence or incident timeline |
| Vendor root cause listed in PIR | Any PIR where root cause is attributed to "vendor" or "third-party" — automatic review for problem record |

### Judgment Triggers (Assess, Then Decide)

| Trigger | When to Open |
|---------|-------------|
| Single significant vendor-caused incident | P1 customer impact even without recurrence |
| Degraded vendor performance (not full failure) | If degradation recurs 3+ times in 60 days |
| Vendor change caused incident | If vendor change management process failed — systemic, not one-time |
| Vendor missing contractual SLAs even without incident | Pattern of late response or resolution even when incidents are low severity |

### When Not to Open a Vendor Problem Record

- Vendor-caused incident that was a true one-time anomaly with no recurrence risk and vendor has confirmed root cause and delivered fix
- Vendor feature limitation that is expected and documented (known limitation, not a failure)
- Vendor scheduled maintenance that ran over window — if this is first occurrence; if recurring, open a record

## Section 2 — Vendor Problem Record Structure

Use the standard problem record template and add the following vendor-specific fields:

### Standard Fields (Complete as Normal)

Priority, Affected CI, Problem Owner, Source Incident IDs, Description, Status, Root Cause (when known)

### Vendor-Specific Additional Fields

| Field | Content | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Vendor Name | Full legal/commercial name | Match contract language exactly |
| Vendor Product/Component | Specific product and version | Version matters for fix tracking |
| Vendor Ticket ID | Their internal reference number | Required for all vendor communication |
| Vendor SLA — Response Time | Contracted response time for this incident severity | Pull from contract or SLA addendum |
| Vendor SLA — Resolution Time | Contracted resolution time | Pull from contract |
| Vendor RCA Commitment Date | Date vendor has committed to provide RCA | Get this in writing in the ticket |
| Vendor Fix Commitment Date | Date vendor has committed to deploy fix | Get this in writing in the ticket |
| Contractual SLA Credit Exposure | Dollar value or credit formula from contract | Finance/Legal can assist |
| Internal Workaround Status | Active / Not available / Partial | Updated as workaround status changes |
| Internal Workaround Description | What your team is doing while waiting | Link to KEDB entry if published |

### Status Tracking Specific to Vendor Problems

Vendor problem records need more granular status tracking because multiple parties own different phases:

| Status | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Vendor Ticket Filed | Support ticket opened; awaiting vendor acknowledgment |
| Vendor Investigating | Vendor has acknowledged and investigation is underway |
| Vendor RCA Provided | Vendor has delivered root cause analysis |
| Vendor Fix in Development | Vendor has committed to a fix and it is in their pipeline |
| Vendor Fix Released | Fix has been released by vendor; testing/verification underway |
| Fix Verified — Closed | Fix verified effective in your environment; problem closed |
| Accepted Risk — Vendor | No vendor fix available; risk formally accepted |

## Section 3 — Opening Vendor Escalation

### Evidence Package

Assemble the evidence package before opening the vendor ticket. A ticket with insufficient evidence gets deprioritized. A ticket with a complete evidence package signals operational maturity and gets escalated internally by the vendor.

**Evidence Package Contents**:

| Item | Detail | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Incident timeline | Chronological events from first anomaly to restoration | Sanitize internal IP addresses, internal service names, internal hostnames |
| Log excerpts | Error messages, relevant log lines from the failure window | Include timestamps, remove any internal architectural detail that exposes security-relevant information |
| Customer impact statement | Specific: users affected, transactions impacted, duration | "Some users were affected" is not sufficient — quantify |
| Alert evidence | Screenshot or export of alerts that fired, with timestamps | Shows your monitoring detected the vendor condition |
| Metric evidence | CPU, latency, error rate, queue depth graphs — time-aligned to failure | Shows systemic impact, not just log noise |
| Prior incident references | If recurrence: ticket IDs for previous occurrences | Demonstrates pattern, not one-time event |
| Workaround description | What you did to restore service | This demonstrates vendor dependency — you had to work around their failure |
| Contract reference | SLA tier, response/resolution commitments | Paste the relevant contract clause or attach the SLA addendum |

**Sanitization rule**: before sending any logs to a vendor, strip internal hostnames, internal IP ranges, internal service architecture details, and any customer PII. Vendor support teams are not cleared for your internal architecture. Provide enough to diagnose the vendor component's behavior, not a map of your system.

### Step-by-Step: Opening the Vendor Escalation

1. **File vendor support ticket**:
   - Use the vendor's P1 or P2 designation that maps to your incident priority
   - Include the complete evidence package in the first ticket submission
   - State explicitly: "This incident caused [customer impact statement]. We are tracking this as a formal problem record and will be monitoring your response against contracted SLA commitments."
   - Reference the SLA: "Per our contract [contract number or reference], your [P1/P2] response SLA is [X hours] and resolution SLA is [X hours]. Our ticket opened at [timestamp]."

2. **Reference contract SLA commitments explicitly**:
   - Vendors respond differently when they know you are tracking against contractual obligations
   - Do not be apologetic — you are holding them to a signed agreement

3. **Link to your internal problem record**:
   - In the ticket, state: "We have opened internal problem record [ID] to track this issue. We will reference your ticket ID [vendor ID] in all our communications."
   - This signals organizational maturity and that this is being tracked, not just reported

4. **Set calendar reminders for SLA deadlines**:
   - Response SLA deadline: calendar alert 2 hours before deadline
   - Resolution SLA deadline: calendar alert 24 hours before deadline
   - These are not optional — missing them means you cannot claim SLA breach

5. **Update your internal vendor problem record** with vendor ticket ID and timestamp of submission

## Section 4 — Escalation Ladder

Work this ladder in sequence. Do not skip levels except in genuine emergency (P1 sustained outage with no vendor response).

| Day | Action | Channel | Escalation Target |
|-----|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Day 1 | File support ticket with full evidence package | Vendor support portal | L1/L2 Support |
| Day 1 | Confirm ticket receipt and P1/P2 classification | Vendor portal or email | Support team |
| Day 3 | If no substantive response or response is not satisfactory | Email + Phone | Account Manager (TAM/CSM) |
| Day 7 | If AM/TAM escalation produces no meaningful progress | Email + escalation call | Vendor executive — VP/Director of Support or Engineering |
| Day 14 | Director-to-Director escalation | Formal letter/email from your Director to their VP Engineering/Support | Your Director → Their VP |

### What Constitutes "Satisfactory Response"

For Day 3 assessment:

- Satisfactory: vendor has acknowledged the issue, assigned a named engineer, provided a preliminary analysis or hypothesis, committed to a next update time
- Not satisfactory: automated acknowledgment only, generic response not specific to your evidence, routing to wrong team, no committed next update

### Escalation Communication Template (Day 3 — Account Manager)

```
To: [Account Manager / TAM / CSM Name]
CC: [Your Problem Owner], [Your Director if P1]
Subject: Escalation — [Vendor Product] — [Ticket ID] — [Your Company] P1/P2 Impact — Day [X]

[Account Manager Name],

I am escalating our support ticket [vendor ticket ID] to your attention.

TICKET FILED: [date and time]
CURRENT STATUS: [last update from vendor]
IMPACT: [specific customer impact — still ongoing or restored with workaround]
CONTRACTED SLA: Response [X hours] / Resolution [X hours]
SLA STATUS: [On track / At risk / Breached — specify which SLA if breached]

The current response does not meet our expectations based on impact severity and contractual commitments.

WHAT WE NEED:
- Named engineer actively working this issue
- Preliminary root cause assessment or hypothesis within [X hours]
- Next update time commitment (not "we'll update when we have something")

We have internal operations impacted by this issue. If I do not receive a satisfactory update by [specific date and time], I will escalate to your engineering leadership.

[Your Name]
Problem Manager / SRE
[Company]
```

### Escalation Communication Template (Day 14 — Director to VP)

```
To: [Vendor VP Engineering / VP Support]
From: [Your Director of Operations]
CC: [Your SRE Manager], [Your Problem Manager], [Vendor Account Manager]
Subject: Formal Escalation — [Vendor Product] — Sustained Customer Impact — [Date]

[VP Name],

I am writing to formally escalate a service-impacting issue with [Vendor Product] that has been unresolved for [X days] despite multiple escalations through your support and account management teams.

SUMMARY:
Our customers have experienced [impact description] since [date]. Our team implemented an internal workaround to restore service, but we remain at risk of recurrence without a permanent fix.

TICKET REFERENCE: [vendor ticket ID]
INTERNAL PROBLEM RECORD: [your ID]
CONTRACTED SLA: [response and resolution commitments]
SLA STATUS: [which SLAs have been breached and by how much]

WHAT HAS HAPPENED TO DATE:
[3-4 bullet points: ticket filed date, escalation to AM date, responses received or not received]

WHAT WE NEED FROM THIS ESCALATION:
1. A named engineering owner assigned with authority to drive resolution
2. A root cause analysis with committed delivery date
3. A fix plan with committed delivery date
4. Weekly executive-level status calls until resolved

We value our relationship with [Vendor] and are committed to a collaborative resolution. However, this issue's duration and impact to our customers requires executive attention and commitment.

I am available for a call this week. Please have your team reach out to [contact].

[Your Director's Name]
Director of Operations, [Company]
```

## Section 5 — Tracking Vendor Commitments

### Weekly Tracking Routine

Every week that a vendor problem is open:

1. Check vendor ticket for update. Was an update posted since last week?
   - Yes: document update in your vendor problem record (what was said, date/time)
   - No: send status request to vendor ticket and to Account Manager

2. Is vendor RCA commitment date still on track?
   - Missed RCA date with no new commitment: open formal SLA breach claim

3. Is vendor fix commitment date still on track?
   - Slipping fix date: escalate to next level immediately; do not wait for the date to pass

4. Is your internal workaround still adequate?
   - Workaround degrading or incident frequency increasing: update risk assessment in vendor problem record; notify Director

### Formal SLA Breach Claim Process

When vendor misses a contracted SLA commitment (response OR resolution):

1. Document the breach in the vendor problem record:
   - SLA type breached
   - Contracted commitment
   - Actual performance
   - Impact during the breach window

2. Submit formal SLA breach notification to vendor:
   - Reference the contract clause
   - State the specific breach: "Your contracted [response/resolution] SLA for P[1/2] incidents is [X hours]. Ticket [ID] was opened at [timestamp]. You [did not respond within SLA / did not resolve within SLA]."
   - Request credit per contract terms
   - Copy Account Manager and your Finance/Legal team

3. Log the breach notification in your vendor problem record with date and recipient

4. Track credit claim: credit may not appear automatically — follow up with vendor Finance team

### KEDB Entry for Vendor Known Error

Create a KEDB entry for vendor known errors the same way you would for internal known errors, with two additions:

- **Fix Owner field**: "[Vendor Name] — Ticket [ID] — Expected fix: [date]"
- **Workaround feasibility**: note whether the workaround requires vendor assistance or is fully internal
- Mark the entry: "Vendor Known Error — Fix timeline dependent on [Vendor Name]"

This ensures incident responders know the fix is not in internal control and set expectations accordingly.

## Section 6 — Quarterly Business Review (QBR) Integration

### Compiling Vendor Problem History for QBR

Pull from your vendor problem records for each vendor being reviewed:

| Metric | Formula | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Vendor-caused incidents (quarter) | Count incidents where vendor is confirmed root cause | Break out by vendor if multiple |
| MTBI — Mean Time Between Incidents (vendor) | Total days in quarter / number of vendor incidents | Trending up = improving; down = worsening |
| Average vendor fix delivery time | Sum of days from ticket to fix verified / count of closed records | Exclude open records |
| SLA breaches | Count of breached response + resolution SLAs | Separate response from resolution |
| SLA credit claims filed | Count and dollar value | Demonstrates you are holding to contract |
| Workaround hours (operational cost) | Engineering hours spent on vendor workarounds | Quantifies the true cost of vendor problems |

### What to Present at QBR

Structure your vendor problem history section for QBR:

1. **Performance summary**: one slide or page per major vendor — incident count trend, MTBI trend, fix delivery time trend
2. **SLA compliance**: are they meeting their commitments? Track quarter over quarter.
3. **Operational cost**: how much engineer time went to workarounds for this vendor? This is a dollar cost.
4. **Open problem records**: what is still unresolved? What is the risk exposure?

### Leverage Points

Your documented problem history is contractual evidence. Use it for:

| Situation | Leverage |
|-----------|----------|
| Recurring vendor-caused incidents | "Our problem records show [X] incidents in 12 months. Our contract requires [Y]. We are requesting a service improvement plan with committed metrics." |
| Consistent SLA breaches | "We have [X] documented SLA breaches this year. We are exercising our contractual right to [credit / renegotiation / termination for cause clause] per Section [X] of our agreement." |
| Inadequate root cause quality | "Your RCA quality does not meet the standard in our agreement. We are requesting a process improvement commitment or an SLA adjustment for RCA delivery." |
| Fix delivery failures | "Of [X] committed fix dates, [Y] were missed. We cannot plan our operations around unreliable vendor fix timelines. We need contractual improvement commitments or alternative resolution terms." |

> **Director/VP framing**: vendor problem data is not just an operational record — it is a business asset. The Problem Manager who brings quantified vendor performance data to QBR is operating at a strategic level. The one who shows up without data is asking for a favor; the one with documented breach history is negotiating from evidence.
