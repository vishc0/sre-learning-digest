# Playbook — Board Briefing Preparation

## Purpose

Step-by-step preparation guide for a Director contributing to or presenting a platform reliability or security briefing at a board or executive leadership meeting. Timeline assumes a 4-week preparation window — the minimum for a well-prepared board briefing.

---

## 4 Weeks Out

**Objective:** Establish the narrative arc, identify the 3 key messages, begin data gathering.

### Step 1 — Clarify the Brief
Meet with your CTO or VP to confirm:
- What is the board asking for? (Routine quarterly update? Response to a specific concern? Investment request?)
- How many slides, how many minutes?
- What is the format — formal presentation, Q&A, or both?
- Are you presenting or feeding inputs to the CTO who will present?

**If you are feeding inputs:** Your job is to make your CTO look informed and in control. Your inputs should be pre-translated into board language. Do not give your CTO raw technical metrics to translate under board scrutiny.

**If you are presenting:** Confirm whether you are the primary presenter or a resource for technical Q&A. Know before you enter the room.

### Step 2 — Define 3 Key Messages
A board briefing should communicate exactly three things. Not four. Not six. Three things the board should remember after the presentation is over.

Example for a reliability briefing:
1. "Our reliability performance this quarter was above target — we delivered on our SLA commitments."
2. "We have identified a forward risk in [area] with a defined mitigation plan and a cost."
3. "We are requesting a $2.1M investment to address [specific gap] — here is what it buys."

Everything in your presentation should reinforce these three messages. Data that does not reinforce a key message goes in the appendix.

### Step 3 — Begin Data Gathering
Identify all data you will need:
- [ ] SLA performance data (current quarter vs. target, trend vs. prior quarters)
- [ ] Incident log (count, duration, business impact by incident)
- [ ] Investment vs. outcome data (what was spent this quarter, what improved)
- [ ] Benchmark data (industry reliability benchmarks, DORA metrics if relevant)
- [ ] Forward risk data (capacity projections, threat intelligence summary, technical debt inventory)
- [ ] Cost data for any investment requests

Assign data pulls to specific engineers or analysts with a due date: **end of week 3**.

## 2 Weeks Out

**Objective:** Complete slide draft, conduct peer review, finalize risk quantification.

### Step 4 — Draft Slides
Use the template in `templates/board-reliability-briefing-template.md` as the starting point.

Apply the board test to every slide (Chapter 6):
- Does this slide have a headline that states the conclusion (not just the topic)?
- Can a board member extract the key message in 30 seconds?
- Is every metric labeled with its target?
- Are technical terms translated?

**Slide count discipline:** If you have more than 3 slides (plus appendix), cut. Ask: does removing this slide cause a board member to miss something material? If the answer is no, the slide goes to the appendix.

### Step 5 — Peer Review
Share the draft with one Director peer and one person outside your function (a product manager or finance partner works well). Ask them specifically:
- "Is there anything in here you do not understand?"
- "After reading this, what would you say the three key messages are?" (They should match your Step 2 answers.)
- "What question would you ask if you were a board member?"

Revise based on this feedback.

### Step 6 — Finalize Risk Quantification
Every forward risk in your presentation must have a business-impact number. Use `reference/business-risk-quantification-reference.md`.

If a risk cannot be quantified: state the qualitative exposure and the data gap explicitly. "We do not have precise failure-rate data on this component — we are implementing instrumentation in Q3. The potential impact if this component fails is [X] based on traffic dependency analysis."

Never present an unquantified risk without acknowledging the gap. A board member who notices you presented a risk without quantification and asks about it will assume you did not do the analysis.

---

## 1 Week Out

**Objective:** VP review, first dry run, final content lock.

### Step 7 — VP Review and Alignment
This is not a check-in. This is a working session where your VP reads every slide and gives feedback. Allocate 60-90 minutes.

Questions to cover:
- "Does this match what you want the board to hear from this domain?"
- "Is there anything the board members have been asking about that I have not addressed?"
- "Is the investment ask positioned the way you want to position it?"
- "Are there any board members with strong views on reliability spending that I should anticipate?"

Incorporate VP feedback before proceeding to dry run.

### Step 8 — Dry Run #1
Present the full briefing out loud, timed, to an audience that will challenge you. Options: your leadership team, a peer Director, or a mentor.

**What to test:**
- [ ] Total time within your allotted window (if 15 minutes, you should be at 12 minutes — boards always run over on Q&A)
- [ ] You can state each key message in one sentence without looking at notes
- [ ] You can answer the top 10 board questions (see below) without hesitation
- [ ] You know which slides are most likely to trigger questions and have backup data ready

---

## 2 Days Out

**Objective:** Final version locked, dry run #2 with C-suite proxy.

### Step 9 — Final Deck Lock
No content changes after this point except corrections of factual errors. Version lock prevents the "one more change" pattern that leaves presenters with an unfamiliar final deck.

### Step 10 — Dry Run #2 with C-suite Proxy
Ideal: practice with your CTO or an executive who has board experience. If not available: use a finance partner or Chief of Staff who attends board meetings.

This dry run should be adversarial. Ask your proxy to challenge every number, question every risk assessment, and ask the hard questions board members actually ask. The goal is to have no surprises in the actual meeting.

---

## Day Of

### Step 11 — Pre-Brief with CEO/CTO
15-30 minutes before the board session. Confirm:
- Any context from earlier board conversation today that affects your portion
- Any board member who has expressed concern about your domain that you should be prepared to address
- Whether the agenda has shifted (timing changes happen in board meetings)

### Step 12 — What to Bring
- Printed one-page executive summary (in case projector fails)
- One-page backup data sheet for each key claim (NOT to present — to reference if challenged)
- Your contact information on a card or the shared channel where questions can be sent after the meeting

### Step 13 — In the Room
- State each key message explicitly — do not let the board infer it from slides
- When a board member asks a question: pause, repeat the question back to confirm you understood it, then answer
- If you do not know the answer: "I do not have that data in front of me. I will follow up in writing before end of day." Do not speculate.
- Keep answers to board questions to 2-3 sentences unless asked for more detail

## Slide-by-Slide Guide — 15-Minute Reliability Briefing

**Slide 1 — Executive Summary (3 minutes)**
Headline: one sentence summary of quarterly reliability posture. RAG status visible. Three bullets: performance vs. target, major events, key forward risk. This slide must stand alone — if a board member misses everything else, this slide tells them what they need to know.

**Slide 2 — Risk and Investment (6 minutes)**
Split into two sections. Top half: the forward risk (what could affect reliability next quarter, quantified). Bottom half: the investment request (specific, with risk-of-inaction). This is where you spend the most time because it is where the decision lives.

**Slide 3 — Supporting Data (3 minutes)**
One reliability scorecard table: service | target | actual | trend | status. Brief incident summary table if there were material events. Reference that full data is in the appendix.

**Reserve 3 minutes for Q&A.**

## Top 10 Board Questions on Platform Reliability

Prepare a 2-sentence answer for each:

1. "What is our current uptime and how does it compare to our SLA commitments?"
2. "How do we compare to industry benchmarks?"
3. "What is our biggest reliability risk entering next quarter?"
4. "What was the business impact of [specific recent incident the board knows about]?"
5. "What are we doing to prevent a repeat of that incident?"
6. "How much of our infrastructure is single-point-of-failure?"
7. "What would a major outage cost us in revenue and reputation?"
8. "Is our reliability getting better or worse as we scale?"
9. "What does [the investment you're requesting] actually buy us?"
10. "If we do not make this investment, what is the risk?" (risk-of-inaction)
