# Playbook — Executive Situation Report (Sev1 Sitrep)

## Purpose

This playbook governs the writing and delivery of executive situation reports during a Sev1 incident. It is an operational checklist — reference it during execution, not after.

---

## Trigger: When to Activate

Activate this playbook when:
- A **Sev1 is officially declared** by the incident commander
- A Sev2 with **customer-facing revenue impact** is declared
- Any incident that **generates media or customer social escalation**
- A Sev2 that **engineering leadership predicts will escalate to Sev1**

Do NOT wait for explicit instruction from your VP to activate. Activation is your responsibility as the on-call Director. If your VP has to ask you why they have not received a sitrep, you are already late.

---

## Who Writes It

**Primary:** On-call Director or Director of the affected service area.

**Delegate trigger:** If the on-call Director is also the incident commander (IC), they must delegate sitrep responsibility to another Director, the Engineering Manager on the incident, or a designated communications lead. The IC cannot both command the incident and write executive communications without degrading both.

**If no Director is available:** VP of Engineering steps in. This should never happen twice before your escalation matrix is revised.

---

## Distribution List

Executive sitreps go to a **narrow, senior distribution**. Sending to the whole engineering org creates noise and leaks incident details before they are confirmed.

**Standard distribution:**
- VP of Engineering
- CTO
- CEO (for Sev1 with material revenue or customer impact)
- CISO (if the incident has a security vector)
- Head of Customer Success (if enterprise customer accounts are affected)
- Legal (if the incident may trigger regulatory notification obligations)

**Do NOT include:** your engineering team, all-hands distribution lists, product managers, general #incidents Slack channels. The technical bridge handles that. Sitreps are for executives.

---

## Step 1 — First Sitrep (Within 15 Minutes of Sev1 Declaration)

**Timer starts:** when the IC declares Sev1, not when you learn about it. If your pager fires at 14:25 and Sev1 was declared at 14:20, your first sitrep is due at 14:35.

**Format — five fields, nothing more:**

```
SITREP #1 — [Service Name] — [Date] [Time] [Timezone]

IMPACT:    [Customer/business impact in plain language. Scale: how many users, 
           what functions, estimated revenue impact if known. "Unknown" is 
           acceptable for revenue; "investigating" is not acceptable for 
           customer scope — make your best estimate.]

STATUS:    Active Incident | Monitoring | Resolved
           Sev[1/2] declared [time] by [IC name]

ACTIONS:   [What is the response team doing right now. Two sentences maximum.
           Do not list diagnostic hypotheses. List actions being taken.]

ETA NEXT:  [Exact time of next update, or "on status change"]

OWNER:     IC: [Name, contact] | Exec contact: [Your name, your contact]
```

> **NEVER SAY IN SITREP #1:**
> - "We're still investigating" without a next-update time
> - "It might be [X]" (diagnostic hypotheses belong on the tech bridge)
> - "We don't know yet" as a complete statement
> - "We think we have it under control"
> - Any technical term without a plain-language translation

---

## Step 2 — Confirm Delivery

After sending, confirm receipt from your VP within 5 minutes. If no confirmation: call their mobile. Do not assume email delivery in a Sev1.

Send a Slack message to the VP simultaneously with the email: "Sitrep #1 sent to exec distribution. [One-line summary of impact]."

---

## Step 3 — Subsequent Updates (Every 30 Minutes or on Status Change)

Use the same format. Add the DELTA field at the top:

```
SITREP #[N] — [Service Name] — [Date] [Time] [Timezone]

DELTA:     [What changed since the last sitrep. One sentence. 
           If nothing changed: "No change in status or impact — 
           investigation continuing."]

IMPACT:    [Updated. Refine the estimate as information improves.]
STATUS:    [Updated]
ACTIONS:   [Updated — what is the team doing right now]
ETA NEXT:  [Updated]
OWNER:     [Updated if IC changed]
```

**Cadence discipline:** Set a calendar alert for each 30-minute update. When the timer fires, send the update — even if it says "no change." Missing a scheduled update is the same as going dark, and executives will call.

---

## Step 4 — When You Do Not Have Answers

This is the most common failure mode. Directors go silent because they do not have confirmed information. This is wrong.

**When you do not have the root cause:** "Root cause under investigation. Engineering team has isolated the fault domain to [general area]. Candidate hypotheses are being tested. Confirmed finding by [time]."

**When you cannot estimate impact:** "Customer impact scope is still being quantified. Current observable signals: [brief description of what you can see]. Impact estimate will be in the next sitrep."

**When the ETA to resolution is unknown:** "Resolution timeline is not yet established. The team has [X] being investigated, and we expect to have a resolution path or escalation decision by [time]."

> **RULE:** An honest "I don't have this yet, but I will at [specific time]" is always better than silence. Silence implies chaos. A time-bounded uncertainty implies managed progress.

---

## Step 5 — All-Clear Communication

When the incident is resolved and service is confirmed restored:

```
ALL CLEAR — [Service Name] — [Date] [Time] [Timezone]

RESOLVED:      [Time service confirmed restored]
DURATION:      [Sev1 declaration time to resolution time]
IMPACT:        [Final impact statement — customers affected, revenue impact, 
               SLA breach status]
ROOT CAUSE:    [One sentence in business terms]
IMMEDIATE FIX: [What was done to restore service]
MONITORING:    [What you are watching to confirm sustained recovery]
FOLLOW-ON:     [PIR date, corrective actions planned, timeline for completion]
OWNER:         [Director responsible for post-incident work]
```

---

## Step 6 — Post-Incident Executive Summary

Send within **24-48 hours** of all-clear for any Sev1 or significant Sev2.

Structure:
1. What happened (impact-first)
2. Root cause (business terms)
3. What we did (timeline of key actions)
4. What we learned (non-obvious insights, not just "we need more monitoring")
5. What changes (specific, committed, time-bound corrective actions)

This is the artifact that builds long-term trust. Write it carefully.

---

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Correction |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| First sitrep after 30 minutes | Executives fill the gap by calling engineers | Set a 10-minute reminder on Sev1 declaration |
| Sending diagnostic hypotheses | Executives make decisions on unconfirmed information | Only share confirmed facts and actions |
| Vague impact ("some customers affected") | Executives cannot assess severity or brief their stakeholders | Always give a number, even a rough estimate with a label |
| Missing a 30-minute update | Reads as loss of control | Set calendar alerts; send "no change" if nothing has changed |
| All-clear without post-incident summary | Executives have no record of resolution or improvement | Post-incident summary is mandatory for all Sev1 events |
| Copying the all-hands list | Leaks unconfirmed details; creates noise | Maintain a separate exec distribution list |
