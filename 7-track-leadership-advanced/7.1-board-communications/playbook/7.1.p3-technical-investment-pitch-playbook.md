# Playbook — Technical Investment Pitch

## Purpose

Step-by-step guide for building and presenting an engineering investment proposal to a VP, CTO, or executive leadership team. Follow these steps in order. Do not skip to the slide deck before completing Steps 1-4.

---

## Step 1 — Quantify the Problem (Week 1)

Before any slides, before any ask, you need numbers. An investment proposal without quantified current-state costs will not survive CFO review.

**Data to gather:**

| Metric | Source | What It Proves |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Current reliability rate (SLA actual vs. target) | Monitoring/observability platform | Scale of the gap |
| Incident frequency and duration (12-month trailing) | Incident management tool (PagerDuty, etc.) | Pattern, not exception |
| Revenue impact per incident | Finance / revenue ops | Translates engineer-hours to dollars |
| Customer contacts per incident | Support platform | Operational cost signal |
| Engineering toil hours per month on this problem | Time tracking / team estimate | Opportunity cost |
| SLA penalty exposure | Contracts / legal | Hard financial obligation |
| Regulatory fine exposure | Legal / compliance | Non-optional risk |

**Checkpoint:** If you cannot quantify the problem with at least 3 of the above data points, your proposal is not ready to build. Get the data first.

**Common shortcut failure:** Estimating numbers you should have from your systems. If you say "we think we have about 8-10 incidents per year" in a CFO review, the CFO will ask for the actual data. Know the actual number: 11 in 2025, 9 in 2024, 7 in 2023. Show the trend.

---

## Step 2 — Define Options (Week 1-2)

Always present at least three options. Never present one option — that forces a yes/no and removes executive agency.

**Option structure:**

| Option | Description | One-Time Cost | Annual Run Cost | Risk Reduction | Recommendation |
|--------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Do Nothing | Status quo | $0 | $0 | 0% | Not recommended |
| Minimum Viable | [Process/tooling fixes without architecture change] | $X | $Y | ~Z% | Considered |
| Full Investment | [The complete solution] | $X | $Y | ~Z% | **Recommended** |

**On "Do Nothing":** Always include it. It establishes the baseline and makes the risk-of-inaction argument explicit. A CFO who sees "Do Nothing" as a row in a table is being shown: this is what we are currently choosing by not acting. That is more powerful than a narrative argument.

**On the middle option:** Design it as a real option, not a strawman. If the middle option is clearly inferior to the full investment on all dimensions, sophisticated executives will notice and trust your analysis less. The middle option should have genuine merit in specific circumstances (e.g., if budget is severely constrained this year).

---

## Step 3 — Risk-of-Inaction Framing (Week 2)

This is the CFO's language. ROI asks: "What do we gain?" Risk-of-inaction asks: "What are we already losing?"

**Build the risk-of-inaction case:**

1. **Current cost:** "This problem costs us $X per year today." (From Step 1)
2. **Trend:** "This cost is increasing/stable/accelerating because..."
3. **Cliff risk:** "If this problem hits a critical threshold (regulatory trigger, major customer incident, competitor breach), the cost becomes non-linear: [$Y to $Z]."
4. **Compounding:** "Every quarter we delay, we add $X in accumulated debt that the eventual solution must also address."

**Example statement:** "At current incident rates, the notification platform reliability gap is costing us $9M annually in lost promotional revenue, support cost, and MTTR-related outage losses. This cost will increase as subscriber volume grows — we project $11M by Q4 2027 at current growth rates. Additionally, we are within 18 months of an FCC-reportable reliability event based on current trajectory. Regulatory response to such an event carries fine exposure of $100K-$1M and mandatory remediation with FCC oversight, which would take engineering resources away from growth-enabling work at a critical scaling phase."

---

## Step 4 — ROI Calculation (Week 2)

Even when benefits are hard to quantify, you must provide a return calculation. Use the framework from `reference/business-risk-quantification-reference.md`.

**When benefits are hard to quantify:**
- Use conservative estimates and label them: "Conservative estimate, 25th percentile case"
- Cite comparable industry data: "IBM Cost of Data Breach Report 2025 estimates..."
- Use sensitivity analysis: "At 50% realization of estimated benefits, payback is 14 months. At 25% realization, payback is 28 months."
- Never present a benefit number without a confidence label

**ROI table:**

| Benefit Category | Annual Value | Confidence | Basis |
|-----------------|-------------|------------|-------|
| Recovered promotional revenue | $4.6M | Medium | A/B data on delivery rate vs. engagement |
| Reduced MTTR outage losses | $1.5M | Medium | 12-month incident data × revenue per hour |
| Support cost reduction | $1.15M | High | Current contact volume × cost per contact |
| Regulatory fine avoidance | $0.5M (expected value) | Low | FCC fine history × incident probability |
| **Total annual benefit** | **$7.75M** | | |
| **Investment (Year 1)** | **$2.1M** | | |
| **Payback period** | **< 4 months** | | |

## Step 5 — The Ask (Week 3)

Be specific. Vague asks do not get approved. Vague asks get scheduled for "the next planning cycle."

**The ask must include:**
- Headcount: number of engineers, role type, whether new hire or reallocation
- Budget: total dollar amount, split between CapEx and OpEx, by quarter
- Timeline: start date, major milestones, expected completion
- Decision date: when you need the decision to hit your start target
- Dependencies: what else needs to happen for this to proceed (hiring approval, vendor contract, architecture sign-off)

**Example ask:**
"I am requesting approval for a $2.1M investment in the notification platform reliability program:
- 2 SRE headcount additions, starting by Q3 2026: $600K (fully loaded, 18 months)
- Observability tooling upgrade: $270K (18-month contract)
- Infrastructure redundancy build-out: $1.23M (FY2026 CapEx, 60% in H1, 40% in H2)

I need a go/no-go decision by August 1 to initiate headcount requisitions in time for Q3 hiring. Delay beyond August 1 pushes delivery of reliability improvements to Q2 2027 and extends our regulatory exposure window by one additional quarter."

## CFO Objection Handling

**"We can't afford this right now."**
Response: "I understand the budget pressure. Let me show you what we can accomplish with a phased approach — starting with the automation investment at $270K in Q3, which reduces our on-call engineering cost enough to partially self-fund the next phase. Do you want me to model the phased option?"

**"What if we just fix the process instead of the infrastructure?"**
Response: "We have been running process improvements for 8 months — our runbook completion rate is now 94%. The remaining MTTR and delivery rate gap is structural: the platform architecture has single points of failure that process cannot address. I can show you the incident breakdown by root cause type — the process-addressable incidents are already under control."

**"Can this wait until next year?"**
Response: "It can. Here is what 12 months of delay costs: $9M in continued current-state losses, plus the regulatory trajectory moves us within 6 months of an FCC-reportable threshold rather than 18 months. I can model a delay scenario if that would help the decision."

**"Your ROI numbers seem optimistic."**
Response: "Fair challenge. The table includes confidence labels. If we take the conservative 25th-percentile case — cutting benefit estimates by 50% — payback extends to 8 months and annual return is $3.9M on a $2.1M investment. Even at that reduced estimate, the investment case holds. Do you want me to walk through the assumptions on any specific line?"

## Dry Run Checklist (Before VP or Board Presentation)

**Content:**
- [ ] Every number in the proposal has a documented source I can reference
- [ ] Three options presented, not one
- [ ] Risk-of-inaction is quantified, not just described
- [ ] The ask is specific: headcount, budget, timeline, decision date
- [ ] I can defend every assumption if challenged

**Presentation:**
- [ ] Dry run completed with a peer who will ask hard questions
- [ ] VP pre-briefed — they know the content before the meeting
- [ ] I know the CFO's/VP's primary concern and have prepared for it
- [ ] I can summarize the entire proposal in 90 seconds for the elevator version
- [ ] Backup data slide prepared for each key claim

**Logistics:**
- [ ] Slides distributed 24 hours in advance if required
- [ ] Decision-by date confirmed with VP
- [ ] Escalation path clear if VP approval is not sufficient
