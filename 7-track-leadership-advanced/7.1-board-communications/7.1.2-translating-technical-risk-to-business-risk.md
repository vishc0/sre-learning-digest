# Chapter 2 — Translating Technical Risk to Business Risk

## The Core Skill

Everything in Director-level communication rests on one discipline: the ability to take a technical statement and convert it into a business risk statement that an executive can act on. This is not dumbing things down. It is a precision skill that requires you to understand both the technical reality and the business context deeply enough to draw the correct causal chain between them.

The failure mode is almost always the same: technical leaders present technical facts and expect executives to draw the business implications themselves. Executives do not have the context to do this accurately, and even when they can, making them do your analytical work is a communication failure. Your job is to complete the chain.

## The "So What" Test

Every technical statement you deliver to an executive must pass the "so what" test. After stating the technical fact, ask yourself: so what? Why does this matter to someone who does not care about the underlying systems? If the answer is not immediately obvious from your statement, you have not finished the translation.

Apply it recursively:

- "Our p99 latency increased to 800ms." So what?
- "That means 1% of our transactions take 0.8 seconds." So what?
- "Our SLA target is 200ms. We are 4x over SLA." So what?
- "Our A/B data shows that response times above 400ms correlate with 12% cart abandonment." So what?
- "At current transaction volume, 12% cart abandonment on 1% of transactions represents approximately $47,000 in lost revenue per day."

That is a complete translation. The executive now understands the technical event, its SLA implication, and its financial consequence — without needing to understand what p99 means.

## Worked Examples

### Latency

**Engineer version:** "Our p99 latency is 800ms, up from 200ms baseline."

**Executive version:** "Our slowest 1% of transactions now take 0.8 seconds — four times our SLA target of 200ms. Our A/B data links response times above 400ms to a 12% increase in cart abandonment. At current volume, this latency degradation is costing us approximately $47,000 per day in abandoned transactions. Mitigation is underway; estimated resolution in 6 hours."

### Error Rate

**Engineer version:** "We are seeing a 2.3% error rate on the checkout service."

**Executive version:** "2.3% of checkout attempts are failing. At our current volume of 40,000 daily checkouts, that is approximately 920 failed transactions. At an average order value of $85, we are losing roughly $78,000 in daily revenue. Additionally, failed checkouts that result in customer contacts cost us an estimated $12 per incident in support labor — adding another $11,000 in operational cost. Root cause has been identified and a fix is deploying now."

### Delivery Rate

**Engineer version:** "Push notification delivery rate dropped from 99.9% to 99.5%."

**Executive version:** "Our push notification delivery rate fell 0.4 percentage points this week. On a subscriber base of 8 million, that means 32,000 subscribers did not receive notifications they expected. For our promotional notifications, we estimate each undelivered message represents $0.40 in lost engagement value — approximately $12,800 in lost promotional impact per affected campaign. If this degradation persists across our weekly notification cycle, the cumulative impact exceeds $90,000. We are also monitoring for subscriber churn signals, as repeated missed notifications are a known churn driver. Fix is deployed; delivery rate restored as of 14:00."

### Infrastructure Aging

**Engineer version:** "We are running on EOL Kubernetes 1.21, which has 14 unpatched CVEs."

**Executive version:** "Our container platform is running on a version that the vendor stopped supporting 8 months ago. It has 14 known security vulnerabilities with no patches available. Three of those vulnerabilities are rated 'critical' — meaning exploitation could give an attacker control of our platform infrastructure. A breach through one of these vulnerabilities would trigger mandatory customer notification under CCPA, expose us to regulatory fines of up to $7,500 per affected record, and likely generate negative press coverage. The upgrade path is 6 weeks of engineering work. We are recommending this as a Q3 priority."

## The Business Risk Translation Framework

Use this four-step framework to convert any technical statement before presenting it upward:

### Step 1 — State the Technical Fact
What happened or is happening? Be precise enough that you can defend the numbers. This is the input — not the output you deliver.

### Step 2 — Map to Business Domain
Which business function does this affect? Revenue, customer experience, compliance, competitive velocity, operational cost, security posture? If you cannot map it to a business domain, it is not board-level information.

### Step 3 — Quantify the Impact
What is the measurable business consequence? Use the formulas in the `reference/business-risk-quantification-reference.md` file. Order of preference: revenue impact > customer impact (in numbers of customers) > operational cost > regulatory exposure. If you cannot quantify with confidence, give a range and label it: "we estimate $X to $Y based on..."

### Step 4 — State the Response
What is being done, by whom, and by when? An executive who receives a risk statement without a response plan has no option but to escalate or intervene. Give them the response plan so they can choose to let it run.

## Reliability Risk Framing

Reliability risks are the most common technical risks Directors translate. The language that lands with executives is:

**Outage cost:** "Each minute this service is down costs the business approximately $X, based on $Y in hourly revenue processed through this path."

**Customer churn:** "We estimate that subscribers who experience more than two service disruptions per quarter churn at 2x the baseline rate. This incident affected N customers — at our average 18-month LTV of $X, if churn rate on affected customers increases by 1%, that represents $Y in lost lifetime revenue."

**SLA penalties:** "We have SLA commitments with 3 enterprise customers that carry penalty clauses. This outage duration triggers penalties on 2 of those contracts, totaling $X."

**Regulatory exposure:** "As a licensed telecommunications provider, persistent delivery failures exceeding the FCC's reliability thresholds expose us to formal inquiries and potential fines. Our current incident rate is approaching the threshold that triggers mandatory FCC reporting."

## What This Is Not

Translation is not spin. You are not looking for language that makes a bad situation sound acceptable. You are looking for language that gives an executive the accurate business context to make an informed decision. If the situation is bad, the translation should reflect that — in business terms. Honest translation of a serious risk builds executive trust. Spin that is later discovered destroys it permanently.

The discipline is: same facts, right language, complete causal chain, honest framing.
