# Chapter 3 — The Executive Status Update

## Why the Format Matters More Than You Think

Your VP reads their email at 6am before their first call. They have 90 seconds per message before deciding whether it needs immediate attention, can wait, or can be deleted. If your status update requires them to read three paragraphs before understanding whether anything is wrong, you have failed the communication — regardless of whether the content was accurate.

This is not about brevity for its own sake. It is about engineering your communication for the actual consumption context. An ICS 201 form is not short because brevity is a virtue — it is short and structured because incident command requires that any incoming responder can absorb the situation in two minutes and be effective. Your executive status update has the same design constraint: the reader must be able to triage it in 90 seconds and know exactly whether they need to act.

## The Inverted Pyramid Structure

Journalists invented the inverted pyramid for exactly this reason: readers stop reading at any point, so put the most important information first and work toward the least important. The same logic applies to executive communication.

```
[Most Important: What is the headline? What is the status?]
         ↓
[Important: What are the top risks?]
         ↓
[Useful: What actions are needed from executives?]
         ↓
[Background: What is upcoming? What is the supporting detail?]
```

The anti-pattern — writing chronologically or building to a conclusion — guarantees that busy executives miss the key signal. Never bury the lead.

## The RAG Status System

Red/Amber/Green status is the fastest triage signal in an executive update. It works because it requires no reading — a VP scanning their inbox at a glance knows before they open your update whether it is an emergency or routine.

**GREEN:** Everything is operating within normal parameters. No executive action needed. Update is informational.

**AMBER:** Something is elevated — a risk materializing, a metric trending in the wrong direction, a deadline at risk, a dependency that is not yet resolved. No immediate action needed from the executive, but they should be aware. This may escalate to RED if conditions worsen.

**RED:** Something requires executive attention now. This may mean a decision, an escalation, an approval, or simply executive awareness of an event with material business impact. RED should never appear for the first time in a status update — executives should already know about a RED situation through real-time communication before it appears in writing.

A common Director mistake: using AMBER too liberally until it loses meaning. If everything is always amber, executives stop differentiating. Reserve AMBER for situations you are actively watching and managing. Reserve GREEN for situations where you have nothing material to report.

## One-Page Maximum — Non-Negotiable

Executive status updates are one page. Not one page for important things and two pages when things are complex. One page. Always.

If your situation is complex enough to require more than one page, you have two options: (1) schedule a synchronous conversation, or (2) write a one-page summary with a link to a detailed appendix that the executive can read if they choose to. The one-page summary is the primary deliverable. The appendix is optional reading.

The discipline of one-page writing forces you to prioritize. If you cannot choose what your top three risks are, you are not exercising Director-level judgment. Choosing is the job.

## The Standard Structure

**Header block:**
- Date and distribution list
- RAG status (visible at the top — do not make them hunt for it)
- Author and coverage period

**Headline (one sentence):**
The single most important thing the executive needs to know. If they read nothing else, what would you need them to know? Write that sentence. It is harder than it sounds.

**Status by area:**
Three to five areas, each with its own RAG and a single sentence of context. Do not write paragraphs. Write one sentence per area. If an area is green with nothing notable, say "GREEN — no issues" and move on.

**Top 3 risks:**
Use a table: Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation | Owner. Three rows maximum. If you have more than three material risks, you have a staffing conversation to have, not a longer table to write.

**Actions required:**
This is the most important structural element that most Directors omit. If you need the executive to do something — approve a budget, make a call, unblock a dependency — say so explicitly with a deadline. Executives are not mind-readers. If you do not explicitly ask, you will not receive.

**Upcoming:**
Two or three bullets on what is coming in the next two weeks that the executive should be aware of. Not a project plan. Signals they need so they are not surprised.

## Anti-Pattern: The Status Novel

The status novel is a three-to-five page email that covers everything the Director's team worked on that week, organized chronologically, with technical context included for each item. It takes 20 minutes to write and 30 seconds for the executive to decide they will read it later — which means never.

The status novel is usually written from anxiety: the Director wants to demonstrate that their team is busy and productive. The executive audience does not need to know everything that happened; they need to know what requires their attention. These are very different documents.

The cure for the status novel is the "so what" test applied to every paragraph. If a paragraph does not change what the executive should know, think, or do — cut it.

## The Analogy to ICS 201

In incident command, an ICS 201 (Incident Briefing Form) is the structured artifact that every incoming Section Chief or Operations Section Chief receives on arrival. It covers: incident name, date/time, map, current objectives, current organization, resources assigned, and current actions. It is designed to get an incoming leader operational in two minutes, regardless of the complexity of the incident.

Your executive status update is the ICS 201 of your domain. It should make your VP fully situationally aware in 90 seconds. Incident name = your domain. Map = the area overview (status by area). Current objectives = what you are trying to accomplish this period. Resources = your team's focus. Current actions = the top 3 risks and their mitigations. Incoming request = actions required.

If you would not write an ICS 201 that way, do not write an executive status update that way.

## Delivery and Cadence

Weekly is the standard cadence for executive status updates at the Director level. Send it at the same time every week — Friday afternoon or Monday morning are both acceptable depending on your organization's rhythm. Consistency matters: an executive who receives your update at the same time every week will start to notice when it does not arrive.

For format: email is standard, with a direct-paste of the update in the body — never an attachment. Executives will not open attachments from routine emails. The update goes in the body. If your organization uses a wiki or dashboard, the email links to it — but the triage-level content lives in the email body.
