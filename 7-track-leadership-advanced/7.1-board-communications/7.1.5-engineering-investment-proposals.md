# Chapter 5 — Engineering Investment Proposals

## Why ROI Is Not Enough

Return on investment framing works well when the benefit is direct and measurable: invest $X, earn $Y. Infrastructure reliability investments are structurally different. The primary benefit is often the avoidance of a cost that has not happened yet. ROI framing for "preventing a future outage" requires the executive to mentally model a probability-weighted future event and compare it to a certain present expenditure — this is cognitively difficult and emotionally unsatisfying.

Risk-of-inaction framing is more powerful for infrastructure investments because it anchors to a concrete, existing problem: what is this situation already costing you? What will it cost you if left unaddressed? The executive is comparing present cost to future investment, which is easier to evaluate and more emotionally urgent. A CFO who asks "why should we invest?" will resist. A CFO who is shown "you are losing $X per month today, and that loss will compound" has a different conversation.

Use both — ROI for the upside, risk-of-inaction for the cost of not acting. The risk-of-inaction framing typically lands harder.

## Competitive Framing

After risk-of-inaction, competitive framing is the second most effective lever for infrastructure investment. The question is not "should we invest?" but "can we compete without this?" If your deployment pipeline is 3x slower than your competitors' because of accumulated technical debt, that is a velocity disadvantage that affects time-to-market on every feature. If your reliability posture is 99.5% when the market standard is 99.9%, customers who have options will choose competitors.

Competitive framing works best when you can cite either (a) publicly disclosed competitor metrics, or (b) analyst benchmarks (e.g., DORA metrics for deployment frequency, Gartner reliability benchmarks). Avoid framing that requires you to state what competitors do without evidence — this invites pushback. Use industry-standard benchmarks as your comparison point.

## Worked Example: Notification Platform Reliability Investment

**Background:** A telecom-scale push notification platform currently delivers at 99.5% with a 30-minute MTTR. Engineering has proposed investing to achieve 99.9% delivery rate and 5-minute MTTR. Total investment: $2.1M (headcount, tooling, infrastructure over 18 months).

### Step 1 — Quantify the Current State Cost

**Delivery failure cost:**
- Current delivery rate: 99.5% (0.5% failure rate)
- Subscriber base: 8 million
- Failed notifications per day: 40,000 (on a typical notification day of 8M × 0.5%)
- Undelivered promotional notifications: estimated $0.40 in lost engagement value per missed push
- Daily promotional impact cost: $16,000
- Annual promotional impact cost: ~$5.8M

**MTTR cost:**
- Average Sev1/Sev2 incidents per year: 12 (based on last 3 years)
- Average outage duration at 30-min MTTR: 45 minutes (30-minute restore + tail investigation)
- Revenue processed through notification-triggered actions per hour: $800,000
- Revenue at risk per incident: $600,000 (45-minute × $800K/hour)
- Annual revenue at risk from outage events: $7.2M
- Actual experienced loss (not all incidents affect revenue directly): estimated $1.8M/year at 25% realization rate

**Support cost:**
- Customer contacts per significant notification failure event: ~15,000
- Cost per contact (front-line support): $8
- Support cost per event: $120,000
- Annual support cost from reliability events: $1.44M

**Total annual cost of current state:** approximately $9M

### Step 2 — Project Target State

At 99.9% delivery rate and 5-minute MTTR:
- Delivery failure reduction: 80% (0.5% → 0.1%)
- Promotional impact recovery: $4.6M/year
- MTTR reduction: 83% (30 min → 5 min), incident revenue loss reduced from $1.8M to ~$300K
- Support contact reduction: 80%, saving $1.15M/year
- Total annual benefit: approximately $6M/year

### Step 3 — Regulatory Risk Framing

The notification platform delivers emergency alerts, billing notifications, and legally mandated service communications under FCC rules (47 CFR Part 4). Persistent delivery failures above FCC thresholds trigger mandatory outage reporting. A formal FCC inquiry can result in:
- Fines of $100,000 to $1,000,000 per violation day
- Mandatory remediation plans with FCC oversight
- Reputational damage during any merger, acquisition, or spectrum licensing activity

Current 99.5% delivery rate is approaching the threshold that requires formal reporting. At 99.9%, we are well within compliance bounds with headroom for growth.

### Step 4 — The Ask

**Investment:** $2.1M over 18 months
- 2 additional SRE headcount: $600K (fully loaded, 18 months)
- Observability tooling upgrade: $180K/year → $270K for 18 months
- Infrastructure redundancy build-out: $1.23M (one-time CapEx)

**Expected annual return:** $6M in recovered revenue, reduced support costs, and avoided regulatory exposure

**Payback period:** < 6 months at full realization

**Decision needed by:** Q3 planning cycle close (2026-08-01) to hit 2027 delivery target

**Alternative considered:** Minimal fix (process improvements only, no infrastructure investment) — reduces MTTR to 15 minutes, does not address delivery rate. Saves $1.4M upfront, leaves $4.6M/year on the table and full regulatory exposure intact.

**Risk of inaction:** At current trajectory, we will have an FCC-reportable event within 18 months. A single FCC fine at the lower end ($100K) combined with one lost enterprise contract due to reliability SLA breach ($500K) erases 30% of the investment savings from the "do nothing" option.

## Investment Proposal Structure (Summary)

1. **Problem** — what is happening today, quantified in business terms
2. **Options** — at minimum three: do nothing, minimal fix, full investment
3. **Recommendation** — your specific recommendation with rationale
4. **ROI / Risk-of-Inaction** — numbers first, narrative second
5. **The Ask** — specific: headcount, budget, timeline, decision date

Never present a proposal without options. Presenting a single option forces a binary yes/no decision and removes executive agency. Three options — with the middle option often being your actual recommendation — gives executives the choice architecture they prefer and positions your recommendation as the reasonable center.
