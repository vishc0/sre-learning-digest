# Reference — Communication Anti-Patterns

Twelve communication anti-patterns that derail Director careers. For each: what it is, what it looks like in practice, why it fails, and the corrected version.

---

## Anti-Pattern 1 — Jargon Flooding

**What it is:** Using technical terminology in executive communication without translation, assuming the audience shares your technical context.

**Example in action:**
"We're seeing elevated p99 latency on the API gateway due to a connection pool exhaustion in the Redis cluster, which is causing upstream timeout cascades in the BFF layer."

**Why it fails:**
The executive cannot assess severity, make a decision, or brief their own stakeholders. They either ignore it (if they do not ask for clarification) or feel excluded from their own organization's decision-making. Both outcomes erode trust in the Director.

**Corrected version:**
"Our platform is responding slowly for approximately 1% of customers right now due to a database configuration issue. We estimate this is causing $12,000/hour in abandoned transactions. Engineering is on it — estimated fix in 90 minutes."

---

## Anti-Pattern 2 — Burying the Lead

**What it is:** Writing chronologically or building to a conclusion, so the most important information appears in paragraph 3 or later.

**Example in action:**
"This week the team completed the Q2 security audit, finalized the capacity planning model for H2, and deployed the observability tooling upgrade. We also had a payment service incident on Thursday that affected 18,000 customers and generated $1.2M in abandoned transactions."

**Why it fails:**
The executive who reads the first sentence decides this is a normal update. They may never reach paragraph 3. The critical information — a $1.2M incident — is buried after routine updates.

**Corrected version:**
"**Critical: Payment service incident Thursday affected 18,000 customers and generated an estimated $1.2M in abandoned transactions. Service restored; post-incident review scheduled.** This week the team also completed the Q2 security audit and deployed observability tooling."

---

## Anti-Pattern 3 — False Precision

**What it is:** Presenting estimates with spurious decimal places or precise numbers that imply a confidence you do not actually have.

**Example in action:**
"The outage cost us $1,247,389.43 in lost revenue."

**Why it fails:**
No estimate of outage revenue impact is accurate to the cent. When a CFO or board member challenges the methodology, the false precision collapses and takes the credibility of the entire analysis with it. A sophisticated financial executive recognizes spurious precision immediately and discounts everything that follows.

**Corrected version:**
"We estimate the outage cost $1.2-1.5M in lost transactions, based on hourly revenue rates and outage duration. We will have a refined figure after reconciling with finance this week."

---

## Anti-Pattern 4 — The Infinite Caveat

**What it is:** Qualifying every statement so heavily that nothing meaningful is communicated.

**Example in action:**
"Depending on how we measure it, and of course this is subject to change, but roughly speaking, and assuming conditions stay consistent, our reliability might be improving somewhat, though it's hard to say definitively given the data quality issues we've been working through..."

**Why it fails:**
Executives cannot act on information that contains no actionable signal. The Infinite Caveat reads as either (a) the Director does not actually know what is happening, or (b) the Director is protecting themselves from accountability. Both interpretations are damaging.

**Corrected version:**
"Platform reliability improved from 99.6% to 99.8% this quarter — measured by SLA definition. Our data quality issues affected minor metrics only; the headline figure is solid. Confidence: high."

---

## Anti-Pattern 5 — The Status Novel

**What it is:** A weekly status update that spans 3+ pages and covers everything the team did that week, organized chronologically.

**Example in action:**
A Friday status email that runs 1,200 words, covers 14 separate work streams, includes technical context for each, and ends with "let me know if you have questions."

**Why it fails:**
Executives have 90 seconds. A 1,200-word email requires 8-10 minutes to read. It will be flagged for "later" and never read. The Director who writes status novels is optimizing for appearing busy rather than enabling executive decision-making.

**Corrected version:**
One page. RAG status at the top. Headline sentence. Status by area (5 rows, one sentence each). Top 3 risks. Actions required. Upcoming. Done.

---

## Anti-Pattern 6 — Defensive Framing

**What it is:** Structuring communication in a way that prioritizes protecting yourself from blame over informing the executive.

**Example in action:**
"Despite the fact that we raised concerns about this dependency three months ago in the planning document, and notwithstanding the resource constraints we've been operating under, the incident that occurred was largely attributable to decisions made before our team's involvement..."

**Why it fails:**
Executives hear this as the Director managing their own reputation at the expense of clarity. It signals that the Director's primary concern is self-protection, not leadership. It also tends to make things worse — executives become more interested in the blame story once you introduce it.

**Corrected version:**
"The incident root cause was [X]. We flagged this risk in Q1 planning — I'm including that reference in the post-incident report for context. Here is what we are changing to prevent recurrence."

## Anti-Pattern 7 — The Bad News Dump

**What it is:** Delivering bad news to an executive without a path forward, leaving them with only the problem and no remediation.

**Example in action:**
"I need to let you know that our notification platform had a major delivery failure last night. 200,000 customers didn't receive their notifications. I'm not sure yet what caused it."

**Why it fails:**
An executive who receives bad news without a path forward has no option except to escalate or intervene. The Bad News Dump removes executive agency and almost guarantees unwanted involvement in the resolution. It also signals that the Director did not prepare before communicating.

**Corrected version:**
"The notification platform had a delivery failure last night affecting 200,000 customers. Root cause identified: a misconfigured queue depth limit. The fix was deployed at 06:00 — delivery has been restored and we are processing the backlog. All affected customers will have received their messages by noon. Post-incident review scheduled for Tuesday."

---

## Anti-Pattern 8 — Asking for Permission Instead of Recommendation

**What it is:** Presenting a problem or situation to an executive without a recommendation, asking them to make a decision that the Director should be making.

**Example in action:**
"So that's the situation with the vendor contract. I wasn't sure what we should do — do you think we should renew, renegotiate, or go to market? What would you like me to do?"

**Why it fails:**
Executives expect Directors to exercise judgment. Asking "what should I do?" signals that the Director lacks the confidence or competence to make the call. The executive now has to do the Director's analytical work. This interaction, repeated, is the fastest path to being bypassed for VP opportunities.

**Corrected version:**
"I recommend renewing with renegotiated terms — I've outlined the position in this brief. I need a decision by July 15 to give procurement time to close. If you'd prefer to go to market, I can have a vendor shortlist in 2 weeks, but we'd risk the renewal deadline. Which direction do you want me to take?"

## Anti-Pattern 9 — Peer-Level Language in an Executive Setting

**What it is:** Using language, humor, references, or communication styles appropriate for a team meeting or engineering discussion in an executive setting.

**Example in action:**
In a VP staff meeting: "Yeah so basically the thing that was causing the issue is that we had a gnarly race condition in the deployment pipeline that was eating config state — classic footgun, honestly. Took us a while to track it down but we got there."

**Why it fails:**
Peer-level language signals that the Director has not calibrated for their audience. In an executive setting, this reads as unprofessional — not because the information is wrong, but because the framing shows the Director did not shift register for the context. It undermines the executive credibility that Directors need to build.

**Corrected version:**
"We had a timing issue in our deployment automation that was corrupting configuration state. Root cause was confirmed by 14:00; fix was deployed by 16:00. Post-incident review is scheduled."

## Anti-Pattern 10 — The Metric Dump

**What it is:** Presenting 15-20 metrics or charts without a conclusion, leaving the executive to determine what is good, bad, or actionable.

**Example in action:**
A QBR slide with 18 graphs covering MTTR, MTTD, incident count by severity, error rate by service, deployment frequency, change failure rate, p50/p95/p99 latency for 6 services, and on-call load per engineer — with no context, no targets, and no headline.

**Why it fails:**
Executives are not equipped to synthesize 18 metrics into a reliability posture assessment in 30 seconds. They will look at the slide, see complexity, and either skip it or ask "is this good?" — which means you failed to complete your job before presenting.

**Corrected version:**
One headline: "Platform reliability exceeded all SLA targets this quarter." One scorecard table: 5 services, each with target and actual. One chart: MTTR trend over 4 quarters. Everything else goes in the appendix.

---

## Anti-Pattern 11 — Scope Creep in Status Updates

**What it is:** Adding context, background, and information that the executive did not ask for and does not need to take action.

**Example in action:**
A weekly status update that includes a 3-paragraph explanation of why the team chose Prometheus over Datadog for metrics, a summary of the engineering team's learning goals for the quarter, and a description of how the new intern program is going.

**Why it fails:**
Scope creep signals that the Director does not understand what their audience needs. It buries the relevant signals in irrelevant context, and it trains executives to skim status updates — which means they may miss the one thing that was actually important.

**Corrected version:**
Status updates cover: overall health, top risks, executive actions needed, and upcoming. Nothing else. Team context, learning goals, and tooling decisions stay in team-level communications.

---

## Anti-Pattern 12 — The Invisible Ask

**What it is:** A status update or proposal that requires something from the executive but never explicitly states what.

**Example in action:**
A weekly status update that describes a critical vendor contract expiring in 2 weeks, explains that renewal will cost $200K, and closes with "let me know if you have thoughts."

**Why it fails:**
"Let me know if you have thoughts" is not an ask. The executive reads this as informational. They may think the Director is handling it. The contract expires. The Director wonders why the VP didn't act. The VP did not know action was needed.

**Corrected version:**
"Actions required: Approve $200K vendor contract renewal by July 12. Contract expires July 26 — a 10-day procurement cycle is required. If I do not have approval by July 12, I will need to begin an emergency sole-source process that carries additional cost and risk."

## Quick Reference Summary

| Anti-Pattern | Core Failure | One-Line Fix |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Jargon Flooding | Audience cannot decode | Translate every technical term |
| Burying the Lead | Critical info is never read | Lead with the conclusion |
| False Precision | Credibility collapses under scrutiny | Label confidence on every estimate |
| Infinite Caveat | No signal survives the noise | Make one clear statement; caveat once |
| Status Novel | Never read | One page, always |
| Defensive Framing | Signals self-protection, not leadership | Facts + remediation, not blame context |
| Bad News Dump | Triggers executive intervention | Always pair bad news with a path forward |
| Permission-Asking | Signals lack of judgment | Bring a recommendation, not a question |
| Peer-Level Language | Signals calibration failure | Shift register for the audience |
| Metric Dump | Executives cannot synthesize | Conclusion first, data in appendix |
| Scope Creep | Buries relevant signals | Only include what executives need to act |
| Invisible Ask | Nothing gets done | State the ask explicitly with a deadline |
