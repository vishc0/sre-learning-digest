# Reference — Business Risk Quantification

Formulas and methods for converting technical risk into financial and business impact figures. Use these when building investment proposals, incident post-mortems, or board briefings. Every formula includes: the formula, a worked example, data sources, and caveats.

---

## Formula 1 — Outage Cost

**When to use:** Any time a service outage (full or partial) has revenue or operational impact.

### Formula

```
Outage Cost = (Revenue per Minute × Downtime Minutes)
            + (Support Contacts × Cost per Contact)
            + (SLA Penalty per Breach, if applicable)
```

### Worked Example

A payment processing service is down for 47 minutes. Revenue through this service averages $800K per hour ($13,333/minute). Support generates 2,000 contacts at $8/contact. No contractual SLA penalty applies.

```
Outage Cost = ($13,333 × 47) + (2,000 × $8)
            = $626,651 + $16,000
            = $642,651
```

### Data Sources

- Revenue per minute: Finance / revenue ops team. For e-commerce: order volume × average order value ÷ 60.
- Support contacts: CRM/support ticketing system. Estimate 2-5% of affected customers contact support.
- Cost per contact: Support team operational data. Industry average: $6-$15 for digital support, $15-$45 for phone support.
- SLA penalties: Contracts / legal team.

### Caveats

- Not all revenue "lost" in an outage is truly lost — some transactions will be retried and succeed. Actual realized loss is typically 60-80% of the calculated figure for transactional services.
- For awareness-type services (notifications, dashboards), revenue per minute must be calculated as downstream impact (e.g., missed promotional push → lost purchase).
- Reputational cost is real but not directly quantifiable in this formula — address it separately in churn risk.

## Formula 2 — Customer Churn Risk

**When to use:** When a reliability failure or degradation affects customer experience enough to drive churn decisions.

### Formula

```
Churn Risk Value = Affected Customers
                × Churn Rate Increase (percentage points)
                × Average Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)
```

### Worked Example

A notification platform failure affects 50,000 subscribers. Historical data shows that subscribers who experience 2+ service failures per quarter churn at 1.5 percentage points above baseline. Average subscriber LTV is $180 (18 months × $10/month).

```
Churn Risk Value = 50,000 × 0.015 × $180
                = 750 × $180
                = $135,000
```

### Data Sources

- Affected customers: Monitoring platform, impacted user session count.
- Churn rate increase: Product analytics / customer success data. If unavailable, use 0.5-2% incremental churn for a meaningful service failure as a reasonable range.
- LTV: Finance / product team. For SaaS: ARPU × average customer tenure. For telecom: ARPU × average months before churn.

### Caveats

- Churn attributable to a specific incident is hard to isolate — customers rarely cite one event. Use as a risk estimate, not a precise loss calculation.
- Enterprise customers have much higher LTV and may have SLA-driven churn triggers — handle separately with contract-specific numbers.
- Label as "Medium confidence estimate" unless you have direct behavioral data linking service failures to churn rate.

## Formula 3 — SLA Penalty Calculator

**When to use:** When you have enterprise contracts with penalty clauses for reliability breaches.

### Formula

```
SLA Penalty = Contract Annual Value × Penalty Rate per Breach
            × Number of Breaches in Period
```

### Worked Example

Three enterprise contracts each valued at $500K annually. Each carries a 5% credit penalty per SLA breach month. You breach SLA twice in a quarter for two of those contracts.

```
SLA Penalty = ($500K × 5%) × 2 breaches × 2 contracts
            = $25,000 × 4
            = $100,000
```

### Data Sources

- Contract values and penalty structures: Legal / revenue operations.
- Breach thresholds: Typically 99.9% monthly availability for enterprise SaaS; telecom standards vary by contract type.

### Caveats

- Read contracts carefully — some penalties are tiered (escalating per additional breach), some are capped.
- Some contracts include cure periods (you have 30 days to correct before penalty triggers).
- Penalty credits reduce recognized revenue — flag this for the CFO as an accounting item.

## Formula 4 — Regulatory Fine Estimation

**When to use:** When reliability failures or security breaches may trigger regulatory action.

### Reference: FCC (Telecommunications)

- **Network Outage Reporting:** Carriers must report outages affecting 900,000+ user minutes under 47 CFR Part 4.
- **Base fines:** $10,000 per day per violation during investigation, up to $1,000,000 per violation.
- **Consent decree costs:** FCC consent decrees for persistent reliability issues have ranged from $750K (small carrier) to $17.5M (major carrier) historically.
- **Expected value formula:** Violation Probability × Median Fine Amount

### Reference: GDPR (EU Data Subjects)

- **Tier 1 violations** (minor compliance gaps): Up to €10M or 2% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher.
- **Tier 2 violations** (core principles, consent, data subject rights): Up to €20M or 4% of global annual revenue.
- **Expected value formula:** Breach Probability × (Affected Records × Average Fine per Record)
- **Average GDPR fine per affected record:** Historically $25-$150 per record for mid-range cases.

### Reference: CCPA (California)

- **Unintentional violation:** Up to $2,500 per violation (per affected consumer).
- **Intentional violation:** Up to $7,500 per violation.
- **Data breach exposure:** Private right of action — $100 to $750 per consumer per incident.
- **For a 10,000-record breach:** $750K to $7.5M potential exposure.

### Reference: HIPAA (Healthcare Data)

- **Tier 1 (unknowing):** $100 to $50,000 per violation, annual maximum $1.5M.
- **Tier 4 (willful neglect, uncorrected):** $50,000 per violation, annual maximum $1.5M.

### Worked Example

A B2C SaaS company has a data breach affecting 25,000 California customers. The company is subject to CCPA.

```
Expected regulatory exposure = 25,000 customers × $750 (minimum private right of action)
                             = $18,750,000 (upper bound of base exposure)
```

### Caveats

- Fine amounts vary widely by investigation outcome, cooperation, and prior violations. Use as a range, not a point estimate.
- Always involve legal counsel before presenting regulatory exposure estimates to executives — misstating exposure creates liability.
- Label as "estimated regulatory exposure range pending legal review."

## Formula 5 — Engineering Opportunity Cost

**When to use:** When making the case for toil reduction, automation investment, or technical debt paydown.

### Formula

```
Opportunity Cost = Hours Spent on Toil per Month
                × Loaded Engineer Cost per Hour
                × 12 (annualized)
```

Plus: Velocity impact — hours on toil are hours not spent on features.

### Worked Example

A team of 8 engineers spends an average of 6 hours per week on manual on-call toil (runbook execution, alert triage, manual deploys). Fully loaded engineer cost: $250/hour ($500K annual fully loaded ÷ 2,000 hours).

```
Toil cost = 8 engineers × 6 hours/week × 52 weeks × $250/hour
          = 2,496 hours × $250
          = $624,000/year in engineering labor on non-productive work
```

Additional framing: 2,496 hours of toil is equivalent to 1.2 additional engineering headcount — capacity the team could deploy on feature development.

### Data Sources

- Toil hours: Team tracking, on-call log analysis, sprint retrospective data.
- Loaded engineer cost: Finance / HR. Rule of thumb: 2-2.5x base salary for US tech roles.

## Formula 6 — Security Breach Cost

**When to use:** When quantifying security investment proposals or presenting security risk to executives.

### Industry Reference Data (IBM Cost of Data Breach Report 2024)

| Breach Type | Global Average Cost | US Average Cost |
|-------------|--------------------|-----------------| 
| Data breach (any type) | $4.88M | $9.36M |
| Ransomware attack | $5.13M (plus ransom if paid) | Varies |
| Business email compromise | $2.95M | — |
| Breaches involving AI/automation gap | 30-40% higher than average | — |
| Mean time to identify + contain | 258 days (without AI) | — |

### Component Breakdown (IBM 2024)

- Detection and escalation: 29% of total cost
- Post-breach response: 27% of total cost
- Lost business (customer churn, reputation): 26% of total cost
- Notification and legal/regulatory: 18% of total cost

### Expected Value Calculation

```
Expected Annual Security Cost = Probability of Breach × Average Breach Cost
```

For a mid-market SaaS company with 500K customer records:

```
Industry probability of breach in a given year: ~28% (IBM 2024)
Average breach cost for this profile: ~$4.5M

Expected annual security cost = 0.28 × $4.5M = $1.26M/year
```

### Investment Framing

If a security investment of $300K/year reduces breach probability by 50%:

```
Expected cost reduction = 0.28 × 0.5 × $4.5M = $630K/year
Net annual benefit = $630K - $300K = $330K/year
```

### Caveats

- Breach cost averages include large-enterprise incidents that skew the mean. Use industry-segment-specific data when available.
- Reputational cost (future lost sales from breach disclosure) is included in "lost business" but is difficult to isolate.
- Cyber insurance coverage must be factored into net exposure — get current policy terms from legal/finance.
- Source and date all external data in your proposals: "Source: IBM Cost of Data Breach Report 2024."
