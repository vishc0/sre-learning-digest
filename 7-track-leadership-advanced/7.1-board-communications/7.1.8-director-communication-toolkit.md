# Chapter 8 — The Director Communication Toolkit

## Overview

A Director's communication work is not reactive — it is calendared. The most effective Directors maintain a set of recurring communication artifacts that keep their VP informed, give executives predictable signals, and demonstrate operational discipline. Reactive communication (only writing when something is wrong) creates a perception of crisis management. Calendared communication creates a perception of control.

This chapter defines the four primary recurring artifacts: weekly VP status, monthly board summary contribution, quarterly business review, and annual planning presentation.

---

## Artifact 1 — Weekly VP Status Update

**Purpose:** Give your VP complete situational awareness on your domain in 90 seconds so they are never surprised.

**Audience:** Your direct VP. Potentially their VP if they forward it.

**Format:** One page, email body. RAG status visible at the top. See Chapter 3 and the template in `templates/executive-status-update-template.md`.

**Cadence:** Weekly, same day and time every week. Recommend Friday by 4pm or Monday by 9am.

**Time investment:** 30-45 minutes per week if you maintain a running draft throughout the week. 90 minutes if you write it cold on Friday afternoon.

**The one mistake that undermines it:** Missing a week without notice. Inconsistency in a cadenced artifact is a reliability failure. If you will be out, send it early or delegate it explicitly. A VP who does not receive your expected Friday status on two consecutive weeks will begin to wonder what you are hiding or whether you are managing your domain. Consistent cadence is itself a trust signal.

## Artifact 2 — Monthly Board Summary Contribution

**Purpose:** Feed your inputs to the CTO/VP who writes the engineering section of the monthly board summary.

**Audience:** Your VP or CTO, who will synthesize it. The board is the indirect audience.

**Format:** 3-5 bullet points in a shared document or email. Each bullet is one sentence. Each sentence answers one of: (a) what notable happened, (b) what is the key risk, (c) what investment or improvement was completed.

Example:
- Platform reliability: 99.92% SLA achieved this month vs. 99.9% target. Three Sev2 incidents, all resolved within SLA, no customer escalations.
- Security: Completed quarterly CVE remediation. Zero critical vulnerabilities outstanding. SOC2 Type II audit preparation on track for Q4.
- Investment milestone: Notification platform observability upgrade deployed. MTTR reduced from 28 minutes average to 9 minutes — ahead of target.
- Forward risk: Redis cluster approaching capacity limits. Scaling plan approved; migration begins next month. Board awareness recommended given transaction-volume dependency.

**Cadence:** Monthly, typically the first week of the month covering the prior month.

**Time investment:** 20-30 minutes per month if you write from your weekly status updates. These bullets should be synthesized from four weeks of status updates, not drafted from scratch.

**The one mistake that undermines it:** Sending bullets that require your VP to do the translation work. If your bullet says "p99 latency was 340ms this month," your VP has to translate it before they can put it in the board summary. Do that translation yourself. "Response time for the slowest 1% of transactions averaged 340ms, within our 400ms SLA target." Same fact, board-ready language.

## Artifact 3 — Quarterly Business Review (QBR) Presentation

**Purpose:** Present your domain's performance, risks, and investment roadmap to the VP layer and potentially the C-suite in a structured 30-minute session.

**Audience:** VP of Engineering, possibly CTO, other Directors as peers. Occasionally the CEO if the company is small.

**Format:** 12-15 slides, 30 minutes including Q&A. Typically 20 minutes of presenting and 10 minutes of questions.

**Suggested slide structure:**
1. Executive Summary (1 slide — headline numbers, RAG status, key message)
2. SLA Performance vs. Target (1-2 slides — trend chart, variance analysis)
3. Incident Summary (1 slide — table of major incidents, business impact, resolution status)
4. Team Performance (1 slide — velocity, toil reduction, on-call health)
5. Risk Register (1 slide — top 3 risks entering next quarter)
6. Investment Summary (1 slide — what was spent, what it delivered in business terms)
7. Next Quarter Plan (2-3 slides — commitments, investments requested, dependencies)
8. Appendix (detailed data for questions)

**Cadence:** Quarterly, typically in the first 2-3 weeks of each quarter covering the prior quarter.

**Time investment:** 4-6 hours to produce. 2 hours for dry run with your team. 1 hour for VP pre-brief.

**The one mistake that undermines it:** Presenting to your QBR audience without a pre-brief with your VP. Your VP should not be learning your QBR content in the QBR. Brief them the week before. Incorporate their feedback. This prevents surprises, aligns messaging, and demonstrates that you operate as a team with your VP rather than as an independent operator. Directors who ambush their VPs in QBRs — even with positive news — erode trust.

## Artifact 4 — Annual Planning Presentation (Engineering Investment Roadmap)

**Purpose:** Secure the budget, headcount, and organizational commitment needed to execute next year's platform strategy.

**Audience:** VP of Engineering, CTO, and often CFO for budget approval. Sometimes presented to the full executive team.

**Format:** 20-25 slides, 45-60 minutes. This is the longest formal presentation a Director typically makes.

**Suggested structure:**
- Part 1 — State of the Platform (5 slides): How did we perform this year? What is the current reliability, security, and velocity posture? What changed?
- Part 2 — Gaps and Risks (5 slides): What will limit us in the next year if unaddressed? Each gap quantified in business terms.
- Part 3 — Investment Options (5-7 slides): Prioritized list of investments with cost, benefit, and risk-of-inaction for each. Three-option framing for each major investment (do nothing, minimum, full).
- Part 4 — Recommended Plan (5 slides): Your recommendation for next year's investment portfolio. How it maps to company OKRs. What it enables. What the sequencing is.
- Part 5 — Resource Ask (2-3 slides): Headcount, budget, tooling. Timeline. Decision gates.

**Cadence:** Annually, typically in Q4 for the following fiscal year.

**Time investment:** 20-40 hours over 4-6 weeks to produce well. The research and data-gathering phase (weeks 1-2), the draft and internal review phase (weeks 3-4), and the dry run and revision phase (weeks 5-6).

**The one mistake that undermines it:** Presenting a wish list rather than a prioritized plan. Annual planning presentations that request everything the team needs without prioritization put the CFO and VP in the position of making the prioritization decisions — which they will do without your context. Present your prioritized recommendation. Make the trade-offs explicit. Show that you made hard choices. This demonstrates the judgment that distinguishes a Director from a Senior Manager in a planning context.

---

## Maintaining the Toolkit

These four artifacts do not exist in isolation. The weekly status update feeds into the monthly board contribution. The monthly contributions roll up into the QBR. The QBR informs the annual plan. If you maintain your weekly status with discipline, the other three artifacts are largely assembly jobs — organizing and synthesizing material that already exists.

The Director who writes no weekly status, skips monthly contributions, and then writes their QBR from scratch every quarter spends 3x the effort and produces 0.7x the quality. The discipline of the weekly artifact is the investment that makes everything else efficient.
