# Chapter 4 — Incident Communication at the Executive Level

## The Executive Sitrep vs. the Technical Bridge

When a Sev1 is declared, two parallel communication streams activate. The technical bridge — your engineers, the IC, the on-call SMEs — is focused on diagnosis and resolution. This is where "it might be DNS" and "still investigating" belong. This is technical coordination, and it is correct to be exploratory and uncertain there.

The executive sitrep is a completely different artifact for a completely different audience. Executives are not on the technical bridge. They do not need diagnostic hypotheses. They need to know: what is the customer and business impact right now, what is being done about it, and when will they hear from you next. The executive sitrep exists so that executives can be informed without interrupting the technical resolution team. Done correctly, it reduces executive interference in the incident and gives engineers space to work.

Done incorrectly — or not done at all — executives fill the information vacuum by calling engineers directly, joining technical bridges uninvited, and making decisions with incomplete information. This is how incidents become worse.

## The 15-Minute Rule

Your first executive sitrep must go out within 15 minutes of Sev1 declaration.

This is the same urgency discipline as the IC's 5-minute triage in incident command: the first responsibility of an incoming IC is to rapidly assess the situation and establish command, not to perfectly understand every detail. The 15-minute first sitrep does not require you to know the root cause. It does not require you to have a resolution timeline. It requires you to tell executives what you know right now, what is being done, and when they will hear from you next.

The reason for the 15-minute rule is credibility, not information. When executives receive a structured sitrep within 15 minutes of a Sev1, they trust that the incident is being managed. When they receive nothing for 45 minutes, they assume chaos and intervene. The timeliness of your first communication is itself a signal that leadership is in command.

If you do not have complete information at 15 minutes, send what you have. The five-field format below was designed for exactly this situation.

## First Executive Sitrep Format (Within 15 Minutes)

Five fields. Nothing more.

```
SITREP — [Service/Product] — [Date/Time]

IMPACT:     [What customers or business functions are affected and at what scale]
STATUS:     [Active incident | Monitoring | Resolved]
ACTIONS:    [What the response team is doing right now — 1-2 sentences]
ETA NEXT:   [When you will send the next update — exact time, not "soon"]
OWNER:      [Name of the incident commander and escalation contact]
```

**Example:**

```
SITREP — Notification Platform — 2026-07-06 14:32 PT

IMPACT:     Push notification delivery degraded. Estimated 15-20% of 
            notifications not delivered since 14:00 PT. Affects all 
            subscribers in Western region. Revenue impact under assessment.

STATUS:     Active incident. Sev1 declared 14:25 PT.

ACTIONS:    Engineering team investigating root cause. Rollback to prior 
            release being evaluated. On-call SRE lead (Chen) is IC.

ETA NEXT:   15:00 PT or on status change, whichever is first.

OWNER:      IC: J. Chen (+1-206-555-0194) | Director: V. Chippa
```

## Subsequent Update Format (Every 30 Minutes or on Status Change)

Same five fields, updated. Add one additional field: **DELTA** — what changed since the last sitrep.

```
SITREP UPDATE #2 — [Service/Product] — [Date/Time]

DELTA:      Root cause identified. Rollback decision made.
IMPACT:     [Updated impact — improve the estimate if you can]
STATUS:     [Updated status]
ACTIONS:    [Current actions — rollback deploying, ETA 15 minutes]
ETA NEXT:   [Next update time]
OWNER:      [Same or updated]
```

The DELTA field is the most important field in subsequent updates. Executives reading update #3 need to know immediately what changed since update #2. Do not make them diff two walls of text. Tell them explicitly.

## What to Never Say in Executive Communications

These phrases create anxiety, undermine confidence, and often invite unwanted executive intervention:

**"We don't know yet."** This is never acceptable without a qualifier. Acceptable version: "Root cause is under investigation; we will have initial findings by 15:30." You have not answered the question, but you have given them a time when you will.

**"It might be DNS."** All diagnostic hypotheses stay on the technical bridge. If you share diagnostic uncertainty with executives, they will make decisions based on incomplete hypotheses. Share conclusions, not hypotheses.

**"We're still working on it."** This is not an update. It is the absence of an update dressed as one. Say what you are working on, who is doing it, and what the expected outcome and timeline are.

**"This is complex."** Every Sev1 is complex. This phrase reads as excuse-making. Executives already assume complexity; they want to know what you are doing despite the complexity.

**"We think we have it under control."** "We think" signals uncertainty you have not resolved. Either you have it under control or you do not. If you are uncertain, say: "We have a mitigation deployed and are monitoring for confirmation of recovery. Next update at 16:00."

## All-Clear Format

When the incident is resolved, send a structured all-clear. Do not send a casual message. The all-clear is the transition from incident to post-incident review, and it should be as structured as the sitrep.

```
ALL CLEAR — [Service/Product] — [Date/Time]

RESOLVED:   [Date/Time service was confirmed restored]
DURATION:   [Total incident duration from Sev1 declaration]
IMPACT:     [Final impact statement — how many customers, what revenue/operational impact]
ROOT CAUSE: [One sentence — what actually caused this]
IMMEDIATE FIX: [What was done to restore service]
FOLLOW-ON:  [What post-incident actions are planned — PIR date, corrective actions, timeline]
OWNER:      [Director responsible for follow-on]
```

## The Post-Incident Executive Summary

Within 24-48 hours of a significant incident (Sev1 or a Sev2 with material business impact), send a post-incident executive summary. This is not the full PIR document — that is an internal engineering artifact. The executive summary is a one-page business-facing document structured as:

- What happened (customer-impact-first, not system-first)
- Why it happened (root cause in business terms — "a configuration change deployed without sufficient testing" not "a misconfigured Nginx upstream")
- What we did about it (resolution and immediate corrective actions)
- What we are changing (process, tooling, or architectural improvements, with timelines)
- Current status (what is the reliability posture now)

The post-incident summary is a trust-building artifact. It demonstrates that incidents are analyzed, not just survived. Directors who produce clear, honest post-incident summaries build disproportionate credibility with their VP and CTO because these summaries demonstrate the operational maturity that separates Directors from Senior Managers.

## Cadence Discipline

The value of structured cadence during an incident is that it removes the unknown from executive minds. When executives know they will receive an update at exactly 15:00, 15:30, 16:00, and 16:30, they do not need to chase updates. The cadence is the communication.

Miss one update without a heads-up, and executives will call. Get one call from your VP during a Sev1 because they did not receive an expected update, and you have demonstrated that the incident is not managed. Maintain cadence even when the update is "no change — still monitoring — next update 16:30."
