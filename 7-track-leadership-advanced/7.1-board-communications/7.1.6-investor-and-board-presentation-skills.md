# Chapter 6 — Investor and Board Presentation Skills

## What Investors Actually Care About in Infrastructure

Investors — particularly VCs on the board — evaluate infrastructure and reliability through a specific lens that is different from how operators evaluate it. Understanding their frame prevents you from presenting the wrong content.

**Uptime as a product feature.** For SaaS, fintech, and consumer platform companies, reliability is not a technical metric — it is a feature of the product that customers pay for. Investors understand this in the same way they understand NPS: it is a leading indicator of retention. A Director who presents reliability metrics to a board and frames them as engineering KPIs is missing the frame. The correct frame is: "Our reliability posture is part of the product our customers bought. Here is how we are performing against that commitment, and here is what that means for retention."

**Security incidents as valuation risk.** This is the one that most technical leaders underweight. A material security breach does not just cost the immediate remediation — it creates drag on future financing rounds, enterprise sales cycles, and potential M&A valuations. Investors are acutely aware that security incidents at portfolio companies surface in due diligence for every subsequent transaction. When you brief a board on security posture, you are briefing them on something that directly affects the company's tradable value. Frame it that way.

**Engineering velocity as competitive moat.** The speed at which your engineering organization can ship directly affects the company's ability to outmaneuver competitors, respond to market shifts, and capture opportunities before they close. Investors — especially in high-growth companies — track deployment frequency, feature lead time, and time-to-market as proxy metrics for competitive health. A Director who can articulate the connection between platform investment and engineering velocity is speaking the investor's language directly.

## Startup-Specific Board Communication

At a Series B-D company, board communication has constraints that do not exist in enterprise settings. The most important is **runway awareness**.

Every engineering investment competes with runway. A $2M infrastructure investment at a company with 18 months of runway is a different conversation than the same investment at a company with 36 months. Directors who present investment proposals without acknowledging runway constraints are perceived as operationally naive, regardless of how sound the technical case is.

The correct approach is to price your proposal in runway terms: "This $2M investment represents approximately 1.4 months of runway. Our payback period of 6 months means we are net-positive by month 8, and our burn reduction from efficiency gains partially offsets the initial spend."

**Burn rate vs. reliability trade-off.** This is a real conversation that happens at board level. "Can we operate at 99.5% reliability instead of 99.9% to save the infrastructure investment?" This is not a technically unreasonable question — it is a capital allocation question. The Director's job is not to resist this question but to give the board the information to make it well: what does the 0.4% reliability gap cost in customer terms, churn terms, and regulatory terms? Then let them decide. A Director who dismisses this question as inappropriate has not learned their audience.

**Technical risk as due diligence item.** Investors conduct technical due diligence during financing rounds, and M&A acquirers conduct it before closing. Infrastructure decisions you make today will be scrutinized in a due diligence process 18-36 months from now. Technical debt, unpatched security vulnerabilities, unlicensed open-source dependencies, and single-point-of-failure architecture all appear in due diligence reports and can affect deal terms or close probability. This is a powerful framing for infrastructure investment: "Addressing this now costs $X. Addressing it under due diligence time pressure costs $3X and risks the deal timeline."

## Board Deck Structure for Infrastructure

The rule for board presentations is: three slides maximum for any infrastructure update. Boards are reviewing 15-20 slides in a 90-minute session. Your three slides are competing with the financial update, the product roadmap, the hiring plan, and the go-to-market review. If your content requires more than three slides, the implicit message is that you do not know how to prioritize.

**Slide 1 — Status.** SLA performance vs. target this quarter. Major incidents (count, total downtime, business impact). Progress on committed investments. One paragraph of narrative. RAG status at the top.

**Slide 2 — Risk.** What are the top 2-3 reliability or security risks entering next quarter? What is the business exposure for each? What is the mitigation status? A simple table works better than bullets — it signals that you have done the risk classification work, not just listed concerns.

**Slide 3 — Investment.** If you have an ask, one slide. Current state vs. target state. Cost of the investment. Risk of not investing. The specific decision you need. A decision-by date.

If you have no ask, use Slide 3 for forward risk and planned actions. Never waste a board slide on a retrospective unless it adds information the board needs to make a decision.

## The Board Test

Before any board presentation, apply the board test to every slide: if a board member picks up this slide and has 30 seconds before the presenter moves to the next one, can they extract the key message?

Slides that fail this test have one or more of the following problems:
- More than 5 bullet points (board member reads 2, stops)
- A chart with no labeled conclusion (board member sees a graph, does not know if it is good or bad)
- Technical terminology without translation (board member reads past it)
- A wall of text (board member decides to read it later, which means never)

Slides that pass the board test have:
- A headline that is the conclusion, not the topic ("Reliability improved 18% this quarter" not "Reliability Update")
- One primary chart or table per slide
- Every metric labeled with its target and whether it is above or below
- A clear call-out box for the most important number or finding

## Preparation for Board Presentations

You will rarely present directly to a board as a Director — your CTO or VP typically presents, with inputs from you. But you may be asked to attend to answer technical questions, and your materials will be the basis for the CTO's presentation. Prepare accordingly.

Know the top 10 questions a board member could ask about your domain. Prepare two-sentence answers for each. Know what numbers you can defend to three significant figures. Know what numbers you should caveat. Know which board members have the most technical background and what they are likely to probe. Bring one page of backup data for every key claim — not to present, but to reference if challenged.

The worst thing that can happen in a board question-and-answer is a Director saying "I'll have to follow up on that" for a question about their own domain. Prepare until that answer is not possible.
