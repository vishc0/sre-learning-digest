# Chapter 2: Performance Management and Documentation — PIPs Done Right

> **DISCLAIMER**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner. Nothing here constitutes legal advice.

---

## Why Documentation Is an Act of Leadership, Not Paranoia

Most engineering managers who struggle with documentation think of it as defensive bureaucracy — something you do when you're about to fire someone and want cover. That framing is exactly backward, and it's what produces bad PIPs, wrongful termination exposure, and damaged manager credibility.

Documentation is how you demonstrate that your leadership is consistent, fair, and based on observable facts rather than personality. A manager who documents well:
- Gives employees real-time feedback grounded in specifics, not vague impressions
- Builds a record that performance decisions are based on work outcomes, not protected class characteristics
- Creates continuity across time — the performance conversation from six months ago isn't reconstructed from memory, it's a record
- Is protected when an employee claims "I had no idea there was a problem"

The goal of this chapter is to change your relationship with documentation from "something I do when things go wrong" to "something I do as a normal part of leadership," and to ensure that when a formal PIP is necessary, you know how to build one that actually serves its intended purpose.

---

## The Fundamental Reframe: What a PIP Is For

A Performance Improvement Plan, when used correctly, is a structured due process mechanism that protects both parties. It tells the employee: here is precisely where your performance is not meeting expectations, here is what meeting expectations looks like, here is the support you'll receive, here is the timeline, and here is what happens if performance improves or does not.

That is not punitive. It is transparent and fair — which is exactly what the law requires.

The PIP that creates legal exposure is the one that functions as a termination prelude with a 30-day countdown: no real support, unmeetable targets, no genuine expectation of improvement, outcome predetermined. This pattern is common, it is recognizable to employment attorneys, and it converts what should be a protected management action into evidence of bad faith. Courts and arbitrators have seen it hundreds of times.

**The test for whether a PIP is legitimate:** If the employee fully meets every requirement in the PIP, would you retain them? If the honest answer is no, you are not running a legitimate PIP — you are running an exit process without calling it that. Exit processes have their own legitimate procedures. Conflating them with PIPs is where the legal risk lives.

A real PIP is written with the genuine expectation that the employee might succeed. It reflects the company's investment in that person's success. And if they do succeed, the PIP closes, the record reflects improvement, and you move forward.

## The Documentation Standard for All Performance Conversations

Before getting to formal PIPs, establish this baseline habit: every significant performance conversation — coaching, verbal feedback, concern raised, missed expectation noted — should be documented. Not necessarily as a formal HR record. As manager notes.

**What manager notes require:**
- Date and time of the conversation
- Who was present
- What specific concern was raised (quote the behavior or outcome, not the personality)
- The employee's response (as accurately as you can capture it)
- Next steps agreed to, by whom, by when

**The SBAR adaptation for performance documentation:**

*Situation:* What happened? Be specific. "On the Q3 reliability review on October 14, the on-call runbook for the payments service had not been updated following the architecture change in August. The runbook still reflected the old routing logic."

*Background:* What context is relevant? "This was the third consecutive quarter where runbook currency was an expectation discussed in the 1:1 and not completed. Previous conversations occurred June 12 and August 3."

*Assessment:* What is the impact? "During the October 8 incident, the response team spent 22 minutes following the outdated runbook before identifying the discrepancy. MTTD was extended directly as a result."

*Response:* What was said and what happens next? "Discussed with the engineer on October 15. They acknowledged the oversight and stated the competing OKR prioritization made runbook work feel lower priority. We agreed on a specific deadline of October 29 for full runbook update and a shared calendar reminder for quarterly review going forward."

This level of specificity is not excessive — it is what distinguishes a performance conversation that could defend a termination decision in arbitration from one that could not.

**The "bad attitude" problem.** Vague characterizations — "attitude issues," "not a team player," "culture fit concerns" — are legally dangerous and managerially useless. They are legally dangerous because they cannot be falsified (the employee cannot prove they don't have a bad attitude) and they often function as proxies for protected class bias. They are managerially useless because the employee cannot change an attitude — they can only change behaviors. Document the behaviors. "Interrupted colleagues three times in the architecture review on September 22, including after being asked to hold questions" is documentable. "Has a bad attitude in meetings" is not.

## What a Legally Defensible PIP Contains

Every element below serves a purpose. Missing any of them weakens the PIP both as a management tool and as a legal record.

**1. Specific, observable performance gaps.**
Name the expectation. Name the gap. Cite specific instances with dates.

Wrong: "Does not communicate effectively with stakeholders."
Right: "In Q3, three sprint demos were delivered without advance stakeholder notification (July 18, August 22, September 5). The expectation in our team operating agreement is 48-hour advance notice with agenda. This created scheduling conflicts for the Director of Product and two external partners."

**2. SMART improvement expectations.**
State what success looks like. Make it measurable. Make it time-bound.

Wrong: "Improve stakeholder communication."
Right: "For the duration of this PIP (October 15 – January 15), all sprint demos will be scheduled with stakeholder notification no less than 48 hours in advance with an agenda attached. Zero exceptions are expected. This will be tracked in the team calendar and reviewed in our weekly 1:1."

**3. Support the company will provide.**
This is the element most managers omit — and its absence is a significant red flag to investigators and arbitrators. If the company has not offered to help the employee succeed, the PIP is not a good-faith improvement effort.

Support might include: dedicated coaching time with the manager, pairing with a senior peer, access to a specific training course, reduced scope during the PIP period to allow focused improvement, or modified project assignments. The support must be genuine and resourced, not cosmetic.

**4. Timeline.**
Thirty, sixty, or ninety days are the most common PIP durations. The timeline should be appropriate to the complexity of the improvement required. Complex technical skill gaps may require 90 days. Behavioral expectations that can be observed immediately may warrant 30. Discuss the timeline with your HRBP before publishing it.

**5. Measurement criteria.**
How will you know the employee has improved? Who will assess it? What data will you use? Be precise. "Manager discretion" as the only measurement criterion is not defensible.

**6. Consequences — stated clearly.**
The PIP must state what happens at the end of the period. "If the performance expectations outlined in this plan are not met by January 15, employment may be terminated" is clear. Omitting consequences, or burying them in ambiguous language, creates an implied promise that the employee will be retained regardless of outcome.

## What PIPs Cannot Legally Do

**Set impossible targets.** If the success criteria cannot be met by a competent professional in the time allotted, the PIP is not a good-faith improvement effort — it is a termination in disguise. Judges and arbitrators recognize this.

**Deny the employee resources to succeed.** If an employee is on a PIP for missed deadlines and you simultaneously double their workload, reduce their team support, or withhold access to tools they need — the PIP has been sabotaged. This creates both a moral and legal problem.

**Accelerate because of unrelated protected activity.** If an employee on a PIP files an internal discrimination complaint and you accelerate the PIP timeline in response, you have created a retaliation claim that is legally distinct from and often more serious than the underlying PIP.

**Be applied inconsistently.** If similarly situated employees with similar performance gaps are not put on PIPs, the decision to PIP one employee — particularly one from a protected class — requires careful documentation of the legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for the differential treatment.

## Progressive Discipline: The Documented Path

US law does not require progressive discipline before termination (except in states with specific statutes, union contracts, or company policy commitments). But progressive discipline — verbal warning → written warning → PIP → termination — creates a documented record of due process that is the most reliable protection against wrongful termination claims.

The value of the progression is not legal mandate. It is evidentiary: it shows that the employee had notice of the problem, had opportunities to correct it, received support, and still did not improve. That record is what makes a termination defensible.

If you skip steps — for example, terminating an employee whose performance issues were never formally documented — you are relying on the at-will doctrine alone. That is a thin defense in a jurisdiction with robust public policy exceptions, and it is an indefensible position if the employee is in a protected class.

**The Director's rule:** The closer you get to termination, the more critical the documentation trail becomes. If you are considering termination and the documentation record is thin, that is a signal to involve your HRBP immediately — not to retroactively build documentation (which is its own problem), but to assess what the appropriate next step actually is.

---

## When to Involve Your HRBP

- Before any formal PIP conversation — not to ask permission, but to ensure the PIP is structurally sound and legally compliant
- If the employee challenges the PIP or refuses to sign
- If the employee raises a discrimination or retaliation concern during the PIP period
- Before the end of a PIP, to confirm the appropriate outcome and process
- Before any termination conversation, regardless of how clear-cut the case appears

The HRBP review before a PIP is not bureaucratic friction. It is a second set of eyes that catches structural problems — an unmeetable target, missing support commitments, inconsistent application — before they become legal problems.

---

## Chapter Summary

- Documentation is a leadership practice, not a defensive measure. Build the habit before you need it.
- A PIP is a structured due process mechanism, not a termination countdown. It should be built with a genuine expectation that the employee might succeed.
- The SBAR-adapted documentation format — Situation, Background, Assessment, Response — creates records that are specific, grounded in observable facts, and defensible.
- Document behaviors, not personalities. "Bad attitude" is not documentable; specific behavior in specific meetings on specific dates is.
- A legally defensible PIP contains: specific performance gaps, SMART expectations, genuine support commitments, a clear timeline, measurable criteria, and clearly stated consequences.
- Loop in your HRBP before any formal PIP, before any termination, and whenever you are uncertain.
