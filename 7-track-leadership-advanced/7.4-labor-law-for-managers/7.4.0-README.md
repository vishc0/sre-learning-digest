# Labor Law for Engineering Managers — Framework Overview

> **DISCLAIMER**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner. Nothing here constitutes legal advice.

---

## Why This Framework Exists

Directors who don't understand employment law basics create legal exposure — for themselves, their companies, and the people they manage. Not knowing is not a defense. A wrongful termination claim, a harassment complaint that wasn't escalated, a PIP that was designed to fail — these aren't just HR problems. They become personal liability issues, career-defining moments, and sometimes federal litigation.

This framework does not turn you into an employment attorney. It turns you into an engineering manager who recognizes when a situation requires immediate escalation, avoids saying things that create liability, makes documentation decisions in the moment, and can speak credibly about people management in senior leadership interviews.

The professional posture throughout is: **competence, not fear.** You are learning what protects you, your team members, and the company — in that order, simultaneously.

---

## The Three Mental Models That Anchor Everything

**1. Documentation is your best protection.**
Memory is unreliable and inadmissible. Notes with dates, quotes, and next steps are evidence. A manager who documents consistently is protected in termination disputes, discrimination claims, and investigation proceedings. A manager who documents selectively looks like they were building a case — which is different.

**2. HR is your partner, not your substitute.**
HR Business Partners are your co-pilots on people decisions. They carry institutional knowledge, legal awareness, and process discipline that you don't and shouldn't have to. But they are not a liability firewall. If you make a bad call, delegating to HR doesn't protect you from the consequences of that call. Loop them in early. Be transparent. Follow their guidance. But understand the decision is still yours.

**3. The law is the floor, not the ceiling.**
Employment law defines the minimum standard of treatment employees are owed. Leading with "is this legal?" is the wrong question. Lead with "is this fair, consistent, and documented?" If yes, it is almost certainly also legal. If no, it may be legal but still wrong — and the gap between "legal" and "right" is where employee trust and team performance live.

## The Interview Angle

When a Director-level interviewer asks "tell me about a difficult personnel situation you managed," they are not asking for a story about how tough it was. They are testing:

- **Process discipline**: Did you follow the right steps? Did you loop in HR at the right time?
- **Documentation instinct**: Did you document the situation as it developed, or scramble afterward?
- **Judgment**: Did you recognize the severity early or escalate only when forced?
- **Outcome ownership**: Did you own the decision, or did you hide behind HR and legal?
- **People balance**: Were you fair to the individual while protecting the team and the company?

A strong answer demonstrates that you knew the process, followed it, involved the right partners at the right time, and made a decision you can defend. This framework gives you that vocabulary and that process discipline — which is why reading it before an interview matters as much as reading it before a difficult conversation.

## A Note on State Law Variation

US employment law is a layered system: federal law sets the floor, state law adds requirements on top, and company policy adds more on top of that. This framework covers federal law as the baseline. If you manage teams in California, New York, Washington, or Illinois — and at a company like T-Mobile, you likely do — assume state requirements are stricter than what is documented here. Your HRBP will know the state-specific requirements. Always ask.
