# Chapter 08 — Director Documentation Discipline: Your Professional Protection System

> **Disclaimer**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner.

---

## Why Documentation Comes First

In any disputed employment situation, the party with better documentation wins. Not always in court — most employment disputes never reach court. But in internal investigations, EEOC charges, unemployment hearings, arbitration proceedings, and executive conversations about what actually happened — the person who documented accurately, consistently, and contemporaneously has an enormous advantage over the person who is reconstructing from memory.

Directors who document well are not covering themselves defensively. They are doing their jobs. They are creating the record that protects their employees' interests (a fair account of what was said and agreed to), protects the company's interests (documented business rationale for decisions), and protects their own professional integrity (evidence that they managed professionally when it mattered).

The goal is professional competence, not paranoia. A Director who documents well sleeps better — not because they are afraid, but because they are not relying on memory and goodwill when things get complicated.

## The Fundamental Divide: What to Document and What Not To

Not everything belongs in a manager's log. Knowing the difference is as important as the documentation habit itself.

### Document These

**Performance-related discussions in 1:1s**: any conversation where you addressed a gap, set an expectation, or discussed an area for improvement. Not every 1:1 — but any 1:1 where something substantive was said about performance, conduct, or accountability. Date, brief description of what was discussed, what was agreed, follow-up date.

**Formal performance conversations**: the full record — what behavior or output was observed, the specific example, the date you observed it, the impact, the employee's response, what was agreed, follow-up date.

**Reasonable accommodation requests**: date received, substance of the request (not medical details — just "requested accommodation related to a medical condition"), date HR was notified, confirmation HR received the referral.

**Complaints received**: date, who raised the concern, the substance of what was raised (in the employee's words as closely as you can capture), your response, date HR was notified.

**Disciplinary actions**: verbal warnings, written warnings, performance improvement plans — every step in the process, with dates, specifics, and employee responses documented contemporaneously.

**Key decisions and their rationale**: when you make a significant call about a team member's role, compensation, project assignment, or promotion — document the reasoning, not just the outcome. "Chose not to promote based on X and Y, communicated on [date], employee's response was Z" is the record that protects both of you.

**Situations where "he said / she said" is possible**: any conversation where there is potential for different recollections of what was said or agreed — document your account the same day.

### Do Not Document These

**Personal medical information not tied to an accommodation**: if an employee tells you they are managing a chronic illness in casual conversation, that information does not belong in your manager's log. It is not your data to hold. If it leads to an accommodation request, that request gets documented and HR gets notified; the underlying medical information stays with HR.

**Informal vent conversations**: if a team member vents frustration about a colleague or project in a private conversation and you both treat it as informal — document it only if it rises to the level of a reportable concern. Not every difficult conversation belongs in a log.

**Speculation about performance causes**: "I think John's performance issues are related to his personal situation" is not documentation — it is speculation that can be harmful in a legal record. Document observable behavior and business impact, not hypothesized causes.

**HR investigation notes you are involved in as a witness or subject**: if HR is conducting an investigation and you are providing information, your notes from that process belong to HR, not your personal log. Hand them over and delete your copy.

## The 1:1 Documentation Standard

The standard is minimal, consistent, and contemporaneous. You are not writing a legal brief — you are creating a retrievable record.

**Format:**
```
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Participants: [you and employee name]
Key discussion: [2-4 sentences — what topic was covered, what was said that matters]
Agreed next step: [specific, time-bound]
Follow-up: [date you will revisit]
```

**Where to keep it**: a system you control, that is not visible to the employee, and that you can retrieve in 90 seconds when HR calls you. Options include a password-protected local document, a private OneNote notebook, or a secure manager journal tool if your company provides one. Not your email inbox. Not an unsaved document on your desktop.

**Timing**: the same day as the conversation. Memory degrades fast. A note written two weeks after a conversation is worth less than one written the same evening — both legally and practically.

Handwritten notes in a dated notebook are legally valid. If that is how you work, date every entry and keep the notebook.

## The Performance Conversation Documentation Template

When a performance conversation rises above informal feedback — when you are addressing a real gap, setting a formal expectation, or documenting a concern that may eventually become a disciplinary matter — use this structure:

```
Date of conversation: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Date of observation (if different): [YYYY-MM-DD]

Situation: [What specific behavior, output, or pattern was observed]
Example: [The specific instance — sprint, meeting, deliverable, interaction]
Impact: [Why this matters — to the team, the product, the stakeholder, the employee's growth]

Employee response: [What they said when you raised it — their words, not your interpretation]

Agreement: [What the employee committed to, specifically and measurably]
My commitment: [What you committed to — support, resource, follow-up]

Follow-up date: [Specific date]
Next step if not resolved: [Be explicit with yourself about what escalation looks like]
```

This is not a formal HR record unless you escalate it. It is your management log. The value is that if you are ever asked to describe the pattern of performance issues that led to a formal action, you have a contemporaneous record — not a reconstruction.

## Reasonable Accommodation: The Documentation Obligation

When an employee requests an accommodation or discloses a condition that may affect their ability to perform their job, the documentation sequence is:

1. **Document the disclosure**: date, that the employee requested an accommodation or disclosed a condition, without recording the underlying medical details
2. **Notify HR immediately** — this is not optional
3. **Document your referral to HR**: date, that you referred the employee to HR, confirmation that HR received the referral
4. **Stop**: HR owns the interactive process (the legally required dialogue between employer and employee to explore accommodation options). You do not continue to gather information, propose solutions, or make decisions about accommodation outside of HR's guidance

What you must not have in your manager's log:
- Diagnoses
- Medication information
- The substance of any medical documentation the employee shared with HR
- Speculation about whether the condition is "real" or severe enough to warrant accommodation

Your log should reflect: "Employee requested accommodation on [date]. Referred to HR on [date]. HR confirmed receipt on [date]." That is all.

## When You Receive a Complaint

The moment a team member raises a concern that involves another employee's behavior — harassment, discrimination, hostile work environment, bullying, retaliation — you have an obligation that starts immediately.

**Document the initial conversation:**
- Date and time
- What the employee said, as closely as you can capture their words
- Whether they requested confidentiality (note this, though you cannot guarantee it if an investigation is required)
- Your response in the moment

**Notify HR the same day.** Not the next morning. The same day.

After HR is involved, you are not the investigator. You may be asked to provide a written statement of what you heard. Write it, give it to HR, and do not create additional records about the investigation outside of what HR requests. If you conduct your own parallel investigation — even informally, even with good intentions — you can compromise the integrity of HR's process and create legal exposure for the company.

## Records Retention: General Guidance

Retention standards vary by document type and jurisdiction. These are general guidelines — always verify with your company's records retention policy, which may differ:

| Record Type | Suggested Retention | Notes |
|-------------|--------------------|----|
| 1:1 performance notes | Active tenure + 2 years post-separation | Helpful context for future references; longer if a dispute is anticipated |
| Formal performance records (PIPs, written warnings) | 2-3 years post-separation | Key evidence if a wrongful termination claim follows |
| Hiring records (interview notes, decision rationale) | 1-2 years post-hire decision | EEOC requires retention during the relevant statute of limitations |
| Accommodation requests (your log entries only) | Refer to HR — they hold the official record | Your log should be minimal; HR's file is the official record |
| Complaint documentation (initial conversation notes) | Hand to HR immediately; delete your copy | Once HR has it, you do not retain it separately |
| I-9 forms | 3 years from hire date OR 1 year from separation, whichever is longer | HR/Payroll manages; you should not have I-9s in your files |
| Investigation records | HR retains; do not maintain in manager files | These are not manager records |

The retention guidance for I-9s is worth knowing as context — it signals how seriously the government treats employment eligibility records. You will not manage I-9s directly, but if you are ever asked to help locate one, now you know where it should be and how long it should have been retained.

## The Director's Documentation Hygiene Practices

**Weekly review**: spend 10 minutes each week reviewing your manager's log. Are there conversations from the past week that should have been documented and were not? Is there a follow-up date you set that is approaching?

**Consistent format**: the format does not matter as much as the consistency. If you use the same structure every time, retrieval is fast and the record reads as reliable — not as reconstructed to serve a particular outcome.

**Controlled storage**: your manager's log is not a shared document, not a forwarded email chain, not a sticky note stack. It is a stored, retrievable system that you can access from any device and that is not visible to your employees.

**Before year-end or performance cycles**: review your documentation from the past cycle. If you are giving an employee a performance rating — positive or negative — you should be able to point to specific documented conversations that support it. If you cannot, your memory is the only evidence, and memory is not a defense.

## The Interview Signal

When a Director-level interviewer asks you to describe how you handled a difficult personnel situation, they are running a specific assessment: did you manage this with professional discipline, or did you manage it informally and get lucky?

The signal they are looking for: "Here is what I observed and documented. Here is when I involved HR and why. Here is the outcome and how I verified it."

The signal they are not looking for: "I had a conversation with him and we worked it out."

The ability to describe your documentation practices — what you captured, when, where it was stored, and how you used it to make decisions — signals that you operate at Director-level maturity. It tells the interviewer that when something goes wrong on their team after they hire you, you will manage it in a way that protects the company, the employee, and the integrity of the process.

That is the professional protection system. Build it before you need it.
