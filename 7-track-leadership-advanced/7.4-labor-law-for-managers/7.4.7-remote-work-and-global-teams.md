# Chapter 07 — Remote Work and Global Teams: The Legal Complexity Directors Inherit

> **Disclaimer**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner.

---

## Why This Is Now a Core Director Competency

Remote work did not create multi-state employment complexity — it exposed it at scale. Before 2020, most engineering teams were co-located enough that HR and legal could manage the exceptions. After 2020, the exceptions became the norm. A Director managing a distributed team today is de facto operating across multiple legal jurisdictions, each with its own wage laws, leave requirements, privacy rules, and termination standards.

The core principle: the law that governs an employee's employment is generally the law of the state where the employee works — not where the company is headquartered, and not where you as the manager sit. This single fact has more implications for Directors than almost anything else in employment law.

---

## Why State Law Variations Are Not a Legal Team Problem Anymore

When you approve a hire in a new state, you are not just adding a headcount — you are adding a jurisdiction. That jurisdiction brings:
- Different overtime rules (some states have daily overtime, not just weekly)
- Different paid leave requirements (sick leave, family leave, bereavement)
- Different expense reimbursement obligations
- Different non-compete enforceability
- Different WARN Act thresholds
- Different pay transparency requirements

HR cannot manage this retroactively. They need to be in the process before you tell a candidate "yes." A Director who verbally commits to a hire in a new state — particularly one where the company has not previously had employees — may be making commitments the company is not legally or operationally prepared to fulfill.

**Your rule of thumb**: before approving any hire where the employee will work in a state your team has not previously had employees in, get HR clearance first. The question to ask HR is not "is this allowed?" — it is "are we registered to employ people there, and what do we need to set up?"

## The California Difference: High Stakes, High Specificity

California operates as a distinct legal environment. If you have team members working in California — or are considering hiring there — these rules apply to those employees regardless of your company's home state.

**What makes California distinctive for engineering team Directors:**

**At-will employment with strong implied contract protections**: California is at-will, but courts have historically been receptive to implied contract claims — situations where an employer's handbook, manager statements, or established practice created a reasonable expectation of continued employment. Be careful with language like "you'll have a long career here" or "as long as you perform, you'll always have a place."

**Expense reimbursement mandate**: California Labor Code Section 2802 requires employers to reimburse employees for all necessary expenditures incurred in performing their job duties. This is a legal requirement, not a policy option. For remote workers in California, this includes proportional home internet, personal cell phone use for work, and any equipment the company requires but does not provide. Directors managing California remote employees should ensure their team members are not bearing work costs out-of-pocket.

**Non-compete agreements**: largely unenforceable in California with very limited exceptions. If your company's employment agreement includes a non-compete clause, it is generally not enforceable for California employees. This also means that when you are hiring engineers from California-based competitors, the non-compete they signed is likely unenforceable — though you still need legal's assessment before acting on that assumption.

**Meal and rest break requirements**: non-exempt California employees are entitled to a 30-minute unpaid meal break for shifts over 5 hours and a paid 10-minute rest break for every 4 hours worked. If your non-exempt engineers in California are working through meals during crunch periods, the company faces premium pay liability.

**California WARN Act**: lower thresholds than the federal act. Applies to companies with 75+ employees, and the trigger is 50+ employees laid off in a 30-day period (versus federal thresholds that are higher). California also requires notice to employees 60 days in advance.

**CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act)**: any engineering team handling California consumer data — which includes most software companies with California users — has obligations around data access, deletion, and transparency. This is less a Director's operational concern and more a product/engineering concern, but Directors managing data-adjacent teams should understand the regulatory environment their work sits in.

## New York: Second-Most Consequential State for Engineering Teams

New York — and New York City specifically — has created a distinct employment law environment:

- **Pay transparency**: New York City employers with 4+ employees must list a good-faith salary range in all job postings for NYC-based roles. This includes remote roles that can be performed from NYC.
- **Paid leave**: New York has robust paid family leave (up to 12 weeks at 67% of salary), separate from FMLA
- **NYC Human Rights Law**: broader protected classes than federal law, including protections based on arrest record, credit history, and status as a domestic violence victim
- **Non-compete reform**: New York has been actively legislating to restrict non-compete enforceability — verify current status with HR/legal before relying on any non-compete for a New York employee

## Texas and Other Employer-Friendly States: Federal Law Still Applies

Texas, Florida, and several other states provide fewer state-level employee protections. However:
- Federal law (FLSA, Title VII, ADA, FMLA, NLRA) still applies fully
- This is not a reason to treat employees in these states differently — consistency is a legal and ethical requirement
- The absence of state protections does not change the Director's documentation and process standards

## What to Do Before Approving a Remote Hire in a New State

This is a process, not a checkbox:

1. Identify the candidate's intended work location — not just city, but home address if they will be working from home
2. Before extending an offer, notify HR of the state and ask for clearance
3. HR confirms: company is registered to do business there, payroll is set up, benefits are compliant, state-specific leave policies are in place
4. Legal confirms: any employment agreement terms (non-compete, arbitration, IP assignment) are enforceable in that state
5. Only then does an offer go out

If you are working from a hiring plan with targets in states your company has not employed in before, build in 4-6 weeks of lead time for HR/legal setup before the hire date.

## Remote Work Agreements: Why They Exist and What Belongs in Them

A remote work agreement is not just an HR formality — it establishes the legal record of where, how, and under what conditions an employee is working. This matters because it:
- Documents the work location (relevant for state law, H1B LCA compliance, tax nexus)
- Sets expectations for data security obligations
- Clarifies equipment ownership and return expectations
- Establishes the employer's right to monitor work systems (without creating privacy violations)

What should be in a remote work agreement:
- Designated primary work location (specific address)
- Confirmation that location changes require HR approval
- Data security obligations (VPN, screen lock, clean desk)
- Equipment inventory and return conditions
- Monitoring acknowledgment (if applicable — see below)
- Confirmation of compliance with applicable state law

Directors should not create remote work agreements from scratch — that is HR's domain. But you should know that they need to exist for your remote employees, and that informal arrangements ("she just works from home, no paperwork") create gaps.

## Employee Monitoring and Privacy: The Tension You Need to Navigate Carefully

There are legitimate business reasons to monitor employee activity on company systems: security, compliance, productivity management. There are also significant legal constraints on how this monitoring can be conducted, and those constraints vary significantly by state.

**California**: employees have a constitutional right to privacy that extends into the workplace. Monitoring of personal communications, even on company devices, can be challenged. Any monitoring program must be disclosed to employees and should be reviewed by legal before implementation.

**New York**: New York law requires employers to provide written notice to employees before monitoring their electronic communications. This includes email, internet usage, and phone calls.

**General principle**: any monitoring tool — productivity trackers, screen recording, keystroke logging, continuous video — requires HR and legal review before deployment. A Director who deploys a monitoring tool unilaterally, even with good intentions, may be creating a privacy violation that exposes the company to claims.

The question to ask HR before implementing any monitoring: "What disclosure obligations do we have for employees in each state on this team, and has legal reviewed this tool?"

## International Teams: The Employer of Record Structure

If you manage engineers in other countries — common for engineering Directors at T-Mobile scale — you are almost certainly not their direct legal employer. Most large US companies employ international workers through an **Employer of Record (EOR)**: a local entity in the employee's country that handles legal employment, payroll, benefits, and compliance under local law.

**What Directors need to understand about the EOR structure:**

- **The EOR is the legal employer** — your authority over these employees flows through the EOR's contractual relationship with your company, not through a direct employment relationship
- **Termination is not at-will internationally** — in the UK, India, much of Europe, and many other jurisdictions, employment cannot be terminated without notice periods, statutory severance, and often procedural requirements (formal performance improvement process, consultation). A Director who says "we're going to let this person go next week" without understanding the local notice requirements may be creating liability
- **Notice periods are real and long** — a 3-month notice period for a senior engineer in the UK is standard. "Two weeks" is a US concept that does not export
- **Works councils in Europe** — in Germany, France, and other EU countries, employers may be required to consult with a works council (employee representative body) before making significant staffing changes. This applies to layoffs, restructuring, and sometimes significant role changes

**Performance management internationally:**
- Many countries require documented performance warnings, an improvement period, and consultation before termination is permitted
- The documentation standard is higher than US practice — in some jurisdictions, failure to follow the prescribed process means the termination is automatically unfair, regardless of the underlying performance issue
- Always involve HR (and the EOR) before beginning any performance management process for an international team member

**GDPR for European team members:**
- Any performance documentation, investigation records, or people data about employees covered by GDPR must be handled with additional care
- Data access rights: employees may have the right to request access to documentation you have created about them
- Data minimization: collect only what is necessary for the documented purpose
- In practice: any investigation notes, performance records, or HR communications involving EU-based employees should be handled in coordination with your privacy/legal team

## The T-Mobile Context

A large US company with a distributed remote workforce across multiple states — the compliance complexity described in this chapter is not theoretical. When you are hiring across state lines, managing engineers in India, or building teams that span California, Washington, Texas, and New York simultaneously, you are operating in a multi-jurisdictional legal environment every week.

The Director's role in this environment is not to know every state's employment law. It is to:
1. Know that these differences exist and are material
2. Know the escalation path (HR before hire approval, HR before termination, HR before monitoring implementation)
3. Ask the right questions early enough that HR/legal can answer them before commitments are made
