# Chapter 3: Protected Classes and Discrimination — What Directors Cannot Afford to Not Know

> **DISCLAIMER**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner. Nothing here constitutes legal advice.

---

## Why This Is a Director-Level Risk, Not Just an HR Problem

Discrimination claims do not begin in HR. They begin in 1:1 conversations, in hiring panels, in performance reviews, in promotion decisions, in project assignments, and in the quiet patterns of who gets opportunities and who does not. By the time a claim reaches HR or an attorney, the evidence has already been created — in emails, Slack messages, meeting notes, and the memories of the people in the room.

Directors are the authors of that evidence. The question asked by investigators is not "did the Director intend to discriminate?" Intent is difficult to prove and rarely the legal standard. The question is: "Did the Director treat similarly situated employees differently based on a protected characteristic?" That question is answerable with documentation, patterns, and testimony — and it is answered long before any legal proceeding begins.

Understanding protected classes is not about walking on eggshells. It is about applying the same standard to everyone, documenting the basis for every consequential decision, and recognizing the specific phrases and practices that create legal exposure even when the underlying intent is neutral.

---

## Federal Protected Classes: The Legal Landscape

Federal law protects specific characteristics from discriminatory employment decisions. These protections apply to hiring, firing, compensation, promotion, assignment, training, and any other term or condition of employment.

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964):** Race, color, religion, sex, and national origin. This is the foundation.

**The Pregnancy Discrimination Act (1978):** Extends sex discrimination protection to pregnancy, childbirth, and related medical conditions. An employee cannot be demoted, reassigned, or passed over for promotion because they are pregnant or recently gave birth.

**The Age Discrimination in Employment Act (1967):** Protects employees and applicants age 40 and over from discrimination based on age. Note: the ADEA protects older workers, not younger ones. Favoring a 55-year-old over a 45-year-old is not ADEA discrimination.

**The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990):** Prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with a disability — defined as a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity. "Qualified" means the person can perform the essential functions of the job with or without reasonable accommodation.

**Title II of the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (2008):** Prohibits using genetic information in employment decisions and restricts employers from requesting genetic information.

**The Bostock Decision (2020):** The Supreme Court held in Bostock v. Clayton County that discrimination based on sexual orientation or gender identity constitutes sex discrimination under Title VII. This is now settled federal law.

**State law additions:** Many states add protected classes beyond the federal floor — marital status, military status, credit history (in some contexts), criminal history (in some jurisdictions), political affiliation. California, New York, and Illinois are consistently more expansive. If you manage teams in those states, treat state law as the operative standard.

## The Two Types of Discrimination Directors Must Understand

### Disparate Treatment: Intentional Different Treatment

Disparate treatment is the form most people recognize: treating someone differently because of a protected characteristic. Refusing to promote a qualified woman while promoting equally qualified men. Assigning the most tedious work consistently to employees of one national origin. Setting more demanding performance standards for employees over 50 than for younger employees.

Disparate treatment requires proof of intent — but intent can be inferred from patterns, statements, and the absence of a non-discriminatory explanation. You do not have to say "I didn't promote her because she's a woman" for a disparate treatment claim to succeed. If all the evidence points in that direction and you cannot articulate a legitimate, documented reason for the decision, the inference stands.

**The Director's practice:** Before any consequential decision — promotion, PIP, project assignment, compensation adjustment — ask whether you could defend this decision with documented, performance-based criteria that apply equally to all similarly situated employees. If yes, document that reasoning. If no, pause and involve your HRBP.

### Disparate Impact: The One Directors Often Miss

Disparate impact is discrimination through facially neutral policies that disproportionately disadvantage a protected class — even without discriminatory intent. This is the type of discrimination that hides in hiring criteria, promotion thresholds, and performance standards.

**Classic examples in engineering organizations:**

A job requirement of a 4-year computer science degree from an accredited university may have disparate impact on candidates from certain national origin groups who received equivalent education internationally or through non-traditional paths. If the degree requirement cannot be shown to be a business necessity for the actual performance of the role, it creates disparate impact exposure.

A promotion criterion of "must be willing to relocate on short notice" may disproportionately screen out women with primary caregiving responsibilities. If relocation is not genuinely required for the role, the criterion creates disparate impact exposure.

A performance evaluation rubric that relies heavily on "visibility" or "executive presence" — without defining those terms precisely — may systematically disadvantage employees from cultural backgrounds where self-promotion norms differ. Vague criteria that give evaluators unchecked discretion consistently produce disparate impact in practice.

**The Director's practice:** When setting hiring criteria, promotion requirements, or evaluation standards, ask whether each criterion is genuinely necessary for job performance. If a criterion is traditional rather than necessary — "we've always required a CS degree" — challenge it. Unnecessary criteria that happen to screen out protected class members are legally vulnerable.

## What Directors Cannot Say or Ask

The following are examples of phrases, questions, and topics that create legal exposure. This is not an exhaustive list — it is a representative set that illustrates the categories of risk.

### In interviews
- Marital status: "Are you married?" "Do you have a partner?" Even indirect questions: "Would your spouse be OK with the travel requirements?"
- Family plans: "Do you have kids?" "Are you planning to have children?"
- Age: "What year did you graduate?" "How long have you been in the industry?" (the latter can be asked but the answer should not be a factor in the decision)
- Country of origin: "Where are you from originally?" "Where did you grow up?" (You can ask whether someone is authorized to work in the US; you cannot ask about citizenship or national origin)
- Disability: "Do you have any health conditions?" "Have you had any surgeries?" "Will you need any accommodations?" (Accommodation discussion happens after a conditional offer, not during the interview)
- Religion: "Do you attend religious services?" "Are there days you cannot work?" (You can tell a candidate the work schedule and ask if they can meet it; you cannot probe religion)

### In 1:1 conversations and informal settings
- "When are you planning to have more kids?" — this is pregnancy and sex discrimination territory
- "Are you planning to retire soon?" — age discrimination territory even if asked casually
- "How is your health?" — disability territory if followed by any work-related inference
- "Your English has really improved" — national origin and race territory
- Any comment connecting a protected characteristic to performance, reliability, or future potential

### In performance discussions
- "People like you often struggle with this kind of work" — any inference about protected class
- "I think you'd be more comfortable in a role with less pressure" — can imply disability or age bias
- "The team needs someone who fits our culture" — without defining culture in terms of behaviors and job functions, this is a proxy for race, national origin, religion, or other protected classes

## When an Employee Voluntarily Discloses Protected Class Information

Employees sometimes share information about their protected class status — pregnancy, disability, religion — without being asked. This is common and should not cause alarm. The correct response follows a pattern:

1. **Acknowledge** — not the protected class characteristic, but the fact that the employee chose to share something personal. "I appreciate you telling me."
2. **Redirect to work implications** — "If there are any adjustments to your work situation that would help, I want to make sure we address those. Let's make sure HR is looped in so we're handling this correctly."
3. **Document that the disclosure was unsolicited** — your notes should reflect that the employee raised this topic, not you. This protects you if the disclosure later becomes relevant to a claim.

What you should not do: probe for additional information, express personal opinions about the characteristic disclosed, or treat the disclosure as relevant to the employee's performance or reliability without an ADA-interactive-process framework in place.

## The Reasonable Accommodation Obligation

When an employee discloses a disability that affects their ability to perform their job, the interactive process begins. This is a legal obligation — not optional, not at the manager's discretion.

The Director's role in the interactive process is narrow and important: **notify HR immediately**. Do not determine on your own what accommodations are appropriate. Do not agree to accommodations without HR and potentially legal involvement. Do not deny a request for accommodation without that same involvement. The interactive process is a structured, legally defined conversation between the employee, HR, and sometimes legal counsel — you participate in it as a source of information about the job's requirements, not as the decision-maker.

What you should never do: tell an employee "we can't accommodate that," "your disability doesn't affect your ability to do this job," or "accommodations aren't available for your role" — any of those statements, made without the interactive process, is legal exposure.

## Pattern Recognition: The Self-Audit

Discriminatory patterns in management practice often develop without conscious intent. The following are patterns that create disparate treatment exposure — Directors should self-audit periodically:

**Opportunity allocation:** Do stretch assignments, high-visibility projects, and leadership opportunities flow consistently to the same demographic subset of your team? If the recipients of your most developmental opportunities look similar in terms of age, gender, race, or national origin, that pattern is legally significant even if each individual decision felt merit-based.

**Feedback quality:** Is the feedback you give equally specific and actionable across your team? Vague positive feedback for some employees and specific critical feedback for others — when divided along protected class lines — is evidence of differential treatment.

**Discipline consistency:** If two employees commit similar performance failures and one receives formal documentation while the other receives informal coaching, and the difference correlates with a protected class, you have a disparate treatment problem regardless of your intent.

**The "me too" evidence problem:** A pattern of similar complaints from multiple employees against the same manager — or about the same policy — is legally significant. Courts treat a pattern of complaints differently than an isolated incident. If you have received informal complaints about the same issue from multiple team members, that pattern is information you are obligated to surface to HR, not manage privately.

## Chapter Summary

- Federal protected classes include race, color, religion, sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity), national origin, age (40+), disability, and genetic information. State law often adds more.
- Disparate treatment — intentional different treatment based on protected class — is the obvious form of discrimination. But disparate impact — facially neutral policies with disproportionate effect — is the one Directors most commonly create without knowing it.
- Hiring criteria, promotion thresholds, and evaluation standards should all be tested for whether they are genuinely necessary for job performance. Unnecessary criteria that screen out protected classes create legal exposure.
- There are specific questions you cannot ask in interviews, specific phrases that create liability in performance conversations, and specific responses required when an employee voluntarily discloses protected class information.
- Self-audit your patterns of opportunity allocation, feedback quality, and discipline application. Consistent disparate outcomes across protected class lines are evidence of discrimination regardless of intent.
- The interactive process for disability accommodation begins when an employee discloses — and your role is to notify HR immediately, not to determine the outcome yourself.
