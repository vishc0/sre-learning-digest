# Chapter 05 — H1B Visa Considerations for Managers: Managing What You've Lived

> **Disclaimer**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner.

---

## Why This Chapter Exists

If you have been on H1B yourself — renewal anxiety, the USCIS receipt notice you refresh obsessively, the moment you understood that your job and your legal right to stay in the country are the same document — then you already know something most managers never will: what it feels like to be in this situation.

That lived experience is an asset. When you manage H1B holders, you have more empathy and more contextual intelligence than the average manager. You also carry more responsibility, because you understand the stakes in a way that makes ignorance inexcusable.

This chapter is about converting your personal experience into professional competence as a manager — understanding the operational implications, knowing exactly when to involve HR, and using your empathy to have honest conversations your employees cannot have with most managers.

---

## What H1B Status Means Operationally for a Manager

The H1B visa is not a general work authorization. It is authorization to perform a specific job, for a specific employer, at a specific location, at or above a specified wage. That specificity is the source of most of the complexity you will encounter as a manager.

**The three axes of H1B specificity:**

1. **Employer** — the visa is tied to the company that filed it. If your employee changes employers, a new H1B petition is required (with some transfer provisions, but not automatic continuity).

2. **Role** — the H1B petition describes the job: title, duties, and the prevailing wage for that role in that location. A material change to duties can mean the person is no longer working in the role that was petitioned.

3. **Geographic location** — the petition specifies where the work is performed. Remote work across state lines, office relocations, or significant travel patterns can create compliance implications.

Your job as a Director is not to know the precise H1B rules for each of these axes. Your job is to know that these axes exist — so that when you contemplate any change to a team member's role, compensation, or work location, your first call is to HR, not your last.

## Changes That May Trigger H1B Notification or Amendment Requirements

The following situations generally require HR to assess whether an amended H1B petition or USCIS notification is needed. This is not an exhaustive legal list — it is a manager's awareness list.

| Change | H1B Risk | Your Action |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Job title change with materially different duties | High | Notify HR before communicating to employee |
| Significant pay cut or restructured compensation | Medium-High | HR must evaluate LCA compliance |
| Change in primary work location (city/state) | High | H1B LCA is location-specific |
| Layoff or termination | High | HR must manage the timing and process |
| Role restructuring that changes core job functions | Medium-High | HR/legal must evaluate |
| Short-term project assignment in a different location | Low-Medium | Depends on duration and nature; still worth flagging |

The rule of thumb: if you are contemplating a change significant enough that you would update the job description, it is significant enough to warrant an HR conversation about H1B implications first.

## The LCA (Labor Condition Application): What Directors Need to Know

When a company files an H1B petition, it must first obtain a Labor Condition Application from the Department of Labor. The LCA certifies that:
- The employer will pay the employee the prevailing wage for that role in that location
- The employment of the H1B worker will not adversely affect the working conditions of U.S. workers

The employer is required to maintain a **Public Access File** at each work location covered by the LCA. This file must be available to the public and specifies the wage level at which the employee is being employed.

**What this means for Directors in compensation discussions:**
- You cannot agree to pay an H1B holder below the LCA-specified prevailing wage
- Any compensation change — increase or decrease — for an H1B holder must go through HR to confirm it does not create an LCA violation
- This is not an exception to your compensation authority; it is a required checkpoint

Never promise or confirm a compensation outcome for an H1B holder without HR sign-off. Not because you lack authority, but because the legal obligation sits at the employer level and HR is the compliance checkpoint.

## Layoffs and H1B: The Clock Starts the Moment You Notify

This is the highest-stakes intersection of your managerial role and H1B law. When an H1B holder is laid off, their authorized status does not end instantly — but it does begin winding down. Under current USCIS rules, there is a 60-day grace period after termination during which the former employee can seek a new employer, change status, or make other arrangements. After 60 days, they are out of status.

What this means for Directors:

**HR must be in the loop before you say anything.** The moment you notify the employee is the moment the clock starts. A Director who informs an H1B holder of a layoff without HR coordination has potentially shortened the employee's grace period without the employee having been given the information they need to act.

HR's job in this situation is to ensure:
- The employee is notified of their 60-day grace period
- Legal resources are made available (some companies provide immigration attorney consultations during RIF)
- The layoff paperwork accurately reflects the termination date

Your job is to ensure HR is involved in the planning before the conversation, not after.

## The I-140 Portability Rule: A Conversation You Can Have That Most Managers Cannot

If your employee is on the green card path — which most long-term H1B holders are — they may have an I-140 (Immigrant Petition for Alien Workers) approved. This matters to you as their manager for one specific reason:

**After an I-140 has been approved for 180 days or more, the employee can change employers without losing their priority date — their place in the green card queue.**

The priority date is the single most valuable thing a green card applicant has, particularly for employees from India or China, where the backlog can span decades. If someone has a priority date from 2010, it is irreplaceable. The I-140 portability rule means they can leave an employer and take that priority date with them to the next company, as long as:
- The I-140 was approved for 180+ days before the job change
- The new job is in the same or a similar occupational classification

Why does this matter to you as a manager?

Because an H1B holder with a 180+ day approved I-140 has meaningfully more career mobility than one without. They may be weighing your compensation offer, your promotion decision, or your restructuring against the risk of what happens to their I-140. When you understand this, you can have a more honest conversation about career planning, timing, and their actual decision calculus.

You cannot — and should not — make immigration decisions for them. But you can acknowledge that you understand what they are navigating.

## What Managers Must Not Do with H1B Employees

| Action | Why It Is Wrong |
|--------|-----------------|
| Make role change decisions without HR | You cannot assess the H1B impact; HR can |
| Conduct layoffs without HR's advance H1B planning | The 60-day clock starts on your word |
| Promise a role change "won't affect their visa" | You do not have the legal knowledge to make that promise |
| Use reduced negotiating leverage as a management tool | This is both ethically wrong and creates legal exposure |
| Ignore the visa dimension during performance management | A PIP or termination has immigration consequences; HR must be aware |

On the last point: a Director who places an H1B holder on a performance improvement plan without informing HR is not managing — they are creating an undisclosed legal risk for the company and leaving the employee without information they need.

## The Empathy Advantage — Used Professionally

You know what it is like to sit in a 1:1 with a manager who holds your employment status in their decisions. You know the calculation of whether to raise a career concern when raising it might put you in an ambiguous status position. You know the anxiety of an H1B renewal during a difficult performance cycle.

Use that knowledge to:
- Create psychological safety around career conversations for H1B holders on your team
- Be proactive in looping in HR when changes are contemplated, so the employee is not caught off-guard
- Have frank conversations about timing — "if we are considering a role change, I want to make sure we understand the full picture for you before we move"

Do not use it to:
- Cross into immigration advice (that is the attorney's job)
- Make promises about visa outcomes
- Blur the manager/employee boundary by sharing your personal immigration experience in ways that undermine your authority

---

## Escalation Standard

Any people decision affecting an H1B holder — role change, compensation adjustment, location change, performance action, reduction in force — must involve HR before execution. Not as a formality. Because the legal obligations are real, the consequences of getting it wrong fall on the employee first, and you are the person who is supposed to protect them from that.
