# Protected Classes Quick Reference

> **Educational content only. Consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner for specific situations.**

---

## Purpose

This reference is a fast-lookup guide for Directors who need to understand which employment laws apply, which characteristics are protected, and what that means operationally. It is not a legal treatise — it is a field guide.

**Core operational principle**: Every employment decision (hiring, firing, promoting, paying, scheduling, assigning) must be made on legitimate, documented, job-related business reasons. When a decision affects someone in a protected class and the business reason is weak or undocumented, that is where legal exposure concentrates.

## Federal Law Quick Reference Table

| Law | What It Covers | Employer Size Threshold | Protected Classes / Characteristics | Enforcement Agency | What Directors Should Know |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964, amended 1991)** | Hiring, firing, pay, promotion, job assignment, training, any term/condition of employment | 15+ employees | Race, color, religion, sex (including gender identity and sexual orientation per Bostock v. Clayton County, 2020), national origin | EEOC | The broadest federal employment discrimination law. Applies to virtually everything you do as a manager. Both disparate treatment (intentional) AND disparate impact (policy that disproportionately affects a protected group) are prohibited. |
| **Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA, 1967)** | Hiring, firing, pay, promotion — any employment action | 20+ employees | Age — protects workers 40 and older. Younger workers are not protected under ADEA. | EEOC | Cannot use age as a factor in any employment decision. Layoff selection that disproportionately affects workers 40+ carries heightened risk. Avoid any language referencing "fresh ideas," "digital native," or "long tenure" in performance or hiring decisions. |
| **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990, amended 2008)** | Hiring, firing, reasonable accommodation, access | 15+ employees | Disability (physical or mental impairment substantially limiting a major life activity); history of such impairment; being regarded as having such impairment | EEOC | Reasonable accommodation is an interactive process — you must engage, not just say no. The accommodation must be provided unless it causes "undue hardship." Never make employment decisions based on assumptions about what a person with a disability can or cannot do. |
| **Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 2008)** | Hiring, firing, pay, any employment action | 15+ employees | Genetic information (genetic tests, family medical history) | EEOC | Never request, require, or use genetic information in employment decisions. Do not ask about family medical history. Be careful about wellness programs that solicit health information — GINA compliance must be built in. |
| **Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA, 2023)** | Reasonable accommodation for pregnancy-related conditions | 15+ employees | Pregnancy, childbirth, and related medical conditions — requires accommodation even when the condition is temporary | EEOC | Newer than ADA; specifically covers pregnancy-related limitations. Accommodation requests from pregnant employees or those who recently gave birth must be treated like any other accommodation request. "Temporary" is not a reason to deny. |
| **Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA, 1978)** | Hiring, firing, leave, any employment action | 15+ employees | Pregnancy, childbirth, related medical conditions — prohibits treating pregnancy differently from other temporary medical conditions | EEOC | If your company allows medical leave for other temporary conditions, you must allow equivalent leave for pregnancy. Terminating, demoting, or reassigning a pregnant employee is high-risk territory. Always loop in HRBP before any action. |
| **National Labor Relations Act (NLRA, 1935)** | Concerted activity — employees' right to discuss wages, working conditions, and unionize | Most private employers (not covered by Railway Labor Act) — no size threshold | "Concerted activity" — employees acting together or on behalf of coworkers regarding wages, hours, or conditions | NLRB | This is the one law most managers don't think about. You cannot discipline or terminate employees for discussing their pay with coworkers, complaining collectively about working conditions, or engaging in protected concerted activity. "Don't talk about your salary" policies are often NLRA violations. |
| **Equal Pay Act (EPA, 1963)** | Pay — requires equal pay for substantially equal work | Most employers — no size threshold | Sex (pay differences between men and women doing substantially equal work) | EEOC | Pay disparities between men and women doing the same job require a documented, legitimate business justification (seniority, merit, production-based system). "That's what they asked for" is not a sufficient defense in many states. |
| **Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA, 1993)** | Leave — up to 12 weeks unpaid, job-protected | 50+ employees within 75 miles | Serious health condition (employee or immediate family member), childbirth, adoption, military family leave | DOL | Managers cannot penalize employees for taking FMLA leave. Cannot count FMLA absences in attendance tracking. Cannot use FMLA leave as a factor in layoff selection. Cannot deny leave to eligible employees. |
| **Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA, 1994)** | Military leave and reemployment rights | All employers — no size threshold | Military service (active duty, reserves, National Guard) | DOL / Veterans' Employment and Training Service | Employees on military leave must be restored to their position (or comparable position) on return. Cannot discriminate based on past, present, or future military service. |
| **Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA, 1986)** | Work authorization verification (I-9) and prohibition on citizenship discrimination | I-9: all employers. Citizenship discrimination: 4+ employees | National origin, citizenship status — prohibits discrimination against work-authorized individuals based on national origin or citizenship | DOJ (Office of Special Counsel) | I-9 must be completed for all employees. Cannot demand more documents than required or reject valid documents based on national origin. Cannot refuse to hire based on citizenship status if the person is work-authorized. |

## Employer Size Thresholds

| Employee Count | What Kicks In |
|---|---|
| **All employers** | FLSA (minimum wage, overtime), USERRA (military leave), IRCA (I-9, work authorization), NLRA (concerted activity) |
| **4+ employees** | IRCA citizenship/national origin discrimination provisions |
| **15+ employees** | Title VII (race, sex, religion, national origin), ADA (disability), GINA (genetic information), PWFA (pregnant workers accommodation), PDA (pregnancy) |
| **20+ employees** | ADEA (age 40+) |
| **50+ employees within 75 miles** | FMLA (12 weeks job-protected leave) |
| **100+ employees** | WARN Act notice requirements (mass layoff / plant closing advance notice) |

## California FEHA Additional Protected Classes

California's Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) covers all classes in federal law and adds:

| Additional Protected Class | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Marital status** | Cannot discriminate based on whether someone is married, single, divorced, etc. |
| **Sexual orientation** | Explicit protection (also now covered federally under Bostock, but FEHA was earlier and broader) |
| **Gender identity and gender expression** | Explicit and broad protections including appearance and dress |
| **Medical condition** | Broader than ADA — includes cancer and genetic characteristics |
| **Political activities or affiliations** | Cannot discriminate based on political activity outside work |
| **Military and veteran status** | Additional to USERRA |
| **Immigration status** | Cannot use immigration status as a basis for discrimination |
| **Source of income** (housing context) | Relevant if your company provides housing assistance |

**FEHA applies to employers with 5+ employees** — lower threshold than most federal laws.

## New York State Human Rights Law (NYSHRL) Additional Protections

New York State's Human Rights Law adds:

| Additional Protected Class | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Age** | Covers all ages — no 40+ floor like ADEA |
| **Marital status** | Same as California |
| **Sexual orientation and gender identity** | Explicit since GENDA (2019) |
| **Domestic violence victim status** | Cannot discriminate against victims |
| **Criminal history** | Limitations on using conviction records; Article 23-A applies |
| **Citizenship and immigration status** | Explicit protection |
| **Reproductive health decision-making** | Cannot discriminate based on reproductive health decisions |

**NYSHRL applies to employers with 4+ employees** for most provisions; sexual harassment protections apply regardless of size.

## Practical Director-Level Summary

| Situation | What to Know |
|---|---|
| Making a hiring decision | Decision criteria must be job-related and documented. If you pass on a candidate in a protected class, have a clear, written, job-related reason. |
| Making a promotion or assignment decision | Same as hiring. "Cultural fit" is not sufficient justification — define what you mean in behavioral terms. |
| Starting a PIP or discipline | If the employee is in a protected class (everyone is in multiple), the performance record must be contemporaneous, specific, and applied consistently with how you handle similar issues for employees not in that class. |
| Selecting employees for layoff | Selection criteria must be documented, neutral, and consistently applied. Review outcomes for disparate impact on protected classes before finalizing. |
| Receiving an accommodation request | Engage the interactive process — do not say no without exploring options. Loop in HRBP. |
| Hearing employees discuss their pay | Do not intervene. NLRA protects this conversation. |
| Employee mentions a health condition | Do not probe. Refer to your HRBP about whether an accommodation process should be initiated. |
