# H1B Manager Quick Reference

> **Educational content only. Consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner for specific situations.**

---

## Purpose

H1B visa holders are among the most common non-citizen employees in US engineering organizations. As a manager, you make decisions every day — title changes, team restructuring, location changes, promotions, PIPs, layoffs — that can have profound and sometimes irreversible consequences for an H1B employee's immigration status and their ability to remain in the country.

This reference is not a substitute for immigration counsel. It is a field guide to know when to stop and call HR before proceeding.

---

## Section 1 — H1B Basics for Managers

### What the H1B visa is

The H1B is a non-immigrant work visa for "specialty occupations" — roles requiring at least a bachelor's degree in a specific field. Engineering, software development, data science, and finance roles commonly qualify.

H1B status is tied to **a specific employer, a specific job, a specific location, and a specific salary**. This is the most important thing a manager needs to internalize. Unlike a green card, the H1B is not portable to any employer. The employee's legal right to work in the US is dependent on the conditions of the petition filed on their behalf.

### Who controls it

The **employer** (legally: the "petitioner") controls the H1B. The employee (the "beneficiary") does not hold the visa independently. The employer files and maintains the petition through USCIS.

Your company's immigration team (often a law firm retained by HR) manages the H1B petition. You do not deal with USCIS directly — but your decisions trigger obligations for the immigration team.

### What the LCA is

The **Labor Condition Application (LCA)** is a document the employer files with the Department of Labor as part of the H1B process. It certifies:

- The wage being paid is at least the "prevailing wage" for that role in that geographic area
- The employment of the H1B holder does not adversely affect US workers
- Working conditions are the same as for similarly employed US workers

The LCA is specific to a **job title, wage level, and location**. When any of those change materially, the LCA may need to be updated or a new petition filed — before the change goes into effect.

## Section 2 — Change Trigger Table

Use this table before implementing any employment change affecting an H1B employee. When in doubt, call HR before acting.

| Change Type | H1B Impact | Manager Action | Timeline Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Title change only** (same duties, same pay, same location) | May require amended petition if USCIS considers it a material change | Notify HR before effective date | Give HR 4–6 weeks lead time minimum |
| **Pay increase** (within same role and location) | Usually does not require amended petition; LCA may need update if wage tier changes | Notify HR before effective date | Generally lower risk; still notify |
| **Pay decrease** | Almost always requires amended petition; cannot pay below LCA-certified prevailing wage | Do NOT implement without immigration counsel review | High — may not be permissible |
| **Location change — same metropolitan statistical area (MSA)** | Generally no amendment required; may need LCA notification | Notify HR; they will confirm whether posting required | Moderate — give HR 2–3 weeks |
| **Location change — different MSA, same state** | Almost always requires new LCA and amended petition | Notify HR immediately; do NOT direct employee to new location until cleared | High — can put employee out of status if change precedes filing |
| **Location change — different state** | Requires new LCA and likely amended petition | Notify HR immediately; do NOT implement until cleared | Very high — employee cannot work from new state until approved |
| **Work-from-home / remote work** | USCIS guidance has evolved post-COVID; remote locations may constitute new worksite locations | Notify HR for any new permanent remote work arrangement | Moderate to High depending on jurisdiction |
| **Restructuring / new duties (>50% change in job duties)** | Material change — requires amended petition | Notify HR before restructure is communicated to employee | High — may constitute unauthorized employment if duties change before filing |
| **Team reorganization (title unchanged, duties similar)** | Evaluate with immigration counsel | Notify HR to evaluate | Moderate |
| **Layoff / termination** | H1B status ends; 60-day grace period begins on termination date | Notify HR immediately; special protocol applies (see Section 4) | Very High — legal obligations upon termination |
| **Leave of absence (paid or unpaid)** | Complex — wage payment obligations continue during some leave types | Notify HR before leave begins | High — LCA wage obligations may not pause during leave |
| **Promotion (new title, significant new duties, pay increase)** | Material change — requires new or amended petition | Notify HR before announcing or implementing | High |

## Section 3 — The I-140 Portability Rule

### What the I-140 is

The I-140 is the immigration petition for permanent residency (green card). It is filed by the employer on behalf of the employee. Filing and approving an I-140 is a milestone, but it does not immediately grant the employee a green card — for many countries of birth (India in particular), the wait for an immigrant visa can be 10–20+ years due to per-country annual caps.

### The 180-day portability threshold

Under the American Competitiveness in the Twenty-First Century Act (AC21), an employee whose I-140 has been approved for 180 days or more AND whose underlying priority date is current or nearly current has **portability rights**. This means:

- They can change employers without losing their priority date (their place in the green card queue)
- They can use an H1B extension based on their approved I-140 even if working for a new employer
- They are significantly less dependent on their current employer than an H1B holder without an approved I-140

### Why this matters for career conversations

An employee who has crossed the 180-day I-140 threshold has substantially more flexibility and negotiating power — and substantially less fear of job changes — than one who has not.

An employee who is still waiting for I-140 approval, or who has been approved for fewer than 180 days, is much more tightly bound to your company. This creates a power imbalance that good managers should be aware of and should not exploit. An employee who cannot leave without losing years of immigration progress may tolerate poor management conditions that they would otherwise leave. That is not loyalty — it is constraint.

**Do not use an employee's immigration dependency as leverage in employment negotiations.** It is legal in most contexts, but it is bad leadership and creates cultural damage.

## Section 4 — Layoff Protocol for H1B Holders

### The 60-day grace period

When an H1B employee is laid off, they have a 60-day grace period (or until their visa validity date, whichever is shorter) to:
- Find a new employer willing to sponsor an H1B transfer
- Change to another valid immigration status (e.g., spouse's visa, F-1 OPT, B-2 visitor status)
- Leave the country

**The clock starts on the last day of employment** — defined as when the employment relationship terminates, not when they stop having system access.

### Pre-notification obligations (HR manages these — know they exist)

Before the notification meeting, immigration counsel should have:
- Calculated the exact last day of employment and corresponding grace period end date
- Prepared information to give the employee about their 60-day window and options
- Confirmed whether any ongoing immigration proceedings require special handling (pending I-140 amendments, premium processing underway, etc.)
- Reviewed whether any pending labor condition obligations exist

### During the notification

Confirm with HR that they have briefed (or will brief) the employee on the 60-day grace period in the notification meeting or immediately following. You do not provide immigration advice. If the employee asks you immigration questions, redirect:

> "[HRBP name] has resources prepared on the immigration-related steps and will go through those with you today."

### After the notification

- Do not revoke system access before the last day of employment without Legal/HR direction — premature access revocation can be cited as evidence of the actual termination date
- Notify immigration counsel promptly — they have USCIS notification obligations upon H1B termination
- The company has an obligation to pay for the employee's "reasonable cost of return transportation" to their home country if requested — HR/Legal manages this

### What NOT to do in an H1B layoff

- Do not communicate the termination before immigration counsel has reviewed the situation
- Do not tell the employee their visa is "cancelled" or "void" — the H1B is tied to the petition, which has a wind-down process; you are not the authority on their visa status
- Do not give immigration advice or speculate about their options
- Do not pressure the employee to leave the country immediately — the 60-day grace period is their legal right

## Section 5 — What Managers Must Never Do

These actions create legal exposure for both the company and the employee.

| Prohibited Action | Why |
|---|---|
| **Implement a location change, title change, or significant duty change without HR/immigration review** | The employee may work out of status (unauthorized employment) if conditions change before an amended petition is filed. That creates USCIS violations and can jeopardize the employee's immigration status permanently. |
| **Pay an H1B employee below the LCA-certified prevailing wage** | Wage violations under the LCA can result in DOL investigation, back pay liability, and debarment from future H1B sponsorship |
| **Threaten an employee's visa status as a management tool** | This is potential extortion and will be treated as misconduct. ("If you push back on this, I can't guarantee the company continues your H1B") |
| **Use H1B immigration dependency as leverage in performance management** | This causes the employee to sign agreements or accept conditions under duress and creates hostile work environment risk |
| **Give immigration advice without being qualified to do so** | You are not an immigration attorney. Tell the employee what you do not know and direct them to resources you can confirm. |
| **Delay notifying HR when any change in employment terms is contemplated** | The timing window for H1B amendments is not flexible — late notification can mean the change cannot be implemented lawfully |
| **Ignore an H1B employee's request for a meeting about their immigration status** | If an employee raises a question about how a business decision affects their immigration status, treat it as urgent and loop in HR immediately |

## Section 6 — Escalation Triggers

Stop and call HR **before proceeding** with any of the following:

- Any restructuring, reorg, or role change affecting an H1B employee
- Any pay action affecting an H1B employee (decrease, freeze, demotion)
- Any geographic move, remote work change, or office relocation affecting an H1B employee
- Any H1B employee initiating a leave of absence
- Any performance management action that could lead to termination of an H1B employee
- Any PIP initiation for an H1B employee
- Any termination — voluntary or involuntary — of an H1B employee
- Any H1B employee who discloses they have an approved I-140 pending with a significant queue date
- Any H1B employee who asks you about the company's immigration sponsorship obligations
- Any H1B employee who reports they have received a notice from USCIS requiring response
- Any situation where you are unsure whether an employment action requires an amended H1B petition

When in doubt: stop, call HR, wait for clearance before acting. The cost of getting this wrong is borne by the employee — potentially their ability to stay in the country — and by the company in legal liability.
