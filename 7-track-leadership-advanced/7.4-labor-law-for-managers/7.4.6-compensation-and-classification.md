# Chapter 06 — Compensation, Classification, and the Legal Framework Directors Navigate

> **Disclaimer**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner.

---

## Why This Matters Before the How

Compensation is where Directors most commonly create legal exposure — not through malice, but through incomplete knowledge. A Director who misclassifies a role, approves a pay decision that creates an equity gap, or handles a RIF without understanding the legal guardrails is not being aggressive; they are being uninformed. The purpose of this chapter is to close that gap.

The framework is straightforward: you are not the compliance officer. You are the decision-maker who needs to know enough to ask the right questions, involve the right people, and avoid the category of mistakes that result in class-action wage claims or EEOC investigations.

---

## FLSA Exempt vs. Non-Exempt: The Foundational Classification

The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) governs minimum wage and overtime. The classification that matters most to engineering Directors is **exempt vs. non-exempt**, because it determines whether your employees are entitled to overtime pay.

**Non-exempt employees** are entitled to 1.5x their regular rate for all hours worked over 40 in a workweek. If a non-exempt engineer works 50 hours in a crunch week and is paid straight salary, the company owes back pay for those 10 hours at the overtime rate — for every week that happened.

**Exempt employees** are not entitled to overtime. To be lawfully classified as exempt, an employee must meet two tests simultaneously:

1. **Salary basis test**: currently paid at least $684/week on a salary basis (verify the current threshold with HR — the DOL has proposed increases and thresholds change). This is a federal floor; some states set it higher.

2. **Duties test**: the actual job must fall into an exempt category. For engineering teams, the relevant categories are:

| Category | Who Qualifies | Common Misapplication |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| Executive | Manages 2+ employees, primary duty is management | Misapplying to a tech lead with informal influence but no direct reports |
| Professional | Requires advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning, customarily acquired by specialized education | Generally fits software engineers with CS degrees or equivalent |
| Computer Employee | Systems analyst, programmer, software engineer — paid $27.63+/hour or $684+/week salary | Most applies here for SWEs; does NOT cover IT support roles automatically |
| Administrative | Office work related to management or business operations, exercises discretion and independent judgment | Rarely applies to engineers; sometimes misapplied to senior technical program managers |

**The misclassification risk**: if you reclassify a role, create a new role during a restructuring, or hire into a newly defined position without an FLSA analysis, you may inadvertently create an exempt classification for what is legally a non-exempt role. The consequence is back overtime wages for every affected employee for the entire period of misclassification — plus penalties.

**Your escalation trigger**: any new role definition, any role restructuring, any reclassification from contractor to employee — run it by HR before finalizing the job description.

## Pay Equity: The Law Has Expanded Beyond Equal Pay Act

The federal Equal Pay Act (1963) requires equal pay for substantially equal work, regardless of sex. That was the floor. The current landscape is considerably more complex.

**State-level expansions** now extend pay equity obligations to additional protected classes (race, ethnicity, age, disability in many states) and impose transparency requirements that did not exist a decade ago:

- **California**: pay equity law covers race and ethnicity in addition to sex; requires employers to affirmatively correct identified disparities
- **Colorado**: requires pay range disclosure in all job postings, including internal postings
- **New York City**: requires salary range disclosure in job postings for roles that can be performed in NYC (which includes remote roles based in NYC)
- **Washington**: similar pay transparency requirements with salary ranges required in postings

**What this means for Directors hiring or setting compensation:**

You are operating in a pay transparency world, whether or not your personal state has passed legislation yet. Candidates are comparing your offer to the posted range. Employees are comparing their compensation to publicly posted ranges for similar roles. Pay decisions that were defensible as individually negotiated outcomes are increasingly visible.

**The equity framework for Directors:**

Before finalizing a compensation offer or adjustment:
1. Confirm the role's compensation band with HR
2. Apply the band consistently — do not negotiate exceptions informally
3. If a candidate asks for above-band compensation, that request goes through HR, not a handshake agreement

**When a team member raises a pay equity concern:**
- Listen and document the conversation (date, substance, what was said)
- Do not investigate informally — do not ask the peer they are comparing themselves to what they make
- Do not make any commitment or dismiss the concern
- Escalate to your HRBP the same day
- Your role ends at the escalation; the HRBP and potentially legal own the investigation

A Director who informally investigates a pay equity claim — even with good intentions — can taint the employer's defense in any subsequent legal proceeding.

## Reductions in Force: The Legal Scaffolding

Layoffs are high-stakes legally, and the legal risk is not just employment law — it is pattern-of-discrimination risk. A RIF that appears neutral but disproportionately impacts employees over 40, or employees of a particular race, is legally actionable even if no individual selection was discriminatory.

**The sequence that protects everyone:**

1. **Document the business rationale before selection** — "reducing headcount in the infrastructure platform team by 2 FTE due to consolidation of platform responsibilities following the migration completion." The rationale must exist and be documented before names are attached to it.

2. **Apply selection criteria consistently** — if the criteria is "least tenure in the role," apply it to everyone in scope. If it is "skill set alignment with forward roadmap," document how each candidate was assessed against the same rubric. Inconsistent application is the primary source of wrongful termination claims in RIFs.

3. **Disparate impact analysis** — before notifications go out, HR and/or legal should run an analysis of whether the selected population has a statistically disproportionate representation of any protected class. This is not your job — but you should ask HR whether it has been done. If you are not getting that assurance, escalate before proceeding.

4. **WARN Act awareness** — for companies with 100+ employees, the federal Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act requires 60 days advance written notice to employees, state agencies, and local governments for:
   - Plant closings affecting 50+ employees
   - Mass layoffs of 100+ employees (or 33% of the workforce) at a single site in a 30-day window
   - Some states have stricter thresholds (California WARN applies at 75 employees and 50+ affected)
   
   The 60-day window is not a suggestion. If a RIF is large enough to trigger WARN, the company cannot accelerate the notification timeline without financial penalties. Directors who agree to accelerated timelines without understanding this create liability.

**Individual layoffs vs. RIF:** even a single-employee layoff requires consistent documentation. "We are eliminating the position" is a business decision; "this person's performance was the reason for selection" is performance management and requires a different evidentiary record. The two are different and should never be conflated in the termination conversation or the documentation.

## Wage Theft and Expense Reimbursement

Several states — California most prominently — treat failure to reimburse legitimate business expenses as a wage claim, not merely a policy violation. This distinction matters because wage claims carry statutory penalties and can be brought as class actions.

**What may be reimbursable by law (not just policy) in California and similar states:**
- Home office internet used for work (proportional reimbursement)
- Personal cell phone used for business calls or email
- Equipment purchased for remote work
- Software subscriptions required for job duties

**Director implication:** if your team members are using personal resources for work without reimbursement, and your company is not proactively offering reimbursement, raise this with HR. In states with strong wage claim laws, the exposure scales with team size and duration.

For remote teams spread across multiple states, this is not a hypothetical risk — it is an active compliance question that HR and Finance must answer, not Directors unilaterally.

## The Director's Escalation Map for Compensation

| Situation | Your Action |
|-----------|-------------|
| Candidate asks for above-band compensation | Route to HR; do not commit informally |
| Team member raises pay equity concern | Document, escalate to HRBP same day, make no commitment |
| Considering a role reclassification | HR review before any communication |
| Planning a RIF | HR/legal involvement from day one, before selection criteria are applied |
| Team member reports expense reimbursement issues | Document, route to HR and Finance |
| New role definition during restructuring | HR must confirm FLSA classification before posting |

## Interview Framing

When an interviewer asks how you handle compensation decisions or RIFs, they are not just asking about process. They are assessing whether you understand that these decisions carry legal weight, and whether you operate with the judgment that prevents legal exposure.

The signal of Director-level maturity: "Before any RIF selection is finalized, I confirm with HR that the criteria are documented, consistently applied, and have been evaluated for disparate impact. I do not treat compensation decisions as purely negotiation — I work within bands and involve HR on any exceptions."
