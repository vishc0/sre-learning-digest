# Harassment Complaint Response Playbook — What to Do in the First 24 Hours

> **Educational content only. Consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner for specific situations.**

---

## What This Playbook Protects

When a team member brings a harassment, discrimination, or hostile work environment complaint to you, your response in the first 24 hours determines:
- Whether the employee feels safe enough to continue working
- Whether the company's legal obligation to investigate is triggered and properly handed off
- Whether you personally become part of the problem or part of the solution
- Whether any subsequent adverse action against the complainant looks like retaliation

Your role is not investigator, judge, or mediator. Your role is listener, reporter, and protector.

---

## When This Activates

This playbook activates any time an employee reports to you directly any of the following:
- Sexual harassment (quid pro quo or hostile work environment)
- Racial, religious, national origin, age, disability, or any other protected-class-based harassment
- Bullying or intimidation that may rise to a hostile work environment
- Retaliation for prior protected activity (filing a complaint, taking FMLA, reporting safety concerns)
- Discrimination in any employment action (pay, assignment, promotion, opportunity)

It also activates when you **observe** behavior that may constitute any of the above, even if no one has formally reported it to you. Managers have an independent obligation to report what they observe.

---

## Immediate Actions — First 30 Minutes

### 1. Stop and give full attention
If you are in the middle of something else, stop. If this comes in via Slack or email, do not respond asynchronously — call or meet in person or video. This conversation requires your full presence.

### 2. Listen and take notes
You are not gathering evidence. You are creating a factual record for HR. Write down:
- Date and time of the conversation
- Who is present
- What the employee says, as close to verbatim as you can capture
- What happened (alleged behavior)
- Who was involved (alleged harasser)
- When it happened (date(s), time(s), location(s))
- Whether there were witnesses

### 3. Do not minimize, dismiss, or probe for inconsistencies

Do not say:
- "Are you sure that's what they meant?"
- "That doesn't sound like something [person] would do."
- "Have you tried talking to them directly?"
- "Are you sure you want to make a formal complaint?"
- "I've never had any problems with them."

These responses — even if well-intentioned — can be characterized as discouraging the employee from pursuing a complaint, which is itself a violation.

What to say instead:
> "Thank you for trusting me with this. I take this seriously. I'm going to make sure the right people are involved. I'm going to contact HR today."

### 4. Do not promise confidentiality
You cannot guarantee confidentiality. HR may need to disclose information to conduct an investigation. If the employee asks for confidentiality, say:

> "I understand you want this to stay private. I have to be honest with you — I have an obligation to report what you've told me to HR. I can't promise complete confidentiality, but HR will handle this as carefully as possible. Your safety and your ability to do your job are the priority."

### 5. Do not tell the alleged harasser anything
Not informally, not as a "heads up," not as a chance to explain themselves. Any contact you have with the alleged harasser before HR has a chance to plan the investigation can:
- Contaminate the investigation
- Enable witness coaching or evidence destruction
- Create a retaliation claim against you personally

The only exception: if there is an immediate safety concern, involve HR and Security together.

---

## Within 2 Hours — Notify Your HRBP

Call your HRBP. Do not email (calls create urgency; email can be delayed or missed).

### What to tell HR

Report facts only. Do not interpret, editorialize, or share your opinion of either party's credibility.

Tell them:
- Who came to you (employee name, role, team)
- Who the alleged harasser is (name, role, relationship to complainant — peer, manager, skip-level, external)
- What was reported (brief factual summary — what happened, when, how often if it's a pattern)
- Whether there are apparent witnesses
- Whether the employee is in immediate distress or safety concern
- The notes you took during the conversation (share or read)

Do not tell them:
- "I don't really think this is a big deal."
- "Honestly, I've had issues with [complainant] too."
- "I think this is a misunderstanding."

### Transfer documentation
Give HR your handwritten or typed notes. Do not keep the only copy. Do not share notes with anyone else.

---

## Within 24 Hours — Confirm Investigation Is Initiated

Follow up with your HRBP to confirm:
- HR has opened or is opening an investigation
- You understand your role going forward (typically: cooperate, do not conduct your own)
- You have a point of contact for questions

Document this confirmation — date, time, who you spoke with. This is your record that you fulfilled your reporting obligation.

---

## During the Investigation — Your Role

### Do
- Cooperate fully with HR and/or Legal when they request information
- Answer questions honestly and only about what you directly observed or were told
- Continue managing the team's work as normally as possible
- Maintain confidentiality — do not discuss the investigation with anyone not directly involved

### Do not
- Conduct your own investigation: do not ask witnesses what they saw, do not gather documents independently, do not interview the complainant again beyond what HR asks you to do
- Share investigation status with the team: "We're investigating an HR matter" is the end of the conversation if anyone asks
- Take any adverse action against the complainant during the investigation period: do not reassign, downgrade, exclude, or change anything about their work situation without HR approval
- Tell the alleged harasser what was reported or that an investigation is underway (unless HR specifically instructs you otherwise)

---

## Managing the Team During the Investigation

People on the team may notice tension, a change in someone's behavior, or the presence of HR. You will be tempted to address this to maintain team cohesion. Be careful.

### What you can say
> "I take all concerns raised by team members seriously. When concerns come to me, I involve HR to make sure they're handled appropriately. Beyond that, I'm not able to share details. I know ambiguity is hard. Our work continues."

### What you cannot say
- Anything that identifies the complainant or the alleged harasser
- Anything about the substance of the complaint
- Any opinion on the likely outcome
- Any reassurance that "things are fine" if you do not know that to be true

---

## Retaliation Prevention Checklist

Retaliation is one of the most common and most successful legal claims following a harassment complaint. Retaliation does not require intent — it only requires that the complainant experienced adverse treatment after filing a complaint.

For the 90 days following a complaint, actively monitor and document:

- [ ] No changes to the complainant's shift, schedule, or work assignment without HR review
- [ ] No exclusion from meetings, projects, or opportunities they were previously included in
- [ ] No changes in performance evaluation approach or timing
- [ ] No change in 1:1 frequency or tone
- [ ] No informal social exclusion (off-site events, Slack channels, lunch groups)
- [ ] No comments — direct or indirect — about the complaint from any team member
- [ ] Prompt action if you observe any team member treating the complainant differently

If you observe any of the above, document it and contact HR immediately.

---

## After the Investigation Concludes

HR or Legal will communicate findings to you at whatever level is appropriate. Outcomes vary:

- **Substantiated finding**: HR/Legal will direct you on any disciplinary action. Do not act unilaterally — even if you believe the outcome is clear.
- **Unsubstantiated finding**: This does not mean nothing happened. It means the investigation did not produce sufficient evidence. The complainant's experience was real. Continue monitoring for retaliation.
- **Inconclusive finding**: Similar to unsubstantiated. HR may recommend team-level interventions.

### Managing the aftermath
If the alleged harasser remains on the team after the investigation, your job is to manage them professionally without signaling that you've pre-judged the outcome. That is difficult. Talk to your HRBP about how to handle it.

If the complainant is on your team, check in with them — not about the investigation, but about how they are doing, how the work is going, what they need to succeed. This is your job regardless of outcome.

---

## A Note on Your Own Experience

Receiving a harassment complaint is stressful. You may feel uncertain, exposed, or anxious about the right steps. You may feel loyalty to one or both parties. You may be frustrated by the ambiguity or the disruption to your team.

All of that is normal. None of it changes your obligations.

You can and should debrief with your own HRBP or manager about your experience — not to relitigate the complaint or share confidential details, but to process what you are carrying. This work is hard. You are allowed to acknowledge that.
