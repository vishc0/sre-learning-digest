# PIP Development Playbook — Building a Legally Defensible Performance Improvement Plan

> **Educational content only. Consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner for specific situations.**

---

## What This Playbook Protects

A well-built PIP protects three parties simultaneously:
- **The employee**: clear expectations and a genuine opportunity to succeed
- **The company**: documented process that demonstrates fairness before any adverse action
- **You, the manager**: proof that you managed performance rigorously and in good faith

A poorly built PIP — vague goals, no support documented, rushed timeline — is worse than no PIP. It signals bad faith and creates legal exposure.

---

## When to Use This Playbook

Use this playbook when **all three** of these are true:
1. A performance gap exists that has been verbally addressed at least once and has not improved
2. You are considering a termination if performance does not improve
3. You have not yet had the termination conversation

Do **not** start a PIP without an HRBP alignment meeting. Do not use a PIP as a paper trail when the decision to terminate has already been made — that creates liability.

---

## Before You Start: HRBP Alignment Meeting

Schedule this meeting before drafting anything. Cover:

- Summary of the performance gap in observable, behavioral terms
- What prior conversations have occurred and when (dates matter)
- Business impact of the gap (what is failing as a result?)
- Why you believe a PIP is appropriate versus a written warning or immediate termination
- What support you can realistically provide during the PIP period
- What timeline makes sense given the nature of the gap
- Whether there are any complicating factors: leave, accommodation requests, protected class membership, visa status

Your HRBP will tell you if there are legal constraints you are not aware of. Let them.

## Step 1 — Document the Performance Gap

The performance gap is the foundation. Everything else rests on it. If the gap is vague, the PIP is invalid.

### What "observable and behavioral" means

Write what you saw or measured, not what you concluded about the person.

| Wrong (Trait-Based) | Right (Observable and Behavioral) |
|---|---|
| "Poor work ethic" | "Submitted deliverables late on 4 of 5 agreed dates in Q2 (May 3, May 17, Jun 7, Jun 21)" |
| "Bad attitude" | "In the May 14 sprint review, interrupted two teammates before they finished speaking on three separate occasions" |
| "Not a team player" | "Declined three requests from peers to participate in cross-functional design sessions in Q1 (Jan 12, Feb 3, Mar 8)" |
| "Doesn't meet quality standards" | "Pull requests contained an average of 11 reviewer-identified bugs per PR over the last 6 weeks, against a team average of 3" |
| "Poor communication" | "Responded to Slack messages from stakeholders with an average delay of 3+ days in June, despite a team norm of same-business-day response" |

### For each performance gap, document

```
Performance Area: [e.g., Sprint Commitment Delivery]
Expected Standard: [e.g., Complete committed sprint items at a rate of ≥80% per sprint]
Observed Gap: [e.g., Completed 45%, 40%, 38%, and 50% of committed items in the last 4 sprints]
Dates of Observation: [Sprint endings: Apr 12, Apr 26, May 10, May 24]
Prior Discussion Dates: [Mentioned in 1:1s on Apr 14 and May 1; written summary sent via email May 2]
Business Impact: [Team missed external API delivery milestone by 2 weeks; required 2 engineers to re-scope downstream work]
```

Limit the PIP to 2–4 performance gaps. If you have more than 4, you may have an earlier management problem. Focus on the gaps with the clearest business impact.

---

## Step 2 — Define SMART Improvement Expectations

For each gap, define what "improved" looks like in SMART terms. This is the most important section of the PIP for legal defensibility — because at the end, you will evaluate against these criteria.

### SMART Criteria

| Criterion | What It Means in a PIP Context |
|---|---|
| **Specific** | Names the exact behavior, deliverable, or metric |
| **Measurable** | Can be objectively evaluated — not "better" but "≥80%" |
| **Achievable** | The employee has the skills and authority to reach this bar |
| **Relevant** | Directly tied to the job responsibilities and the observed gap |
| **Time-bound** | Has a clear deadline within the PIP window |

### Bad Example vs. Good Example

**Performance Area**: Code Review Participation

Bad: "Engage more actively in code reviews."

Good: "Complete all code review requests assigned within 48 business hours. Provide substantive feedback (minimum 3 specific, actionable comments) on each PR reviewed. Target: 100% on-time review completion rate, measured weekly by manager review of GitHub activity, for the duration of the 60-day PIP period."

---

**Performance Area**: On-Call Response

Bad: "Improve response time to incidents."

Good: "Acknowledge all P1 and P2 alerts via PagerDuty within 5 minutes of trigger during on-call rotation. Achieve 100% compliance during the 30-day PIP period. Manager will review PagerDuty logs weekly."

## Step 3 — Define the Support the Company Will Provide

This section is legally required and often skipped. Courts and arbitrators look for evidence that the employer offered genuine support, not just a countdown to termination.

Document specifically:

- **Training**: any formal training, courses, or certifications the company will provide
- **Mentoring or coaching**: who specifically will provide it, how often, for how long
- **Manager availability**: meeting cadence you commit to (e.g., weekly 30-minute check-in)
- **Resource access**: tools, documentation, shadowing opportunities
- **Reduced scope**: if workload will be temporarily adjusted to allow focus on improvement

Example:
```
Support Provided During PIP Period:
- Manager will hold weekly 30-minute 1:1 specifically for PIP check-in (Fridays, 10am)
- Employee will be enrolled in "Effective Code Review" course on internal LMS (company-paid, due Week 2)
- Employee will shadow senior engineer [Name] on two client-facing incidents during the PIP period
- HRBP available for confidential conversations if employee has questions about process
```

## Step 4 — Set the Timeline

Standard PIP windows:

| Duration | Use When |
|---|---|
| **30 days** | The gap is narrow and behavioral (e.g., attendance, response times). Skill remediation in 30 days is rare. |
| **60 days** | Most common. Appropriate for technical skill gaps where improvement is plausible within 2 months. |
| **90 days** | Reserved for complex skill or leadership gaps where genuine remediation requires more time. |

Avoid arbitrary durations. The length should be determined by what a reasonable person would need to demonstrate sustained improvement — not by how quickly you want to close the case.

### Checkpoint structure

Build in checkpoints at 50% and 75% of the PIP window. At each checkpoint:
- Review measurable progress against each expectation
- Document the check-in in writing (even an email summary to the employee is sufficient)
- If performance is improving, say so explicitly — this is motivating and legally protective
- If performance is worsening, document that and notify HRBP immediately

---

## Step 5 — Write the Measurement Criteria

How will you evaluate whether the employee met the expectations at the end of the PIP period?

Make this binary where possible: **Met** or **Not Met**. Avoid language that requires subjective judgment at the evaluation stage ("significant improvement," "much better").

Example measurement criteria table:

| Expectation | How Measured | Data Source | Met Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Code review response within 48 hours | Weekly review of GitHub timestamps | GitHub PR logs | 90%+ of reviews completed on time across 8-week window |
| Sprint delivery ≥80% | Sprint completion rate per 2-week sprint | Jira sprint reports | ≥80% in 4 of 4 sprints during PIP |
| On-call P1/P2 acknowledgment within 5 min | PagerDuty alert logs | PagerDuty | 100% compliance during on-call rotations in PIP period |

If you cannot define how you will measure it, you have not defined the expectation clearly enough.

---

## Step 6 — State the Consequences

This section must be explicit and unambiguous. Vague consequence language creates legal exposure because it implies the employee was not clearly warned.

Standard language (use your company's approved version — confirm with HRBP):

> "Failure to meet the improvement expectations outlined in this plan by [End Date] may result in further disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment. Meeting these expectations during the PIP period does not guarantee continued employment if other performance concerns arise."

Do not soften this section. The employee has a right to know what is at stake.

---

## Step 7 — Delivery Conversation

### Setup
- Schedule a dedicated meeting. Do not deliver a PIP at the end of a 1:1 with no warning.
- Your HRBP should be present, or available by phone if remote.
- Have the printed/digital PIP document ready to share.
- Budget 45–60 minutes.

### Opening script

> "I want to start by telling you that this is going to be a difficult conversation, and I want to give it the time it deserves. I've asked [HRBP name] to join us today."
>
> "We've talked before about [specific gap]. I haven't seen the improvement I was hoping for, and I've worked with HR to put together a Performance Improvement Plan. What that means is a structured set of expectations and a clear timeline — with the goal of getting you to where you need to be."
>
> "I want you to read through this with me, so there are no surprises, and I want to make sure you have a chance to ask questions."

### When the employee pushes back

**"I didn't know my performance was this bad."**
> "I understand this is hard to hear. We discussed [specific instance] on [date] and [specific instance] on [date]. I know those conversations are difficult. The reason I'm formalizing this now is to give you a clear roadmap and a genuine opportunity to succeed."

**"This is unfair / other people do the same thing."**
> "I hear you. My job right now is to focus on what I've observed in your work. If you feel there are broader team issues, I encourage you to raise those with HR directly — that's a separate conversation from this one."

**"I refuse to sign this."**
> "Your signature acknowledges that you received this document and that we had this conversation. It doesn't mean you agree with everything in it. If you'd like to add a written response, you can attach it to the document. But I do need you to sign indicating receipt."

### How to handle emotional responses

- Allow silence. Do not rush to fill it.
- "Take a moment" is appropriate and human.
- If the employee becomes very distressed, offer a short break.
- Do not apologize for the PIP itself — you can acknowledge difficulty without undermining the message.
- Do not extend false hope: "I'm sure you'll turn this around" creates ambiguity about the seriousness.

---

## Step 8 — PIP Period Management

### Weekly check-ins
Keep a brief written record of each check-in. It does not need to be formal — an email summary sent to the employee after the meeting works.

Include: date, what was discussed, progress status per expectation, any concerns raised, next steps.

### If performance improves mid-PIP
Acknowledge it explicitly and in writing. This is both the right thing to do and legally protective — it shows you applied the PIP in good faith and were not predetermined on termination.

> "I want to note that your sprint delivery rate has been at 85% for the last three sprints. That's real improvement and I want you to know I see it."

### If performance does not improve or worsens mid-PIP
Contact your HRBP immediately. Do not wait for the end of the PIP period. Depending on severity, the response may be accelerated action.

### At PIP end
- Evaluate against each measurement criterion. Document your assessment.
- If Met: schedule a meeting to close the PIP formally. Communicate next steps (continued monitoring, what you'll be watching for, return to standard performance management).
- If Not Met: do not have this conversation without HRBP present. This is the termination discussion.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | Why It Matters | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Starting the PIP after the decision to terminate is already made | Creates a "sham PIP" allegation — constructive discharge risk | If the decision is final, work with HRBP on a separation path instead |
| Vague goals ("improve communication") | Impossible to evaluate objectively | Write measurable criteria before finalizing the PIP |
| No documentation of prior conversations | Makes the PIP look like it came out of nowhere | Keep brief written records of every performance conversation |
| Skipping the support section | Suggests the company set the employee up to fail | Document support before the PIP is delivered |
| Inconsistent enforcement | Favoritism claims arise when some employees are PIP'd for behaviors others are not | Confirm with HRBP whether similar behaviors have been handled similarly |
| Changing expectations mid-PIP | Undermines the employee's ability to meet a moving target | Set expectations at the start and only update them via formal written amendment |
| Not involving HRBP | PIP process varies by company and jurisdiction | Loop in HRBP before, during, and at conclusion |
| Ignoring visa status | H1B holders have specific employer obligations | Always flag immigration status to HRBP before initiating any adverse action process |
