# Layoff Notification Playbook — Conducting an Individual Notification with Dignity

> **Educational content only. Consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner for specific situations.**

---

## What This Playbook Protects

An individual layoff notification is one of the hardest things a manager does. Done poorly, it causes lasting harm to the person being let go, creates legal exposure for the company, and damages your reputation as a leader. Done with care and precision, it allows the employee to leave with dignity and clarity.

This playbook protects:
- **The employee**: clear, direct, respectful communication so they can begin processing and planning
- **The company**: legally compliant process, documented, with no promises made that legal cannot keep
- **You**: a defensible record that the notification was conducted professionally and consistently

---

## Before the Notification — Director Checklist

Do not schedule the notification meeting until every item on this list is confirmed with HR and Legal.

### HR Confirmation
- [ ] The decision is final and has been approved at the appropriate management level
- [ ] HR has confirmed the effective date of the layoff
- [ ] The severance package (if any) is confirmed in writing and reviewed by Legal
- [ ] The separation agreement (if any) is ready and has been reviewed by Legal
- [ ] Reference policy has been confirmed (what you can and cannot say to future employers)
- [ ] Company property return process is defined (laptop, badge, access cards)

### Legal Confirmation
- [ ] WARN Act review is complete if this is part of a mass layoff event (federal WARN Act: 100+ employees, 60-day notice for plant closings/mass layoffs; state WARN Acts vary — California, New York, New Jersey have lower thresholds)
- [ ] Employee's immigration status has been reviewed (H1B, L1, O-1, Green Card sponsorship — see H1B-specific section below)
- [ ] Any active EEO complaints, FMLA leave, accommodation requests, or recent protected activity has been reviewed (these do not prevent layoffs but must be disclosed to Legal for risk review)
- [ ] Non-disclosure and non-compete provisions in the separation agreement have been reviewed under applicable state law (California largely prohibits non-competes; others vary)

### IT Confirmation
- [ ] IT has a plan for access revocation timing — coordinate so access is revoked after the notification meeting, not before (employee discovering their access is gone before the meeting is a dignity failure and a process failure)
- [ ] System for handling any business-critical knowledge transfer or documentation handoff

## Scheduling the Notification

### Timing
- **Best**: Monday through Thursday, mid-morning (10–11am local time)
- **Avoid**: Friday afternoon — leaves the employee with no access to HR, EAP, or support resources over a weekend
- **Avoid**: The day before a company holiday
- **Avoid**: Within a week of a significant personal event if known (death in family, recent medical leave return) — consult HR on whether to delay

### Logistics
- Duration: 30 minutes blocked. The actual conversation may be shorter; block more time to allow for questions and emotional response.
- Participants: You (the manager) + HRBP. Do not add additional managers unless HR specifically recommends it.
- Location: Private room. If remote: private video call. Do not do this in a shared space.
- Do not hint, schedule cryptically, or create anxiety in the lead-up. A simple calendar invite: "1:1 with [your name]" is sufficient.

## The Notification Script

### Opening — Direct, Not Prolonged

Do not spend five minutes on small talk. Begin within the first 30 seconds.

> "Thank you for meeting with me. I want to get straight to the reason for this meeting because I don't want you to have to wait. [Pause.] I have very difficult news. Your position is being eliminated, effective [date]."

Do not say "We've made the decision to part ways." Do not say "We're going to have to let you go." Be direct. The employee deserves clarity immediately.

### Business Reason Statement

Brief and factual. Do not over-explain or get into strategic details that invite debate.

> "This is the result of [restructuring / a reduction in force / the elimination of the [team/function]]. This decision reflects changes in the business, not a reflection of your contributions."

If the reason is truly confidential or has legal constraints on what can be said, your HRBP will have given you approved language. Use it exactly.

### What Happens Next

This is what the employee most needs to hear after the initial shock. Deliver it clearly and in order.

> "Here's what I want to make sure you know about what happens next:
>
> Your last day is [date]. [Or: Your employment ends today.]
>
> You will receive [severance details — weeks of pay, lump sum, continuation basis]. HR will walk you through the details today and you will receive a written summary.
>
> Your benefits continue through [date]. [Or: COBRA continuation information is included in your separation package.]
>
> You have [X days/weeks] to review and sign the separation agreement. You are not expected to sign today.
>
> As for references — our company policy is [describe policy, e.g., title and dates of employment only / we will confirm employment / direct references to HR].
>
> [HRBP name] will be your primary contact for any questions from this point forward."

### Transition Support Offered

Mention it, but briefly. The employee is in shock and will not absorb details.

> "We're also providing access to [outplacement services / career transition support] — [HRBP name] will share that information with you."

## What NOT to Say

| Do not say | Why | Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm so sorry, I feel terrible about this." | Excessive apology shifts focus to your feelings and can imply wrongdoing | "I know this is very hard news." |
| "If it were up to me, this wouldn't be happening." | Suggests the decision was wrong or that you were overruled; undermines trust in the organization | Say nothing about your personal view of the decision |
| "I'm sure you'll land somewhere great." | You cannot know this; sounds dismissive | "I wish you well in what comes next." |
| "This is really about [cost / budget / the economy]." | Over-explanation invites negotiation about why they specifically were chosen | Stick to the approved business reason statement |
| "You can reach out to me anytime." | Creates an obligation you may not be able to fulfill; your contact post-separation may be constrained by HR/Legal | Direct them to HRBP for process questions |
| "We'll give you a great reference." | You may not be authorized to give personal references; your company may have a reference policy that limits what you can say | State the company reference policy exactly |

## How to Handle Emotional Responses

### Silence and shock
Allow it. Do not fill silence with words. A few seconds of silence feels long but is appropriate and respectful.

### Crying
Have tissues in the room (or easily visible on desk). Do not attempt to stop the person from crying. Do not say "don't cry" or "it's going to be okay." Let them have the moment.

> "Take as much time as you need."

### Anger
Do not mirror the anger. Remain calm and consistent.

> "I hear you. This is a lot to take in."

Do not defend the decision or argue about whether it is fair. If the employee begins to escalate toward an unsafe situation, the HRBP should step in.

### Bargaining / negotiation
> "I understand you want to know if there are options. [HRBP name] will go through everything with you, including the timeline and what the agreement covers. This isn't a decision I can reverse, but I want to make sure you have all the information you need."

### Denial / "This can't be right"
> "I know this is a shock. This is real. Your last day is [date]. I want to make sure you have time to ask questions and get what you need."

## H1B-Specific Considerations

If the employee is on an H1B visa, this situation has additional legal complexity that HR must have addressed **before** the notification meeting.

**The 60-day grace period**: H1B holders get a 60-day grace period from the date of layoff to find new sponsoring employment, transfer their visa, or change immigration status. The clock typically starts on the last day of employment (confirm with Legal).

**Before the meeting, confirm with HR that**:
- Legal has reviewed the H1B implications
- HR has prepared information about the grace period for the employee
- The separation timeline accounts for immigration status where legally required

**During the meeting**: Do not give immigration advice. If the employee asks immigration questions, defer to HR and Legal.

> "HR has resources to help you understand the immigration-related steps. [HRBP name] will walk through those with you today."

**Never** terminate an H1B holder's access in a way that contradicts their legal status obligations without Legal sign-off. Contact HR immediately if there is any uncertainty.

## After the Notification

### Communication to the team

Same day, after the employee has been notified and has had time to collect their belongings (at minimum). Do not let the team hear through the rumor mill.

**What to say to the team**:
> "I want to share that [Name] is no longer with the team, effective today. This was a business decision and not a reflection of [their] performance. I know this news can be unsettling. I am available to talk with each of you individually if you have questions. Our work continues."

**What not to say**:
- Why they were specifically selected
- Details of severance or any agreement
- Any characterization of their performance or conduct
- Anything that could be interpreted as relief that they are gone

### Access revocation
Coordinate with IT so that access is revoked promptly after the notification but not before. The employee should have time to send personal contacts their personal email, retrieve personal files from their device per company policy, and say any goodbyes they choose to make.

### Equipment return
The HRBP will coordinate this. You do not need to manage it directly. If the employee asks you, direct them to HR.

---

## Your Own Debrief

Layoff notifications — especially for employees you have managed, mentored, or respected — carry emotional weight. That weight does not go away because the meeting is over.

Debrief with your manager or your HRBP after the fact. Not to relitigate the decision, but to process the experience. Asking for support after doing a hard thing is not weakness; it is professionalism.

If you are conducting multiple notifications in a single day or week (mass reduction event), make sure your own support structure is in place before you begin.
