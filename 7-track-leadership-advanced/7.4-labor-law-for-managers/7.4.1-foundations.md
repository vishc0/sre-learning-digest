# Chapter 1: Employment Law Foundations — What Every Director Needs to Know

> **DISCLAIMER**: This is educational content for engineering managers. For specific situations, consult your company's legal counsel or HR Business Partner. Nothing here constitutes legal advice.

---

## Why This Chapter Comes First

Most engineering managers reach the Director level without having formally studied employment law. That's normal — it wasn't in the SRE handbook, and no one gave you a primer when you got promoted. The problem is that by the time you need this knowledge, you're already in the middle of a situation where saying the wrong thing, skipping the right step, or missing the right escalation trigger creates exposure you cannot walk back.

This chapter is about foundational orientation. It explains the landscape — what legal structures govern your relationship with your employees, where your authority ends, and where your obligations begin. You don't need to become an attorney. You need enough fluency to recognize when you're in legally significant territory and to act accordingly.

## At-Will Employment: What It Means and What It Does Not

The United States operates on an at-will employment doctrine in 49 states (Montana is the exception). At-will means: either the employer or the employee can end the employment relationship at any time, for any reason, or for no reason at all, without legal liability — with specific, significant exceptions.

The critical word is "exceptions." At-will is not a blank check.

**What at-will DOES cover:**
- Ending employment because the role is no longer needed
- Ending employment because performance doesn't meet expectations
- Ending employment because the company is restructuring
- An employee quitting without notice

**What at-will DOES NOT cover:**

*Protected class discrimination.* You cannot end employment because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age (40+), disability, genetic information, or other protected classes under federal and state law. At-will does not override anti-discrimination statutes. The fact that you could fire someone for any reason does not mean you fired them for a neutral reason — if the termination disproportionately affects a protected class or follows a pattern of differential treatment, you have exposure regardless of at-will status.

*Retaliation for protected activity.* Employees who report discrimination, file an EEOC complaint, take FMLA leave, report OSHA violations, or participate in union organizing have engaged in legally protected activity. Adverse employment action — firing, demotion, reduction in hours, hostile reassignment — following protected activity creates a retaliation claim. The timing alone can create liability: if you terminate someone 30 days after they filed an internal complaint, you need a well-documented, pre-existing performance record to establish that the termination decision was made for independent reasons.

*Implied contract exceptions.* If your offer letter, employee handbook, or verbal statements during the hiring process implied continued employment contingent on performance, you may have created an implied contract that limits at-will termination. This is why most offer letters include explicit at-will language. But handbooks that say "employees will be terminated only for cause" can be interpreted as contractual commitments. Directors should not make statements like "as long as you do good work, you'll have a job here" — those words have legal weight.

*Public policy exceptions.* Most states prohibit terminating employees for reasons that violate public policy — for example, firing someone for serving jury duty, for filing a workers' compensation claim, or for refusing to commit an illegal act at the employer's direction.

## Employee vs. Contractor: The Classification Risk

Engineering organizations frequently use contractors, consultants, and independent contractors. Misclassification — treating someone who is legally an employee as a contractor — is a significant legal and financial risk that sits at the Director level because Directors control resource decisions.

**Why this matters to you personally:** If you engage someone as a contractor for years, give them a company laptop, direct their daily work, require them to work specific hours, and include them in team stand-ups, you have likely created an employee relationship regardless of what the contract says. The consequences include back taxes (payroll, Social Security, Medicare), retroactive benefit entitlements, and — critically — exposure to discrimination and harassment claims because employment law protections now apply.

**The IRS 20-Factor Test (distilled for Directors):**
The IRS looks at three categories when assessing worker classification:

*Behavioral control* — Does the company control how the work is done, not just the result? Do you direct the worker's daily schedule, methods, tools, and sequence of tasks? If yes, it points toward employment.

*Financial control* — Does the worker have business expenses they aren't reimbursed for? Do they work for multiple clients? Can they make a profit or loss on the engagement? Contractors who work exclusively for you, are reimbursed for all expenses, and have no independent business presence look like employees.

*Type of relationship* — Is there a written contract characterizing the relationship? Are permanent benefits provided? Is the relationship indefinite or project-scoped? Indefinite, benefits-inclusive relationships look like employment.

**The California ABC Test (relevant if you manage West Coast teams):**
California applies a stricter standard. A worker is an employee unless the hiring entity can show: (A) the worker is free from control in how they perform the work; (B) the work is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business; and (C) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade or business. Engineering work is typically inside a software company's usual course of business — which is why California makes contractor classification particularly difficult.

**The Director's decision rule:** If you are extending a contractor engagement past 12 months, increasing their daily involvement in team operations, or giving them access to company systems equivalent to full-time employees — call your HRBP before renewing the contract.

## Why Directors Need Labor Law Fluency

You are not a substitute for HR or legal counsel. But you are the first person in the room when these situations begin — and the decisions you make in the first 30 minutes of a personnel situation often determine the legal exposure that follows.

Labor law fluency enables you to:

**Recognize escalation triggers in the moment.** When an employee says "I think I was passed over for promotion because of my age," you need to know immediately that you've just received a legally significant disclosure — and that your next sentence matters. Without fluency, you might say "I don't think that's what happened" (a dismissal that can be characterized as retaliation) instead of "I want to make sure that concern is heard properly — let me connect you with HR today."

**Avoid creating liability through casual language.** Performance conversations, 1:1s, team meetings, and Slack messages are all potential evidence. Directors who say "we need to make way for younger talent" or "I need someone who can keep up with the pace here" — even casually — have created discoverable evidence of age discrimination intent. Fluency teaches you which phrases carry legal weight.

**Make documentation decisions in real time.** When a situation is developing, you need to know whether to send an email summarizing the conversation, save a Slack thread, or write personal notes with timestamps. That judgment call is yours, not HR's — because HR isn't in the 1:1 with you.

**Protect yourself when managing up.** If a senior leader directs you to take a personnel action that you believe is discriminatory or retaliatory, you need to know what "document and escalate" means — and what happens if you execute the directive without escalating. Willful execution of an unlawful directive is not a defense.

## The Role of Your HR Business Partner

Your HRBP is your co-pilot on people decisions. They bring institutional knowledge of company policy, state-specific legal requirements, past precedent on similar situations, and coordination with legal counsel. They are not your liability shield — but they are your best protection against making a well-intentioned mistake that becomes a documented failure of process.

**Loop in your HRBP before:**
- Issuing any formal PIP
- Any termination conversation, even one that feels straightforward
- Making any change to an employee's role, compensation, or scope that they didn't request
- Any restructuring that affects headcount
- Any time you receive a complaint — formal or informal — about discrimination, harassment, or retaliation

**The principle:** HR involvement is not bureaucratic box-checking. It creates a record that the company followed due process, applied consistent standards, and made decisions based on documented legitimate business reasons — not protected class characteristics.

---

## The Role of Legal Counsel

When your HRBP says "I need to loop in legal," that is a signal, not a formality. It means the situation has crossed into territory where litigation risk is real or where company policy requires legal sign-off. Examples: terminating a protected class employee with no prior documented performance issues, separating an employee who recently filed an EEOC charge, executing a reduction in force that requires WARN Act compliance.

Directors interact with in-house legal counsel through HR, not directly. You do not call legal on your own. When legal is involved, your documentation and communication become subject to attorney-client privilege — which affects what you write, to whom, and how. Your HRBP will guide you through that.

## The Large-Company Context

At a large public company operating across multiple states, the compliance bar is significantly higher than at an early-stage startup. You have:

- **Scale exposure**: discriminatory practices that would affect one or two people at a small company affect hundreds across a large organization. Class action risk is real.
- **Multi-state complexity**: an employment decision that is lawful in Texas may not be lawful in California or New York. If you manage distributed teams, your HRBP needs to know the states involved before you act.
- **Regulatory scrutiny**: public companies have additional reporting obligations and are more visible targets for regulatory agencies.
- **Unionized segments**: some employees may be covered by collective bargaining agreements that define terms of employment, discipline processes, and grievance procedures. At-will doctrine does not apply to those employees in those areas.

The answer to all of this complexity is not paralysis — it is process. Document, involve HR early, apply consistent standards, and ask before acting when you're uncertain.

---

## Chapter Summary

- At-will employment is the baseline in the US, but its exceptions — protected class discrimination, retaliation, implied contract, public policy — are where legal exposure lives for Directors.
- Contractor misclassification is a Director-level risk. The tests are behavioral control, financial control, and type of relationship. California's ABC test is stricter.
- Labor law fluency isn't about replacing HR and legal — it's about recognizing escalation triggers, avoiding liability-creating language, making real-time documentation decisions, and protecting yourself when managing up.
- Loop in your HRBP before PIPs, terminations, role changes, headcount restructuring, and any complaint received.
- The law is the floor. Fair, consistent, documented treatment is the ceiling you should be building toward.
