# Chapter 5: Applying Change Management to SRE Transformation

## The First Mistake: Treating It as a Technical Problem

Every SRE transformation I have seen fail shared a common root cause: leadership treated it as a tooling rollout. They bought the observability stack, defined SLOs in a spreadsheet, and then wondered why nothing changed six months later. The Prometheus dashboards were green. The postmortem template was in Confluence. And the team was still doing what they always did — firefighting, blaming each other quietly, and deploying on Friday afternoons.

SRE transformation is a change management problem that happens to have a technical implementation layer. The tooling is not the transformation. The transformation is a shift in identity, values, and daily behavior. Until you accept that framing, you will keep deploying tools into a culture that will quietly ignore them.

## The Four Scenarios and Their Change Challenges

### 1. Introducing SRE Culture to an Existing Ops Team

The hardest scenario because you are not adding something new — you are reclassifying what the team already does. When you tell an ops team they are now "SREs," you are implicitly telling them that the way they have been working was wrong. That is an identity threat, not a job description change.

The resistance here is rarely verbal. It surfaces as performative compliance: the team nods in the SRE training, takes the SLO quiz, passes the certification, and then continues operating exactly as before. The reason is simple: they built their professional identity on the old model. They know where the bodies are buried, who to call at 2am, and which config file to never touch. SRE culture asks them to document all of that, automate it, and share it — which, from their perspective, is asking them to give away the only leverage they have.

The Director's job here is to make the new identity feel like an upgrade, not an erasure. That means celebrating the institutional knowledge while explicitly rewarding the new behaviors. The engineer who wrote the runbook that replaced a 3am escalation is not being automated out — they are being recognized as someone who solved a problem permanently.

### 2. Implementing On-Call Rotation on a Team That Never Had One

Two fears dominate this conversation: workload and compensation. Both are legitimate, and treating either as irrational will permanently damage your credibility with the team.

The workload fear is real. Teams that have never been on-call often have systems that were never designed for on-call — no alerting hygiene, no runbooks, no tiered severity. Implementing on-call before cleaning up the alert signal is guaranteed to burn people out. The sequencing matters: you cannot ask people to carry a pager into a system that pages 40 times a night for non-actionable conditions.

The compensation question is one that Directors often try to avoid. Do not. If your company does not compensate for on-call burden — either through differential pay, time-off-in-lieu, or explicit workload reduction — then you owe the team that honest answer before you ask them to sign up. Transparency here is not optional. It is the only way to build the trust you will need when the first bad on-call week happens.

### 3. Moving from Manual Deployments to CI/CD

This resistance pattern is more sophisticated than it looks. Engineers who have owned manual deployments are not resisting automation because they are lazy. They are resisting because their institutional knowledge about the deployment process is the currency of their organizational influence.

"I know where the bodies are buried" is a real phenomenon. Every manual deployment process accumulates undocumented tribal knowledge: the environment variable that has to be set in this exact sequence, the database migration that will fail if run before the service restart, the stakeholder who has to be notified or they will escalate. The people who hold this knowledge have real power, and CI/CD — correctly — makes that power legible to the entire team. That democratization feels like a loss to the person who held the knowledge.

The frame that works is not "we're automating your job away" but "we're making sure the team can survive a vacation." The goal is not to eliminate expertise but to capture it in code so it can scale.

### 4. Implementing Error Budgets

Error budgets are a political instrument that happens to be grounded in math. The moment you tell a product team that they have "spent" 40% of their reliability budget in two weeks, you have entered a negotiation about organizational power — who has the right to slow down feature delivery, who gets to say "no more deployments," and whether the platform team's judgment is trusted.

The most common failure mode is introducing error budgets before the relationship between SRE and product is established. If product teams do not already trust SRE's reliability data, they will dispute the measurement. If they do not already understand SLOs, the budget concept is meaningless. Error budgets only work when they are the culmination of a trust-building process, not the opening move.

## Blameless Culture as a Change Program

Blameless postmortems fail not because the template is wrong but because the manager running them has not changed their behavior. The postmortem form can say "blameless" at the top while the meeting dynamic punishes the on-call engineer for every sentence.

Directors need to watch for the proxy blame patterns: "walk me through what you were thinking when you made that decision" (said with a tone that means "explain yourself"), action items that are assigned exclusively to the person who caused the incident, and postmortems that end with "we need better discipline around change management" rather than "what made this failure mode possible."

When you see these patterns, the intervention is not a policy change. It is a direct conversation with the manager running the postmortems, naming the specific behavior and its organizational consequence.

## The Sequencing Problem

The correct order for introducing SRE practices is not arbitrary. It follows a logic of prerequisite trust:

**Observability first** — because you cannot have a facts-based conversation about reliability until the team agrees on what the data is. Instrumentation before SLOs.

**Blameless culture second** — because teams will not surface real failure data in a punitive environment. Culture change before metrics.

**SLOs third** — because once you have good data and psychological safety, the team can have a real conversation about what "reliable enough" means.

**Error budgets last** — because they require all three prerequisites to work. Error budgets without observability are guesses. Error budgets without blameless culture become weapons. Error budgets without SLOs are meaningless.

Never lead with error budgets. Organizations that do reliably produce the same outcome: SRE becomes the team that tells product "no," product learns to route around SRE, and the program quietly dies in about 18 months.

## The 6-Month Dip

Every SRE transformation goes through a period where things feel worse than they did before the change began. Incidents get documented, which means the incident count appears to rise. Toil gets measured, which means the backlog suddenly looks enormous. Postmortems surface systemic problems that were always there but invisible.

This is not failure. This is the observability problem becoming visible for the first time. But it looks like failure to executives who are watching the dashboards without the context.

Prepare your team for the dip explicitly. Tell them before it happens: "In months three through six, the data is going to look worse. That is not because things got worse. It is because we started measuring." Then prepare your executive stakeholders with the same framing. The Directors who survive SRE transformations are the ones who gave their sponsors enough context to hold the line during the dip instead of pulling funding.

---

## What "Done" Looks Like

SRE transformation is complete when the behaviors are self-sustaining. Not when the tooling is deployed. Not when the SLOs are documented. Not when the team has passed a certification.

Done looks like this: A team runs a postmortem on a minor incident without being asked, because they have internalized that it is worth doing. Engineers reference the error budget in sprint planning without prompting from SRE, because they understand it is their budget too. On-call engineers file toil reduction tickets as a matter of habit, because they trust that the work will be prioritized.

That behavioral shift takes 12 to 18 months in a healthy organization. Plan accordingly.
