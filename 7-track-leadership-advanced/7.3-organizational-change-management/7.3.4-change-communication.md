# Chapter 4: Change Communication — The Discipline Most Directors Skip

---

## The Core Principle: WHY Before WHAT Before HOW

Communication during organizational change is not the same as information delivery. Information delivery is transactional — here is a thing you need to know. Change communication is motivational — here is why the current state is untenable, here is what the future state looks like, and here is how you fit into getting there.

The sequence matters because of how human beings process change. When people hear about a change, the first question is not "how does this work?" It is "why is this happening, and what does it mean for me?" If the "why" is absent or unclear, every subsequent piece of communication — the timeline, the process, the tooling details — lands in an anxiety context. People interpret the "what" and "how" through the lens of their uncertainty about the "why." The result is that technically accurate communications are received as threat signals.

The WHY must be credible and specific. "We need to move to SRE to improve reliability" is not a why — it is a category label. A credible why sounds like: "We lost approximately 40 hours of engineering productivity per engineer per quarter to reactive incident work in the last two quarters, and three of our five highest-severity incidents were traced to operating models that did not have clear ownership. This is not sustainable at our growth rate." That is a why that people can evaluate, agree or disagree with, and orient around.

Once the why is established, the WHAT becomes meaningful. People know what problem the change is solving, which means they can evaluate whether the proposed change actually addresses it. And once the what is clear, the HOW — the mechanics, the timeline, the process details — has a context that makes it legible rather than threatening.

## The Rumor Half-Life Problem

In large organizations like T-Mobile, information vacuums fill with fear within 48 to 72 hours. This is not a cultural problem unique to any particular company — it is a structural property of large organizations. When people know that a significant decision is being made and they do not have the information about what it is, their brains fill the gap with the most salient threat available to them: job security, workload, loss of autonomy, disruption to their team's cohesion.

The practical implication is that the window between when a decision is made and when it is communicated is a danger zone. Every day of that window is a day that informal communication networks are generating and distributing narratives that you did not write and cannot control. By the time the official announcement arrives, many people have already formed a view — based on hallway conversations, interpreted signals, and worst-case speculation — that the official message now has to overcome rather than simply inform.

Directors who understand this are not the ones who say "we need to get our communications perfect before we announce anything." They are the ones who communicate early with what they know, clearly labeled as incomplete, and commit to specific follow-up. "Here is what has been decided, here is what is still being worked out, and here is when you will hear more" is a far more stable communication environment than silence followed by a polished announcement.

This requires accepting that early communications will be imperfect. You will not have all the answers. Some details will change between the first communication and the final one. That is acceptable. What is not acceptable, from a change management standpoint, is allowing the rumor network to run unopposed for days or weeks because you were waiting for a communication that was ready to be comprehensive.

## Communication Frequency and Channel Selection

Different channels serve different functions in a change communication architecture. Using the wrong channel for the wrong content is one of the most common and most damaging communication errors Directors make.

**All-Hands Meetings — Vision and Urgency**

The all-hands is the right venue for communicating at the organizational level: the case for change, the vision for the future state, and the executive-level commitment to the initiative. It is a broadcast medium — everyone hears the same message simultaneously, which prevents the message fragmentation that happens when the vision is communicated team by team. The appropriate cadence during a major change initiative is quarterly, or at significant milestones.

All-hands is the wrong venue for individual concerns, tactical details, or anything that requires dialogue. Questions in a 200-person meeting are a performance, not a conversation. Important concerns raised in that setting require a public response, which puts both the Director and the questioner in an uncomfortable spotlight. Save that format for what it is good at: shared context, shared direction, shared evidence.

**Team Meetings — Tactics and Progress**

Team-level meetings are where the all-hands vision becomes concrete for each group. What does this change mean for this team's work this quarter? What is changing about how they are measured? What decisions do they get to make, and which are being made above them? This is also the right venue for updates on progress — what happened, what is next, what is changing in the plan.

Cadence should be weekly during active change phases, or at minimum biweekly. The team meeting is also the right place to surface and address concerns that affect the whole team — not individual anxieties, but shared questions that the team manager does not have the authority to answer alone.

**One-on-One Conversations — Individual Concerns**

The 1:1 is the only communication format that can address individual anxiety directly. An engineer who is worried about whether their role exists in the new operating model cannot ask that question in an all-hands. The question is too personal, the answer too dependent on specifics that the Director may not be able to address publicly. In a 1:1, the conversation can be direct, specific, and confidential.

Directors who are running large change initiatives often underestimate how many 1:1 conversations they need to have — not just with their direct reports, but with key stakeholders, informal influencers, and the people whose visible reaction will shape others' behavior. These conversations are high-return investments of time. They surface concerns early, they build trust, and they give the Director real-time intelligence about how the change is landing.

**Slack and Email — Status and Artifacts**

Written channels are the right venue for status updates, links to documentation, summaries of decisions made, and anything people will need to reference later. They are the wrong venue for explaining complex reasoning, addressing contested concerns, or trying to move someone from skeptical to aligned. Written-only communication on difficult topics creates interpretation space that fills with the reader's existing anxiety.

The primary function of Slack and email in change communication is to create a reliable artifact layer — a place where people can go to find out what has been decided, what is next, and what resources exist to help them. This reduces the cognitive load on team meetings and 1:1s, which can then focus on dialogue rather than information transfer.

**Skip-Levels — Temperature Checks**

The skip-level conversation is the Director's primary mechanism for getting unfiltered signal about how the change is actually landing two or more levels below them. Managers, however well-intentioned, tend to filter and interpret before reporting upward. The result is that a Director who only hears from their direct reports is hearing a curated version of organizational sentiment.

During active change phases, skip-levels every four to six weeks with a representative sample of ICs and senior managers provides early warning of emerging resistance, communication failures, and implementation friction that would otherwise only surface in a retrospective six months too late to act on.

## Executive vs. Team Communication: Same Message, Different Frame

A common and costly mistake is using the same communication for both executive audiences and team audiences. These groups have different primary concerns, and a message optimized for one will land poorly with the other.

**Executives care about:** business impact (revenue risk, cost, competitive position), risk and its mitigation, resource requirements and timeline, and strategic alignment with organizational priorities. When communicating an SRE transformation to an executive audience, the frame is: "Unreliable services cost us X in engineering time and carry Y in customer attrition risk. The transformation requires Z investment over 18 months and reduces incident-driven engineering costs by W."

**Teams care about:** how this affects their day-to-day work, whether their job security is at risk, whether their workload is going up or down, and whether the change is being imposed on them or developed with them. When communicating the same SRE transformation to a team, the frame is: "Today, you are getting paged for problems that are not yours to own. You are spending a third of your time on reactive work that interrupts your flow. The goal of this change is to get you out of that reactive cycle — here is specifically what changes and here is what stays the same."

Neither of these framings is dishonest. They are the same truth, translated into the language of the audience's actual concerns. Directors who send the same message to both audiences are communicating to neither of them effectively.

## Handling Resistance in Public Forums

When a skeptic or blocker raises a concern in a team meeting or all-hands, the Director's response sets the tone for how the organization understands it is safe to engage with the change. A dismissive or defensive response closes down the space for honest dialogue. An authentically engaged response opens it.

The "yes, and" technique — borrowed from improvisational theater — is more useful than the "no, but" reflex that most leaders default to under public challenge. "No, but" treats the concern as an objection to overcome. "Yes, and" treats it as information to incorporate. "You are right that the platform is not yet ready to handle the traffic patterns your team generates — that is a real constraint, and here is where we are in addressing it" is a more credible and more stabilizing response than "that concern has been addressed in the platform roadmap." Even if both statements are technically accurate, only one of them demonstrates that you have genuinely engaged with the specificity of the challenge.

## SRE Theater: The Danger of Over-Communicating Vision Without Action

There is a failure mode that appears in the later stages of change initiatives: leaders continue to communicate the vision energetically, and visible action stops keeping pace. The all-hands slides still show the SRE maturity model. The Confluence pages still describe the target state. But teams have not seen tooling improvements, the approval gates have not changed, the performance review criteria still reward feature delivery without reliability weighting.

This pattern — which engineers will name immediately as "SRE theater" or "SLO theater" — is more damaging than no change communication at all, because it combines high visibility with zero follow-through. The credibility cost is severe. Once a team has concluded that the change initiative is performative, re-engaging them requires concrete action that is visibly tied to the stated vision, repeated over enough time to overcome the earned skepticism.

The antidote is to ensure that your communication cadence is always trailing action, not leading it. Communicate what has changed before communicating what will change. Show the wins before claiming them.

## What to Say When You Do Not Have Answers

The hardest communication challenge in change management is the moment when people ask questions you cannot yet answer — about job security, about team structure, about resource allocation — and the honest answer is that the decision has not been made.

The temptation is to give a holding answer that sounds more certain than it is, in order to reduce anxiety in the moment. This is a short-term trade for a long-term credibility cost. When the decision eventually arrives and it is different from the holding answer you gave, you have lost trust that is very difficult to rebuild.

The honest uncertainty message is: "That decision has not been made yet. Here is what we do know, here is what is still being worked out, and here is when I expect to have more clarity. When I do, I will communicate it directly and specifically." That message is uncomfortable because it does not resolve the uncertainty. But it is the only message that is true, and it is the only message that preserves your credibility as a source of reliable information through the rest of the change initiative.

## The Multi-Level Distortion Problem at T-Mobile Scale

At T-Mobile's organizational scale, a message that starts at the Director level and travels through multiple management layers to reach an individual contributor is not the same message when it arrives. Each layer adds interpretation, removes context that seems obvious to the layer above it, and applies the concerns of that layer's position. The result is that the IC who heard about the SRE transformation from their manager heard a version of the message that may bear only a family resemblance to what the Director actually said.

This is not a failure of the management chain — it is a structural property of hierarchical communication. The Director's response is not to blame the chain for distorting the message. It is to design a communication architecture that operates at multiple levels simultaneously. The all-hands reaches everyone directly. The skip-level provides unfiltered signal back. The Confluence artifact gives people something to read for themselves rather than through another person's interpretation. The manager is briefed with talking points that help them translate, not just relay.

The message will still distort to some degree. The Director's job is to make the original signal strong and the reference materials clear enough that the distortion stays within acceptable bounds — and to use skip-levels to detect when it has not.
