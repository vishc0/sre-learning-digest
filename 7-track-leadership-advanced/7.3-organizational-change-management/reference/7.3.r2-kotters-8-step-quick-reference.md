# Kotter's 8-Step Change Model — Quick Reference

*Use this as a diagnostic tool: where in the 8 steps is your change stuck? Use the failure patterns to identify the root cause.*

---

## Step 1 — Create a Sense of Urgency

**What it means**: People need to feel, not just understand, that the status quo is dangerous or costly. Without urgency, the organization will default to inertia. Urgency is not panic — it is a shared, data-backed recognition that the cost of not changing exceeds the cost of changing.

### What Good Looks Like

- Leaders at multiple levels can articulate the business cost of the current state in specific, concrete terms (not "we need to be more agile")
- Data is visible and regularly referenced: incident rate, toil hours, deployment frequency, customer complaint trends
- The pain of staying is more salient than the pain of changing — people are talking about the problem, not just being told about it

### What Failure Looks Like

- Urgency is manufactured through hype or fear without data — the team sees through it
- Leaders declare urgency but then allocate no protected time or budget to the change — the signal is that it's not actually urgent
- Urgency is felt only at the leadership level; the team thinks "management wants this" but doesn't feel the problem themselves

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- Share the actual MTTR numbers from the last quarter alongside a competitor's publicly stated deployment frequency — make the gap visible
- Calculate and publish the total engineer-hours spent on toil in the last sprint: "We spent 340 hours last month on work a machine should be doing"
- Present a customer impact summary from the top 5 incidents of the year: downtime in minutes, SLA credits issued, support volume spike — this is why we need SRE practices

## Step 2 — Build a Guiding Coalition

**What it means**: No single leader can drive organizational change. You need a coalition — a group of people with enough credibility, authority, expertise, and relationships to make the change happen. This coalition is not your org chart; it is a chosen group that spans functions and levels.

### What Good Looks Like

- The coalition includes both positional power (manager, director, VP) and informal credibility (respected senior engineers, product leads)
- Coalition members actively advocate for the change in their own domains, not just in formal settings
- There is visible disagreement within the coalition that gets resolved — it's not a rubber stamp group
- Coalition membership is recognized and rewarded (at least informally)

### What Failure Looks Like

- The coalition is just the manager and their direct reports — not enough diversity of perspective or credibility
- A coalition forms but never meets, or meets but has no decision-making authority
- Coalition members are Champions in the room but silent or passive outside of coalition meetings
- The coalition does not include anyone from the team that is being changed (only leaders, no engineers)

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- A guiding coalition for an SRE transformation might include: the SRE manager, two senior engineers (the champions), one product manager whose team depends on reliability, and a VP sponsor who attends monthly
- For a platform adoption campaign: platform lead + 1–2 beta team engineers (by name, publicly recognized as "Platform Pioneers") + the engineering director who controls headcount decisions
- The coalition should be able to answer a skeptical engineer's question without the manager in the room

## Step 3 — Form a Strategic Vision and Initiatives

**What it means**: A clear, compelling picture of what the future looks like — specific enough to guide decisions, simple enough to be remembered and repeated. Without a clear vision, individual decisions during the change pull in different directions.

### What Good Looks Like

- The vision can be stated in 2–3 sentences by anyone on the team — not just the manager
- The vision answers: what will be true in 12 months that is not true today?
- The initiatives (the roadmap of actions to achieve the vision) are prioritized — not everything at once
- The vision is honest about what will be hard, not just aspirational

### What Failure Looks Like

- The vision is a slide deck that no one can recall two weeks later
- The vision is so abstract ("become a world-class SRE organization") that no decision can be derived from it
- There are too many initiatives — the team cannot distinguish priority from noise
- The vision changes when it encounters its first obstacle — it wasn't actually a commitment

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- SRE vision (good): "In 12 months, every P1/P2 incident has a blameless postmortem within 5 days, every customer-facing service has a defined SLO, and our error budget policy is agreed to by both engineering and product."
- Platform vision (good): "By Q4, 80% of new services are deployed through the IDP, and the average time-to-first-deployment for a new service is under 4 hours."
- Bad vision: "We want to be more reliable and reduce toil." (No measurable target, no timeframe, no shared ownership)

## Step 4 — Enlist a Volunteer Army

**What it means**: Change led only by those with authority does not scale and does not stick. You need people throughout the organization who have chosen to be part of the change — not assigned to it, not required, but voluntarily participating because they believe in the direction.

### What Good Looks Like

- A visible, named group of change advocates who are not all managers (engineers, ICs, even junior team members)
- Volunteer advocates are given real work — not cheerleading, but ownership of specific change activities
- The volunteer army is growing: more people join over time as success is demonstrated
- Volunteers are recognized by name in communications and all-hands discussions

### What Failure Looks Like

- The "volunteers" were actually voluntold — their participation is mandatory, just called voluntary
- Volunteers have enthusiasm but no time or mandate to actually do the work
- The volunteer program fades because there is no structure (no regular meeting, no visible roadmap, no recognition)
- Volunteers burn out because they carry the change effort on top of their regular work

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- The SRE champions program: 3–5 engineers who run blameless postmortems, co-facilitate SRE reviews, and demo the new processes to skeptical peers
- The "Platform Pioneer" badge: beta team engineers who are named publicly in the all-hands and who run lunch-and-learns for other teams
- An SRE community of practice that any engineer can join: not mandatory, but has a structured agenda, rotating facilitation, and produces artifacts (runbooks, postmortem templates) that benefit the whole team

## Step 5 — Enable Action by Removing Barriers

**What it means**: People who want to change are often blocked by structural obstacles — legacy processes, old tooling, risk-averse approval chains, or managers who say yes but withhold the time or resources to do the work. The leader's job at this step is to actively remove what's in the way.

### What Good Looks Like

- Barriers are systematically identified (not assumed) — through feedback sessions, 1:1s, and retrospectives
- Barriers are removed visibly and quickly: when a champion says "we can't get access to the deployment system," the manager fixes it within 48 hours
- Time and capacity are explicitly protected: toil reduction sprints are on the roadmap, not just in the aspirations
- Structural blockers (approval chains, risk review processes) are negotiated with the relevant owners — the team doesn't have to fight them alone

### What Failure Looks Like

- Leaders talk about enabling change but do not remove specific named blockers
- The change is announced but no protected time is allocated — teams are expected to "fit it in"
- An escalation process exists in theory but takes weeks and is quietly abandoned by the team
- Barriers that were removed reappear because the underlying structure wasn't changed (e.g., access is granted, then revoked at the next quarterly audit)

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- "Our postmortem process requires three approvals before it's published" — the SRE manager gets the approval process waived for postmortems and replaces it with a team review
- "Engineers don't have access to the production metrics they need to define SLIs" — the manager escalates to the infosec team, gets read-only access provisioned within a week
- "We want to do a toil reduction sprint but product won't release capacity" — the director has a direct conversation with the product VP to protect one sprint per quarter as a reliability investment

## Step 6 — Generate Short-Term Wins

**What it means**: Long change journeys require proof that the effort is worth it. Short-term wins — visible, meaningful, early successes — maintain momentum, justify continued investment, and neutralize skeptics who are waiting for the change to fail.

### What Good Looks Like

- Wins are planned in advance — not left to chance — and timed to coincide with periods of potential doubt
- Wins are publicly recognized and attributed: the team knows what happened and who made it happen
- Wins are genuinely meaningful — not manufactured metrics ("we ran three training sessions!") but outcomes ("we cut MTTR in half for our top-2 services")
- Skeptics are shown the wins specifically — not just broadcasted to the already-convinced

### What Failure Looks Like

- Wins are too small or too delayed to change the narrative
- Wins are communicated only at the leadership level — the team doesn't hear about them
- A win is announced, then followed immediately by a visible failure — the story is undermined
- Leadership declares victory based on leading indicators before lagging outcomes have materialized

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- After Phase 1: "We defined SLOs for our top 3 services and our error budget dashboard went live this week. Here's the first snapshot: all 3 services are green."
- After first embedded SRE sprint: "The Checkout team deployed 4 times this week — their previous record was once a sprint. Here's what they said about working with an embedded SRE."
- First blameless postmortem: run it, share the output in the all-hands, highlight the systemic fixes that came out of it — not the individuals who were involved

---

## Step 7 — Sustain Acceleration

**What it means**: After early wins, there is a temptation to declare success and move on. Kotter calls this the most dangerous moment. Early wins create credibility for deeper change — use that credibility to tackle the harder parts: entrenched behaviors, systemic barriers, and cultural inertia.

### What Good Looks Like

- The change roadmap explicitly includes second-wave initiatives that would have been too hard to start with
- New leaders are developed from within the volunteer army — the change is no longer dependent on the original champions
- The narrative evolves: "we made progress" becomes "we are the organization that does it this way"
- Momentum is tracked: if the rate of adoption slows, it is noticed and diagnosed — not assumed to be normal

### What Failure Looks Like

- The first wave of changes locks in and the organization stalls at "good enough" — the deeper transformation never happens
- The original change champions leave or are reassigned, and the change loses its energy source
- Success metrics are declared without checking lagging indicators — the win was real but the underlying culture hasn't shifted
- Urgency fades as early wins reduce pain — the team relaxes before the change is anchored

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- After SLOs are defined, push to the harder next step: error budget policy — because now the organization has the language and data to have that conversation
- After the first embedded SRE sprint, expand the model from one product team to three — the single success creates credibility to scale
- After blameless postmortems become standard for P1s, extend the expectation to P2s and then to significant near-misses that did not cause an incident

## Step 8 — Institute Change

**What it means**: The change is complete when it no longer depends on individuals advocating for it — it is embedded in the systems, processes, and culture of the organization. Promotions, hiring decisions, onboarding, and stated organizational values all reflect the new way.

### What Good Looks Like

- A new hire joins and learns the changed way as "how we work" — not as a special program or initiative
- The behaviors and practices are reflected in hiring criteria, interview questions, and performance reviews
- When a senior leader leaves, the change continues — it does not depend on any one person
- The language of the change is in everyday use: engineers say "error budget," "toil reduction," "blameless postmortem" without prompting

### What Failure Looks Like

- The change exists in the documentation but not in behavior — especially under pressure or deadline stress
- A new leader joins and says "why do we do it this way?" and the team cannot answer without invoking the original change sponsor
- The practices are done in good times and abandoned in bad times (high-incident periods, reorgs, budget cuts)
- The hiring and onboarding processes still reflect the old way — new people join and have to be "caught up" on the transformation

### SRE / Platform Engineering Examples

- SRE practices are anchored when: new engineer asks in their first week where to find the SLO dashboard (they've already heard about it), the product roadmap includes reliability OKRs written by product managers (not just engineering), and the on-call runbook is updated by the team without manager prompting
- Platform adoption is anchored when: a new team starting a service asks "should we use the platform?" and the answer from a peer engineer is "of course, why wouldn't you?"
- The transformation is anchored when: you, the manager who drove it, could leave — and the team would continue operating the same way
