# SRE Transformation Patterns Reference

*5 organizational patterns for implementing SRE. Each pattern has a different org design, a different change management challenge, and different applicability depending on where your organization is today.*

---

## Pattern 1 — Greenfield SRE Team

### What It Is
Build a new, dedicated SRE team from scratch alongside the existing operations or engineering organization. The existing team does not change structurally. The SRE team starts with a blank sheet — new headcount, new charter, new mandate.

### When to Use It
- Your current ops team is deeply embedded in operational firefighting and does not have the capacity or skill set to transform in place
- Leadership has made a strategic decision to invest in a dedicated SRE function as a new capability
- You have the headcount approval to hire 4–8 SREs over the next 12 months
- There is a clearly scoped set of services or a platform that the new SRE team can own from day one

### How It Works — Org Design
```
CTO / VP Engineering
├── Existing Ops/NOC Team (unchanged structure, existing scope)
└── SRE Team (new) ← new reporting line, new headcount
    ├── SRE Manager
    ├── SRE Engineers (hired or transferred from Ops team)
    └── Charter: Platform reliability, SLO framework, error budget policy
```

The SRE team initially owns a defined scope (e.g., the production platform, or the top-5 critical services). As it matures, ownership expands. The existing ops team retains ownership of everything outside the initial scope.

### Advantages
- Clean start: no inherited culture debt, no legacy process baggage
- Can design the SRE operating model from first principles
- SRE team can achieve visible early wins that demonstrate the model's value to skeptics in the existing org
- Does not require asking the existing team to change before the new model is proven

### Risks
- Duplication: two teams covering overlapping services creates confusion about who owns what
- Resentment: existing ops team may view the SRE team as a resource grab or a signal that leadership thinks they failed
- Recruiting difficulty: SRE roles are competitive; hiring 4–8 experienced SREs in 12 months is harder than it appears
- The Greenfield team operates in a bubble if it doesn't actively engage the existing ops team

### Change Management Considerations
The primary OCM challenge is managing the relationship between the new SRE team and the existing ops organization.

**Critical actions**:
- Communicate the charter boundary explicitly: "The SRE team owns [X]. The ops team owns [Y]. There is no overlap."
- Design explicit handoff and escalation protocols between the two teams before the SRE team goes live
- Give the existing ops team a credible path forward — what happens to them in 12–24 months? Are they candidates to join the SRE team? Will their scope evolve?
- Make the SRE team's wins visible in a way that invites, not threatens: "Here's what the SRE model produced for Service X. We'd like to extend it to Services Y and Z in Q3 — here's how we'd work with the ops team to do that."

**Failure mode to avoid**: The SRE team and ops team developing into competing factions. This is common and toxic. Prevent it by designing shared rituals (joint incident reviews, shared postmortem process) from the beginning.

### T-Mobile Applicability
High applicability in large product lines with dedicated platform investment (e.g., T-Mobile's API platforms, network service platforms). The scale and headcount available at a Tier 1 carrier makes greenfield viable where it wouldn't be in smaller organizations. Watch for the silo risk — T-Mobile's org structure can amplify team boundary friction.

## Pattern 2 — Embedded SRE Model

### What It Is
Individual SREs are embedded directly inside product teams. There is no central SRE organization (or a minimal one as a center of excellence). SREs attend product team standups, participate in sprint planning, and own reliability for that team's services. They report either to the product team's manager or to a central SRE manager in a matrix.

### When to Use It
- Your product teams have been running their own reliability practices informally, but inconsistently
- You want to accelerate reliability improvement at the team level without adding a separate operational silo
- You have SRE-skilled engineers (or engineers who can be trained) who are strong collaborators and communicate well cross-functionally
- Your organization has strong team autonomy — product teams own their services end-to-end

### How It Works — Org Design
```
Engineering Organization
├── Product Team A (checkout, payments)
│   ├── Dev Engineers (n)
│   └── Embedded SRE (1–2) ← attends standups, owns SLOs, on-call rotation
├── Product Team B (search, catalog)
│   ├── Dev Engineers (n)
│   └── Embedded SRE (1–2)
└── Platform SRE Team (optional, small)
    └── SLO framework, tooling, cross-team standards, postmortem process
```

In the matrix variant, embedded SREs report to the platform SRE manager for career development and standards, but have day-to-day prioritization from the product team EM.

### Advantages
- Reliability expertise is where the problems are — in the team, not in a distant central function
- Embedded SREs develop deep domain knowledge of the services they support
- No "submit a ticket to SRE" friction — the SRE is in the room
- Product teams own their reliability outcomes, which creates clearer accountability

### Risks
- SREs embedded in product teams can "go native" — prioritizing features over reliability to maintain team harmony
- Without a central SRE organization, SRE standards and practices can drift across teams
- Career path risk: embedded SREs may feel isolated from a peer community
- Matrix reporting is structurally complex — who resolves conflict when the product EM and SRE manager disagree on priority?

### Change Management Considerations
The primary OCM challenge is managing the transition from central ops ownership to embedded SRE accountability.

**Critical actions**:
- Define the embedded SRE operating model in writing before any SRE is embedded — what they own, what they don't, how they interface with the product EM
- Run a 60-day pilot with one product team before broad rollout — learn from the first embedding before scaling
- Create a SRE community of practice that all embedded SREs participate in — this prevents drift and provides career community
- Explicitly address "what happens to the central ops team" — this transition typically means the central team shrinks or changes scope

**Failure mode to avoid**: Embedded SREs losing their SRE identity and becoming generalist engineers. Prevent with regular community of practice meetings, SRE-specific OKRs, and a central standards body.

### T-Mobile Applicability
Strong applicability for T-Mobile's product engineering org (consumer apps, API products). Less applicable for network operations, which has different reliability patterns and often requires centralized on-call expertise. The matrix reporting structure aligns with T-Mobile's existing matrixed org model in some divisions.

## Pattern 3 — SRE as Consulting

### What It Is
A central SRE team acts as an internal consulting function. They do not own services. They advise product teams on SLO design, review postmortems, recommend reliability investments, and define the SRE standards and tooling that teams can adopt. Product teams retain full ownership of their services and reliability practices.

### When to Use It
- Your organization is not ready for a cultural shift to embedded SRE, but leadership wants to start moving
- You have a small number of senior SREs and cannot staff embedded roles across all teams
- Product teams are protective of their autonomy and would resist SREs owning their services
- You want to build SRE capability in the organization before deciding on the long-term model

### How It Works — Org Design
```
Central SRE Team (consulting function)
├── SRE Manager
├── Senior SREs (specialists in SLO design, on-call practice, toil reduction, postmortem facilitation)
└── Output: recommendations, frameworks, tooling, reviews — not operational ownership

Product Teams (retain full ownership)
├── Own their SLOs, on-call, postmortems (with SRE advisory support)
└── Opt-in engagement with SRE consulting team
```

Engagement model: product teams request SRE advisory time via a defined process (e.g., quarterly SRE review, ad-hoc consult for new service launches).

### Advantages
- Low organizational disruption — no reporting line changes, no service handoffs
- SRE team can support many teams with a small headcount
- Product teams retain autonomy, which reduces political friction
- Good for building SRE knowledge across the org before committing to a structural model

### Risks
- Advisory without authority: SRE team makes recommendations, product teams can ignore them
- Incentive misalignment: product teams are rewarded for features; reliability is someone else's "consulting" concern
- SRE team can become a compliance checkbox rather than a genuine change driver
- "We talked to SRE" becomes the deliverable, not actual reliability improvement

### Change Management Considerations
The primary OCM challenge is ensuring advisory engagement has real impact rather than being performative.

**Critical actions**:
- Define what the SRE team can and cannot do — advisory scope must be explicit, or the team will drift into operational ownership by default
- Create accountability mechanisms: SRE reviews should produce action items that are tracked, not just recommendations that are noted
- Give the SRE team a mechanism to escalate when recommendations are ignored: a quarterly reliability report to VP level that names which teams adopted recommendations and which did not
- Plan for the transition to a structural model — SRE as consulting is a transitional state, not a permanent one

**Failure mode to avoid**: SRE team becomes a documentation factory with no measurable reliability outcome. Measure the SRE consulting team's impact by the reliability outcomes of the teams they advise, not by the number of reviews conducted.

### T-Mobile Applicability
Highest applicability in divisions with strong team autonomy culture or where a reorg is not currently politically feasible. Also a good first-year model while hiring SRE talent: you can start with 2–3 senior SREs in a consulting model and grow from there.

## Pattern 4 — Blameless Culture Retrofit

### What It Is
Rather than changing the org structure, this pattern focuses on introducing psychological safety, learning culture, and blameless retrospective practices into the existing ops or engineering team. No reporting lines change. No new teams are created. The transformation is cultural and behavioral.

### When to Use It
- Your organization has good technical practices but incident reviews are punitive or blame-focused
- Team members are afraid to share failure information openly, which slows learning and incident response
- You want to build the cultural foundation before introducing structural SRE practices (SLOs, error budgets)
- A recent high-profile incident was handled poorly from a human standpoint — blame was assigned, trust was damaged

### How It Works — Org Design
No org design change. The pattern operates within the existing structure.

**The three behavioral changes to install**:
1. **Blameless postmortem** for every P1/P2 — documented, reviewed, action items tracked
2. **Learning retrospectives** for projects, not just incidents — regularized and not tied to individual evaluation
3. **Psychological safety norms** — explicit team agreements about how failure is discussed (naming the system, not the person)

### Advantages
- No structural disruption — the lowest-friction entry point to SRE culture
- Produces immediate, visible improvement in team morale and incident quality
- Creates the cultural foundation for everything else (SLOs, error budgets, on-call best practices) to actually work
- Applicable to any team size, any maturity level

### Risks
- Cultural change without structural change can stall — blameless postmortems can be done correctly in isolation while blame culture persists everywhere else
- Leadership behavior must match the stated values: one punitive response from a senior leader after a postmortem undoes weeks of progress
- Without measurement, it is hard to demonstrate the business value of psychological safety improvements
- The "blameless" label can be co-opted: postmortems called "blameless" that still implicitly assign blame

### Change Management Considerations
The primary OCM challenge is leadership modeling. Culture change is leadership change first.

**Critical actions**:
- Senior leaders must visibly apply blameless principles to themselves — sharing their own mistakes, their own "what I would do differently," in team settings
- Run the first 3–5 blameless postmortems with the manager as facilitator, then systematically transfer facilitation to the team
- Celebrate learning publicly: when a postmortem produces a systemic fix that prevents a future incident, name it, share it, credit the team
- Create an explicit escalation path for when blame occurs: if someone is blamed in a postmortem or meeting, there is a named process for addressing it (ideally, a private conversation with the manager the same day)

**Failure mode to avoid**: The manager runs blameless postmortems but the skip-level manager references incidents in performance reviews in a way that re-introduces blame. This destroys psychological safety faster than any postmortem can build it.

### T-Mobile Applicability
High applicability across all of T-Mobile's SRE and engineering org, particularly in teams that have experienced high-profile incidents with poor handling. This pattern is often the right first step before any structural SRE transformation, because structural changes fail if the underlying culture is punitive.

## Pattern 5 — Error Budget Introduction

### What It Is
This pattern applies to organizations that already have SLOs defined but have not formalized the consequences of budget consumption. The change is specifically: adding an error budget policy that determines what happens when the budget is healthy (deploy more) and what happens when it is exhausted (reliability sprint, feature freeze, escalation).

### When to Use It
- Your team has SLOs defined and measured for at least 2–3 services
- Engineering and product teams have a basic shared understanding of what an SLO is
- You want to move from "SLOs as a measurement tool" to "SLOs as a decision-making tool"
- Feature vs. reliability tension is a recurring conflict that needs a structured resolution mechanism

### How It Works — Org Design
No org design change. The pattern adds a decision framework on top of existing SLO infrastructure.

**The error budget policy (the artifact to produce)**:
- At X% budget consumed: automated alert to engineering and product leads
- At Y% budget consumed: joint review; deployment pace decision required
- At 100% budget consumed: reliability sprint mandated; feature work paused; VP notification
- Exception process: how to override the policy when genuinely needed (VP approval, documented rationale)

**The error budget review (the ritual to add)**:
- Monthly meeting with engineering and product leadership
- Review of SLO performance, budget consumption rate, and trend
- Explicit decision made: deploy faster (if budget is healthy) or invest in reliability (if budget is at risk)

### Advantages
- Creates a shared language and a shared decision framework for the feature vs. reliability trade-off
- Removes the negotiation from engineering-product tension: the budget is the arbiter, not a manager's judgment call
- Builds on existing SLO investment — this is an extension, not a reinvention
- Demonstrates the practical value of SLOs in a way that SLO definition workshops alone do not

### Risks
- If product teams do not genuinely accept the error budget policy, they will route around it through escalations and exceptions
- An overly rigid policy (feature freeze at every budget exceedance) drives SLO inflation — teams set lower targets to protect their error budget
- The error budget policy requires VP-level enforcement: a policy that the Director cannot backstop is not a policy
- Data quality risk: error budget decisions are only as good as the SLO measurement accuracy

### Change Management Considerations
The primary OCM challenge is product team buy-in. The error budget policy only works if product leadership treats it as their constraint, not just engineering's.

**Critical actions**:
- Co-create the error budget policy with product leadership — do not hand it to them as an engineering policy
- Pilot with one service before rolling out to all — learn how the policy works in practice, find the edge cases, then expand
- Make the first error budget review a collaborative discussion, not a reporting meeting — the point is shared decision-making, not accountability
- Communicate what the policy is NOT: "This is not a way to block feature work. This is a shared agreement about when we prioritize which kind of investment."

**Failure mode to avoid**: Product teams experience the error budget policy as a recurring tax or a "no" machine. If the policy creates adversarial dynamics instead of shared ownership, the issue is that product does not feel co-ownership of the SLO. Go back and renegotiate the SLO targets jointly.

### T-Mobile Applicability
High applicability in T-Mobile's consumer and enterprise product engineering divisions where SLOs exist but error budget thinking has not been formalized. The T-Mobile scale (millions of subscribers, strict SLA commitments) makes error budget discipline a genuine business need — the business case writes itself with one or two large-impact incident cost analyses.

## Pattern Comparison Summary

| Pattern | Org Change | OCM Complexity | Time to First Value | Best First Step When... |
|---------|-----------|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Greenfield SRE Team | High (new team, new headcount) | High | 6–12 months | Existing ops has no path to SRE adoption in place |
| Embedded SRE Model | High (reporting line changes) | High | 3–6 months | Product teams are ready for accountability ownership |
| SRE as Consulting | Low (advisory only) | Medium | 2–4 months | You have few SREs and need to start without structural change |
| Blameless Culture Retrofit | None (behavioral only) | Medium | 1–3 months | Blame culture is the primary blocker to any other SRE practice |
| Error Budget Introduction | None (policy addition) | Medium | 1–2 months | SLOs already exist; you need to operationalize them |

**General recommendation**: Start with Blameless Culture Retrofit and/or Error Budget Introduction (if SLOs exist) before attempting Greenfield or Embedded patterns. The cultural and decision-framework foundation makes structural changes dramatically more likely to succeed.
