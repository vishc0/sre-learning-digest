# Change Resistance Patterns — Director Response Reference

*10 resistance patterns, their root causes, and the specific response a Director-level leader should take. Use this as a diagnostic: identify the pattern first, then apply the response.*

---

## Pattern 1 — The "Yes-But"

**How it manifests**:
The individual agrees verbally in meetings. "Yes, we should do this." "Totally on board." Then, in the next meeting, a new objection appears. "But wait, what about X?" Once X is addressed, Y appears. Once Y is addressed, Z appears. The objections are never exhausted. Each one sounds reasonable in isolation.

**Root cause**:
This person has a fundamental reservation about the change that they are unwilling or unable to state directly — because they fear conflict, because they don't fully understand their own objection, or because openly opposing leadership feels risky. The "yes-but" pattern is a face-saving way to delay without declaring resistance.

**Director Response**:
Name the pattern without shaming the person.

> "I've noticed that in each conversation, we resolve one concern and a new one surfaces. I want to understand what you're really worried about — because I don't think it's the specific technical issues we keep addressing. Can we set aside the agenda for 20 minutes and talk about what's actually driving this?"

Then: listen without defending the change. The goal is to surface the real objection. Once you hear it — even if it's vague — reflect it back: "So it sounds like what you're worried about is X. Is that right?"

If the real objection is substantive: address it and modify the plan if warranted. If it's preference-based: acknowledge it and be direct: "I hear you, and I understand this isn't your preference. We're moving forward with this approach. What would make it easier for you to work within it?"

**When to escalate**: If the "yes-but" pattern continues after you've named it and surfaced the real objection, this person is no longer a Skeptic — they are a Blocker. Escalate to your executive sponsor.

## Pattern 2 — The Silent Resister

**How it manifests**:
The individual attends every meeting. Nods when others speak. Does not raise objections. Does not ask questions. Returns to their desk and changes nothing. Their behavior before the change and after the change is identical. The resistance is invisible until you look at outcomes.

**Root cause**:
This person has decided the change is not real, not permanent, or not worth resisting openly. They have seen initiatives come and go. They are waiting it out. In some cases, they are also passive-aggressively demonstrating that the change has no teeth.

**Director Response**:
Do not mistake silence for agreement — and do not punish it. The response is a direct, low-stakes 1:1 conversation.

> "I want to check in specifically with you on [change name]. I've noticed you haven't flagged any concerns, but I also haven't seen you apply [specific behavior] in your work yet. I want to understand — is there something in the way, or is there a concern you haven't shared?"

The key move: make it safe to express the real reason for inaction. "If the training wasn't useful, tell me. If you don't have capacity, tell me. If you think this is going away in a month, tell me — because that's important for me to understand."

Then: follow up. If you say "let me know what you need" and they say "I need X," provide X within 48 hours. Responsive action is what signals to a silent resister that this change is real.

**When to escalate**: If the inaction continues after two direct conversations and provision of stated needs, this is a performance issue. Document the conversations and move to a performance management discussion with HR.

## Pattern 3 — The Credible Skeptic

**How it manifests**:
This person is a high-performer, respected by peers, and vocal about their opposition. They raise specific, technically coherent objections in meetings — not just complaints, but reasoned arguments. Other team members listen to them carefully. When the Credible Skeptic says "I don't think this will work," the room shifts.

**Root cause**:
Genuine concern, based on real experience. This person has usually seen similar initiatives fail, or has domain knowledge about why the proposed approach has limitations. Their opposition is not personal and not political — it is substantive.

**Director Response**:
This is not a problem to neutralize — it is a resource. Treat the Credible Skeptic as an expert consultant, not an opponent.

> "I've heard your concerns and I take them seriously. I'd like to do this: can we spend an hour together where you walk me through specifically where you think this approach breaks down? I want to understand the failure mode you're anticipating."

Then: listen, document, and evaluate the concern honestly. There are two outcomes:
- Their concern reveals a real gap in the plan → modify the plan, credit them with surfacing it, and bring them back to show them the adjustment. A Credible Skeptic whose concern changes the plan becomes a powerful ally.
- Their concern reflects a preference or a past experience that doesn't apply here → address the comparison directly. "You mentioned [past initiative]. Here's what's different this time: [specific differences]."

Never dismiss or minimize a Credible Skeptic in front of the group. It will harden them and damage your credibility with the team.

**When to escalate**: If the Credible Skeptic is actively organizing resistance (lobbying peers, escalating to your manager without having come to you first), name it in a 1:1 and address it directly. This becomes a professional conduct issue, not just a change management issue.

## Pattern 4 — The Executive Sponsor Who Pulls Support

**How it manifests**:
Your executive sponsor committed to the change. They were present at the launch. Then: they stop attending the monthly SRE review. Their proxy attends instead. Their proxy stops attending. The change is no longer mentioned in their all-hands updates. When you ask for help removing a blocker, the response is slow or vague.

**Root cause**:
Competing priority has emerged (new product launch, reorg, budget pressure, a different initiative has their attention). The change is no longer on their personal radar. This is usually not malicious — it is a bandwidth and priority shift.

**Director Response**:
Request a 30-minute 1:1 directly, not through email chains.

> "I want to give you a quick update on [change name] and also get your read on priority. We're at [phase / milestone]. The team needs [specific thing] in the next 2–3 weeks. I want to make sure this is still a priority from your perspective before we proceed."

Then: be concrete about the cost of disengagement. Not as a threat, but as information: "If we don't get [blocker resolved] in the next 3 weeks, [specific consequence] happens. I wanted to make sure you're aware." Give them a specific ask — not "please be more engaged" but "I need you to have a 15-minute conversation with [Director Torres] about the platform transition this week."

**When to escalate**: If the sponsor explicitly deprioritizes the change, treat it as a change in mandate. Document it. Adjust the change plan accordingly — a change without active executive sponsorship typically requires a slower timeline, smaller scope, or a new sponsor.

## Pattern 5 — The Martyred Overworker

**How it manifests**:
"I'm already at 120%. I support this in theory, but there is no possible way my team can take on anything else right now." This concern is raised repeatedly, in team settings and 1:1s. The individual is genuinely busy. They may also be using busyness as a shield — it is hard to argue with.

**Root cause**:
Real capacity constraint, possibly combined with a prioritization problem (if everything is priority 1, nothing is). In some cases: a belief that the change is additional work rather than a replacement for existing work, or a genuine lack of understanding of what adoption actually requires.

**Director Response**:
Separate the capacity concern from the change resistance.

First: acknowledge the capacity reality without dismissing it. "I hear you, and I know your team is stretched. I want to be specific about what this actually requires from you and when."

Then: quantify the ask. "The specific ask for your team this quarter is: one engineer participating in the beta onboarding (8 hours total), and one deployment via the new platform. That's it. We can figure out the full migration timeline after that."

If capacity is genuinely the issue: negotiate the timeline, not the commitment. "If this quarter is impossible, what does Q3 look like?" Get a specific date on the calendar.

If it's a prioritization shield: "What would you need to drop to make room for this? Let's look at the list together." This puts the choice back to them — they either deprioritize something or they own the prioritization decision.

**When to escalate**: If this pattern persists for 2+ quarters across multiple conversations, escalate to the executive sponsor to clarify organizational priority. "Your team has not been able to make capacity for this. I want to make sure we're aligned on where this falls in their priority stack."

## Pattern 6 — The False Implementer

**How it manifests**:
This person reports progress in status meetings. "Yes, we've adopted the new process." "We're using the platform." Then you look at the metrics and: there are no deployments in the platform logs, no postmortems in the shared folder, no evidence in the system. Surface-level compliance — the meeting behaviors change, the actual work does not.

**Root cause**:
This person understands what they're supposed to say and has learned to say it. The underlying issue is one of two things: they don't actually know how to do the thing they're claiming to do (skill gap disguised as compliance), or they are actively deceiving, which is a values issue.

**Director Response**:
Respond to the data, not the self-report.

> "I want to talk about [metric]. You've reported that [X is happening], but when I look at [system log / dashboard / shared folder], I see [what the data shows]. Can you walk me through what's happening there?"

Listen carefully to the response. A skill gap will surface as confusion or a process question: "Oh, I didn't know that's where it was supposed to be logged." A values issue will surface as defensiveness, a new excuse, or a blame shift.

If it's a skill gap: address it immediately — a brief working session, a specific piece of documentation, or a champion pairing. Then check back in one week.

If it's a values issue: this is a straight performance conversation. "I'm not seeing the actual behavior match what you're reporting. I need to understand what's in the way, and I need it to be honest." Document the conversation. Involve HR if it continues.

**When to escalate**: False implementation that persists after being directly named is a performance management issue, not a change management issue.

## Pattern 7 — The "We Tried This Before"

**How it manifests**:
"We tried SLOs in 2022. It didn't work." "We had a blameless postmortem process. It fell apart after two months." "We've gone through three platform migrations. Each one cost us six months." These statements are presented as permanent verdicts, not historical data points.

**Root cause**:
Real scar tissue from genuine past failures. The person learned something painful and is trying to protect the team from repeating it. This is not cynicism — it is experience-based risk aversion. The failure is being used as a permanent barrier, but the underlying concern is legitimate.

**Director Response**:
Validate the history before engaging the future.

> "That's important context. Can you tell me what specifically failed? What was the root cause — was it the process itself, the tooling, the support level, or something else?"

Then: be specific about what is different this time. Not "this time will be different" (vague and unconvincing) but "in 2022, the SLO initiative failed because [reason you've heard]. The specific thing we're doing differently this time is [concrete change]. Does that address the root cause you saw?"

If they say yes: you've won them. They now have a reason to believe.

If they say no: their objection is more specific than you thought. Go back to step one — "what else was different about how this failed?"

**When to escalate**: This pattern rarely requires escalation. It resolves through direct conversation and specificity. If it does not resolve, the person may be using past failure as a proxy for a different, unstated concern — return to Pattern 1 (The "Yes-But") response.

## Pattern 8 — The Technical Objector

**How it manifests**:
The individual raises technically specific, often legitimate, issues that make the change seem infeasible. "Our service uses a non-standard deployment pattern that the platform doesn't support." "The SLO definition workshop assumes we have latency metrics, but our system doesn't instrument that way." "The runbook format requires Confluence, but our team is on Notion."

**Root cause**:
There are two possibilities: the technical issue is real and the change plan has a genuine gap, or the technical issue is being used as a proxy for change resistance (because technical objections are harder to argue with than personal preference objections). Both are possible simultaneously.

**Director Response**:
Take the technical concern seriously first.

> "That's a real constraint. Let me bring in [platform engineer / relevant SME] and let's look at it specifically. I want to understand whether this is a gap we need to fix in the platform, or whether there's a workaround we haven't identified."

Then: convene a working session (30–60 minutes, specific people, specific question). Either the gap is real — in which case you fix it, which removes the objection and usually converts the person — or the gap turns out to be addressable with a workaround, which removes the technical cover.

If a real technical gap is identified: log it as a platform requirement, communicate a remediation timeline, and agree on a provisional workaround in the meantime. Ask: "If we address [gap] by [date], does that remove your concern?"

**When to escalate**: If the technical issues are valid but the platform team cannot address them within a reasonable window, escalate the roadmap priority. A technically legitimate objection that is ignored does not go away — it becomes organizational debt.

## Pattern 9 — The Informal Leader Resister

**How it manifests**:
This person has no formal authority — they might be a Staff Engineer, a senior IC, or even a mid-level engineer with a long tenure. But they are the social center of the team. Other engineers check with them before forming opinions. When this person is skeptical, the team is skeptical. When they are energized, the team is energized. They are not saying anything directly opposing — they are shaping the informal narrative.

**Root cause**:
This person's identity and status are tied to the current way of working. The change challenges not just a process but their role as the person who knows how things work. Alternatively: they have legitimate concerns but express them laterally (to peers) rather than upward (to you) because they don't trust that upward expression will be heard.

**Director Response**:
Bring them in — do not try to work around them.

> "I want to get your read on [change name]. Not what you think I want to hear — your real read. You see things in this team that I don't always see, and I want your honest assessment of what's working and what isn't."

This is a significant investment of trust. It is also the only thing that works. An informal leader who feels heard and respected becomes your most powerful ally. An informal leader who is ignored becomes your most effective opponent.

If their concern is substantive: modify the plan, credit them, and watch the team dynamic shift as they begin to reflect a more positive — or at least neutral — posture.

If their concern is about their own identity/role: find a way to give them an ownership stake in the new way. Can they run the first blameless postmortem? Lead the runbook writing session? Become the team's SRE champion? They need to see that the new world has a place for them.

**When to escalate**: This pattern almost never requires formal escalation. It requires personal investment. If you are not willing to invest the time in this individual, the change will cost more than the time the investment would have taken.

## Pattern 10 — The "Not My Team's Problem"

**How it manifests**:
"The reliability issue is because the infrastructure team hasn't fixed their provisioning. Talk to them." "The deployment velocity problem is a product prioritization issue, not an engineering one." "We'd love to adopt the platform, but we're blocked by the security team." Every friction point is located in another team's domain. Responsibility is perpetually outside the room.

**Root cause**:
A combination of genuine cross-team dependencies (the blocker may be partially real) and a pattern of learned helplessness or political self-protection. This person has learned that naming blockers in other teams deflects pressure from their own team.

**Director Response**:
Separate the external dependency from the internal action.

> "You may be right that [other team] is contributing to the problem. Let's separate that. Setting aside what they need to do — what is your team doing this week to move forward? What is the part of this that is genuinely in your team's control?"

Then: take the external dependency seriously. "If the security team is a blocker, let's make that visible and fix it. I'll escalate that if you need me to. But I'm also going to track what your team is doing independently of that."

This approach validates the concern (the cross-team issue is real) while closing the escape hatch (it doesn't excuse inaction on the team's own scope).

**When to escalate**: If the external dependency is real and is genuinely blocking your team, escalate it. Going to the other team's manager or director and naming the blocker specifically is part of your job. Do not allow an external blocker to be used as permanent cover — but also do not ask a team to proceed without clearing a genuine dependency.
