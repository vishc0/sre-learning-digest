# Organizational Change Management Framework
## Director-Level Fluency for Platform Engineering and SRE Transformation

---

## Purpose

A Director who cannot manage organizational change will fail — not at the technical work, but at everything surrounding it. You can architect a flawless SRE operating model, design a zero-downtime platform migration, or write an airtight reliability charter. None of it matters if the people who need to adopt it resist, stall, or quietly route around it.

This is not a soft-skills concern. Change management is an operational discipline. It has models, failure modes, diagnostic tools, and measurable outcomes. The directors who succeed at large-scale transformations — SRE culture adoption, platform engineering consolidation, on-call modernization, team restructuring — treat change management with the same rigor they bring to system design.

This framework exists because engineering leaders are disproportionately underprepared here. The skills that get engineers promoted (technical depth, system reasoning, delivering reliable software) are mostly individual and technical. The skills required at Director level (coalition building, communication architecture, resistance mapping, stakeholder influence) are organizational and human. The gap between what got you here and what is required next is real, and this framework is designed to close it.

## Key Mental Model

Think of a change initiative as a living system. Every successful transformation has three components that must work together:

**Kotter's 8 Steps — the spine.** This is the sequential structure that ensures you do not skip steps. Most change efforts fail because they skip urgency (step 1) or anchoring (step 8). The 8 steps give you a map of the terrain. Without them, you are navigating blindly.

**Stakeholder Management — the muscle.** The 8 steps tell you what to do and when. Stakeholder management tells you who you need to move, and in what direction, to make each step succeed. A change initiative with no stakeholder map is like a product launch with no customer discovery. You are solving for the wrong audience.

**Communication — the nervous system.** Information is what connects the change initiative to the people who must live inside it. In large organizations, the nervous system degrades over distance. A message that is clear at the Director level becomes ambiguous at the manager level and unrecognizable at the individual contributor level. Communication architecture — channels, cadences, framing by audience — is how you keep the signal intact.

When any one of these three fails, the transformation fails. Most engineering leaders invest in the spine (process), underinvest in the muscle (stakeholders), and nearly ignore the nervous system (communication). This framework is designed to correct that imbalance.

## What Interviewers Are Testing

When a Director-level interviewer asks "tell me about a significant organizational change you led," they are not asking for a project narrative. They are running a structured diagnostic across several dimensions:

**Complexity recognition.** Did you understand that the change had a human dimension, not just a technical one? Did you distinguish between deploying new tooling and changing how people think and work?

**Stakeholder sophistication.** Who did you identify as the critical players? Did you have a deliberate strategy for each? Did you find the blockers before they found you?

**Communication discipline.** How did you communicate? At what frequency? Through which channels? Did you adapt your message for different audiences? Did you manage the information vacuum?

**Measurement and feedback loops.** How did you know the change was working? What signals did you use? Did you course-correct mid-stream, and how?

**Resilience under resistance.** How did you handle the people who did not want the change? Did you dismiss resistance, manage around it, or address it directly?

A strong answer covers all five dimensions with specificity. A weak answer is a project delivery story — we shipped the platform, adoption went up, everyone was happy. Interviewers at VP-and-above organizations know that answer is incomplete, and they will probe until they find the gaps.

This framework prepares you to give a complete answer — and more importantly, to lead in a way that produces one.
