## Section 1 — Change Description [REQUIRED]

### What Is Changing

*Write 3–5 sentences describing the change in plain language. Avoid jargon. If a team member who has no context read this, would they understand what is different?*

**Example fill-in**:
> The SRE team is moving from a central shared-services model to an embedded model, where SREs will be co-located with product teams. Each product team will have 1–2 dedicated SREs who attend their standups, participate in sprint planning, and own reliability for that team's services. The central SRE team will remain as a platform and tooling function.

### What Is NOT Changing

*Explicitly state what stays the same. This is as important as what changes — it prevents rumor and reduces anxiety.*

**Example fill-in**:
> - Compensation and titles for all SREs are not changing
> - The on-call rotation tooling (PagerDuty) is not changing
> - SRE headcount is not changing — no roles are being eliminated
> - The SRE manager remains the people manager for all SREs, regardless of which product team they are embedded in

[YOUR TEXT HERE]

### Why Now

*State the business, technical, or organizational driver for this change. What happens if we don't change? What opportunity does this create?*

**Example fill-in**:
> Product teams have consistently cited slow response times from the central SRE team as a blocker to their deployment velocity. Embedded SREs will reduce this latency and build deeper domain knowledge. The 2025 engineering strategy identified reliability as a top-3 investment area — this restructuring is the operational execution of that strategy.

## Section 8 — Rollback / Pause Criteria

*Define in advance what would cause you to stop the change, slow it down, or reverse it. This is not weakness — it is disciplined change management.*

### Pause Criteria (stop forward progress, assess)

The change will be paused if:
- [ ] [e.g., Executive sponsor formally withdraws support]
- [ ] [e.g., More than 20% of directly affected team members indicate they plan to leave as a result of the change]
- [ ] [e.g., A critical system failure is directly attributable to the change in progress]
- [ ] [e.g., Legal or HR identifies a compliance issue with the current approach]

**What happens during a pause**: Change plan owner convenes a 48-hour review with executive sponsor and HR partner. A decision to continue, modify, or stop is made and communicated within 72 hours.

### Stop/Reverse Criteria (reverse the change)

The change will be reversed if:
- [ ] [e.g., The business driver for the change is no longer valid (merger, acquisition, strategy pivot)]
- [ ] [e.g., The change is causing measurable, significant harm to team retention or morale with no corrective path]
- [ ] [e.g., The change cannot achieve its stated success metrics and there is no viable adjustment]

**Reversal is not automatically a failure.** A well-managed reversal — acknowledged, explained, and cleanly executed — preserves more trust than a struggling change held together by force. Document the decision and the rationale. Learn from it.
