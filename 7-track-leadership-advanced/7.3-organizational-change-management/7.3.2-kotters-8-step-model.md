# Chapter 2: Kotter's 8-Step Model Applied to Engineering Org Change

---

## Why Kotter Is Director Table Stakes

John Kotter's 8-step change model, introduced in his 1996 book "Leading Change," has become the dominant framework for large-scale organizational transformation in the Western management canon. It is taught in every major MBA program, referenced in nearly every Director and VP-level interview conversation about change leadership, and used as a diagnostic lens by organizational consultants and senior executives across industries.

You do not need to agree with every claim Kotter makes to benefit from fluency in his model. What the model gives you is a shared vocabulary and a structured sequence that interviewers, executives, and peers will recognize immediately. When a VP of Engineering asks "how did you build the case for the SRE transformation?", the Kotter-fluent answer is not just a story — it is a story framed around urgency, coalition, vision, and anchoring, with explicit acknowledgment of where the resistance emerged and how you addressed it. That framing signals Director-level thinking. A pure narrative answer signals project management.

Beyond interview preparation: the model is genuinely useful as a diagnostic. When a change initiative is stalling, locating the stall within the 8-step sequence is often the fastest way to identify the right intervention.

## The 8 Steps — Engineering Applications

### Step 1: Create a Sense of Urgency

**What Kotter says:** Change does not happen without urgency. If the people who need to change do not believe the status quo is unsustainable, they will not take on the friction and risk of changing it.

**Why most engineering orgs skip it:** Technical leaders often believe the technical case speaks for itself. "Our error rates are high, our on-call is broken, our deployment velocity is a third of industry standard — obviously we need to change." The problem is that this is obvious to the person who is immersed in the data, not to the product manager whose primary concern is feature delivery velocity, or the finance leader who approved the current tooling investment three years ago.

**Engineering application:** Build the urgency case from data that matters to your audiences. SLO burn rates tell reliability engineers there is a problem; they do not tell a product VP the same thing. Translate: a service burning its error budget at 3x the normal rate means the engineering team is spending 40% of sprint capacity on reactive incident work instead of shipping features. That framing lands differently. Use toil metrics, incident frequency, time-to-detect, and cost-of-downtime to quantify the urgency in terms each stakeholder group recognizes as real.

**Common failure mode:** Declaring urgency by executive fiat. "Leadership has decided we are moving to SRE." This creates compliance, not urgency. People comply with directives; they mobilize around genuine threats. The urgency must be felt, not just announced.

---

### Step 2: Build a Guiding Coalition

**What Kotter says:** No single leader can drive large-scale change alone. You need a coalition — a group of influential people across the organization who are aligned on the direction and willing to actively sponsor it.

**Engineering application:** For an SRE transformation at T-Mobile scale, the guiding coalition typically needs to include: the platform engineering lead (technical credibility), a VP or SVP from the product organization (cross-BU legitimacy), the CISO or a security representative (compliance and risk framing), a finance stakeholder (budget and resource allocation authority), and at least one respected senior IC or manager from the teams being asked to change (grassroots credibility that senior leaders alone cannot provide).

The coalition's function is to make the change visible from multiple positions of authority simultaneously, so it cannot be waited out by any single skeptical group. If only the SRE Director is advocating for the change, an engineering manager can reasonably conclude that this is one leader's initiative and it may pass. When the product VP, the platform lead, and the CISO are all aligned, the calculus changes.

**Common failure mode:** A coalition of people who all have the same perspective. A room full of SRE leaders who agree with each other is not a coalition — it is a committee. The coalition's power comes from its diversity of organizational position and influence network.

---

### Step 3: Form a Vision and Strategy

**What Kotter says:** People cannot mobilize toward an undefined destination. The vision must be clear enough that it can be communicated in under five minutes and evaluated against concrete decisions.

**Engineering application:** "We are going to adopt SRE practices" is not a vision. It is a category. A vision is: "By end of next fiscal year, every Tier 1 service will have defined SLOs, product teams will own reliability decisions within their error budget, and the platform team will focus on reducing toil rather than managing incidents on behalf of product teams." That statement is specific enough to evaluate: does this decision support that future state, or work against it?

The strategy component is where the sequencing lives: which teams go first, what the maturity model looks like, what the definition of "done" is at each phase. Without a strategy, the vision remains aspirational. With a strategy, it becomes a plan people can align to or push back on — and pushback on a specific plan is vastly more productive than vague resistance to a vague direction.

**Common failure mode:** A vision that is inspirational but not operational. If your vision cannot be used to resolve a concrete disagreement — "should this team be included in the first wave or the second?" — it is not specific enough.

---

### Step 4: Communicate the Vision

**What Kotter says:** The vision must be communicated relentlessly and through multiple channels. Most leaders under-communicate by a factor of ten.

**Engineering application:** Channel strategy matters. An all-hands is the right venue for vision and urgency — the whole organization hears the same message simultaneously. Team meetings are for tactics and progress updates — what is happening this quarter, what is changing for this team specifically. One-on-ones are for individual concerns — what does this mean for my role, my workload, my career? Confluence and Slack are for artifacts and status — the source of truth people can return to. Skip-levels are for temperature checks — what is the actual sentiment two layers below you?

Each channel has a different role and a different audience posture. Using an all-hands to address individual concerns creates public forums for private anxiety. Using 1:1s to introduce the vision means every person hears a slightly different version. The channel selection is not incidental — it shapes what the message can do.

**Common failure mode:** Communicating once and assuming retention. In large organizations, people need to hear a significant message multiple times before it moves from awareness to internalization. The feeling that you have said it enough is almost always wrong.

---

### Step 5: Enable Broad-Based Action

**What Kotter says:** Once people understand and accept the vision, the organization must remove the barriers that prevent them from acting on it.

**Engineering application:** The barriers in an engineering org change are often structural, not motivational. People want to adopt SRE practices but the tooling is not provisioned. They want to participate in game days but their OKRs are measured entirely on feature delivery. They want to own their reliability but approval gates for deployment changes require a separate team's sign-off that takes three business days.

Enabling action means identifying these structural blockers and removing them, which often requires the Director to exercise authority outside their direct span of control. Tooling access may require a conversation with the platform team. OKR misalignment may require a conversation with a peer Director and a product VP. Approval gates may require a process change that lives in another org's workflow. None of this happens automatically — it requires active sponsorship from someone with enough organizational reach to navigate those dependencies.

**Common failure mode:** Assuming people are not changing because they are resistant, when in fact they are not changing because they are structurally blocked. Diagnose before attributing.

---

### Step 6: Generate Short-Term Wins

**What Kotter says:** Long-running change initiatives without visible early wins lose momentum. People need evidence that the change is working before they will sustain the effort required to maintain it.

**Engineering application:** In an SRE transformation, early wins might look like: the first properly conducted postmortem that produces an action item that gets implemented within two weeks. The first monthly error budget review meeting where a product team makes a concrete reliability investment decision. The first SLO that catches a degradation before a customer reports it. These are small in scope but significant as proof of concept — they demonstrate that the new operating model produces outcomes that the old one did not.

The Director's role is to identify, stage, and amplify these wins. You identify them by staying close to the work. You stage them by sequencing the rollout to ensure early participants are teams that are most likely to have early successes. You amplify them by making them visible — in all-hands, in Slack, in conversations with the guiding coalition — so they become the shared evidence base that the skeptics have to contend with.

**Common failure mode:** Waiting for organic wins instead of engineering them. Early wins require deliberate selection of pilots and deliberate communication of results.

---

### Step 7: Sustain Acceleration

**What Kotter says:** After early wins, organizations often mistake the first sign of success for the finish line and reduce the urgency and investment before the change has fully taken root.

**Engineering application:** The six-month dip is real. The teams that participated in the first wave of the SRE rollout have momentum. The teams in the second and third waves are watching skeptically to see if the first-wave teams' situation actually improved. The guiding coalition's attention has fragmented as other priorities compete. The senior executive who sponsored the initiative has moved on to the next transformation.

Sustaining acceleration requires the Director to actively maintain the change's priority position — which means continuing to show up, continuing to connect the change to business outcomes, and continuing to expand the scope rather than consolidating too early. It means using the early wins as evidence to bring the next cohort of teams into the change, not as justification for declaring victory.

**Common failure mode:** Initiative fatigue. Directors who have been running the change for 6-9 months are often genuinely tired of talking about it. That fatigue is not shared by the teams who are just entering the change, for whom it is still new and disruptive. The communication and attention required at month seven is not less than at month two — it is different, but not smaller.

---

### Step 8: Anchor New Approaches in the Culture

**What Kotter says:** Change is not complete until it is embedded in how the organization operates by default — in the systems of accountability, selection, onboarding, and reward that new people enter and existing people navigate.

**Engineering application:** This step is where most engineering transformations ultimately succeed or fail in the long term. If SRE practices are adopted only because a particular Director is championing them, they will not survive that Director's departure or the next reorganization. The practices must be encoded in the systems that outlast any individual's tenure.

Anchoring looks like: hiring criteria that include reliability engineering skills and cultural fit for ownership-based operating models. Onboarding processes that introduce SRE concepts and expectations before a new hire writes their first line of code. Performance review cycles that explicitly evaluate reliability ownership as a dimension of engineering excellence. Architectural review processes that require SLO definition before a new service enters production. These institutional mechanisms are what transforms a change initiative into a changed organization.

**Common failure mode at step 8:** Confusing adoption with anchoring. Adoption means people are doing the new things. Anchoring means the new things are what the organization expects and selects for, whether or not the original sponsor is in the room.

---

## The Two Steps Most Often Skipped

Kotter's research — and practical experience — consistently finds that steps 1 and 8 are the most frequently shortcut.

Step 1 is skipped because urgency-building feels slow. Leaders want to move to action, and building a compelling urgency case requires time, data synthesis, and stakeholder conversations before any visible progress is made.

Step 8 is skipped because by the time a change initiative reaches institutional embedding, the sponsoring leader has already moved on to the next initiative. The anchoring work is unglamorous and does not produce the visible milestones that get reported upward. It is also slow — changing hiring criteria and performance review structures operates on an annual cycle, not a quarterly one.

Directors who want to build durable transformations budget explicitly for both steps and resist the organizational pressure to skip to the middle.
