# Chapter 7: Managing Change Resistance — The Director's Most Important Skill

## Start From Respect

The standard framing of change resistance treats it as an obstacle — something to overcome, manage, or route around. That framing is both strategically wrong and ethically suspect. People who resist change in engineering organizations are almost always resisting for legitimate reasons. They have seen programs fail before. They understand the system in ways that the change sponsor does not. They are absorbing real risk that the change leader is not carrying personally.

The Director who leads with respect for the resister — not despite the resistance but because of it — has access to information that the change-at-all-costs leader never gets. Resistance, properly engaged, is the highest-quality feedback available to a change program. Dismiss it and you lose both the information and the person.

This chapter assumes that resisters have legitimate concerns until the evidence proves otherwise, and treats the Director's job as understanding those concerns precisely enough to distinguish the problems that need to be fixed from the dynamics that need to be navigated.

## The Six Resistance Patterns

### 1. "This Is How We've Always Done It"

This is almost never about attachment to a process. It is almost always about competence anxiety. The person saying this has built their professional identity, their peer reputation, and possibly their promotion history around mastery of the current state. The change threatens not just their workflow but their standing as an expert.

The Director's response is to surface the real fear before addressing the stated concern. The conversation has to create an exit ramp from the old identity to the new one. "You know this system better than anyone — what I need is your help making sure we don't lose what works while we change what doesn't" is not a manipulation. It is an accurate description of what change leadership actually needs from long-tenured engineers. Make that a genuine role, and the resistance often dissolves on its own.

### 2. "We Tried This Before and It Failed"

This is institutional memory, not resistance. Treat it as data. The correct response is to take the history seriously before defending the current initiative. Ask what specifically failed, who owned it, what the conditions were. If the failure conditions still exist and the current plan has not addressed them, the resister is right and the plan needs to change.

If the conditions genuinely differ, the Director's job is to make that case specifically: "The last SLO program failed because we didn't have the observability foundation. Here's what we've done differently this time." Vague reassurance ("this time is different") produces vague acceptance that evaporates when the first problem surfaces. Specific acknowledgment of past failures, paired with specific evidence of what changed, is the only argument that carries weight with someone who lived through the failure.

### 3. "The Timing Is Wrong"

This objection is the hardest to evaluate because it is often both valid and a delay tactic simultaneously. Timing resistance is legitimate when the organization is carrying a genuine competing priority — a merger integration, a regulatory deadline, a major product launch — that will actively undermine change adoption. It is a delay tactic when "not now" never becomes "now" regardless of what else is happening.

The test is specificity. Ask: "If not now, when? What would have to be true for the timing to be right?" A legitimate timing concern produces a concrete answer. A delay tactic produces another set of conditions that will never be met. If you cannot get a specific future state where the change would be acceptable, you are dealing with position, not timing.

### 4. "This Will Never Work Here Because [Unique Reason]"

The local exception argument. Every organization has people who believe their context is categorically different from anywhere the new practice has worked. Sometimes they are right. More often, the uniqueness is real but the implication — that it makes the change impossible — is not.

The way to engage this is not to dismiss the uniqueness claim but to separate it from the feasibility claim. "I hear that our incident volume is higher than a typical SRE team would manage — walk me through how that would break the on-call rotation model specifically." That question either reveals a real design constraint you need to account for, or it reveals that the uniqueness claim does not actually create the barrier the resister believes it does. Either outcome is useful.

When the local constraint is real and the change does need to be adapted, acknowledge it and adapt it. Forcing a change that genuinely does not fit the local context in service of organizational consistency is not leadership — it is stubbornness wearing the clothing of standardization.

### 5. The Silent Resister

The most dangerous pattern because it is the least visible. The silent resister attends the kickoff meeting, completes the training module, raises no objections in the group setting, and then continues doing exactly what they have always done. They have learned that surface compliance is less costly than visible opposition.

Detection requires 1:1 conversations, not group observation. In a 1:1, you are looking for: absence of specific examples of the new behavior, vague language about adoption ("we're working on it"), and a pattern where their team consistently lags on implementation metrics even though they personally express no objections.

The intervention is to name the pattern without accusation: "I notice we haven't seen the postmortems from your team in the last two sprints. Help me understand what's getting in the way." Then actually listen. Silent resisters often have real concerns they have decided are not safe to raise in public. Making those concerns safe to name is not just good change management — it is the most important information you can get about what is actually blocking adoption.

### 6. The Credible Skeptic

A high-performer. Long-tenured. Respected by their peers and by senior leadership. And deeply skeptical of your change program.

This person is simultaneously the most dangerous resister and the most valuable one. Their organizational credibility means their skepticism is contagious in ways that no other resister's skepticism is. If they decide the change is wrong, their peers will hear about it. If they decide the change is poorly designed, their opinion will reach executives who will start asking you questions.

But if they conclude, based on evidence and engagement, that the change is sound — even if they would have done it differently — that conclusion is also contagious. The credible skeptic who moves from skeptic to critical friend is worth more to the change program than ten enthusiastic early adopters.

The Director's investment here should be disproportionate. Spend time understanding their concerns at depth. Bring them into the design process where it is possible. Ask them explicitly what they would change and mean it. You are not guaranteed to convert them. But the process of genuine engagement — not persuasion, engagement — is the only approach that has a real chance.

## The 1:1 Conversation Playbook

The structure that works: listen fully first (do not formulate your response while they are speaking), validate the concern without validating the resistance behavior, separate the person from the behavior, and ask a generative question.

The generative question is the most important element: "What would have to be true for this to work for you?" That question does the work that none of the other elements can do. It moves the conversation from adversarial (here is why this is wrong) to collaborative (here is what would make this right). It also surfaces information the change plan may not have. The resister who answers that question honestly is giving you the requirements for a better change program.

---

## When to Route Around a Resister

There is a point past which continued engagement with a resister becomes an organizational liability. The signals: they have received genuine engagement and have not updated their position in response to any evidence, they are actively working to prevent others from adopting the change, their resistance has become about winning rather than about the quality of the change.

At that point, the Director's job is to contain the damage rather than convert the resister. That may mean restructuring who owns the change initiative so the resister is less central to it, or it may mean having a direct conversation about the behavioral line between productive skepticism and organizational obstruction. That conversation is not about their concern — it is about their conduct. Hold the line between the two.

---

## The Ethics of Change Management

Change management done poorly is manipulation dressed in process. Change management done well is one of the most honest things a leader can do, because it forces you to be clear about what is actually negotiable and what is not.

You owe people in a change program honesty about the non-negotiables: what is going to happen regardless of their input, what decisions have already been made. You also owe them genuine flexibility about everything that is not locked: the implementation approach, the timeline, the sequencing, the support structure. The failure mode is pretending that more is negotiable than actually is, which is discovered quickly and permanently damages trust. Better to be honest about the constraint and flexible about everything around it.

People can adapt to hard truths. They cannot adapt to false choices.
