# Chapter 1: Why Change Fails — and What Directors Do Differently

---

## The 70% Statistic and Why It Is Real

John Kotter's oft-cited finding — that approximately 70% of large-scale organizational change efforts fail to achieve their intended outcomes — is sometimes dismissed as a consultant's marketing figure. It is not. It is consistent with decades of organizational research across industries, reinforced more recently by McKinsey data on digital transformation and by the observable graveyard of initiatives that never made it past the pilot phase inside large companies.

The question worth sitting with is not whether the statistic is accurate. The question is: what is actually failing? The answer is almost never the technical design. The platform architecture was sound. The tooling was appropriate. The migration plan was well-sequenced. What failed was the human adoption surrounding the technical change. The on-call model was rolled out but the team never internalized ownership. The SRE function was created but the product teams kept routing reliability problems back to infrastructure. The platform was built but engineering teams kept bypassing it because no one made a compelling case for why they should use it.

Kotter identified eight reasons — which map to his eight steps — but they compress into three root causes: insufficient urgency to motivate change, insufficient coalition to sustain it through resistance, and insufficient anchoring to prevent regression once the initiative's sponsors stop paying close attention.

Directors who understand this do not ask "why is our change failing?" after the fact. They ask "which of these three failure modes are we most exposed to right now?" before the initiative launches.

## The Director as Change Agent, Not Change Announcer

There is a distinction that separates directors who succeed at transformation from those who do not: the difference between announcing a change and leading one.

Announcing a change means you have been involved in the decision, you communicate it to your organization in an all-hands or a Confluence page, and you then expect adoption to follow. This is what most engineers-turned-managers do by default. It feels complete. You have shared the information. What more is required?

Leading a change means you actively work the system of human resistance, stakeholder interests, communication gaps, and organizational inertia that will predictably slow or stop the adoption. It means you are present in the resistance — in the 1:1 conversations where an engineer tells you why this new model does not make sense for their team, in the meeting where a peer director raises concerns you had not anticipated, in the skip-level where ICs tell you the message they actually received bears no resemblance to what you intended to communicate.

The change agent role is operational. It requires ongoing investment of time and attention, not a one-time communication event. Directors who do it well think of the change initiative as a product they are responsible for — one that has customers (the people who must adopt it), a value proposition (why this change makes their situation better), and adoption metrics (how you know it is working).

## Four Root Causes of Change Resistance

Resistance is not irrational. It is nearly always a rational response to a perceived threat or genuine uncertainty. Directors who dismiss resistance as obstruction miss the information it contains. Each resistance signal is a diagnostic: what does this person believe they are losing, or risking, by adopting this change?

**1. Fear of Failure**

Engineers and engineering managers in particular have strong professional identities built on competence. When a change initiative requires them to operate in new ways — new tooling, new processes, new mental models — they face a period of competence reduction. They will be slower, more error-prone, more dependent on others. In a culture that equates speed and independence with performance, this is experienced as threatening.

In the SRE context: when you introduce SLO-based alerting to a team that has operated on symptom-based paging for five years, you are asking them to let go of a system they understand deeply and trust implicitly. The resistance is not laziness — it is a rational preference for known competence over uncertain learning.

**2. Loss of Status or Power**

Organizational change frequently redistributes decision-making authority. Platform engineering consolidations, for instance, often move infrastructure ownership from individual product teams to a centralized platform team. For a senior engineer who has spent years owning the deployment pipeline for their product, that consolidation is not just a process change — it is a reduction in their sphere of influence and expertise.

Resistance from this root cause often presents as technical objections. "Our workload has unique requirements the platform won't handle." The real objection is underneath: "I do not want to lose ownership of this domain." Directors who address only the technical objection without addressing the underlying power shift will see the same objection re-emerge in different forms.

**3. Genuine Uncertainty**

Some resistance is simply the reasonable response of thoughtful people to incomplete information. When you announce a structural change without explaining the reasoning behind it, the implications for people's roles, or the timeline for decisions that affect them directly, uncertainty fills the void. In large organizations like T-Mobile — where a Director's message passes through several layers of management before reaching individual contributors — information loss is predictable. People resist what they do not understand.

This form of resistance is the most correctable. The solution is more communication, more specificity, and more opportunity for questions before people draw their own conclusions.

**4. Workload Increase**

This one is often overlooked because it sounds like an excuse, but it is frequently accurate. Many change initiatives require real additional work from the people who are already at capacity. Asking an on-call engineer to participate in game days, postmortem reviews, and a new on-call rotation while they are still responsible for their existing feature delivery commitments is not a culture change — it is a workload problem. Resistance to it is correct.

Directors who skip over this root cause lose credibility with the teams they are asking to change. People can tell when their concerns are being dismissed. If the resistance is legitimate, acknowledge it. Build a plan that addresses the real constraint — headcount, reduced delivery commitments during the transition period, tooling that removes overhead rather than adding it.

## Technical Change vs. Organizational Change

This is perhaps the most important distinction in this entire framework. A Director who conflates the two will consistently misdiagnose their problems.

Technical change is about systems: deploying Kubernetes, migrating to a new observability platform, adopting a new CI/CD pipeline. It has a defined state change (before/after), can be verified through objective measures, and can in many cases be rolled back if it fails.

Organizational change is about how people work, measure themselves, relate to each other, and understand their role. Moving from a ticket-based infrastructure request model to a self-service platform is not just a technical change — it fundamentally alters the relationship between platform teams and product teams. The platform team stops being a service provider that is called when needed and becomes an enabler whose value is demonstrated by how rarely they are called directly. That shift in identity, accountability, and measurement is organizational. It does not get deployed in a sprint.

The failure mode is to run the technical change on the technical timeline and expect the organizational change to follow automatically. It does not. The organizational change needs its own plan, its own timeline, and its own set of interventions.

## Why Engineers Promoted to Director Underestimate This

The careers of most engineers and engineering managers are built on solving technical problems. The feedback loop is fast and legible: write code, run tests, observe results. Problems have root causes that can be identified and fixed. Success is measurable and attributable.

Human resistance does not behave this way. It is ambiguous, indirect, and often delayed. An engineer who nods in agreement in a team meeting and then does not change their behavior has not lied to you — they may have genuinely intended to change, or they may have been signaling agreement to avoid conflict while privately holding a different view. The signal and the underlying state are decoupled.

Directors who bring an engineering mindset to change management without adaptation will keep trying to "fix" resistance as if it were a bug. They will interpret silence as agreement. They will underestimate the lag between communication and behavioral change. They will grow frustrated when "we already addressed that concern in the FAQ" does not result in adoption.

The mental shift required is from debugging to listening. Resistance is information. The director's job is to create enough space and safety for that information to surface, and then to engage with it directly rather than route around it.

## What a Director Who "Gets It" Looks Like

They talk to the skeptics first, not last. They find out what people are actually worried about before they announce anything in a group setting. They identify the informal leaders — the senior engineers whose opinion shapes the team's view — and invest in those relationships before the change is announced.

They communicate in layers: vision to the organization, context to the managers, specifics to the ICs. They repeat the message more than feels necessary, because they know the first time most people hear something important, they are still processing whether it affects them.

They treat early resistance as useful feedback, not as insubordination. When someone raises a concern they had not considered, they say so. They update their approach when the feedback warrants it, and they explain why when it does not.

They take the long view. They know the first three months of a major organizational change are often characterized by decreased productivity and increased friction — and they communicate that expectation to their leadership so it is not misread as the change failing.

Most importantly, they stay in the change. They do not announce it and disappear. They are visible, present, and engaged throughout the adoption cycle — which is how they know early enough to correct course when something is not working.
