# Chapter 3: Stakeholder Mapping — Who Holds the Keys

---

## Why Stakeholder Mapping Is Not Optional

Most engineering leaders treat stakeholder management as a communication task. They identify the people who need to be informed, send them updates, and consider the work done. This is a category error. Stakeholder management is not a communication task — it is an influence task. The question is not "who needs to know about this change?" The question is "whose behavior, decision-making, or resource control has the power to accelerate or kill this change, and what do I need to do to move them in the right direction?"

In this framing, stakeholder mapping becomes one of the most consequential planning activities a Director performs. Getting it wrong means you will invest time and attention in people who do not actually hold the keys to your change succeeding, while leaving unaddressed the actual blockers who will quietly route around or actively undermine your initiative.

The most common version of this mistake is directional: leaders map their direct chain. They identify who they report to and who reports to them, and build their stakeholder strategy around that chain. In reality, the people who most frequently determine whether a Director-level change initiative succeeds are outside the direct chain: peer directors with competing priorities, hidden influencers who control budget or approval gates, and the informal opinion leaders at the IC and manager level whose credibility shapes team sentiment far more than organizational title does.

---

## The Four-Quadrant Model

Stakeholder mapping uses two axes: **Organizational Influence** (how much power this person has to affect the outcome of your initiative) and **Current Support** (whether they are currently working with or against the change). This produces four quadrants, each requiring a distinct engagement strategy.

```
                   HIGH INFLUENCE
                         |
         CHAMPIONS       |      BLOCKERS
         (High Inf,      |      (High Inf,
          High Support)  |       Low Support)
                         |
   ──────────────────────┼──────────────────────
                         |
         LATENT ALLIES   |      SKEPTICS
         (Low Inf,       |      (Low Inf,
          High Support)  |       Low Support)
                         |
                    LOW INFLUENCE
```

### Champions (High Influence, High Support)

These are the people who are already aligned with where you are going and have the organizational reach to do something about it. They may be on the guiding coalition. They may be senior ICs whose technical judgment the organization trusts. They may be peer directors or VPs who have a stake in the same outcomes.

Your engagement strategy with Champions is: **activate and protect them.** Activate means give them specific things to do. A champion who is passive is wasted — they need to know that you are counting on them to make the case in rooms you are not in, to reinforce the message in their own team's conversations, to lend their credibility to the coalition visibly. Protect means do not expose them to attacks they are not prepared for. If you know a blocker is going to challenge the technical assumptions of your proposal in an executive review, brief your champions beforehand so they are not caught flat-footed.

A common failure with Champions is taking them for granted. They are on your side — but they have their own priorities and their own organizational pressures. If you stop investing in the relationship, they may not actively work against you, but they will stop actively working for you, which in a contested change environment is equivalent.

### Latent Allies (Low Influence, High Support)

These are people who agree with your direction but are not in a position to move it significantly on their own. Individual contributors who understand why the change matters. Managers a level below the decision makers. Subject matter experts who have been advocating for this direction informally for years.

Your engagement strategy: **inform and convert.** Inform means keep them current and specific — they are motivated and want to know what they can do. Convert means help them develop the vocabulary and the evidence to advocate upward. A senior IC who can articulate the urgency case in terms their VP understands is an amplification of your influence. You cannot be in every conversation; they can carry the message into spaces where you have no access.

This quadrant is particularly powerful in engineering orgs because technical credibility flows horizontally, not vertically. An architect who is known as the smartest person in the room on a particular problem set has influence that a Director two levels above them cannot replicate. Latent Allies in senior IC roles are often the most underutilized asset in a change initiative.

### Skeptics (Low Influence, Low Support)

These people are not currently on board, but they also do not have the organizational reach to kill the initiative on their own. They represent friction, not force. Left unaddressed, their skepticism can grow into louder resistance, especially if they are influencing peers or if their visible non-adoption becomes a signal that others can follow safely.

Your engagement strategy: **listen, acknowledge, and address concerns with specificity.** The mistake here is dismissal — assuming that because this person cannot derail the initiative, their concerns do not matter. In practice, understanding what the skeptics believe is wrong with the change is often where the most useful diagnostic information lives. They have thought about why it will not work. Some of those reasons are wrong. Some of them are correct and pointing at real risks you have not fully addressed.

Engaging skeptics genuinely — with real curiosity, not with a sales agenda — accomplishes two things. It surfaces risk early, when you can still address it. And it converts a percentage of skeptics into latent allies, because people who feel genuinely heard are more willing to consider updating their position.

### Blockers (High Influence, Low Support)

These are the people who have the organizational reach to materially slow or stop your initiative, and who are currently working against it. They are the highest-stakes quadrant. Getting your engagement strategy wrong here is one of the most common reasons change initiatives stall at the Director level.

The first step is understanding what the real objection is — not the stated one. Blockers rarely say "I do not want this change because it reduces my team's autonomy." They say "I am concerned about the technical readiness of the platform." Or "I think the timeline is too aggressive." Or "I am not sure we have the right headcount to support this model." These stated objections are often real, but they are also often proxies for a deeper concern that the blocker does not feel safe raising directly.

Your engagement strategy: **seek a 1:1 conversation before any group setting where this person might object publicly.** In a private conversation, a blocker has more room to be honest. You have more room to listen without defensiveness. The conversation may reveal a concern you can genuinely address, or it may reveal an objection that is fundamentally about power or territory — in which case you have different options available: escalate to a shared authority, route the decision around the objection, or negotiate a trade that makes the blocker's concern secondary to a benefit they care about more.

What you almost never do with a blocker is surprise them in a public forum. A blocker who feels ambushed in an executive meeting will escalate and entrench. A blocker who has been heard in private may still disagree, but they are less likely to weaponize the disagreement.

## The Hidden Stakeholders in Engineering Changes

Every major engineering transformation has a set of stakeholders who rarely attend the planning meetings but whose authority can stop the initiative without warning. Directors who learn this lesson late are the ones who get to the 80% mark on a change initiative and then discover that compliance, legal, security, or finance has a problem with it that requires six months to address.

**Compliance and Legal.** On-call restructuring may create labor law implications in certain jurisdictions. A platform consolidation that changes data residency patterns may trigger regulatory review. Security policy changes embedded in a platform migration may require legal sign-off before they can be made permanent. These stakeholders do not join engineering planning sessions — you have to go to them, and you have to go early.

**Security.** The CISO's office is a frequent hidden stakeholder in SRE and platform engineering changes. Centralized observability platforms aggregate logs that include sensitive customer data. Self-service deployment pipelines change the access control landscape. Error budget policies may involve decisions about acceptable risk that security teams need to be part of. Treating security as a late-stage reviewer rather than an early coalition member is one of the most common sources of expensive change failures in engineering orgs.

**Finance.** Headcount changes, tooling investments, and the indirect cost of reduced delivery velocity during a transition all have financial implications. Finance stakeholders who are surprised by these costs at budget review time become active obstacles. Finance stakeholders who were briefed early and whose concerns were incorporated into the plan become neutral to positive.

## On-Call Rotation as a Stakeholder Mapping Example

When a Director introduces on-call rotation to an engineering team that has never had one, the stakeholder map is more complex than it appears. The immediate stakeholders are obvious: the engineers who will be on-call, their managers, the Director themselves. But the full map typically includes:

- Product managers and product owners, who will be concerned about how on-call commitments affect feature delivery
- HR, who may need to update compensation structures or working agreements if on-call carries a stipend
- The legal team, who may need to review on-call policies against labor agreements
- Peer directors whose teams interact with this team's services and who will be affected by the new escalation model
- Senior ICs on the team who are informal opinion leaders and whose visible attitude toward the change will shape the team's response more than any announcement will

Building this map before the initiative is announced is what separates a Director who is navigating organizational reality from one who is surprised by it.

## Stakeholder Maps Evolve

A stakeholder map is a living document, not a one-time artifact. People move between quadrants as the change initiative progresses and as organizational dynamics shift. A Champion who was actively engaged in Q1 may have shifted priorities by Q3 and become a passive bystander. A Skeptic who was raising objections in month two may have been converted to a Latent Ally by month five after seeing early wins. A Blocker who seemed intractable may have found their real concern addressed by a policy change you made in response to their feedback.

Directors who update their stakeholder map quarterly — and more frequently during critical phases — are operating with current intelligence. Directors who drafted their map at the start of the initiative and never revisited it are navigating with an outdated map, and they will consistently be surprised by resistance that has been building while they were not watching.

The practice does not need to be formal. A periodic 30-minute review of the key names, their current posture, and what conversations are needed is sufficient. The point is deliberate attention, not bureaucratic process.
