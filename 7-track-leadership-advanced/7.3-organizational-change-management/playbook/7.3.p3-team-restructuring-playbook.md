# Team Restructuring Playbook — From Combined Dev+Ops to Embedded SRE Model

---

## When This Applies

Use this playbook when you are:
- Splitting a combined Dev+Ops team into separate development and SRE functions
- Moving from a central ops team to SREs embedded in product teams
- Creating a platform team from scratch out of engineers currently distributed across the organization
- Promoting a subset of your existing ops team into SRE roles while keeping others in an operational/NOC function

**This playbook does NOT apply to**:
- Headcount reductions (layoffs) — those require HR-led processes with legal review at every step
- Merging two independent teams under a new manager
- Org-wide reorgs affecting more than 3 levels of leadership

**Assumptions**:
- You have executive sponsorship and HR partnership confirmed before starting
- The restructuring has a clear "target state" that can be communicated concretely
- You have at least 30 days before announcement (less than this means you are already behind)

---

## Pre-Announcement (2–4 Weeks Before)

This phase is entirely confidential. Nothing leaks to the broader team until Announcement Day.

### Build the Stakeholder Map

Identify everyone who needs to know before the announcement — and everyone who cannot know until the announcement.

**Must know before announcement**:
- Your manager and their manager (already approved the restructuring)
- HR business partner (required for any role changes)
- Legal (required if any role reclassifications affect compensation, titles, or employment terms)
- Finance (if headcount costs shift between cost centers)
- Peer managers whose teams will be affected (e.g., product managers if embedded SREs move to product teams)

**Should not know before announcement**:
- The broader team
- Other engineering managers not directly affected
- External stakeholders

**Why this matters**: Information asymmetry in pre-announcement phase is unavoidable but must be managed. If team members learn about the restructuring from hallway conversations instead of you, trust is permanently damaged.

### 1:1s with Key Leaders

Before announcement, have a 1:1 with each key leader stakeholder. The purpose of each conversation:

**With your manager**: Confirm the restructuring rationale is aligned, confirm the announcement date, confirm what you can and cannot say (especially on topics like compensation changes).

**With HR business partner**: Walk through the role changes one by one. For each affected individual: what is their current title/grade? What is the new role? Is this a lateral move, a promotion, or a demotion? HR will flag anything that requires additional process.

**With peer managers (e.g., product team EMs)**: If embedded SREs are moving into their orgs, they need to understand the operating model. Spend 30 minutes with each: how does an embedded SRE work? Who do they report to? Who sets their priorities? What are the escalation paths?

### HR Alignment and Legal Review

**HR must confirm before announcement**:
- Role change letters or addenda for any individual whose title, grade, or reporting line changes
- Whether affected individuals need to sign anything
- Whether there is a severance or involuntary separation component (if so, legal must be involved immediately)
- State/country-specific notification requirements (California, for example, has specific notice requirements)

**Legal review is required if**:
- Any role changes reduce compensation or benefits
- Any individuals are being moved to a new employment classification
- There is a risk of constructive dismissal claims (an individual's role changes so significantly it's effectively a new job they didn't agree to)

Do not skip legal review assuming it won't be needed. The cost of a legal review is small; the cost of a wrongful termination claim is not.

### Draft All Communications

Write every communication artifact before Announcement Day. Do not improvise these on the day.

**Artifacts to draft in advance**:
1. All-hands script (what you will say, word for word, to the full team)
2. 1:1 script for each directly affected individual (customized per person)
3. Manager talking points (for any managers under you who need to cascade the message)
4. FAQ document (anticipate every question the team will ask — write an answer for each)
5. Written announcement (email or Confluence page posted after the verbal announcement)

**Review cadence**: Draft → HR review → Legal review (if applicable) → Manager review → Final. Do not skip any review step.

## Announcement Day

Announcement Day has one goal: every affected person hears about their specific situation from you or their direct manager, before the broader team hears the general announcement.

### Sequencing

**The sequence must be**:
1. Individuals who are most significantly affected (role change, new reporting line) hear from you or their manager first — ideally 30–60 minutes before the all-hands
2. The broader team hears the general announcement in the all-hands
3. The written announcement goes out immediately after the all-hands

**Do not do it in reverse order.** If someone hears "the team is restructuring" in the all-hands before they know what it means for them specifically, they spend the rest of the meeting in panic. That is a leadership failure.

### All-Hands Script Structure

**What to include**:
1. Context: Why we are doing this, what problem it solves (2–3 minutes)
2. What is changing: The new structure, clearly stated (2–3 minutes)
3. What is not changing: Be explicit — reporting line for X stays the same, team mission stays the same, etc. (1–2 minutes)
4. What happens next: Timeline for the transition period (1 minute)
5. Q&A: Leave 15 minutes — do not rush this

**What not to do in the all-hands**:
- Do not read from a script (it signals you are not confident in the decision)
- Do not say "I know this is hard" in the opening line (say it when it's true, in context, not as a preamble)
- Do not promise specifics you cannot deliver ("everyone's jobs are safe" — do not say this if it's not fully true)
- Do not take questions you cannot answer in the room — say "I'll follow up on that directly" and actually follow up

### 1:1 Script for Directly Affected Individuals

Customize this for each person. The structure:

**Opening (2 min)**:
> "I want to talk with you today about some changes to how the team is structured. I wanted to make sure you heard about what this means for you specifically before the broader team hears about it."

**What is changing for this person (5 min)**:
Be specific and direct. Do not bury the headline.
> "Your role is changing from [current title/team] to [new title/team]. You'll be reporting to [new manager]. Your day-to-day work will shift from [current work] to [new work]."

**Why (2 min)**:
> "The reason for this is [specific rationale — not a generic org statement, but why THIS PERSON'S role is changing in this specific way]."

**What stays the same (1 min)**:
> "Your compensation is not changing. [Anything else that is stable.]"

**Their questions (remainder)**:
Stop talking and listen. The most important part of this conversation is what they say, not what you say.

**Close**:
> "I'll follow up with a written summary of what we discussed today. I want to make sure you have time to sit with this and come back to me with any questions."

### FAQ Document

Write answers to every question you expect. Distribute this to the full team the same day as the announcement. Common questions to pre-answer:

- Why is this happening now?
- What does this mean for my role specifically?
- Who do I report to now?
- Does this affect my compensation?
- What happens to [specific team / function / project]?
- How were decisions made about who goes where?
- What if I disagree with the new structure?
- What is the timeline for full transition?
- Who do I go to if I have concerns I don't want to raise with my manager?

That last question is important: name an escalation path (HR business partner, skip-level manager, or an ombudsperson if your organization has one). It signals that you expect people to have concerns and you have thought about how to address them.

## Transition Period (Days 1–60)

The transition period is the highest-risk window. New structure is announced but not yet operational. Old habits persist. Ambiguity creates anxiety.

### Old Structure and New Structure Running in Parallel

For the first 30 days, both structures effectively exist. Teams are adapting to new reporting lines and new responsibilities while ongoing work continues.

**What this means in practice**:
- Maintain the old meeting cadences until the new ones are explicitly stood up
- For decisions that span the old and new structure, be explicit about who owns the decision
- Do not assume teams know how to operate in the new structure — tell them

**Common failure at this stage**: The manager announces the new structure, then goes back to running operations as before. The team is confused about whether the restructuring is real or just a reorg on paper.

### Knowledge Transfer Checkpoints

Identify what knowledge needs to move between individuals or teams as part of the restructuring.

**For each knowledge transfer item, define**:
- What is being transferred (system, process, customer relationship, tribal knowledge)
- Who is the knowledge holder (source)
- Who is the knowledge receiver (destination)
- How transfer happens (shadow sessions, documentation, pair work)
- Completion checkpoint (how do you know the transfer is done?)

**Knowledge transfer risks**:
- Key people resist transferring knowledge because it represents their value/leverage
- Knowledge gets documented but the documentation is incomplete or inaccurate
- The knowledge receiver doesn't have enough context to use what's been transferred

**Mitigation**: Have the knowledge receiver demonstrate back the transferred knowledge (not just acknowledge receipt). If they can't do it, the transfer isn't done.

### Role Clarity Sessions

Within the first 2 weeks, run a role clarity session for each newly formed team.

**Format (90 min)**:
- 30 min: Each person states what they think their role is, what they own, and what they expect from peers
- 30 min: Identify gaps (things nobody thinks they own) and overlaps (things multiple people think they own)
- 30 min: Resolve gaps and overlaps — assign clear ownership; document

This session is more important than the org chart. An org chart tells you who reports to whom. A role clarity session tells you who does what.

## Stabilization (Days 60–90)

**Goal**: New structure is operating independently. The restructuring is not complete until a team can operate without the manager facilitating every interaction.

### New Structure Operating Independently

**Signals that the new structure is stable**:
- Teams are scheduling their own rituals (standups, retros, reviews) without manager initiation
- Individuals in new roles are making decisions within their scope without asking the manager for approval
- Cross-team collaboration is happening at the working level (not escalated to managers)
- New reporting-line managers are running effective 1:1s with their new direct reports

**Signals that stabilization has not occurred** (and you need to intervene):
- Individuals still informally operating by the old reporting line
- Managers receiving questions that should be answered by the new structure
- Team members still describing themselves by their old team affiliation
- High volume of "who owns this?" questions in Slack or meetings

### Retrospective on the Transition

At Day 60, run a structured retrospective with the affected teams.

**Format** (one session per newly formed team, 60 min):
- What went well in the transition?
- What was harder than expected?
- What do we still need to resolve?
- What would we do differently if we had to do this again?

Capture the output. Act on the "still need to resolve" items within 2 weeks. Do not let them linger.

### Anchor in Hiring Profiles and Org Charts

The restructuring is permanent when it's reflected in:
- Published org charts (update in Workday, Greenhouse, or your HRIS within 30 days)
- Job descriptions for any open roles (new roles should reflect the new structure)
- Interview rubrics (what are you looking for in someone joining this new team?)
- Onboarding plans for new hires (a new hire should join into the new structure as normal, not as an exception)

If a new hire joins and has to be "explained the restructuring" as context, the anchoring is incomplete.

## The Human Elements

### Managing Individuals Whose Roles Change Significantly

A role change — even a lateral one, even a positive one — is a disruption. The individual is losing something: familiarity, established relationships, a known domain, a sense of mastery.

**What effective managers do**:
- Acknowledge the loss explicitly: "I know this is a significant change, and it's okay if it takes time to settle in."
- Increase check-in frequency during the first 60 days (weekly 1:1 minimum, more if needed)
- Set near-term wins early: find something the individual can succeed at in the new role within the first 30 days
- Connect them to peers in the new structure who will be allies, not just colleagues

**What to avoid**:
- Minimizing the disruption ("it's just a title change")
- Assuming silence means acceptance
- Reducing 1:1 frequency because "they seem fine"

### Supporting Team Members Through Uncertainty

Even team members whose roles do not change are affected by a restructuring. They lose teammates. They gain new teammates. They don't know yet how the new structure will affect their day-to-day.

**What creates stability during uncertainty**:
- Consistency in team rituals (don't restructure the stand-up on the same week as the org change)
- Transparency about the timeline and decision-making process
- A clear statement of what is not changing (mission, team identity, compensation)
- Visible, confident leadership — if you seem uncertain, the team will amplify that uncertainty

**What to avoid**:
- Over-communicating to fill silence with noise
- Making reassurances you can't keep
- Retreating from the team — now is when presence matters most

### Handling the High-Performer Who Doesn't Like the New Structure

In every restructuring, there is at least one high-performer who is unhappy with the change. They are valuable. They are vocal. They are worth retaining. They are also not a veto.

**The conversation to have (directly, early)**:
> "I value you and I want to understand your concern. Can you tell me specifically what you're worried about?"

Listen carefully. There are two types of objections:
- **Substantive concerns**: The new structure creates a real problem (e.g., they'll lose visibility into a system they need to do their job). These deserve a real response and, where possible, a fix.
- **Preference-based objections**: They liked the old way. It worked for them. They don't want to change. These deserve acknowledgment but not reversal.

**If they are thinking of leaving**:
Be direct: "I want to understand if you're considering leaving because of this change. Can we talk about that?" You cannot solve a problem you're not allowed to see.

**What not to do**:
- Do not restructure the structure to accommodate one high-performer's preference
- Do not make exceptions that signal the new structure is negotiable
- Do not ignore the concern — unaddressed concerns from high-performers become loud external signals to the rest of the team
