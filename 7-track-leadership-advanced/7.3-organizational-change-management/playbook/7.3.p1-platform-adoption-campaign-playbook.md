# Internal Developer Platform Adoption Campaign Playbook

---

## Context and Purpose

An internal developer platform (IDP) only delivers value when teams actually use it. This playbook drives adoption from zero to majority adoption in a structured campaign — not through mandates, but through demonstrated value, champions, and well-timed forcing functions where necessary.

**When to use this playbook**: Your platform team has shipped a new IDP (or significantly upgraded one), and you need to migrate existing teams onto it while winning new workloads going forward.

**Assumptions**:
- The platform is production-ready for at least a beta cohort (not alpha — you cannot do a campaign on a broken product)
- You have at least one dedicated platform engineer who can provide white-glove support during beta
- You have a defined success metric for adoption (e.g., "80% of new services deployed via the platform by end of Q4")
- There is organizational support — someone at VP+ level who can backstop forcing functions if needed

---

## Pre-Campaign (2–4 Weeks Before Launch)

### Confirm Platform Readiness

Before you recruit beta teams, do an honest readiness audit. If the platform fails during beta, you lose credibility that is very hard to recover.

**Platform readiness checklist**:
- [ ] Core deployment path works end-to-end without platform engineer hand-holding
- [ ] Basic documentation exists (getting started guide, FAQ, known limitations)
- [ ] An escalation path is defined: who does a beta team contact when something breaks?
- [ ] At least one "happy path" success story is documented (even from internal testing)
- [ ] The platform does not require beta teams to change their application code (path-of-least-resistance principle)

**If the platform is not ready**: Do not start the campaign. Run a closed alpha for 2 more weeks. A failed beta is worse than a delayed campaign.

### Define Success Metrics

Write these down before the campaign starts. Do not define success after you see the results.

| Metric | What It Measures | Target |
|--------|-----------------|--------|
| Time to first deployment | Platform usability | < [X] hours from account provisioning |
| Beta team retention | Is the platform sticky? | All beta teams still using it at end of beta |
| Ticket deflection rate | Platform reducing support burden | [X]% of deployment questions answered by docs |
| Developer satisfaction score | NPS or CSAT survey | > [X] on a 5-point scale |
| DORA improvements | Platform delivering on its value promise | Deployment frequency ↑, change failure rate ↓ |

### Identify 3–5 Beta Teams

**Selection criteria** (pick teams that match at least 3 of 5):
- Willing — they expressed interest or are known early adopters
- Representative — they use tech that is similar to the broader population of teams
- Vocal — their engineers talk to peers; their opinion travels
- Active — they are currently deploying frequently (so you get data quickly)
- Not in a critical period — they don't have a major release in the next 6 weeks

**How to recruit beta teams**:
Send a direct message to the engineering manager — not a team-wide broadcast:
> "We're launching a private beta for [Platform Name] with 3–5 teams before general rollout. Your team came to mind because [specific reason]. The commitment is 4–6 weeks of using the platform for [specific use case], with weekly 30-minute feedback sessions and white-glove support from our team. Interested in hearing more?"

**Do not offer the beta to teams that are skeptical or hostile.** Recruit willing teams first. Skeptical teams can be targeted in the broad rollout phase.

## Beta Phase (Weeks 1–6)

### Week 1 — Onboarding with White-Glove Support

**Day 1 for each beta team**:
- 60-minute onboarding session: platform overview, live demo, answer questions
- Provision accounts and access before the session (do not make them wait)
- Assign a named platform engineer as the team's point of contact for the beta
- Share the escalation path: how to reach the platform team when something breaks (Slack channel, not tickets)

**White-glove standard**: During beta, no beta team should wait more than 2 hours for a response to a blocking issue during business hours. This is a campaign — you are selling the platform as much as you are testing it.

### Weeks 1–6 — Weekly Feedback Sessions (30 min per team)

**Format**:
- 10 min: What worked well this week? (document verbatim — these become testimonials)
- 10 min: What was painful, confusing, or missing? (document verbatim — these become your backlog)
- 10 min: What would make you recommend this to a peer team?

**What to track in every session**:
- Time-to-first-deployment (ask each team: how long did it take?)
- Number of times they had to contact the platform team for help
- Any workarounds they invented (workarounds signal friction in the platform)

**Cadence note**: Weekly is the minimum. For the first 2 weeks, check in daily via Slack.

### Track Time-to-First-Deployment

This is your single most important beta metric. It tells you:
- Whether the getting-started experience is adequate
- Whether the platform is intuitive enough to not require hand-holding
- Whether your documentation is accurate

**How to measure**: Ask each team to note the exact time they started their first deployment attempt and the exact time their first successful deployment completed. Track it in a shared doc.

**Target**: If time-to-first-deployment is more than [4 hours] without platform engineer assistance, the platform has a usability problem. Do not proceed to broad rollout until this is resolved.

### Document Wins and Friction Points

Maintain a live document during beta with two sections:

**Wins** (exact quotes when possible):
> "We deployed in 45 minutes. Our previous process took a half day." — [Team Name], [Engineer Name]

**Friction points** (categorized by type):
- Documentation gaps: things that weren't explained
- UI/UX issues: things that were confusing to navigate
- Feature gaps: things the platform can't do that teams need
- Performance issues: things that were slow or unreliable

Use this document to prioritize platform fixes before broad rollout. Fix the top 3 friction points before you launch the broad campaign.

## Champions Program

### Identify Champions

By Week 4 of beta, you will know which engineers are energized about the platform. They are the ones who:
- Ask questions that go beyond the immediate task ("Can we use this for X too?")
- Voluntarily mention it to other teams before you ask them to
- Help their teammates troubleshoot without involving the platform team

**Target**: 1–2 champions per beta team (6–10 champions total from 5 teams).

### Charter the Champions Community

**What the champions community is**:
- A named, recognized group (give it a name — "Platform Pioneers" or similar)
- A dedicated Slack channel for early access to new features and direct line to platform team
- A monthly 45-minute sync with the platform team lead

**What champions get**:
- Early access to new features and roadmap discussions
- Recognition: named in the broad rollout communications as "platform champions"
- A direct escalation path for their team — their issues go to the front of the queue

**What champions commit to**:
- Being willing to be named publicly as platform users
- Running one demo or lunch-and-learn for a non-beta team
- Providing candid feedback on new features before they ship

### Arm Champions with Talking Points and Demo Scripts

Write a one-page "talking points" document for champions:

**What to say when someone asks "is this worth switching?"**:
- "[Platform] reduced our first deployment from X hours to Y minutes"
- "We stopped worrying about [specific pain point] because the platform handles it"
- "The team asks fewer 'how do I deploy this' questions — that time goes to actual work now"

**Demo script (15-minute version)**:
1. Open the platform UI — show the getting-started screen
2. Walk through the deployment flow for a sample service (use a real service, not a toy example)
3. Show the deployment history and rollback capability
4. Show one platform feature that was previously a manual/tribal-knowledge step
5. Q&A

**Where champions use this**: Lunch-and-learns, 1:1s with skeptical peers, their own team demos.

## Broad Rollout

### Communication Campaign

**All-hands demo (30 minutes)**:
- Platform team lead presents: 5 min context, 10 min live demo, 10 min champion testimonial, 5 min Q&A
- One champion presents alongside the platform team — peer credibility is more persuasive than platform team credibility
- End with a clear call to action: "If your team wants to onboard in the next quarter, here's how"

**Confluence documentation**:
- Getting started guide (30-minute happy path, verified by a non-platform-team engineer)
- FAQ (built from beta friction points)
- Migration guide for teams on the legacy path
- SLA/support model: what the platform team commits to and what it doesn't

**Slack channel** (`#platform-adoption` or similar):
- Announcement of broad rollout
- Weekly tip from the platform team
- Champions answer peer questions (this reduces platform team support burden)
- Success story posts: when a team ships their first deployment, post about it

### Graduation Criteria

Define what "adopted" means. A team has graduated to the platform when:
- [ ] At least one production service is deployed via the platform
- [ ] The team's deployment process is documented in the platform (not legacy tooling)
- [ ] At least one engineer on the team can onboard a new service without platform team help
- [ ] The team is not using the legacy deployment path for any new services

Graduation is a moment worth recognizing: post in the all-hands Slack channel when a team graduates.

### Support Model Transition

**During beta**: White-glove — named platform engineer per team, 2-hour SLA

**During broad rollout (first 60 days)**: Office hours model — platform team holds 3x weekly open Slack office hours (30 min each), Confluence docs as first-line support

**Post-rollout (steady state)**: Self-serve with community support — Slack channel, champions network, documentation; platform team handles only bugs and feature requests

This transition must be communicated explicitly. Do not let teams assume white-glove support continues indefinitely.

## Measuring Adoption

### Platform Utilization Metrics

Track monthly:
- Number of teams with at least one production deployment via platform
- Number of total production services on the platform vs. legacy path
- Deployments per week via platform (total volume trend)
- New services: what % started on the platform vs. legacy

### Ticket Deflection Rate

- Compare support tickets before platform (last 3 months on legacy) vs. after platform
- Target: platform-related support tickets should be decreasing as documentation matures
- Track: tickets that were answered by the docs vs. required platform engineer intervention

### DORA Improvements

Compare pre-platform vs. post-platform for graduated teams:
- Deployment frequency (are teams deploying more often?)
- Change failure rate (are platform deployments more reliable?)
- MTTR (does the platform's rollback capability reduce restore time?)

### Team Satisfaction Survey

Run at 30 days and 90 days post-adoption (keep it short — 5 questions max):
1. How would you rate the platform onboarding experience? (1–5)
2. How much time does your team save per deployment vs. the old process? (None / < 30 min / 30–120 min / > 2 hours)
3. Would you recommend this platform to a peer team? (Yes / No / Maybe)
4. What is the one thing you'd change about the platform?
5. What is the one thing you'd tell a team that hasn't adopted yet?

## Handling Resistant Teams

### Step 1 — 1:1 Discovery Call

Before escalating, understand the resistance. Schedule a 30-minute call with the engineering manager.

**Questions to ask**:
- "What would need to be true for you to consider adopting the platform this quarter?"
- "What's your biggest concern about the migration?"
- "Is there something about how your team deploys today that you're worried the platform doesn't support?"

**Categories of resistance and responses**:

| Resistance Type | Root Cause | Response |
|----------------|-----------|---------|
| "We're too busy" | Capacity constraint | Negotiate a migration date; offer dedicated migration sprint support |
| "The platform doesn't support X" | Genuine feature gap | Log it as a P1 roadmap item; offer workaround in the interim |
| "Our last attempt at standardization failed" | Loss of trust | Share what's different this time; offer beta-phase treatment (white-glove) |
| "We don't see the value" | Value proposition not landing | Share metrics from graduated teams; arrange a champion demo |

### Step 2 — Custom Migration Assistance

Offer resistant teams dedicated migration assistance:
- One platform engineer embedded in their team for a sprint
- Migration plan co-written with the team (they don't just receive the plan — they own it)
- A specific date by which the migration will be complete, with the team's buy-in

This removes the "we don't have capacity" objection. If the team still resists after this offer, escalate.

### Step 3 — Executive Sponsor Conversation

If a team's engineering manager is resistant and a discovery call and migration offer haven't moved them, bring in the executive sponsor.

**What the executive sponsor says** (not the platform team):
> "The platform is a strategic investment for [organization]. I need your team on it by [date]. What do you need from me to make that happen?"

This is not a threat — it is a sponsor clarifying organizational priority. The question at the end is genuine: sponsor should follow through on offers of help.

## Forcing Function Triggers

Voluntary adoption works for most teams. For the remainder, there are legitimate forcing functions — use them deliberately and transparently, not as punishment.

### What Can Be Mandated (Security/Compliance Controls)

- All new services must use the platform's secrets management (security mandate — no exceptions)
- All production deployments must go through the platform's audit log (compliance requirement)
- Legacy deployment path access will be removed on [date] for services that are not in active migration

**How to communicate a mandate**: Give at least 60 days notice. Provide migration support. Do not surprise teams.

### What Should Remain Pull-Based

- Which specific platform features a team uses (beyond the minimum)
- The timeline for migrating existing (non-new) services, within a reasonable window
- How teams structure their pipelines on the platform (standardize the interface, not the internals)

### The "Legacy Path Sunset" Lever

The most effective forcing function is a credible sunset date for the legacy deployment path.

**How to use it**:
1. Announce the sunset date 90 days in advance — this is a hard deadline
2. Offer migration support for all remaining teams immediately
3. At 45 days, escalate teams that have not started migration to their VP
4. At 15 days, send a final warning — any team not migrated will have legacy access revoked on [date]
5. On sunset date: disable the legacy path (with a 48-hour emergency exception process for genuine emergencies)

**When to use the sunset lever**: Only after voluntary adoption has plateaued below target for 2+ months. Do not announce a sunset date if you are not prepared to enforce it.
