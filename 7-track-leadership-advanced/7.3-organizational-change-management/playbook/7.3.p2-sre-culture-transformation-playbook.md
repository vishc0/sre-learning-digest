# SRE Culture Transformation Playbook — 6-Month Phased Plan

---

## Context

**When to use this playbook**: Your team is running in a traditional ops/NOC model — reactive firefighting, no SLOs, toil-heavy work, postmortems that assign blame or don't happen at all. Leadership has decided to adopt SRE practices, and you have 6 months to anchor the change before the next planning cycle.

**What this playbook assumes**:
- You have organizational support at the VP or Director level (a sponsor who will remove blockers)
- The team is 8–25 engineers; smaller teams compress the timeline, larger teams need parallel workstreams
- You are not doing a full reorg — this is a cultural and practice transformation of the existing team
- At least 2–3 team members are open to change (you don't need full consensus to start)
- You have access to basic observability tooling (Splunk, Datadog, or equivalent)

**What this playbook does NOT cover**:
- Headcount decisions or role reclassifications (see Team Restructuring Playbook)
- SLO definition methodology (see SRE reference materials)
- Procurement of new tooling

---

## Phase 0 — Foundation (Weeks 1–4)

**Goal**: Create the conditions for change before announcing it. Change that starts with "here's what we're doing" instead of "here's why we need to" fails at this phase.

### Week 1 — Stakeholder Alignment

**Actions**:
- Schedule 1:1s with every key stakeholder: your manager, product leadership, peer engineering managers, finance (if SLO breach has cost implications)
- Deliver a 3-sentence framing for each conversation: (1) where we are, (2) what breaks if we don't change, (3) what success looks like in 6 months
- Get explicit verbal commitment from your executive sponsor to attend at least one SRE review per month
- Document who is a Champion, Neutral, or Skeptic — this is your first stakeholder map

**Deliverable**: Stakeholder engagement matrix (see templates folder)

**Do NOT do this week**: Announce anything to the full team. Premature announcements without a plan undermine trust.

### Week 2 — Baseline Metrics

Establish your "before" state. You cannot show transformation without a before picture.

| Metric | How to Measure | Target Baseline Source |
|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| MTTR (Mean Time to Restore) | Incident tickets, PagerDuty, Splunk | Last 90 days of P1/P2 incidents |
| Toil % | Time study or manager estimate | 30-minute team survey or JIRA analysis |
| Deployment frequency | CI/CD pipeline logs, release tracker | Last 60 days |
| On-call burden | Pages per week per person, incidents per shift | PagerDuty or equivalent |
| Postmortem rate | % of P1s with a written postmortem | Incident log review |

Write these numbers down. Store them somewhere visible. This is your scoreboard for Phase 3.

### Week 3 — Identify Change Champions

**What you're looking for**: Engineers who are already frustrated with the current state, have credibility with peers, and are willing to be publicly associated with the new way.

**How to find them**:
- In 1:1s, ask: "What's the one thing about how we respond to incidents that you'd change tomorrow if you could?"
- Engineers who give a specific, energized answer are your candidates
- Do NOT pick based on seniority alone — a mid-level engineer with peer trust is more valuable than a principal who keeps to themselves

**What to ask of champions**:
- Be willing to run the first blameless postmortem with you
- Give candid feedback on the rollout plan
- Not be required to evangelize publicly yet — that comes in Phase 1

**Target**: 2–3 champions. More than 3 is fine; fewer than 2 is a risk.

### Week 4 — First Blameless Postmortem

Pick a real incident from the last 30–60 days that the team remembers. Run a blameless postmortem on it using the template format, but do it as an experiment — not a mandate.

**Script for inviting the team**:
> "I want to try something different with [Incident Name]. Instead of a hot wash or a quick summary, I'd like to walk through it as a learning exercise — what happened, why it happened, and what we'd change about the system. No blame, no judgment. It's a 60-minute experiment. Who wants to be part of it?"

**What makes it blameless**:
- Timeline is built collaboratively, not narrated by one person
- Questions are system-focused: "What made this hard to detect?" not "Why did you miss the alert?"
- Action items are assigned to systems, processes, or runbooks — not to individuals as personal failures

**Success signal**: At least one team member says something like "we should do this for every incident" afterward.

**Failure signal**: Nobody speaks up during the postmortem. If this happens, the psychological safety gap is larger than expected — slow down Phase 1 and focus on 1:1 trust-building first.

### Phase 0 Success Metrics

- [ ] Executive sponsor confirmed and committed to monthly SRE review attendance
- [ ] Stakeholder map complete with engagement actions defined
- [ ] Baseline metrics documented for MTTR, toil %, deployment frequency
- [ ] 2–3 change champions identified and briefed
- [ ] First blameless postmortem completed

## Phase 1 — Visibility (Weeks 5–10)

**Goal**: Make reliability visible. The team cannot improve what they cannot see. This phase is about building the instrumentation and cadence that makes SRE work tangible.

### Weeks 5–6 — SLI/SLO Definition Workshop

Run a 2-session workshop with the team to define SLIs and SLOs for your top 3 services.

**Session 1 (90 min) — Service selection and SLI identification**:
- Which 3 services matter most to users/customers?
- For each: what does "good" look like from the user's perspective? (Availability? Latency? Correctness?)
- Define SLIs: specific, measurable, user-centric signals (e.g., "% of API calls returning 2xx within 300ms")

**Session 2 (60 min) — SLO target-setting**:
- Look at the last 60 days of actual performance
- Set SLO targets that are achievable but meaningful (do not start with 99.99%)
- Calculate the error budget implied by the SLO
- Document: SLI definition, SLO target, measurement method, owner

**Who attends**: Change champions + 2–3 additional engineers + optionally, a product manager from one of the services

**Output**: SLO document for 3 services (even if rough — getting it written is more important than getting it perfect)

### Week 7 — Error Budget Tracking Setup

Stand up the measurement before you need to cite the numbers in a review.

**Minimum viable error budget tracking**:
- A dashboard (Splunk, Datadog, or spreadsheet if tooling isn't ready) showing current SLO compliance vs. target for each of the 3 services
- A weekly snapshot shared with the team via Slack or email — even if only the manager shares it at first

**What to watch**: The team's reaction to seeing their error budget. If they're indifferent, you haven't yet connected SLOs to consequences. If they're defensive, connect it to system learning, not blame.

### Weeks 8–10 — Weekly SRE Review Cadence

Establish a recurring 30-minute SRE review. This is your most important habit-building step.

**Meeting structure**:
- 10 min: Error budget status for each service (is budget being consumed? at what rate?)
- 10 min: Top toil item from the week (what was the most painful manual work?)
- 10 min: One action from last postmortem — is it done? blocked? does it need resources?

**Who attends**: The team + product counterpart (invite them — their presence signals that reliability is a shared concern)

**Facilitation note**: For the first 3 sessions, you facilitate. In Week 10, hand facilitation to a champion.

### Phase 1 Success Metrics

- [ ] SLI/SLO defined and documented for 3 services
- [ ] Error budget dashboard live (even if rough)
- [ ] Weekly SRE review running for 3 consecutive weeks
- [ ] Product counterpart attending at least 1 SRE review

## Phase 2 — Cultural Shift (Weeks 11–18)

**Goal**: Move from "SRE practices as initiative" to "SRE practices as how we work." This is the hardest phase — habits are forming but not yet anchored.

### Weeks 11–13 — On-Call Rotation with Runbooks

**Introduce structured on-call** — not just who has the pager, but what they do with it.

**Steps**:
1. Audit current on-call coverage: who is being paged, for what, with what escalation path
2. For the top 5 alert types, write runbooks (champion-led, 2-hour writing session each)
3. Define what a good on-call handoff looks like (a brief written summary of active issues, recent changes, anything to watch)
4. Rotate the facilitator for postmortems — champions take turns, not just the manager

**Runbook minimum standard**: A runbook is good enough when a mid-level engineer who wasn't on-call for the incident can follow it without calling someone.

### Week 14 — First Error Budget Review with Product

Run a formal error budget review with the product team for one service.

**Format (60 min)**:
- 15 min: SLO performance over the last 4 weeks — is the budget healthy?
- 15 min: What consumed the most budget? (incident analysis)
- 15 min: Decision — if budget is healthy: can we increase deployment velocity? If budget is exhausted: do we pause feature work to focus on reliability?
- 15 min: Action items with owners

**Why this matters**: This is the moment SRE stops being an ops thing and becomes a product-engineering shared responsibility. Product seeing the error budget data — and being part of the decision about what to do with it — is the cultural turning point.

**What success looks like**: Product manager says "so if we ship Feature X this sprint, it might cost us budget we don't have" — they are reasoning in the model.

### Weeks 15–18 — Blameless Postmortem as Standard Practice

By Week 15, every P1 and P2 incident should have a blameless postmortem. Not optional, not just when the manager asks.

**What needs to be in place**:
- Postmortem template accessible to all engineers (in Confluence, Notion, or shared drive)
- Postmortem assigned to the incident commander within 24 hours of resolution
- Review completed within 5 business days
- Action items tracked in the same place as team OKRs or sprint backlog

**Common failure here**: Postmortems get written but action items go nowhere. Assign a champion to track postmortem actions in the SRE weekly review.

### Phase 2 Success Metrics

- [ ] Structured on-call rotation with runbooks for top 5 alert types
- [ ] First error budget review with product team completed
- [ ] Blameless postmortem for every P1/P2 (not just high-profile incidents)
- [ ] Postmortem action items tracked and reviewed in weekly SRE meeting

## Phase 3 — Operationalize (Weeks 19–26)

**Goal**: SRE practices are no longer the "new initiative" — they are the operating model. The test: would a new hire experience SRE as how we work, not as a special program?

### Weeks 19–22 — Error Budget Policy in Place

Formalize what happens when error budget is exhausted.

**Policy elements**:
- At 50% budget consumed: engineering team is notified; deployment pace reviewed
- At 75% budget consumed: no new features deploy until budget is restored or SLO is revised
- At 100% budget consumed: mandatory reliability sprint; PM and Director notified; exception requires VP approval

**How to introduce the policy**: Present it in an SRE review. Get the product team's acknowledgment. Publish it to Confluence. It is not a threat — it is a shared agreement about what reliability means.

### Weeks 23–24 — Toil Reduction in OKRs

Add toil reduction as an explicit OKR for the next planning cycle.

**Example OKR**:
- Objective: Reduce operational toil to free engineering capacity for reliability investment
- KR1: Reduce weekly toil hours per engineer from [baseline] to [target] by end of quarter
- KR2: Automate 3 of the top 5 toil items identified in Phase 1
- KR3: On-call incident volume reduced by 20% vs. same quarter last year

**Why this matters**: Once toil reduction is in the OKR, it has budget, visibility, and a review cycle behind it. It is no longer "nice to have."

### Weeks 25–26 — SRE Practices in Hiring and Onboarding

The final anchor: new people join into a world where SRE is normal.

**Hiring criteria additions**:
- Interview question: "Tell me about a postmortem you were involved in. What did the team learn?"
- Rubric: Do they distinguish between system failure and human failure? Do they think in SLIs?

**Onboarding additions**:
- Day 1: Tour the SLO dashboard — new hires see the error budget before they see the sprint board
- Week 1: Shadow on-call for one shift with a runbook in hand
- Week 2: Attend one SRE weekly review

### Phase 3 Success Metrics

- [ ] Error budget policy published and acknowledged by product and engineering leadership
- [ ] Toil reduction OKR in the next planning cycle
- [ ] SRE practices embedded in interview rubric and onboarding checklist
- [ ] MTTR, deployment frequency, and toil % measurably improved vs. baseline

## Common Failure Modes and Interventions

| Failure Mode | Signal | Intervention |
|-------------|--------|--------------|
| Sponsor disengages | Stops attending monthly SRE review, delegates to a proxy | Request a 30-minute 1:1 with sponsor; surface the cost of disengagement in concrete terms |
| Champions burn out | Champions are doing all the work; peers are passive consumers | Redistribute facilitation; formalize champions as a rotating role with visible recognition |
| SLOs defined but nobody uses them | Error budget discussed only in formal reviews, not in sprint planning | Push product to include SLO status in sprint planning check-in |
| Postmortems become blame-free in name only | Postmortems written, but action items always assigned to one person | Facilitate a postmortem review session focused on systemic action items |
| Toil reduction work deprioritized | Engineers start toil automation tickets; tickets get bumped every sprint | Protect one sprint per quarter as reliability investment sprint |
| Team thinks the transformation is done at Phase 1 | "We have SLOs now, we're good" mentality | Publish the phase roadmap; make Phase 3 visible as the actual finish line |

## Exit Criteria — Anchored vs. Still Fragile

**The transformation is anchored when**:
- A new team member learns SRE practices from peers, not just from you
- A postmortem happens after a P2 without you initiating it
- Product management references error budgets in their own planning discussions
- Toil reduction appears in a team member's individual growth goals, not just team OKRs
- A job candidate asks about your SLO practices in an interview

**The transformation is still fragile when**:
- All SRE review facilitation still requires you or a champion
- Error budget reviews only happen when you schedule them
- Postmortems happen for P1s but not consistently for P2s
- Any key champion leaving the team would significantly slow down the practices
- The team describes SRE as "what management wants" rather than "how we work"
