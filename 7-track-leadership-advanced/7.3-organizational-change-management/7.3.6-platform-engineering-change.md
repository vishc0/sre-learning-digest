# Chapter 6: Change Management in Platform Engineering — Building What People Will Actually Use

## The Fundamental Tension

Platform engineering teams are in a structurally unusual position. They are building a product for customers who did not ask for it, who are already serving their own customers with their own tools, and who will judge the platform not on its technical merit but on whether it makes their day easier or harder. Unlike external product teams, platform teams cannot lose customers to a competitor — but they can be routed around, which is functionally the same thing.

This tension does not resolve through better engineering. A better Kubernetes operator does not automatically produce adoption. A slicker internal developer portal does not, by itself, change how product teams onboard new services. The gap between "technically excellent platform" and "platform people actually use" is a change management gap. Bridging it requires applying the same rigor to adoption that you would apply to reliability.

---

## The Field of Dreams Fallacy

"If you build it, they will come" is the organizing myth of platform engineering. It is also wrong in a specific, predictable way.

Platform teams that operate under this assumption build for their own mental model of what developers need, ship behind a Confluence page announcement, and then measure success by features deployed rather than teams onboarded. Twelve months later, they wonder why utilization numbers are low while simultaneously observing that every product team has their own slightly different version of the CI pipeline they were supposed to replace.

The failure mode is not technical. It is that the platform team optimized for building and not for adoption. Adoption is a different discipline. It requires understanding why people do not change even when the alternative is better, which requires humility about the fact that "better" is always contextual. A platform that is objectively more secure and cost-efficient is still asking the product team to spend engineering cycles migrating off tools they understand, during a quarter where they have commitments to their own stakeholders. The platform team's roadmap is not the product team's priority. That asymmetry is the core of the adoption problem.

---

## Forcing Functions vs. Pull-Based Adoption

This is one of the most consequential strategic choices a platform team makes, and it deserves honest analysis rather than ideology.

**Forcing functions** — mandating platform use through policy, compliance gates, or architecture review board requirements — work when the leverage is real and the authority is clear. If a security control requires that all services use the platform's secrets management, that is a forcing function with a legitimate organizational backing. Teams will comply because the alternative is a failed security audit, not because they chose to.

Forcing functions fail when they are mandates without teeth, when the platform is not actually ready for production use, or when teams have enough organizational authority to push back. The worst outcome is a forcing function that gets routed around: the product team nominally adopts the platform (enough to pass the audit) while maintaining a shadow process for their actual work. This produces the worst of both worlds — the platform team claims adoption numbers, the product team carries double the operational burden, and the platform never improves because the real usage patterns stay hidden.

**Pull-based adoption** is slower but produces more durable outcomes. It works by making the platform the path of least resistance for the things developers already want to do — deploy faster, reduce toil, stay out of compliance meetings. The platform team's job is to remove enough friction from the path that teams choose it without being compelled.

The honest answer is that most successful platform programs use both. Forcing functions accelerate adoption past the inertia threshold. Pull-based adoption creates the conditions where teams stay on the platform because it genuinely serves them.

## Golden Paths as Social Contracts

The term "golden path" has become standard platform engineering vocabulary, but its organizational meaning is often undersold. A golden path is not just a technical pattern. It is a social contract between the platform team and the product team.

The implicit promise of a golden path is: "If you follow this path, the platform team has your back. When something goes wrong at 3am and it involves the infrastructure layer, we will be there. When the security audit asks about how your secrets are managed, we will have the answer. When you need to scale your deployment, the path you followed already accounts for it."

That promise has to be kept. A golden path that breaks under production load, or that the platform team cannot support when a product team hits an edge case, is worse than no golden path at all. It teaches the product team that the platform team makes promises it cannot keep. That lesson, once learned, is very hard to unlearn.

Building a golden path, therefore, is not a sprint deliverable. It is an ongoing operational commitment. The technical implementation is the smaller part of the work.

## Platform Team as Product Team

The organizational model that consistently produces high-adoption platforms is the one that treats the platform team as a product team with internal customers. This means product thinking applied to infrastructure work: user research, feature prioritization based on adoption impact, a defined feedback loop, and adoption metrics as a first-class success measure.

User research for platform teams looks different than for consumer products, but the discipline is the same. It means understanding how product teams actually work — not how the platform team assumes they work. It means watching engineers onboard a new service and noting where they get confused. It means reading the tickets that come in to the platform team and categorizing them not by resolution status but by what they reveal about gaps in the platform experience.

At T-Mobile, where the platform serves multiple product lines with different team maturity levels, this user research has to be segmented. A platform feature that unblocks an early-stage product team may create friction for a mature team that has already built their own solution. Platform roadmap decisions need to account for that heterogeneity, or the platform will optimize for one segment while creating drag for another.

## Measuring Adoption as a Change Metric

Adoption metrics for platforms fall into two categories, and tracking only one category produces misleading conclusions.

**Leading indicators** tell you whether teams are engaging with the platform: services onboarded per quarter, time-to-first-deployment for new services on the platform, documentation page views, developer portal active users, tickets filed versus tickets auto-resolved.

**Lagging indicators** tell you whether the platform is producing the outcomes it was built to enable: DORA metrics (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR) for platform-onboarded services versus non-onboarded services, ticket deflection rate (support requests that the platform eliminated), toil reduction measured in engineer-hours.

The gap between leading and lagging indicators is where the most important information lives. High onboarding numbers with flat DORA metrics means teams are nominally on the platform but not getting value from it. Low onboarding with improving DORA metrics for onboarded teams means the platform works but the adoption motion is broken. Reading both together gives you an accurate picture of where to intervene.

## Internal Developer Advocacy

Platform teams are frequently excellent engineers and poor communicators. The gap shows up in how they talk about their work: technically precise, feature-oriented, and almost entirely absent from the places where product teams make decisions about their toolchain.

Internal developer advocacy is the function that closes this gap. It operates like external product marketing, adapted for a closed audience. It means writing internal case studies when a product team has a genuine win on the platform. It means presenting at engineering all-hands not to announce new features but to tell the story of what a team was able to do that they could not do before. It means maintaining a champions program — a small group of engaged developers from product teams who get early access to platform features in exchange for structured feedback and internal evangelism.

The champion program is often the highest-leverage change investment a platform team can make. Champions spread adoption in the peer-to-peer channels that platform announcements cannot reach. They normalize platform use within their teams through daily behavior, not through policy. And they give the platform team a standing source of user research that does not require scheduling a quarterly interview cycle.

The change management principle underlying all of this is the same one that governs SRE transformations: tools do not drive adoption. People who trust each other do.
