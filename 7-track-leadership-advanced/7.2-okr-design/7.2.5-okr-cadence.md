# Chapter 5: The OKR Cadence — Running the Quarterly Cycle

## Bottom Line First

OKRs fail not because of bad writing — they fail because of bad cadence. Most organizations write OKRs in week 1, ignore them until week 12, and then grade them under pressure in an afternoon. That is not an OKR program. That is quarterly theater. The cadence IS the program. Without a structured cycle of Set → Check-in → Score → Reflect, OKRs are just an aspirational document no one reads.

---

## The Four Phases

Think of the quarterly OKR cycle the way you think about a PI in SAFe delivery. PI Planning defines intent. Sprint reviews reveal drift and recalibrate. The PI retrospective extracts learning. The next PI starts with what you learned, not a blank slate. OKR cadence works the same way — each phase serves a distinct function, and collapsing them together destroys the value of all of them.

---

## Phase 1 — Set (Weeks 1–2 of Quarter)

**Purpose**: translate strategy into measurable commitments for the next 13 weeks.

The Set phase is a negotiation, not a broadcast. Directors do not hand down OKRs and tell managers to cascade them. They draft department-level OKRs, share them with their management team, and run a structured review where managers stress-test the wording, flag missing dependencies, and surface conflicts with their team's capacity.

**Week 1**: Director drafts 2–3 department Objectives with placeholder KRs. Shares with direct reports for reaction. The question is not "do you agree?" — it is "what would make this OKR unmeasurable, irrelevant, or sandbagged from your vantage point?"

**Week 2**: Teams draft their own OKRs in response. Director reviews for alignment — not control. The alignment question: does each team OKR connect to at least one department KR? If a team OKR cannot be traced to a department objective, it is either missing from the department OKRs (a problem) or it is a maintenance task that shouldn't be an OKR at all.

Finalization deadline: end of week 2. OKRs published to the full team. No more changes unless a material business context shift occurs.

---

## Phase 2 — Check-in (Month 2 of Quarter, Weeks 5–8)

**Purpose**: learn what has changed, not report what has happened.

The mid-quarter check-in is the most misunderstood phase. Directors who run it as a status meeting — "where are we on KR 1?" — miss the point entirely. The correct framing is a learning meeting. The central question is: **"What have we learned in the first six weeks that changes what we should be doing?"**

This distinction matters operationally. A status meeting produces a score update. A learning meeting can produce a strategic pivot. If your business context shifted in month 1 — a platform migration accelerated, an acquisition changed the roadmap, a regulatory requirement surfaced — the check-in is where you formally decide whether to adjust a KR, retire it, or hold it. Doing nothing and hoping the quarter recovers is not leadership.

**Scoring at check-in**: assess each KR on the 0.0–1.0 scale based on current trajectory, not current position. A KR at 0.3 after 6 weeks might be on track (if the work is back-loaded) or in trouble (if it was expected to be at 0.6 by now). The score is a forcing function for an honest conversation, not a number to be optimized.

**Blocker identification**: any KR scoring below 0.4 at mid-quarter should have an explicit blocker documented. Not "we're behind" — but the specific dependency, decision, or resource gap that is creating the drag. This is what the Director acts on.

---

## Phase 3 — Score (Final Week of Quarter)

**Purpose**: honest accounting of what was achieved.

Scoring happens in the final week, not the final day. Give teams 3–4 business days to score their KRs, write a one-paragraph narrative per Objective, and surface what drove the outcome — execution quality, changed context, or bad goal-setting.

The Objective score is not an average of the KR scores. It is a judgment call. If you hit 3 of 4 KRs but the one you missed was the highest-leverage one, the Objective score should reflect that reality. Mechanical averaging produces misleading signals.

**The grading philosophy that separates Directors from Managers**: a 0.7 on a genuinely hard OKR is a better result than a 1.0 on a sandbagged one. If a team consistently scores 0.9–1.0 every quarter, that is not excellence — it is a signal that the targets were not ambitious enough. The acceptable range for a well-calibrated, stretch OKR is 0.6–0.8. Consistently hitting 1.0 means you are leaving growth on the table. Consistently hitting below 0.5 means either the targets were unrealistic or execution has a structural problem.

---

## Phase 4 — Reflect (Between Quarters, 1–2 Weeks Before Next Set Phase)

**Purpose**: extract learning before the next planning cycle erases the memory of this one.

The Reflect phase is the one most organizations skip. They close out Q1 on a Friday and start Q2 OKR drafts on Monday. This is the equivalent of skipping the PI retrospective and going straight into the next PI Planning. The learning never gets institutionalized.

A structured Reflect session (60–90 minutes with the management team) should produce three outputs:

1. **What we carry forward**: OKRs that are still relevant but not yet complete — transition them into the next quarter with updated baselines, not as "carryovers" that imply failure.
2. **What we retire**: OKRs that were completed, superseded by strategic change, or that proved to be the wrong question entirely. Retiring an OKR is not failure — it is strategic clarity.
3. **What we learned about our own goal-setting**: Were our KRs measurable? Did we have data to score them? Were they outcomes or activities? This meta-learning feeds directly into the quality of the next Set phase.

---

## Rolling OKRs vs. Retiring Them

The decision to carry or retire is not about completion — it is about relevance.

**Carry forward when**: the goal is still strategically important, you have a clear line of sight to completion in the next quarter, and the team is energized by the remaining work.

**Retire when**: the strategic priority shifted, the KR proved unmeasurable or poorly defined, or the team completed the underlying work but the metric was a proxy that no longer matters.

Never carry forward an OKR out of sunk-cost logic. "We put effort into this" is not a reason to continue. Directors who carry irrelevant OKRs quarter after quarter train their teams to write OKRs that no one believes in.

---

## The Cadence at a Glance

| Phase | Timing | Primary Question | Output |
|-------|--------|-----------------|--------|
| Set | Weeks 1–2 | What are we committing to? | Published, aligned OKRs |
| Check-in | Month 2 | What have we learned? | Adjusted priorities, documented blockers |
| Score | Week 12–13 | What did we actually achieve? | Honest scores + narratives |
| Reflect | Between quarters | What do we carry, retire, and learn? | Input to next Set phase |

The cadence is the program. Running all four phases with discipline is what separates an organization that uses OKRs from one that performs them.
