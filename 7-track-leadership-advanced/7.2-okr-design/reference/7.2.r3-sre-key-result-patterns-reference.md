# SRE Key Result Patterns — Reference Library

**Audience**: Director/VP Platform Engineering interviews and OKR design reviews.
**How to use**: Find the category that matches your objective. Start with the Good vs. Bad comparison at the top of each category to calibrate the quality bar. Then select from the pattern table and adapt baselines to your actual data.

**Non-negotiable rule**: Every KR you write must have a named baseline and a named data source before the quarter starts. A KR without both is not a KR — it is a hope.

---

## Category 1: Reliability / SLO Management

**Design principle**: KRs in this category should move a specific reliability metric from a measured starting point, not describe a floor to stand on. "Maintain 99.9%" is an SLO constraint. An OKR starts from where you are and asks where you intend to go.

**Good KR vs. Bad KR**

| Version | KR Text | Why |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Good | Reduce the P1 error budget burn rate for the checkout service from 2.4x to 1.0x over Q3, sustained for the final 4 weeks of the quarter | Names the service, names the metric, requires sustained hold — not a point-in-time measurement that can be hit by one good week |
| Bad | Maintain 99.9% availability for the checkout service | This is the SLO. It was already the target before the OKR cycle. Hitting it requires nothing to improve. Missing it means an SLA violation, not a stretch goal miss. |

---

| Category | KR Text | Baseline Data Source | Measurement Method | Common Pitfall |
|----------|---------|---------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| Reliability | Reduce P1 error budget burn rate for the payment API from 2.4x to ≤1.0x, sustained for the final 4 consecutive weeks of the quarter | SLO burn rate dashboard (Datadog / Grafana SLO report), measured over the 6 weeks before quarter start | Weekly burn rate snapshot from the SLO dashboard, averaged over weeks 9–12 of the quarter | Burn rate can look healthy for 8 weeks and spike in week 10 — "sustained 4 weeks" prevents a false finish |
| Reliability | Increase 30-day rolling SLO compliance from 87% to 95% across the 12 defined tier-1 microservices (list frozen at quarter start) | 30-day SLO compliance per service, from the observability platform, averaged over Q2 | Monthly compliance report from monitoring tooling, averaged across the defined service list | "12 Tier-1 services" must be a fixed list set at quarter start — allow scope to drift and the metric becomes meaningless |
| Reliability | Reduce mean time to detect (MTTD) critical path failures from 8 minutes to ≤3 minutes for payment and auth services | MTTD per incident from incident management system (PagerDuty / ServiceNow), last 90 days | Median MTTD from all P1/P2 incidents in Q3 involving the named services | Alert storms can artificially reduce MTTD — define MTTD as "time to first actionable, non-duplicate alert," not "time to first page" |
| Reliability | Eliminate all customer-impacting incidents caused by the 7 documented known failure modes in the open issue register | Open known-issue register with customer impact classification, reviewed and frozen at quarter start | Close rate on the named list of 7 items, verified via postmortem tagging at quarter end | Teams must maintain the list honestly — adding items mid-quarter to hit a partial close rate is sandbagging |
| Reliability | Reduce P95 API gateway latency from 420ms to ≤200ms at peak load (5,000 RPS), verified by reproducible load test at quarter start and end | P95 latency at 5k RPS from APM tooling (Datadog APM / Dynatrace) or load test results | Reproducible load test run at fixed configuration at quarter start (baseline) and quarter end (result) | Production traffic varies — passive production observation is insufficient; require a reproducible load test as the measurement method |
| Reliability | Reduce the percentage of error budget consumed by infrastructure-layer failures (vs. application code failures) from 78% to ≤40% | Error budget burn source classification from postmortem tagging in incident system, last 2 quarters | Postmortem tagging audit at quarter end — sum budget consumed by infrastructure-tagged incidents | Requires honest postmortem tagging at the time of the incident; retro-tagging at quarter end produces optimistic numbers |

## Category 2: Toil Reduction

**Design principle**: Name the specific toil class, anchor to a measured hour or ticket baseline, and require the automation to be actively running in production — not merged to a repo and waiting to be enabled.

**Good KR vs. Bad KR**

| Version | KR Text | Why |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Good | Reduce manual certificate rotation tickets from 47 per quarter to ≤10 per quarter via automated rotation for all non-legacy services | Names the toil class (cert rotation), the volume baseline (47), and the mechanism scope (non-legacy services). Honest about what's excluded. |
| Bad | Automate certificate management | No baseline, no target, no scope, no measurement method. Hitting it requires writing code; not hitting it requires not writing code. The outcome for engineers is invisible. |

---

| Category | KR Text | Baseline Data Source | Measurement Method | Common Pitfall |
|----------|---------|---------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| Toil Reduction | Reduce manual certificate rotation tickets from 47 per quarter to ≤10 per quarter via automated rotation for all non-legacy services | Ticket count by category from ITSM (ServiceNow / Jira), filtered to cert rotation label, last 2 quarters | Ticket volume at quarter close, filtered to cert rotation category | Teams may reclassify tickets rather than eliminate work — require ticket volume reduction AND verify automation run logs show completions |
| Toil Reduction | Eliminate 100% of manual DNS record update requests for clusters 1–8 by deploying automated DNS management (ExternalDNS or equivalent) | Volume of DNS update tickets per month (ITSM); current cluster inventory (8 clusters identified) | DNS update ticket count at quarter end; automation run confirmation from cluster 1–8 DNS logs | Clusters 9–12 may remain manual — the scope must be explicitly bounded in the KR; "100%" only applies within the defined scope |
| Toil Reduction | Reduce SRE-hours spent on manual scaling interventions from 18 hours per month to ≤4 hours per month by rolling HPA and KEDA across 60 services | Time tracking or ticket effort estimates from last 90 days (Jira story points or time-log, cross-referenced with scaling incident records) | SRE on-call log review plus ticket effort field at quarter end, averaged across 3 months | "Hours spent" requires honest time tracking — if it wasn't tracked before, establish a baseline measurement in week 1 of the quarter; without a baseline, this KR cannot be scored |
| Toil Reduction | Reduce MTTR for the top 5 incident types by volume from 42 minutes to ≤20 minutes by deploying automated runbook execution for those 5 types | MTTR by incident type from incident management system, filtered to the top 5 by Q2 occurrence count; types named and frozen at quarter start | MTTR per incident type at quarter end, filtered to the same 5 types using the same incident tags | Automating low-volume incident types that weren't in the top 5 doesn't count — the five types must be locked at quarter start by actual volume, not by what's easiest to automate |
| Toil Reduction | Reduce engineer-hours required for the quarterly access certification from 120 hours to ≤30 hours via automated role report generation | Last quarterly access review effort estimate from EM and team lead time tracking (or engineer time log) | Effort tracking on the Q3 access certification event — total engineer hours from ticket open to sign-off | Access reviews may be delayed to Q4 — require completion within the quarter; measuring this KR requires the event to actually happen in Q3 |

## Category 3: On-Call Health

**Design principle**: On-call health is measurable. Pages per week, MTTR, escalation rate, and engineer sentiment via survey are all trackable with existing data. "Improve on-call experience" is a sentiment, not a KR.

**Good KR vs. Bad KR**

| Version | KR Text | Why |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Good | Reduce weekly actionable alert volume per on-call engineer from 94 to ≤30 by eliminating noisy, low-fidelity alert rules (audit conducted in week 1) | Baseline is specific, target is specific, "actionable" is defined to prevent gaming by alert suppression, mechanism is named |
| Bad | Reduce alert noise | No baseline, no target, no definition of "noise," no measurement method. The team could silence 500 useful alerts and score 1.0. |

---

| Category | KR Text | Baseline Data Source | Measurement Method | Common Pitfall |
|----------|---------|---------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| On-Call Health | Reduce weekly actionable alert volume per on-call engineer from 94 to ≤30 — where "actionable" is defined as requiring a human decision (not acknowledged and dismissed) | PagerDuty / OpsGenie alert data for the last 90 days, filtered to on-call rotation slots, classified by disposition (acknowledged vs. actioned) | Weekly alert count per on-call rotation slot, averaged over Q3, with actionability classification | Teams may suppress rather than fix — require alerts to be deleted, merged, or reconfigured (not muted); muting preserves the alert and the noise |
| On-Call Health | Reduce after-hours pages (10pm–7am local time per engineer's primary time zone) from 22% of total pages to ≤8% by end of Q3 | PagerDuty incident data with timestamp for the last 90 days; define "after-hours" window per engineer time zone at quarter start | Percentage of total pages falling in the defined after-hours window, measured at quarter end | For distributed teams, define "after-hours" per engineer location at quarter start — failing to do this creates a window that shifts with team roster changes |
| On-Call Health | Reduce Sev-1 on-call MTTR from 38 minutes to ≤20 minutes across all incidents with on-call-initiated response | Incident management system (PagerDuty + incident tickets) MTTR history for Sev-1s in the last 6 months | Median Sev-1 MTTR at quarter end from the incident system, filtered to on-call-initiated responses | Use median, not mean — one 4-hour Sev-1 caused by an unusual failure mode will inflate the mean and obscure a genuine trend. Median is more robust for small incident samples. |
| On-Call Health | Achieve on-call sustainability score ≤2.0 (on a 1–5 scale, where 5 = unsustainable) across all SRE team members in the quarterly survey | Establish baseline in week 1 if no prior survey exists; use last survey score if available (Q2 baseline: establish or retrieve) | Anonymous quarterly engineer survey administered by EM, collected at a stable period (not immediately post-major-incident) | Self-reported scores spike after high-severity incidents; run the survey 2+ weeks after any Sev-1 to get a baseline-representative reading rather than a reaction score |
| On-Call Health | Reduce escalation rate — on-call to senior/principal/manager — from 34% of incidents to ≤15% by improving runbook coverage for all tier-1 services | Escalation rate from incident management system, last 6 months; filtered to on-call-initiated incidents | Escalation rate at quarter end from incident management system, using the same filter criteria | Runbooks may be written but not used — verify runbook utilization rate (how often on-call accessed the runbook during an incident), not just that runbooks exist and are updated |
| On-Call Health | Achieve zero on-call-related voluntary resignations during Q3, tracked via exit interview root cause coding | Exit interviews from last 12 months coded for on-call burden as a contributing factor (HR data) | Exit interview exit coding for any voluntary departures in Q3 | This KR is a lagging indicator — if it fires (someone leaves for this reason), use the other on-call KRs to understand what the leading indicators missed |

## Category 4: Deployment Frequency and Safety

**Design principle**: Deployment OKRs must hold both pace and safety simultaneously. A KR that increases frequency without a corresponding safety constraint rewards the wrong behavior. Speed and stability must be paired.

**Good KR vs. Bad KR**

| Version | KR Text | Why |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Good | Increase deployment frequency for the order management domain from 2 deploys per week to 5 per week while holding rollback rate at ≤3% | Pairs the pace target with a safety constraint. A team cannot hit this KR by deploying faster and breaking things more often. |
| Bad | Deploy more frequently | No domain scope, no baseline, no target, no safety constraint. Satisfying this KR requires only that the team deploys at least once more than they did before. |

---

| Category | KR Text | Baseline Data Source | Measurement Method | Common Pitfall |
|----------|---------|---------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| Deployment | Increase deployment frequency for the order management domain from 2 deploys per week to 5 per week while holding rollback rate at ≤3% | DORA deployment frequency from CI/CD pipeline data, last quarter; rollback rate from deployment + incident logs | Deployment frequency and rollback rate from pipeline telemetry at quarter end, averaged over Q3 | "Deployments" must be defined at quarter start — a hotfix and a feature release should count the same; agree on definition before the quarter begins |
| Deployment | Reduce mean time to recover from failed deployments from 47 minutes to ≤15 minutes by implementing automated rollback across all tier-1 services | Deployment failure MTTR from CI/CD pipeline + incident data for the last 6 months | Rollback time from pipeline telemetry, averaged over all rollback events in Q3 | Automated rollback can mask root cause — require a post-rollback root cause record for every event; without it, the same failure mode will recur |
| Deployment | Reduce change failure rate (deployments that cause a P2+ incident within 2 hours) from 8% to ≤3% across the platform by expanding canary deployment coverage from 20% to 80% of services | Change failure rate from DORA metrics pipeline; canary coverage percentage from deployment configuration audit | Change failure rate at quarter end from deployment + incident correlation; canary coverage from CI/CD config at quarter end | Canary routing without canary health check enforcement doesn't reduce failures — require canary analysis gates, not just canary traffic splitting |
| Deployment | Achieve zero unplanned production deployments (hotfixes that bypass the standard change approval process) in Q3 | Change management system audit — count of deployments with emergency vs. standard CAB approval, last 2 quarters | Change record audit at quarter end — count of deployments tagged emergency or out-of-process | "Unplanned" must be defined in the change management policy before the quarter — teams may reclassify emergency changes as "expedited standard" to avoid the count |
| Deployment | Reduce lead time from code commit to production for the payments domain from 11 days to ≤3 days by removing manual approval gates and replacing with automated quality gates | Lead time from git commit timestamp to production deployment timestamp, last 90 days (DORA metrics from CI/CD pipeline) | Lead time from CI/CD pipeline data, averaged over all commits that reached production in Q3 | Removing approval gates requires risk mitigation first — ensure automated test coverage, SAST gate, and canary rollout are in place before removing human gates |

## Category 5: Platform Self-Service Maturity

**Design principle**: Self-service KRs measure adoption and outcome, not artifact delivery. "We built the portal" is not a KR. "12 teams no longer need to open a ticket to provision an environment" is a KR. The distinction is: who did the work change for?

**Good KR vs. Bad KR**

| Version | KR Text | Why |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Good | Reduce SRE-assisted environment provisioning requests from 60 per month to ≤10 per month by delivering self-service provisioning covering 80% of standard use cases | Measures the ticket reduction (outcome for product teams), not the feature delivery (output for SRE). |
| Bad | Build and launch the self-service environment provisioning feature | Binary delivery milestone. A feature that launches and nobody uses scores 1.0. A feature that engineers love but isn't finished scores 0.0. The metric is about the wrong team's experience. |

---

| Category | KR Text | Baseline Data Source | Measurement Method | Common Pitfall |
|----------|---------|---------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| Self-Service | Reduce SRE-assisted environment provisioning requests from 60 per month to ≤10 per month by delivering self-service provisioning for 80% of standard use cases | Volume of provisioning tickets from ITSM for the last 3 months, by use case category | Ticket volume at quarter end by category; use case coverage from platform team assessment | "Standard use cases" must be defined and frozen at quarter start — teams will debate which remaining tickets are "edge cases" vs. missing functionality at scoring time |
| Self-Service | Increase developer satisfaction with the internal platform from 3.1/5 to ≥4.0/5 on the quarterly DevEx survey (n≥100 respondents) | Q2 DevEx survey results (score + sample size); if no prior survey, establish baseline in week 1 | Q3 DevEx survey score, administered by end of month 3 with minimum response count enforced | Survey scores lag operational changes by 4–8 weeks — run the survey in week 11 of the quarter, not week 13; a survey after the quarter closes cannot be measured in Q3 |
| Self-Service | Reduce median time to onboard a new microservice to the observability platform from 8 days to ≤1 day by automating instrumentation scaffolding | Onboarding ticket duration from ITSM for the last 6 months — ticket open to "instrumentation complete" close | Median onboarding duration at quarter end from ticket timestamps; sample must include at least 5 onboardings in Q3 | Automated instrumentation may not support all tech stacks — require test coverage across the top 5 languages/frameworks in the service catalog before claiming the KR is achievable |
| Self-Service | Achieve ≥90% of product teams capable of executing blue-green deployments independently without SRE involvement — verified by quarterly self-service deployment audit | Current percentage of product teams that open an SRE ticket or Slack message before executing a blue-green deploy (assess in week 1) | Deployment audit at quarter end — review deployment records for blue-green events and flag any with associated SRE tickets or informal Slack requests | Product teams may ask SRE informally via Slack without opening a ticket — the audit must cover both formal tickets and Slack channels; channel audits require prior communication to the team |
| Self-Service | Reduce unique platform how-to questions posted in the #platform-help Slack channel by 60% by publishing self-service guides for the top 20 question types | Slack message count in #platform-help for the prior quarter; question categorization audit in week 1 to identify the top 20 | Slack message count in #platform-help at quarter end, compared to prior quarter baseline | Message volume may shift to adjacent channels or DMs rather than actually decreasing — audit #platform-engineering, #sre-oncall, and direct SRE DMs for the same question patterns |

## Category 6: Security Posture

**Design principle**: Security KRs must be auditable by someone outside the engineering team. If a VP of Product cannot read the KR and say definitively whether it was hit, rewrite it. Jargon and internal acronyms produce accountability blind spots.

**Good KR vs. Bad KR**

| Version | KR Text | Why |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Good | Reduce critical and high CVEs in production container images from 847 to ≤200 by end of Q3 via automated image scanning in CI/CD | Baseline count is concrete, target is specific, mechanism is named, data source is named — a non-engineer can verify this at quarter end |
| Bad | Improve container security posture | No baseline, no target, no mechanism, no measurement method. Anyone can claim this was hit at quarter end. |

---

| Category | KR Text | Baseline Data Source | Measurement Method | Common Pitfall |
|----------|---------|---------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| Security | Reduce critical and high CVEs in production container images from 847 to ≤200 by end of Q3 via automated image scanning enforcement in the CI/CD pipeline | Current CVE count from container scanning tool (Trivy, Snyk, or Prisma Cloud), scoped to production image inventory | CVE count from scanning tool at quarter end, scoped to the same production image inventory defined at quarter start | CVE count changes as new vulnerabilities are published — freeze the measurement to a specific image inventory (images actually running in production at quarter start + quarter end), not a rolling catalog |
| Security | Achieve 100% of Kubernetes workloads running with Pod Security Standards (Restricted policy) across all production clusters by end of Q3 | Current percentage of workloads with Restricted PSS from a cluster audit; run at quarter start | Cluster policy audit at quarter end using the same audit script and cluster scope | Legacy workloads may require exceptions — require exception documentation with owner sign-off rather than silent exclusions from scope; undocumented exclusions are compliance gaps, not exceptions |
| Security | Reduce mean time to remediate critical vulnerabilities (CVSS ≥9.0) from 42 days to ≤7 days, measured across all critical CVEs in the platform asset inventory | Vulnerability remediation timeline data from security tooling (Prisma Cloud / Snyk), last 6 months, filtered to CVSS ≥9.0 | Median MTTR for critical CVEs at quarter end — from CVE publication date (NVD) to production deployment of patched version | "Remediation" must be defined unambiguously: patched version built, scanned clean, and deployed to production — not "patch available in repo" or "ticket created" |
| Security | Achieve zero secrets committed to any production code repository in Q3, enforced via automated pre-commit hook plus CI-level scanning gate | Current secrets detection rate from tooling (Gitleaks, git-secrets, truffleHog), last 90 days | Scanning tool report at quarter end plus incident log search for any secret exposure events in Q3 | Pre-commit hooks can be bypassed with --no-verify — require CI-level enforcement as the authoritative gate; local hooks are a developer courtesy, not a security control |
| Security | Complete evidence collection for 18 SOC 2 Type II platform controls by Q3 end, reducing audit preparation elapsed time from 6 weeks to ≤2 weeks | Prior audit preparation timeline from compliance lead; control list from compliance framework with owner mapping | Control completion rate at quarter end; audit prep time tracked in this cycle from "audit kickoff" to "evidence package submitted" | Control completion does not equal audit readiness — require evidence review by the compliance team or external auditor before claiming this KR is met; a checklist of claimed completions is not evidence |
| Security | Reduce the percentage of platform services with hardcoded secrets detected in the codebase from 23% to ≤3% by enforcing secret scanning in CI for all repositories | Current percentage of services with at least one hardcoded secret (GitGuardian or equivalent, scoped to production repositories) | Scanning tool report at quarter end, scoped to the same repository list; 3% allowance covers services onboarded in the final 2 weeks of the quarter | New services joining the platform mid-quarter need a 30-day remediation window — define the 3% residual as "services onboarded within the last 30 days," not as a general exception allowance |

## Quick Calibration: KR Quality Checklist

| Check | Question | Fail Signal |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Outcome vs. output | Does the KR describe a change in behavior or system state — not a task completion? | Primary verb is "implement," "deploy," "build," "document," "complete," or "train" |
| Baseline exists | Is the starting point measured from a named system before the quarter begins? | "Estimated," "approximately," "TBD," or baseline to be established |
| Data source named | Is the system that produces the quarter-end score identified and accessible now? | "We will track this," "to be determined," or no system named |
| Single metric | Does this KR measure exactly one thing? | "And" connecting two distinct outcomes in one statement |
| Team influence | Can the team move this without requiring another team's decision? | Outcome requires budget approval, another team's delivery, or executive alignment |
| Gaming resistance | Could the team hit this KR while the underlying objective fails? | Metric can be improved by proxy action without real outcome change |
| Layperson readable | Can a VP of Product tell if this KR was hit without a technical glossary? | Internal acronyms, unexplained tool names, or undefined threshold criteria |
| Stretch calibration | Would 50–85% confidence be realistic when the KR is written? | >90% confidence (sandbagged) or <35% (disconnected from team capacity) |
