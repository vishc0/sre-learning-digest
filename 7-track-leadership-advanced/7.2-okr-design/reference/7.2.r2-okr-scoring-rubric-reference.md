# OKR Scoring Rubric Reference
**Audience**: Director/VP Platform Engineering interviews and team calibration sessions
**Bottom line**: 0.7 is the target. Consistent 1.0 is a red flag. Design for failure to exceed.

---

## Score Anchor Table

| Score | Label | What It Means | Example Evidence | Interview-Ready Explanation |
|-------|-------|---------------|------------------|----------------------------|
| **0.0** | Not started / Abandoned | Work never began or was formally withdrawn | No commits, no tickets closed, KR removed from tracker | "We deprioritized this in week 2 due to an incident response surge. It's a 0.0 — we own it and it informs next quarter's capacity planning." |
| **0.3** | Significant miss | Serious effort invested, far from target | Proof of work exists (sprints completed, code merged) but outcome gap is large | "We ran three sprints against this and moved the needle 30% of target. The work was real; the target was too aggressive or the dependency risk was underestimated." |
| **0.5** | Partial | Meaningful progress made; core value partially realized | Baseline improved, some teams onboarded, partial automation in place | "We delivered half the value. That's not failure — that's a data point. We now know the constraint was X, which we'll address in Q3." |
| **0.7** | Target hit | Expected outcome for a well-calibrated stretch OKR | KR met as written; no goalpost movement; measured against original baseline | "We hit 0.7. That's the expectation for a stretch goal — if we hit 1.0 consistently, our targets aren't stretching us." |
| **1.0** | Exceeded | Fully delivered and materially beyond target | KR hit and sustained; secondary benefits realized; team explicitly documented what changed | "This was a 1.0. Worth analyzing — did we sandbag, did something external accelerate us, or did the team execute unusually well? The answer shapes next quarter." |

---

## The Sweet Spot Concept

**Portfolio target**: 0.6–0.7 average across all OKRs for a team or organization indicates appropriate stretch calibration.

| Average Score | Interpretation | Director Action |
|--------------|----------------|-----------------|
| < 0.4 | Targets are too aggressive OR execution is broken | Diagnose first — don't just lower targets |
| 0.5–0.6 | Slightly under-calibrated or execution gap | Identify which KRs dragged; address specifically |
| **0.6–0.7** | **Healthy stretch zone** | Reinforce this as the norm in retrospectives |
| 0.8–0.9 | Targets are likely too conservative | Push teams to recalibrate upward next cycle |
| > 0.9 | Sandbagging — goals written to be achieved, not to drive improvement | Intervene in goal-setting process |

> A portfolio average of exactly 1.0 means you are measuring the wrong things or the wrong people set the targets.

---

## Pattern Detection Table

| Score Pattern | What It Signals | Director Action |
|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Consistent 0.9–1.0 across all teams | Sandbagging — targets written to be hit, not to stretch | Return to objective-setting; require teams to show why the target is hard |
| Consistent 0.2–0.3 | Either targets are too aggressive OR execution problem — these are different diseases | Run a structured diagnosis before changing targets; look at external dependencies, incident load, staffing |
| Consistent 0.2–0.3 with high incident volume | Execution problem is capacity, not capability | Address toil/incident load first; OKR calibration follows |
| Wide variance within a single team (e.g., KR1=0.9, KR2=0.2) | Inconsistent KR calibration — some KRs were easy, others were impossible | Coach team lead on KR baseline selection and difficulty balancing |
| One team consistently 0.7, all others 0.9 | Alignment problem — either that team is genuinely stretched or others are sandbagging | Use the 0.7 team's process as the calibration benchmark; investigate the 0.9 teams |
| Scores drop sharply in Q2 every year | Planning doesn't account for summer capacity reduction or post-Q1 debt | Build capacity buffers into Q2 OKR targets |
| Team scores 0.7 but key incidents spiked | KRs measured the wrong thing — reliability improved on paper, degraded in practice | Audit KR-to-outcome alignment; ensure KRs capture user-visible reliability, not internal metrics |

## Grading Philosophy Statement
*Use verbatim or adapt for interviews when asked "how do you grade OKRs?"*

> "I grade OKRs against the original target, full stop — no adjustments for mid-quarter context changes, because adjustments destroy the signal. A 0.7 is the expected outcome for a well-designed stretch goal; a team that scores 0.7 consistently has good target calibration and good execution. I use the portfolio average as a health indicator: if the average is above 0.8, I'm having a conversation about whether we're being honest with ourselves about what's hard. If it's below 0.5, I'm diagnosing whether the problem is target-setting, execution, or capacity before I touch the targets. The score is information, not a performance grade — I use it to improve how we plan, not to reward or punish individuals."

## Common Scoring Debates

### "We hit 0.7 but in retrospect the KR was easy"
**Resolution**: Score it 0.7. Document the observation explicitly in the retrospective. Next quarter, push the baseline target higher. Flag the team lead if this happens two cycles in a row — it indicates a pattern of conservative commitment. Do not retroactively lower the score to signal difficulty; that corrupts the historical record.

---

### "We scored 0.5 but it wasn't our fault — a dependency team didn't deliver"
**Resolution**: Score it 0.5 against the original KR. Dependency failures are real and common — the retrospective is the right place to capture them. The fix is upstream: during planning, dependency risk must be reflected in the KR target or the KR must be restructured to measure only what the team controls. Granting a retroactive pass devalues the system and teaches teams to externalize accountability.

**Director framing**: "The 0.5 is accurate. What we learn from it is that this KR had uncontrolled dependency risk. Next quarter, we either own the dependency or we change the KR."

---

### "We changed the KR mid-quarter so we hit it"
**Resolution**: Score against the original KR only. Amended KRs are a fundamental integrity failure — the score becomes meaningless and future planning loses its calibration anchor. Mid-quarter amendments are allowed only in documented force-majeure situations (major incident response, org restructure, M&A). In all other cases: score the original, note the amendment, and treat it as a planning failure to be corrected.

**Director framing**: "If we're changing KRs to hit them, we've turned OKRs into a reporting exercise. The point is the learning, not the score. We'll score this 0.5 against the original and use the amendment as a planning lesson."

---

### "We scored 0.0 but delivered important work that wasn't in the OKR"
**Resolution**: Score it 0.0. Acknowledge the delivered work in the retrospective and recognize the team separately. This conversation is actually valuable — it reveals that the team's real work wasn't reflected in the OKRs, which is a planning problem. Use it as an input to next cycle's objective-setting.

## Scoring Quick-Reference Card

```
0.0  ──── Nothing delivered against this KR
0.3  ──── Effort was real; outcome gap is significant (>50% from target)
0.5  ──── Halfway there; partial value realized; root cause documentable
0.7  ──── THIS IS THE TARGET. Well-designed stretch OKR hits here.
1.0  ──── Full delivery + material overage. Investigate cause before celebrating.

Sweet spot:  Portfolio average 0.6–0.7
Red zone low:  < 0.4 portfolio average (capacity or calibration crisis)
Red zone high: > 0.85 portfolio average (sandbagging or wrong metrics)
```
