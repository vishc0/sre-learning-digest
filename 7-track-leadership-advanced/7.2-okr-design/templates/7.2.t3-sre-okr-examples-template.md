# SRE OKR Examples — Director-Level Template

**Audience**: Director/VP Platform Engineering interviews and team OKR design sessions.
**Scale**: T-Mobile-scale enterprise — millions of customers, 40–80 microservices, on-call rotation of 6–8 engineers, multiple product teams consuming platform services.

**How to read this document**: Each of the 10 OKRs includes the real version and the common mistake version side by side. The contrast is the lesson. The mistake versions are not invented — they are the versions that actually appear in OKR planning sessions and get challenged in VP reviews.

---

## Category 1: Toil Reduction

---

### OKR 1.1 — Reclaim Engineer Time from Repetitive Manual Work

**Objective**: Free our engineers from the manual operational overhead that consumes their week so they can work on problems that compound over time.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Toil as a percentage of total on-call engineer working hours per shift | 42% of on-call hours classified as toil (Google toil definition, sampled via quarterly time audit) | 20% of on-call hours | Measures time actually reclaimed, not whether automation was built. Automation that nobody trusts, or that still needs a human to trigger it, does not move this number. |
| KR2: Number of recurring operational tasks that complete end-to-end without human intervention (no manual step, no approval gate) | 11 of 74 documented runbooks fully automated | 38 of 74 runbooks | Measures automation coverage in production conditions — not scripts written or tickets closed. A script that exists but isn't wired to a trigger does not count. |
| KR3: Mean elapsed time for the five highest-frequency recurring tasks, timed and sampled at quarter start and quarter end | 52-minute average across the five tasks (stopwatch-timed sample, Q2) | 14-minute average | Measures speed improvement experienced by engineers — not whether a dashboard was built. If engineers are still spending 52 minutes, the automation isn't working or isn't trusted. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Automate toil across the SRE team.
>
> KR1: Implement an automation framework for runbook execution by end of Q3.
> KR2: Automate 5 runbooks from the toil audit list.
> KR3: Document all toil sources in a central registry.

**Why it fails**: Every KR is an activity completion flag. "Implement a framework" is a project milestone — the framework can exist and be unused. "Automate 5 runbooks" says nothing about whether those 5 runbooks mattered or whether engineers actually stopped doing the manual work. "Document toil sources" is data collection, not reduction. All three could be green at quarter end with toil percentage unchanged or higher.

---

### OKR 1.2 — Target the Highest-Cost Manual Work, Not All Manual Work Equally

**Objective**: Give engineers back meaningful time by eliminating the manual workflows with the highest hour burden per engineer per week — not toil in aggregate, but toil by ROI.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Hours per week across the full SRE team spent on manual ticket triage that an auto-classifier or routing rule could handle | 110 team-hours per week on triageable tickets (Jira time-tracking, Q2 average) | 25 team-hours per week | Measures reclaimed engineering hours, not whether a classifier was deployed. A classifier that misclassifies or is bypassed leaves the hours unchanged. |
| KR2: Percentage of capacity scaling events for tier-1 services that complete without a human executing or approving a step | 18% of scaling events completed without human touch (PagerDuty + deployment logs, Q2) | 75% of scaling events | Measures the outcome of auto-scaling being reliable enough that on-call engineers trust it without intervention. A feature that is enabled but overridden every time is not automation. |
| KR3: Number of P2 incidents per quarter caused by known failure patterns that have existing runbooks (repeat-class failures) | 28 repeat-class P2 incidents in Q2 | 6 per quarter | Measures prevention effectiveness — the highest-value form of toil elimination. If this doesn't move, the automation isn't catching what it was built for. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Reduce operational toil through automation and process improvement.
>
> KR1: Build and deploy ticket auto-classification system.
> KR2: Enable auto-scaling for all tier-1 services.
> KR3: Complete toil audit and prioritization exercise.

**Why it fails**: KR1 is a delivery milestone. KR2 is a configuration action. KR3 is a planning exercise. None of them say anything about whether engineers actually did less manual work. A Director reviewing this at end of quarter cannot tell if the team got better or just shipped features. The three bad KRs measure SRE output; the three good KRs measure engineer experience.

## Category 2: On-Call Health

---

### OKR 2.1 — Make On-Call Sustainable Before It Becomes a Retention Problem

**Objective**: Transform on-call from a rotation engineers dread into one they feel equipped and supported to carry — because burned-out on-call engineers leave, and replacements take 12 months to reach full effectiveness.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Mean actionable, non-duplicate alerts per on-call shift (7-day rolling window) — where "actionable" means the engineer made a decision, not just acknowledged and dismissed | 34 alerts per shift; 31% actionability rate (PagerDuty audit, last 8 weeks) | 8 actionable alerts per shift | Measures signal quality that engineers actually experience. Volume reduction alone can be gamed by suppressing useful alerts — requiring actionability ratio prevents that. |
| KR2: Percentage of engineers who report feeling adequately prepared for solo on-call before their first solo shift (pre-shift survey, anonymous, collected by EM) | 40% of engineers self-rate as "prepared" before first solo shift (Q2 survey, n=8) | 85% of engineers | Measures a human outcome — readiness and confidence — not whether a shadow shift policy was written or a training calendar was filled. Policy without confidence is noise. |
| KR3: Voluntary attrition citing on-call burden as a contributing factor, tracked via exit interview coding by HR | 3 engineers cited on-call load in exit interviews in the last 12 months | 0 in this quarter | Measures the highest-stakes outcome. This KR can only reach zero with sustained improvement across the other two — it validates the entire objective. A single exit for this reason is a 0.0. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Improve on-call experience for the SRE team.
>
> KR1: Reduce total alert volume by 40%.
> KR2: Implement shadow on-call program for all new engineers.
> KR3: Complete on-call runbook refresh for all tier-1 services.

**Why it fails**: KR1 measures volume without measuring usefulness — suppressing real alerts reduces volume and worsens on-call health simultaneously. KR2 is program implementation, not readiness. KR3 is a document refresh, not a measure of whether engineers can resolve incidents using those documents. All three could be green with on-call morale unchanged.

### OKR 2.2 — Build Deep Enough Knowledge That Any Engineer Can Handle Any Service

**Objective**: Eliminate the tribal knowledge bottleneck where only the service author can resolve production incidents — make every on-call engineer equally capable across the full service catalog.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Percentage of P1 and P2 incidents resolved by the on-call engineer without escalating to the service owner | 32% resolved without escalation (incident management system, last 2 quarters) | 70% resolved without escalation | Measures cross-service competency in practice — not training completion rates. If engineers are still escalating at 32%, the knowledge transfer isn't working regardless of what was written or presented. |
| KR2: Median time before first escalation attempt on P2 incidents — longer indicates deeper self-sufficient triage before calling for help | 7-minute median before first escalation (PagerDuty timeline data, Q2) | 28-minute median | Measures triage depth, not delay. An engineer who reaches 28 minutes before escalating is diagnosing, not stalling. An engineer at 7 minutes is reflexively escalating. The change is not one of policy but of capability. |
| KR3: Number of tier-1 services whose runbooks pass a "cold start" validation — a new team member resolves a staged incident using only the runbook, no verbal coaching, in under 45 minutes | 4 of 42 tier-1 services pass cold start test | 28 of 42 services | Measures runbook quality through adversarial testing, not whether runbooks were updated. A document that cannot be followed by an unfamiliar engineer is not a useful runbook, regardless of how recently it was revised. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Improve SRE on-call knowledge and documentation.
>
> KR1: Complete runbook review for all 42 tier-1 services.
> KR2: Hold quarterly on-call training sessions.
> KR3: Create on-call wiki with service ownership map.

**Why it fails**: All three KRs measure document and event creation, not competency. You can complete all three and still have engineers escalating every P2 within 7 minutes because the runbooks are unclear, the training didn't cover real failure modes, and nobody knows the wiki exists. Content creation and knowledge transfer are different things.

## Category 3: Error Budget Management

---

### OKR 3.1 — Turn Error Budget from a Dashboard Number into a Decision-Making Tool

**Objective**: Make error budget status an active input to engineering decisions — not a metric teams consult after an incident to explain why they missed SLO.

**Context for this OKR**: The goal is not to improve error budget consumption (that's an SLO constraint). The goal is to improve HOW teams use budget data to make release, freeze, and investment decisions. The outcome is behavior change, not dashboard coverage.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Percentage of feature-freeze and release-proceed decisions where the decision record (PR description, change ticket, or meeting notes) references current error budget status | 8% of release decisions reference budget status (tracked via decision log audit, Q2) | 80% of release decisions | Measures whether budget data is actually being consulted, not whether dashboards are accessible. A dashboard that is available but not referenced before release decisions is decorative infrastructure. |
| KR2: Number of post-incident reviews per quarter where the pre-incident error budget burn rate is cited as a contributing factor in the release decision narrative | 1 post-incident review cited budget data in the causal chain (Q2) | 8 post-incident reviews | Measures whether teams are doing the causal chain analysis: "we released during elevated burn, which contributed to blast radius." More citations mean the feedback loop is closing — teams are learning to connect budget state to decision risk. |
| KR3: Time from error budget crossing the 20%-remaining threshold to the engineering lead logging a documented acknowledgment (Slack, ticket, or incident record) | 4.5-hour median acknowledgment time (4 threshold events measured, last 2 quarters) | 12-minute median | Measures responsiveness of the budget governance process. A 4.5-hour gap means the alert fired and nothing happened. Alerts that go unacknowledged produce no decisions and no protection. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Manage error budgets effectively across all tier-1 services.
>
> KR1: Maintain error budget above 50% for all tier-1 services.
> KR2: Implement error budget dashboards for all services.
> KR3: Define error budget policies for all service owners.

**Why it fails**: KR1 is an SLO constraint disguised as an OKR — it says "don't let bad things happen," which is a floor, not an improvement direction. Maintaining a floor requires no new behavior. KR2 is infrastructure delivery. KR3 is policy creation. None of these measure whether teams changed how they make decisions. You can satisfy all three and have teams that never look at their budget before releasing.

---

### OKR 3.2 — Stop Burning Error Budget on Problems We Already Know How to Fix

**Objective**: Shift error budget consumption from known, preventable failure patterns toward novel failures we haven't encountered before — because spending budget on repeat problems means the learning loop is broken.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Percentage of total error budget consumed by failure modes previously documented in a postmortem (known-cause failures, tagged in incident system) | 74% of consumed budget came from known-cause failures (incident data, last 2 quarters) | 30% of consumed budget from known-cause failures | Measures whether previous learning is being applied. If postmortems are being completed but known-cause consumption doesn't drop, the action items from those postmortems are not being implemented. |
| KR2: Number of SLO burn rate alerts that converted to an incident record with a postmortem completed within 72 hours | 2 of 14 burn rate alerts triggered postmortem completion (Q2) | 14 of 14 burn rate alerts trigger postmortem completion | Measures the alert-to-learning conversion rate. Burn rate alerts that don't produce postmortems are early warning signals that are being ignored. Higher conversion means the team treats elevated burn as a learning event, not just an alert to acknowledge. |
| KR3: Median time from SLO breach detection to root cause category being tagged in the incident management system (enabling pattern analysis) | 52-hour median from detection to category tag (Q2 incident data) | 6-hour median | Measures speed of the learning loop. Fast categorization enables pattern detection across incidents and feeds prevention planning. Slow categorization means patterns remain invisible until they repeat. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Improve error budget management and SLO compliance.
>
> KR1: Keep all tier-1 SLOs above 99.9% for the quarter.
> KR2: Hold monthly error budget review meetings.
> KR3: Document error budget policy for all new services.

**Why it fails**: KR1 is an SLO floor. Maintaining it requires nothing new from the team. KR2 measures whether meetings happened, not whether they produced decisions that prevented repeat failures. KR3 is onboarding documentation, not prevention. You could satisfy all three and still burn 74% of budget on the same failure patterns every quarter.

## Category 4: Platform Maturity

---

### OKR 4.1 — Make Production Deployment Something Developers Do Without SRE Involvement

**Objective**: Move from a model where SRE teams are gatekeepers in the deployment pipeline to one where SRE teams design the guardrails and product teams ship through them independently — every day, without a ticket, without a Slack ping.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Percentage of production deployments completed without any SRE team member involved in any step — no approval, no Slack message, no manual gate | 38% of deploys complete without SRE involvement (deployment log audit, Q2) | 88% of deploys | Measures actual self-service adoption, not whether CI/CD is configured. If developers are still pinging SRE before every deploy, the platform is not self-service regardless of what tooling is technically available. |
| KR2: Median elapsed time from code merge to production deployment for a standard microservice with no feature flags, no database migration | 3.8-day median lead time (DORA metrics, CI/CD pipeline data, Q2) | 5-hour median | Measures delivery speed as a direct signal of pipeline efficiency and developer confidence. If approvals, queues, and manual gates are removed, lead time collapses. If it doesn't collapse, the constraint is still there — it's just invisible. |
| KR3: Deployment failure rate — percentage of deployments requiring rollback or emergency hotfix within 60 minutes of completion | 9% deployment failure rate (deployment + incident logs, Q2) | 1.5% failure rate | Measures deployment safety as an outcome. Canary, blue-green, and progressive rollout that developers don't understand or trust produce the same failure rate as no guardrails. The metric reveals whether the safety tooling is actually working. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Improve the deployment pipeline for all engineering teams.
>
> KR1: Implement CI/CD for 100% of tier-1 services.
> KR2: Enable canary deployments for all production services.
> KR3: Complete deployment runbook documentation.

**Why it fails**: All three are delivery milestones. "Implement CI/CD" means tooling exists, not that it's used or trusted. "Enable canary" means the feature is on, not that teams understand how to interpret canary analysis and halt a bad rollout. "Complete documentation" is content creation. At end of quarter, you can have 100% pipeline coverage and 90% of developers still asking SRE for deploy approvals.

---

### OKR 4.2 — Give Every Product Team the Observability to Own Their Services in Production

**Objective**: End the pattern where SRE is the only team that can interpret production signals — make observability so accessible and actionable that product teams diagnose and own their own incidents without SRE as interpreter.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Percentage of tier-1 services with SLOs defined, instrumented, reviewed monthly by the owning product team (not SRE), with evidence of review (meeting notes or dashboard comment) within the last 30 days | 18% of tier-1 services meet all three criteria (SLO defined + instrumented + product team reviewed) | 90% of tier-1 services | Measures ownership adoption by product teams — not whether SRE built dashboards for them. A dashboard that SRE built and only SRE reviews is not self-service observability. The review evidence requirement prevents the passive dashboard problem. |
| KR2: Percentage of P2+ incidents where the service-owning product team had access to relevant traces, logs, and metrics without requesting SRE help at the time of incident | 28% of P2+ incidents — product team accessed their own data without SRE (incident records, Q2) | 88% of P2+ incidents | Measures observability usefulness at the moment it matters most. Access during business hours in a dashboard demo is irrelevant. Access during a 2am P2 is the test. |
| KR3: Median time for a new product team to instrument a service, define an SLO, and publish a functioning alerting dashboard using platform tooling without SRE assistance | 3.5 weeks (measured from 4 onboardings in Q2) | 1 business day | Measures platform accessibility through real developer experience. If it takes 3.5 weeks, the platform requires SRE handholding regardless of what features are technically present. The 1-day target is the definition of self-service. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Expand observability coverage across the platform.
>
> KR1: Deploy monitoring tooling to 100% of tier-1 services.
> KR2: Create SLO dashboards for all 42 tier-1 services.
> KR3: Train all product teams on observability tooling.

**Why it fails**: KR1 is tooling deployment. KR2 is dashboard creation by SRE for other teams — which is the opposite of self-service. KR3 is training attendance, not adoption. All three could be green with product teams still unable to diagnose their own incidents and still calling SRE first for every production issue.

## Category 5: Security Posture

---

### OKR 5.1 — Close the Window Between When Vulnerabilities Appear and When We Stop Being Exposed

**Objective**: Compress the exposure window for critical vulnerabilities from weeks to days — because attackers don't wait for quarterly patch cycles, and "we're working on it" is not an acceptable state for a CVE that's been public for three weeks.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Mean time from critical CVE publication (NVD timestamp) to remediated and deployed patch in production for all affected services | 24-day median MTTR for critical CVEs (Prisma Cloud + deployment log correlation, Q2) | 5-day median | Measures end-to-end remediation speed — not whether a patching process document was written. The clock starts at NVD publish, not at internal ticket creation. That distinction alone exposes 3–5 days of detection lag most teams don't account for. |
| KR2: Percentage of container images running in production that pass current vulnerability scan with zero critical findings at the weekly spot check | 31% of production images pass with zero critical findings (Prisma Cloud weekly scan, current) | 92% of images | Measures actual exposure state, not whether scanning is enabled. Scanning without remediation enforcement produces a log of findings, not a reduction in risk. |
| KR3: Percentage of secret rotation operations (API keys, service account credentials, TLS certs) that complete automatically without a human-initiated trigger | 8% of rotation events complete automatically (Vault audit log, current) | 78% of rotation events | Measures security automation maturity, not whether a rotation policy exists. At enterprise scale, manual rotation fails silently when engineers are busy with incidents — which is precisely when attackers probe for stale credentials. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Improve the platform security posture.
>
> KR1: Implement vulnerability scanning for all container images.
> KR2: Document secret rotation policy for all services.
> KR3: Complete annual security review of all tier-1 services.

**Why it fails**: KR1 is scanner deployment — findings without remediation enforcement change nothing about exposure. KR2 is documentation — a policy that says "rotate every 90 days" doesn't rotate secrets when engineers are occupied. KR3 is a periodic review event — an annual review does not protect against a CVE published in week 3 of the quarter. All three produce artifacts, not risk reduction.

---

### OKR 5.2 — Build Security into the Pipeline So Flaws Are Found Before They Reach Customers

**Objective**: Shift from finding security issues in production — after customers are exposed — to finding them in the pipeline, where the blast radius is a blocked deployment instead of a customer-impacting incident.

| KR | Baseline | Target | Why It's an Outcome, Not an Output |
|----|----------|--------|------------------------------------|
| KR1: Percentage of deployments blocked by an automated security gate (SAST, container vulnerability scan, or secrets detection) before reaching the staging environment — higher is better, indicates real early catches | 4% of deployments blocked by a security gate pre-staging (CI/CD pipeline data, Q2) | 42% of deployments | A higher number means the pipeline is catching real issues early. If this number is near zero, the gates exist but are in report-only mode, are misconfigured, or are being bypassed. The target of 42% is based on baseline finding rates — it is not artificially high. |
| KR2: Number of findings from the quarterly external penetration test that were already logged in the internal security backlog before the pentest team reported them | 2 of 18 pentest findings were already in the internal backlog (Q2 pentest report) | 15 of 18 pentest findings | Measures internal security awareness — whether internal tooling surfaces what external adversaries find. The gap between internal and external findings is the blind spot that represents real risk. A mature security posture closes this gap. |
| KR3: Median time from a security finding raised in code review (as a PR comment) to the remediation commit being merged and deployed | 21-day median (code review tag → merge, sampled from 30 findings in Q2) | 3-day median | Measures security response culture, not whether code review guidelines mention security. A 21-day lag signals that security findings are treated as optional feedback, not blocking issues. The 3-day target requires organizational behavior change, not just process documentation. |

**Common Mistake Version**:

> **Objective**: Strengthen application security through improved processes.
>
> KR1: Integrate SAST scanning into CI/CD pipeline for all repositories.
> KR2: Complete security training for all engineers by end of Q3.
> KR3: Achieve SOC 2 Type II compliance renewal.

**Why it fails**: KR1 measures integration, not effectiveness — a SAST tool running in report-only mode with no enforcement gate produces reports, not security. KR2 measures training completion, not security behavior — engineers can attend training and commit hardcoded secrets the same afternoon. KR3 is a compliance audit, which is a contractual obligation and a periodic event, not an improvement trajectory. All three could be green while the penetration test still finds 16 issues that internal tooling missed.

## OKR Self-Review Checklist

Run every KR through this before presenting to leadership or publishing to the team.

| Test | Question | Red Flag Signal |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| Outcome vs. output | Does this measure a change in the world, or a task completed? | "Implement," "deploy," "build," "document," "train," "complete" as the primary verb |
| Baseline exists | Is the starting point measured today from a named system? | "Estimated," "approximately," "TBD," or no baseline at all |
| Data source named | Is the system that produces the score identified and accessible to the team? | "We will track this," "to be determined," or no source listed |
| Single metric | Does this KR measure exactly one thing? | "And" connecting two distinct outcomes in one KR |
| Influence control | Can the team move this without requiring another team's decision? | Outcome depends on a team that doesn't report to this leader |
| Gaming resistance | Can a team hit this KR while the underlying objective fails? | If yes, close the gap — measure the downstream outcome, not the upstream input |
| Stretch calibration | Would 50–85% confidence be realistic at quarter start? | >90% (sandbagged) or <35% (disconnected from reality) |
| Layperson readable | Can a VP of Product tell if this was hit without a glossary? | Internal acronyms, unexplained tech terms, or undefined measurement events |
