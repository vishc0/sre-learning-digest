# Chapter 6: Writing OKRs for SRE Teams

## Bottom Line First

SRE leaders write the worst OKRs in the organization — not because they lack rigor, but because they apply SRE rigor to the wrong problem. They write SLOs and call them OKRs. They confuse "keeping the lights on" with "making things meaningfully better." The central discipline of SRE OKR design is this: **reliability is a constraint, not a goal.** Once you internalize that distinction, the right OKR structure becomes obvious.

---

## The Central Challenge: Constraint vs. Goal

Your SLO — "API gateway p99 latency under 200ms" — is a floor, not a destination. It defines the minimum acceptable state of your platform. Maintaining it is table stakes. Writing it as an OKR implies that the goal is to stay where you already are, which is not a meaningful organizational commitment.

The confusion is understandable. SRE work is largely about sustaining reliability, and reliability metrics are the most visible outputs of SRE investment. But OKRs are about change — measurable movement from a current state to a better one. If your platform is already meeting its SLOs, "maintain SLOs" is not an OKR. It is an operational responsibility. It belongs in your SLO documentation, your error budget policy, and your on-call runbooks — not your quarterly OKR set.

### The Diagnostic Question

Before writing any KR, ask: **"If this KR is green at end of quarter, does that mean something meaningfully improved for the engineers who depend on our platform — or does it just mean nothing got worse?"**

If the answer is "nothing got worse," it is a constraint, not an OKR.

---

## The Two Wrong Templates

### Constraint OKR (Wrong)
> **KR**: Maintain p99 API gateway latency below 200ms throughout Q3.

This is a health metric. It describes a state to preserve. There is no directional movement here. Scoring it at 1.0 tells you the platform held — not that the platform improved.

### Outcome OKR (Right)
> **KR**: Reduce the number of services breaching their latency SLO from 14 to 7 by end of Q3.

Now there is a direction: from 14 to 7. A current state, a target state, a measurable delta. If you hit this, something demonstrably improved. If you miss it, you know exactly how far you fell short and can diagnose why.

The reframe is almost always the same: from "maintain X" to "reduce / increase / eliminate / accelerate X."

## Five SRE OKR Categories That Work

### 1. Toil Reduction

Toil is the most tractable SRE OKR category because it is inherently directional — toil is either going up or going down. The baseline is measurable (hours per sprint, ticket volume, manual deployment steps), the target is clear, and the outcome directly improves engineer quality of life and capacity.

**Good KR**: Reduce manual provisioning steps per service onboarding from 23 to 6 by implementing self-service infrastructure templates.

**Bad KR**: "Automate the provisioning process." (This is an activity, not an outcome. The provisioning process can be automated and still require 23 manual steps post-automation.)

**Trap**: writing toil-producing OKRs. If your OKR requires significant manual tracking, reporting, or process work to measure, you have created toil in the act of measuring toil reduction.

### 2. On-Call Health

On-call fatigue is a retention and reliability risk that most Directors underinvest in measuring. OKRs in this category make the investment legible to leadership above you and create accountability for improving conditions for your team.

**Good KR**: Reduce after-hours pages per engineer per month from 8 to 3 by implementing improved alert deduplication and severity routing.

**Good KR**: Increase the percentage of P3/P4 alerts that resolve via automated remediation from 31% to 60%.

**Bad KR**: "Improve our alerting." (Unmeasurable. Cannot be scored.)

**Trap**: writing KRs that optimize the metric without improving the underlying condition. Alert suppression that hides real issues is not on-call health improvement — it is a reliability time bomb. KRs in this category should always have a paired health check (e.g., no increase in MTTR while reducing page volume).

### 3. Platform Maturity and Self-Service

Director-level SRE teams own platform capability, not just platform uptime. Self-service capability is the multiplier — every team that can provision, deploy, and observe their own services without opening a ticket to yours is engineering capacity recovered.

**Good KR**: Increase the percentage of new service deployments completed without SRE team involvement from 40% to 75%.

**Good KR**: Reduce mean time for a new team to reach production-ready observability from 3 weeks to 4 days.

**Bad KR**: "Launch the internal developer portal." (Project milestone, not an outcome. The portal can launch and adoption can be zero.)

**Trap**: confusing delivery with adoption. Shipping a platform feature is an activity. Engineers using it at scale is the outcome. KRs should measure adoption and impact, not delivery.

### 4. Error Budget Management

Error budget is one of the most powerful SRE concepts — and one of the most underused as an OKR lever. Most teams track error budget but few make improving how teams *use* error budget a strategic goal.

**Good KR**: Increase the percentage of product teams actively using error budget data in sprint planning decisions from 2 of 12 teams to 9 of 12 teams.

**Good KR**: Reduce average time from error budget breach to published post-mortem from 18 days to 5 days.

**Bad KR**: "Maintain 99.9% availability across all platform services." (This is an SLO, not an OKR. See above.)

**Trap**: teams that have burned their error budget write OKRs about "restoring" reliability — which again is a constraint framing. The right OKR is about the structural change that prevents the pattern: improved change management, better canary deployment coverage, faster rollback tooling.

### 5. Security Posture

Security remediation velocity is a measurable, directional outcome that SRE Directors often own or co-own with security teams. It translates well to OKRs because it has a clear baseline (current backlog age or vulnerability count) and a defensible target.

**Good KR**: Reduce mean time to remediate critical CVEs in platform infrastructure from 47 days to 10 days.

**Good KR**: Increase the percentage of production workloads with automated secrets rotation from 15% to 80%.

**Bad KR**: "Improve our security posture." (Direction without measurement is a slogan.)

**Trap**: writing KRs that measure scanning coverage without measuring remediation. Knowing about more vulnerabilities is not the same as fixing them. Discovery OKRs without remediation OKRs produce a growing backlog and the illusion of progress.

---

## Common SRE OKR Traps — Summary

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|------|-------------------|--------------|
| SLO as OKR | "Maintain 99.9% availability" | Describes a floor, not a destination |
| Project milestone as KR | "Launch the observability platform" | Delivery without adoption is not an outcome |
| Activity as KR | "Automate deployment pipeline" | Can be complete even if nothing improved |
| Unmeasurable KR | "Improve developer experience" | Cannot be scored honestly |
| Toil-producing OKR | KRs requiring heavy manual tracking | Creates the problem you are trying to solve |

---

## The Test for a Valid SRE OKR

A KR is valid if you can answer all three of these:

1. **What is the current state?** (Baseline — actual number, not "unknown")
2. **What is the target state?** (Specific number, not "better")
3. **If we hit this, what meaningfully improved for the people who depend on our platform?**

If you cannot answer question 3 with something specific — engineer hours recovered, deployment confidence increased, on-call load reduced, self-service rate increased — you are writing a maintenance metric, not an OKR.
