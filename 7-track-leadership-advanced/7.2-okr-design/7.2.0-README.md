# OKR Design for Platform Engineering Directors

## Why This Framework Exists

OKRs are the most widely misimplemented performance framework in tech. Not because they are complicated — the structure is four sentences. Because most leaders use them as a rebranded to-do list, measure activity instead of change, and confuse alignment theater with actual organizational coherence.

The result is a quarterly ritual where teams paste their existing work into a template, executives nod, and nothing changes about how decisions get made or how progress gets measured. That is not an OKR problem. That is a leadership problem.

A Director who designs OKRs correctly uses them as a translation layer — converting company strategy into platform direction, and platform direction into team accountability. A Director who gets it wrong runs a bureaucratic check-the-box exercise that burns trust and produces no signal.

## What Distinguishes the Two

The Director who designs OKRs correctly:
- Writes Objectives that describe a changed state of the world, not a project to execute
- Writes Key Results that are falsifiable — unambiguously hit or missed at quarter end
- Accepts 70% completion as a signal of ambition, not failure
- Cascades downward by alignment, not decomposition — teams own their own OKRs
- Uses OKRs as a forcing function for prioritization conversations, not a reporting surface

The Director who turns OKRs into bureaucratic theater:
- Writes Objectives that sound strategic but are too vague to act on
- Writes Key Results that are tasks ("Launch the observability platform")
- Sets targets they know the team will hit 100% of the time — sandbagging
- Treats the quarterly review as a grading exercise instead of a calibration
- Runs OKRs in parallel with actual planning rather than integrating them

This is a five-part preparation guide built for an SRE Manager targeting Director/VP Platform Engineering interviews. It is sequenced as a learning progression, not a reference index.

## How to Use This

Read sequentially. Each chapter builds on the one before. After each chapter, practice writing one real OKR for your current or most recent team. Do not write hypothetical OKRs — use real problems you have already lived.

The interview test is not whether you know the theory. It is whether you can produce a credible OKR in the room, explain why you wrote it that way, and defend its Key Results when the interviewer challenges whether you would actually hit them.

The practitioner test is whether your team, reading your Objectives, can tell what the quarter is actually for — without asking you.
