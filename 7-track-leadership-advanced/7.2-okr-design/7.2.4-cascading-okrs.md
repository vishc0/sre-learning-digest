# Chapter 4: Cascading OKRs

## Bottom Line First

Cascading is where OKRs either become an alignment tool or collapse into a reporting hierarchy. The mechanics are straightforward — company sets direction, department translates it, teams own their contribution. The failure modes are not mechanical, they are political. Infrastructure and platform teams operate in a structural misalignment: most company OKRs are product-growth objectives, and a reliability platform does not appear in them by name. The Director of Platform Engineering has to advocate for the visibility of infrastructure OKRs, or the platform team spends the quarter executing work that the company does not formally recognize as strategic.

Understanding how to navigate that is a Director-level skill.

---

## The Alignment Chain

Company-level OKRs express the strategic bets for the year or quarter — what the business is trying to change at the largest scale. Department-level OKRs translate those bets into what a specific function must do to enable them. Team-level OKRs translate the department direction into what a specific group of engineers will drive in the quarter.

```
Company OKR
  └── "Grow enterprise customer revenue 40% by expanding product reliability commitments"
        │
        ├── Product OKR: "Ship three enterprise-tier features with contractual SLA guarantees"
        │
        └── Platform OKR: "Establish the reliability infrastructure that makes contractual SLAs
                           operationally defensible — and prove it with data"
                               │
                               ├── Observability Team OKR: "Give every Tier 1 service real-time
                               │    SLA tracking visible to engineering and account teams"
                               │
                               └── Incident Response Team OKR: "Reduce MTTR for SLA-covered
                                    services to under 15 minutes, verified across 10 incidents"
```

Each level in this chain can answer: "Why does this OKR exist?" and trace the answer up to a company objective. That traceability is what cascading means. It is not decomposition — Platform Engineering did not cut the company objective into smaller pieces. It identified what must be true in infrastructure for the company objective to be achievable.

---

## The Director's Role: Translation, Not Decomposition

The most important distinction in cascading OKRs is the difference between translation and decomposition.

**Decomposition** means cutting a parent objective into smaller pieces and assigning them to teams. This is how many managers implement cascading — it feels organized, but it eliminates team ownership and creates a compliance dynamic: teams are executing assignments, not pursuing their own objectives.

**Translation** means reading the company and department objectives, then asking: "What must become true in my domain for those objectives to be achievable?" The answer is a department-level OKR that is meaningfully different from the company objective — it is your domain's contribution, not a slice of the company's goal.

At the Director level, you are the translation layer between company strategy and platform execution:

| What you receive | What you produce |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Company: grow enterprise revenue 40% | Platform: make SLA commitments operationally defensible |
| Company: reduce time-to-market by 30% | Platform: eliminate infrastructure as a bottleneck for new service launch |
| Company: improve customer retention in core markets | Platform: ensure product teams can respond to customer issues within 24 hours regardless of service complexity |

Each translation preserves the company intent while specifying what platform engineering's distinct contribution must be.

---

## The Alignment Check

Before finalizing any department or team OKR, run the alignment check:

**For each OKR, answer**: "Which company OKR does this contribute to, and how?"

If you cannot answer that in one sentence, the OKR is either not aligned (eliminate it or rewrite it) or is covering work that the company OKR framework does not currently value (see the infrastructure exemption below).

The alignment check also runs in reverse — for each company OKR, ask: "Does platform engineering have an OKR that contributes to this? If not, should it?" Missing contributions signal either that the company objective does not require platform work (valid) or that platform work is invisible in the OKR structure (a problem to fix).

This check is a forcing function for the conversation a Director should have with the executive team every quarter: "Here is how platform engineering's work connects to the company's strategic bets. Here is where we are not connected and why."

## The Infrastructure Exemption Problem

Here is the real problem that most OKR frameworks do not address: when a company's strategic objectives are entirely product-growth-oriented, infrastructure and reliability teams have no natural home in the cascade.

If every company OKR is "grow revenue," "expand market share," or "launch three new product lines," a platform team writing OKRs in alignment with these will either:
- Write fake alignment ("our observability platform enables faster feature delivery which drives revenue") that is technically true but so indirect it is meaningless
- Write reliability OKRs that do not connect to any company objective and therefore appear strategic-orphaned in the quarterly review
- Ignore OKRs entirely and just do the work — which makes the team invisible at the leadership level

None of these outcomes serve the Director or the team.

### The infrastructure exemption pattern

The infrastructure exemption is the practice of advocating for a separate category of OKR at the department level that represents foundational work — work that must be done to keep the platform viable, regardless of whether the company's growth OKRs explicitly call for it.

The argument to make to executive leadership is this: "Product teams can only ship against your growth objectives if the platform is reliable enough to be trusted. That reliability does not happen passively. It requires investment in specific outcomes this quarter. Here are the OKRs that represent that investment — they do not map directly to a company OKR, but they are prerequisites for the company OKRs to be achievable."

This is a Director-level conversation. An SRE Manager waits to be told what to measure. A Director of Platform Engineering makes the case for what must be measured and why.

### Framing the exemption OKR correctly

The infrastructure exemption OKR should still connect to business outcomes — it just does so indirectly. The framing is: "We are building the reliability floor that everything else stands on."

**Example infrastructure exemption Objective**:
"Ensure our platform can absorb the growth the business is planning without service degradation or engineering team burnout."

**Key Results**:
- KR1: Achieve zero Tier 1 SLA breaches in Q3, measured against the new enterprise SLA thresholds
- KR2: Reduce on-call burden per engineer to under 3 hours/week — below the level that drives attrition
- KR3: Demonstrate the platform can support 2x current traffic volume through load testing before the planned growth campaign in Week 8
- KR4: Reduce manual provisioning work to zero for new service onboarding — automated end to end

This OKR does not map to a single company objective. It maps to the implicit precondition underlying all of them: that the platform will not fall apart when the growth arrives.

## SAFe Analogy: The Director as ART-Level Coordinator

In SAFe, PI Planning operates across two levels: the Agile Release Train (ART) and the teams within it. The ART sets Program-level PI Objectives — directional commitments for the entire release train. Each team sets Team-level PI Objectives that contribute to the ART commitments. The Release Train Engineer (RTE) and Product Management own the ART-level view and the cross-team alignment.

The Director of Platform Engineering in an OKR model plays an equivalent role to the ART-level leadership:

| SAFe Construct | OKR Equivalent | Who Owns It |
|----------------|----------------|-------------|
| Program PI Objectives | Department OKRs | Director |
| Team PI Objectives | Team OKRs | Team leads / Managers |
| Program Board (dependency view) | OKR alignment check (which team KRs enable which department KRs) | Director |
| ART sync / Scrum of Scrums | OKR mid-quarter check-ins | Director |
| PI retrospective | OKR retrospective and scoring | Director |

The PI Planning analogy is useful in interviews because it frames OKR cascading as a coordination and alignment problem — not just a reporting structure. In PI Planning, the RTE does not assign team objectives; they create the conditions for teams to write their own objectives in alignment with program intent. That is exactly what cascading OKRs requires of a Director.

The Director who gets cascading right creates the conditions — clear department Objectives, shared context on company strategy, explicit alignment check — and then gives teams the room to write their own Key Results. The Director who gets it wrong assigns Key Results to teams and calls it cascading.

## Cross-Team OKR Dependencies

When multiple teams under a Director have OKRs that depend on each other's work, those dependencies must be made explicit — exactly as they appear on a Program Board in PI Planning.

**Example dependency chain**:

- Observability Team KR: "Deploy real-time SLA dashboards for all Tier 1 services by Week 4"
- Incident Response Team KR: "Reduce MTTR for Tier 1 services to under 15 minutes"

The Incident Response KR depends on the Observability KR — faster MTTR requires the dashboards to exist. If the Observability KR slips to Week 8, the Incident Response KR is at risk regardless of what Incident Response does.

The Director's job is to surface these dependencies in the OKR planning session, record them explicitly, and treat them as a risk to manage throughout the quarter — not discover them at the mid-quarter check-in when it is too late to recover.

This is why OKR planning cannot happen asynchronously in a spreadsheet. It requires the same kind of face-to-face alignment session that PI Planning provides — even if shorter — where teams can see each other's draft OKRs and flag where they depend on each other's delivery.
