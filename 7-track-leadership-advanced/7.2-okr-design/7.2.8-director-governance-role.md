# Chapter 8: The Director's Governance Role in OKRs

## Bottom Line First

The Director's role in an OKR program is not to write the best OKRs in the organization. It is to build a system where good OKRs emerge naturally from the people closest to the work, align coherently with department strategy, and are reviewed with enough rigor and frequency to course-correct before the quarter is lost. The Director governs the program — sets the frame, holds the cadence, ensures alignment, and models the behaviors the program depends on.

---

## The Two-Level OKR Structure

Directors operate at a structural midpoint. They receive Objectives from VP-level leadership (or generate them in response to company-level OKRs) and translate those into department-level OKRs. Senior Managers then write team-level OKRs in response to the department frame.

This creates a two-level structure the Director owns:

```
VP / Company OKRs
        ↓
Director writes: Department OKRs (2–3 Objectives, up to 3 KRs each)
        ↓
Senior Managers write: Team OKRs (2–3 Objectives, up to 3 KRs each)
        ↓
Director reviews and approves for alignment
```

The Director's department OKRs should not be a sum of every team's work. They should represent the department's strategic commitments — the outcomes that, if achieved, demonstrate the department is delivering its purpose. Team OKRs then exist to produce those outcomes, not to describe all team activity.

**The alignment test**: every team KR should be traceable to at least one department KR. A team OKR that cannot be connected to any department Objective is either pointing to a gap in the department OKRs (the Director missed something) or is a maintenance responsibility that should be named as such rather than written as an OKR.

---

## Writing Department-Level OKRs

Department OKRs at the Director level carry a different expectation than team-level OKRs. They need to be legible to VP-level leadership — which means the Objectives must connect clearly to business outcomes, not just engineering metrics.

An Objective that reads "Improve platform observability" is adequate at the team level. At the department level, the framing needs to carry strategic weight: "Enable product teams to self-detect and self-remediate service degradations before customer impact."

The KRs for a department OKR are often leading indicators — metrics that will eventually show up in business outcomes — rather than the business outcomes themselves. That is acceptable, as long as the causal chain is explicit: "If we achieve KR1 and KR2, the impact on X will be visible by end of next quarter." Name the assumption. Do not hide the dependency.

## The Director's Review Cadence

The Director governs the OKR program through three distinct review rhythms. Collapsing them into one meeting defeats the purpose of each.

### Weekly 1:1s with Senior Managers

This is not an OKR review. It is an early warning system. The question is not "where are we on the OKRs?" — it is "is anything happening right now that will affect our OKRs?" Blockers, dependency gaps, team capacity changes, strategic shifts — these surface in 1:1s weeks before they show up in a formal OKR score. The Director's job is to hear them early and remove them before they become mid-quarter problems.

One OKR-specific question that belongs in every 1:1: "Which KR are you most worried about right now and why?" This surfaces sandbagging, stretch theater, and execution drift before they compound.

### Monthly OKR Review with the Full Management Team

This is the substantive governance meeting. All Senior Managers present their current OKR status (not a status deck — a 5-minute verbal update per team, current score, trajectory, blockers). The Director's role in this meeting is not to receive information — it is to identify cross-team dependencies that are creating drag, make resource reallocation decisions in the room, and model the learning-over-reporting culture by asking "what have we learned?" rather than "why are we behind?"

Format matters. The worst version of this meeting is a round-robin of score updates with no discussion. The best version is a discussion led by the two or three KRs most at risk, with the Director actively problem-solving in the room.

### Quarterly Scoring and Reflect Session

The quarterly session has two parts: scoring and reflection. The scoring portion (2–3 hours) is where each team presents their final scores with honest narratives. The Director should explicitly model honest scoring by presenting their own department OKR scores first — including any scores below 0.7 — with a candid explanation of what drove the outcome. This sets the tone for the team sessions that follow.

The reflection portion (60–90 minutes, ideally the following week) is where the management team extracts learnings: what OKRs to carry forward, what to retire, and what the next quarter's OKRs should prioritize differently.

## OKRs and Performance Reviews: The Right Model

The right model is explicit separation with informed judgment.

OKR scores inform performance conversations — they are one data point among many, not a formula. A Senior Manager who scores 0.6 on a genuinely hard OKR while demonstrating strong team development, clear communication, and sound engineering judgment is performing well. A Senior Manager who scores 1.0 by consistently sandbagging targets while avoiding the hard organizational problems is a performance concern.

**What the Director says explicitly, at every cycle**: "I review OKR scores as context for understanding your quarter, not as a performance rating. A 0.7 on a hard goal is a better signal than a 1.0 on a safe one. I want honest scores." Then enforce this by giving positive performance feedback to leaders who score honestly and stretch ambitiously, regardless of whether they hit their targets.

**The wrong model**: any system where OKR scores map mechanically to performance ratings. If Senior Managers know their score will appear in their annual review as a performance metric, they will optimize the score, not the outcome. The OKR system loses its information value within two quarters.

## OKRs in SAFe Delivery Context

For Directors with a SAFe background, the OKR cadence maps cleanly to the PI planning model — which makes it a powerful anchor for both internal team communication and interview answers.

| OKR Phase | SAFe Equivalent | What it Means |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Set (Weeks 1–2) | PI Planning | Align intent, set commitments, expose dependencies |
| Check-in (Month 2) | System Demo review point | Inspect what is actually shipping, recalibrate |
| Score (Week 12–13) | PI retrospective inputs | Honest accounting of what was delivered |
| Reflect (Between quarters) | PI retrospective | Extract learnings before next PI Planning |

The Director in a SAFe context functions as the ART-level coordinator for OKR alignment. Just as an ART sync ensures teams are coordinating across sprint boundaries, the Director's monthly OKR review ensures teams are coordinating across OKR boundaries — catching shared dependencies, shared blockers, and shared KRs that require cross-team execution.

This framing is useful in interviews. It shows that you understand OKRs not as a standalone tool but as a governance mechanism that operates within a larger delivery system.

## The Interview Question: "How Do You Use OKRs?"

This question is asking two things simultaneously: can you operate at the strategic level (set direction, translate company goals into team commitments), and can you govern with enough rigor to make OKRs real rather than performative?

A Director-level answer has four elements:

**1. Frame your level of operation**: "At the Director level, I own department OKRs and review team OKRs for alignment. I'm not writing sprint tickets — I'm setting the strategic frame that my Senior Managers execute against."

**2. Describe the cadence you run**: "I run a quarterly cycle: Set in weeks 1–2 with my management team, a mid-quarter learning review in month 2, scoring in the final week, and a structured reflect session between quarters. The cadence is what makes OKRs real — without it, you have a document, not a program."

**3. Show you understand the failure modes**: "The two things I watch for: teams writing SLOs as OKRs — treating reliability maintenance as a goal rather than a constraint — and score-gaming when OKRs get too close to performance reviews. I separate those explicitly and model honest scoring myself."

**4. Connect to outcomes**: "The signal I track is whether OKRs change behavior in sprint planning. If the quarterly OKR and the Monday sprint planning are disconnected, the OKR is a reporting exercise. I know the program is working when engineers reference OKRs to prioritize trade-offs, not just to fill out a tracker."

This answer demonstrates strategic clarity, operational discipline, and the judgment to distinguish a well-functioning OKR program from a performative one. That is the Director-level answer.

## Governance Checklist — Director's Quarterly Responsibilities

```markdown
### Pre-Quarter (Set Phase)
- [ ] Draft 2–3 department Objectives with draft KRs
- [ ] Share with management team for stress-test and alignment review
- [ ] Review and approve team OKRs for traceability to department OKRs
- [ ] Publish all OKRs by end of week 2

### Mid-Quarter (Check-in)
- [ ] Run monthly OKR review with full management team
- [ ] Identify and unblock KRs scoring below 0.4
- [ ] Adjust or retire KRs where context has materially changed
- [ ] Reference OKRs in weekly 1:1s with Senior Managers

### End of Quarter (Score)
- [ ] Present Director-level OKR scores with honest narrative
- [ ] Receive and validate team OKR scores
- [ ] Document blockers and learnings per Objective

### Between Quarters (Reflect)
- [ ] Run reflect session: carry, retire, and learn
- [ ] Identify OKR program health issues (sandbagging, fatigue, shell)
- [ ] Feed learnings into next Set phase
```

The Director who runs this cycle with consistency — who references OKRs in 1:1s, who models honest scoring, who separates OKRs from performance ratings, and who intervenes when failure modes emerge — builds a program that teams trust. That trust is the asset. The scores are just the instrument.
