# OKR Misalignment Intervention Playbook
## Director / VP Platform Engineering — Operational Reference

**Purpose**: Detect when team OKRs do not connect to company direction, intervene before quarter locks, and resolve with the team or escalate with evidence  
**When to use**: During or after quarterly OKR planning, before VP review, or mid-quarter if company OKRs shift  
**Time investment**: Detection takes 30 minutes; intervention conversation takes 45–60 minutes per team

---

## Part 1: Detection — The Alignment Trace Test

Run this test on every team's OKR set before the VP review meeting. It takes less than 30 minutes across a typical Platform/SRE org.

### The Trace Test

For each team Objective, complete this table:

```
Team Objective: [exact Objective text]
↓
Maps to Company OKR: [company OKR name or number]
↓
The connection is: [one sentence — how does achieving this team Objective move the company OKR?]
↓
Connection type: [ ] Direct (we are the primary owner)
                 [ ] Enable (we unblock another team who owns it)
                 [ ] Support (we contribute partial effort)
                 [ ] No connection found
```

An Objective with "No connection found" is a misalignment flag. It does not mean the work is wrong — it means it is either:
- Infrastructure foundational work that needs an exemption rationale (see Part 4), or
- Genuinely disconnected from company direction and should be cut or reshaped

### Misalignment Signals to Watch For

- Team OKRs that are entirely inward-facing: reliability metrics, internal tooling, team process improvement — with no stated impact on a company outcome
- Team OKRs that are holdovers from last quarter with no re-evaluation against new company OKRs
- Objectives that describe work the team wants to do rather than outcomes the company needs
- Key Results that are entirely activity-based (deploys, migrations, implementations) with no outcome measurement tied to a company priority

### Misalignment Is Not Always the Team's Fault

Before treating misalignment as a team problem, investigate the source:

| Root Cause | Diagnosis | Responsibility |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| Company OKRs are vague or product-only | Platform and infrastructure teams have no obvious hook | Company OKR authors; Director should advocate upward |
| Team was not included in company OKR development | They wrote OKRs in a vacuum | Director's process gap — fix the planning process |
| Team inertia — they wrote what they did last quarter | Team did not re-evaluate against new company priorities | Team lead issue; Director intervention needed |
| Director did not distribute company OKRs before planning | Team had no context | Director's gap — own it and correct quickly |

## Part 2: Root Causes of Misalignment

Understanding the cause determines which intervention to use.

### Root Cause 1: Unclear Company OKRs

Company OKRs are written at a product or revenue level that leaves no obvious entry point for infrastructure teams. Example: "Increase premium subscriber NPS by 8 points" — what does Platform Engineering own in that?

**Intervention needed**: Translate company OKRs into platform-relevant outcomes before the team planning session. Director does this work, not the team leads.

Example translation:
> Company OKR: "Increase premium subscriber NPS by 8 points"  
> Platform interpretation: "Reduce API error rate to <0.1% and p99 latency to <200ms on subscriber-facing services — because performance degrades NPS directly"

### Root Cause 2: Infrastructure Teams Left Out of Company OKR Development

Company OKRs were written by product and business leadership with no SRE or Platform Engineering input. The OKR set describes what to build and sell, but not what it takes to run it reliably.

**Intervention needed**: Make the infrastructure exemption case (see Part 4). Document it formally. Advocate upward to be included in next cycle's company OKR development.

### Root Cause 3: Team Inertia

The team wrote OKRs based on ongoing work and backlog priorities without checking whether those priorities still align to company direction. Common in teams that have operated independently for a long time.

**Intervention needed**: The re-scoping conversation with the team lead (see Part 3).

---

## Part 3: Conversation Guide — Re-Scoping with the Team Lead

This is not a performance conversation. It is a planning correction. Frame it that way before you start.

### Setup

Have the conversation 1:1 with the team lead, not in front of the broader team. Bring:
- The team's draft OKRs
- The company OKRs
- A completed Alignment Trace Test showing the gap

Allow 45–60 minutes. Do not rush this. A lead who feels steamrolled will write compliant OKRs that are meaningless.

---

### Conversation Structure

**Step 1 — Establish shared understanding (5 minutes)**

> "Before we get into the OKRs, I want to make sure we're working from the same picture. Here are the company OKRs for this quarter. [Show them.] I've traced our team's draft Objectives against these, and I want to share what I found."

**Step 2 — Show the gap, not the judgment (5 minutes)**

> "Looking at your Objective [X], I had trouble connecting it to any of these company OKRs. Walk me through how you see that connection — I may be missing something."

Listen first. The lead may have a legitimate connection that was not obvious in the written OKR. If so, add it to the trace table and move on. If not, continue.

**Step 3 — Name the problem directly (3 minutes)**

> "I want to be direct with you. This Objective describes work your team is planning to do, but I can't trace it to a company outcome. That's a problem because when VP reviews our OKR set, the first question will be: 'How does this move the company forward?' I want us to be able to answer that before we get there."

Do not soften this into ambiguity. The lead needs to understand the stakes.

**Step 4 — Offer the three options (see Part 4) (20–30 minutes)**

> "I see three ways to approach this. Let me walk through them and get your reaction."

**Step 5 — Close with a decision and a timeline (5 minutes)**

> "Based on what we've discussed, I think [Option X] is the right path. I'd like to see a revised Objective by [specific date — 2 business days max]. Does that work for you?"

---

### Language Examples for Difficult Moments

**When the lead pushes back ("This work is critical, we have to do it")**

> "I'm not questioning whether the work is necessary. I'm asking whether it's the right work to put in an OKR versus running it as a health baseline outside the OKR system. If it's critical infrastructure hygiene, we can track it operationally without making it an Objective. If it's a strategic investment, we need to connect it to why the company needs it this quarter."

**When the lead is demoralized ("Nothing we do shows up in company OKRs anyway")**

> "That's a real problem, and it's partly a systemic one — infrastructure teams often don't get represented in company OKR development. Here's what I'm doing about it: [state your advocacy plan]. But that doesn't solve this quarter. What I can do is make the case explicitly in our OKR set that platform reliability is a foundational enabler, and document it as such. That protects your team's work from being invisible."

**When the lead refuses to change the Objective**

> "I need to be clear: I can't take this Objective to VP review as written. It doesn't trace to company direction. If you have a strong case for why it should stand, I want to hear it — and if the case is good enough, I'll carry it to VP myself. But the current draft needs to change or that case needs to be made before [date]."

## Part 4: The Three-Option Re-Scoping Framework

Present all three options to the lead. Do not pre-select one before the conversation — their input may change your read.

---

### Option 1 — Adopt

**Definition**: Accept the company OKR framing as-is and map the team's planned work directly underneath it.

**When to use**: The team's work genuinely supports a company OKR, but the team's draft Objective was written at the wrong level of abstraction.

**How it works**: Rewrite the team Objective to echo the company OKR language, then write KRs that describe the team's specific measurable contribution.

**Example**:
> Company OKR: "Achieve 99.95% platform availability across all Tier-1 services"  
> Adopted team Objective: "Own the reliability foundation that makes 99.95% Tier-1 availability achievable"  
> KR 1: Reduce mean time to detect (MTTD) on Tier-1 services from 8 min to 3 min  
> KR 2: Eliminate single points of failure in [Service X] before Q2 load season  
> KR 3: Achieve SLO compliance >99.5% for all 12 Tier-1 services without exception handling

**Tradeoff**: The team's work is visible in company context, but if the company OKR shifts, the team's Objective may need to shift mid-quarter.

---

### Option 2 — Adapt

**Definition**: Reshape the team's original Objective so it connects to company direction while preserving what the team values in the work.

**When to use**: The team's Objective has merit but is too insular as written. There is a real connection to company direction, but it needs to be surfaced and made explicit.

**How it works**: Keep the core intent of the team's Objective; add a "because this enables [company OKR]" clause to the outcome statement. Revise KRs to include at least one business-impact measurement.

**Example**:
> Original team Objective: "Modernize the CI/CD pipeline for the platform team"  
> Adapted Objective: "Accelerate developer throughput by modernizing CI/CD — enabling the company's velocity targets for Tier-1 feature delivery"  
> KR 1: Reduce average pipeline run time from 22 min to 8 min  
> KR 2: Eliminate manual gates in 100% of non-prod deployments  
> KR 3: Reduce failed deployment rate from 12% to <3% (measured in staging and production)

**Tradeoff**: Preserves team ownership and motivation; requires that the business-impact connection is real, not manufactured.

---

### Option 3 — Advocate

**Definition**: Make the case upward that the company OKR set is incomplete — that it does not account for the infrastructure and reliability foundation that platform teams provide — and argue for a formal infrastructure track.

**When to use**: The team's work is genuinely foundational and cannot be mapped to any existing company OKR without distorting either the team's work or the company OKR. The company OKR set is the problem, not the team's draft.

**How it works**: Director builds the infrastructure exemption case (see Part 5) and brings it to VP before finalizing the team's OKR set.

**Tradeoff**: Takes the most time and requires VP engagement. Only use when the other two options would produce OKRs that are inaccurate or misleading about the team's actual contribution.

---

## Part 5: The Infrastructure Exemption Case

Platform and SRE teams often cannot trace their work to a company OKR because company OKRs do not include reliability, developer experience, or foundational platform health. This is a structural gap in how most companies write OKRs, not a failure of the infrastructure team.

### When the Exemption Applies

The infrastructure exemption applies when:
- The team's work is a prerequisite for the company achieving any of its OKRs (e.g., without platform reliability, no product OKR is achievable)
- The company OKR set is silent on reliability, uptime, developer productivity, or platform health
- The team's work enables multiple company OKRs simultaneously but is not the primary owner of any

### How to Build the Exemption Argument

Structure the argument in three parts:

**1. The dependency statement**

> "Company OKRs [A, B, C] all depend on platform availability and developer throughput. Without [platform capability X], these OKRs cannot be achieved. Platform Engineering's OKRs are therefore a prerequisite, not a parallel track."

**2. The risk statement**

> "If Platform Engineering does not have an explicit OKR for [reliability / developer productivity / foundational capability], there is no accountability structure for the infrastructure that every other team depends on. The risk surfaces when something breaks, not when OKRs are written."

**3. The ask**

> "I am proposing that we formalize a Platform Engineering OKR track as a foundational tier — explicitly listed alongside company OKRs, not derived from them. I would like your support to establish this framing with [VP/C-suite] so it carries weight in the planning cycle."

### What to Prepare Before the VP Conversation

- A one-page document showing: the company OKR set, the platform OKR set, and the dependency map between them
- One specific example of a past quarter where platform reliability directly impacted a business outcome (incident, slowdown, deployment failure that affected a product OKR)
- A proposed name for the infrastructure track in the OKR taxonomy: "Platform Reliability OKRs", "Foundational Enablement OKRs", or equivalent

---

## Part 6: When to Escalate to VP

If after running the three-option framework with a team lead, the team's OKR set still has no meaningful connection to company direction — escalate before finalizing.

### Escalation Triggers

Escalate to VP when any of the following are true:
- The team has no Objective that can be traced to a company OKR, and the infrastructure exemption case does not apply
- The lead is unwilling to revise the OKRs and the Director cannot reach agreement
- A mid-quarter company OKR change has made the team's entire OKR set obsolete and a reset is needed
- Two or more teams have the same misalignment pattern — suggesting a systemic planning process failure

---

### Escalation Conversation Template

**Setting**: 1:1 with VP, 20 minutes, before the OKR set is finalized.

**Opening**

> "I want to flag an alignment issue before we finalize [team name]'s OKRs. I've traced their draft Objectives against the company OKRs and I'm not finding a clean connection. I've run through the options with [lead name] and want your input before we lock."

**The data**

Present the Alignment Trace Test output:
> "Here are their three Objectives. Here are the company OKRs. The trace I'm seeing is [strong / partial / absent]. The gap is specifically [state it in one sentence]."

**The options you have already considered**

> "I've considered Adopt, Adapt, and Advocate. [Lead name] and I discussed [which option]. The blocker is [specific reason it did not resolve]. I want your read on whether I'm missing a connection, or whether this is a planning process gap we need to address at the company OKR level."

**The ask**

> "I need one of two things from you: either guidance on how to connect this team's work to company direction in a way that's real and not manufactured, or acknowledgment that this team's work is foundational and should carry as an infrastructure exemption. Which path do you see?"

**After the conversation**

Document the VP's guidance in writing — email or Slack — before changing the OKR set. This protects both the Director and the team lead if the decision is questioned later.

---

### What Not to Do When Escalating

- Do not escalate before attempting the re-scoping conversation with the team lead. VP involvement before a 1:1 attempt signals process immaturity.
- Do not present the team lead's unwillingness as the lead's personal failure — present it as an alignment challenge that needs VP input to resolve.
- Do not escalate without a proposed path forward. "I have a problem" without "here are the options" wastes the VP's time and signals reactive rather than analytical leadership.
