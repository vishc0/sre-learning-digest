# OKR Grading Playbook
## Director / VP Platform Engineering — Operational Reference

**Purpose**: Close the quarter cleanly with defensible scores, documented learnings, and clean input into next quarter planning  
**Cadence**: Last 2 weeks of each quarter  
**Time commitment**: 30-minute async per lead (self-score) + 1-hour grading meeting with Director  

---

## Who Grades What

OKR grading is a three-level process. Each level has a specific role — do not collapse them.

| Level | Role | Action | Timing |
|-------|------|--------|--------|
| 1 — Propose | Team Lead | Self-scores each KR with evidence; submits draft scores | 2 weeks before quarter end |
| 2 — Review | Director | Challenges or confirms each score in the grading meeting | 1 week before quarter end |
| 3 — Confirm | VP | Reviews the final graded OKR set; approves or flags anomalies | Within 1 week of quarter close |

The Director's role in the grading meeting is not to validate effort — it is to ensure that scores are calibrated against evidence, not against narrative.

---

## Scoring Scale: 0.0 to 1.0

The scale is anchored to outcomes, not effort. A team that worked hard but did not achieve the target does not get credit for effort in the OKR score. Effort is acknowledged separately in performance conversations.

| Score | Meaning | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| 0.0–0.2 | Not started, or fundamental blocker made progress impossible | Acknowledge the blocker in the learning capture; no score inflation for partial intent |
| 0.3–0.4 | Significant miss — meaningful progress made but fell well short of target | Core work happened; something structural was wrong with the plan, the resourcing, or the target itself |
| 0.5–0.6 | Partial achievement — core value was delivered but the full KR was not met | This is the "did the right things, ran out of time or capacity" zone |
| 0.7 | Target hit — this is the expected outcome for a well-calibrated OKR | A 0.7 is success. Do not let leads feel like 0.7 is a B-minus. It means the OKR was real work. |
| 0.8–0.9 | Above target — strong execution, slightly exceeded the commitment | Positive signal; investigate whether the target was right or the team was exceptional |
| 1.0 | Fully exceeded — rare by design | If a team consistently scores 1.0, their targets are sandbagged. Address this in the next quarter's planning session. |

**Calibration principle**: A portfolio of scores averaging 0.65–0.75 across the org is healthy. Average above 0.85 means targets are too soft. Average below 0.5 means something structural is broken — planning, resourcing, or company-level blockers.

## Pre-Work: Before the Grading Meeting

### Lead Self-Scoring (2 weeks before quarter end)

Send each lead the grading template with these instructions:

> For each Key Result, assign a score between 0.0 and 1.0. For each score, provide:
> 1. The final measured value (the actual number, not a description)
> 2. The source of that measurement (dashboard name, report, ticket count — with a link or screenshot)
> 3. One sentence explaining the score relative to the target
> 4. If the score is below 0.5: one root cause and one corrective action for next quarter

Leads who submit scores without evidence (assertions like "we made significant progress") should be sent back to provide the measurement before the grading meeting.

### Director Pre-Read (3 days before the grading meeting)

Read every submitted score before the meeting. Flag:
- Scores that appear inflated relative to the evidence
- Scores that appear deflated (leads who undervalue their own results)
- KRs where no measurement was provided
- Patterns: same lead consistently under- or over-scoring

Prepare 1–2 challenge questions per KR where the score seems off. The meeting is not the time to discover the submissions.

---

## Grading Meeting: 1-Hour Agenda

**Participants**: Director + all engineering leads  
**Format**: Each lead presents their scores; Director reviews; group observes calibration  
**Output**: Finalized graded OKR document, ready for VP review

---

### Opening (0:00–0:05)

Set the context:

> "The purpose of this meeting is calibration, not judgment. We are closing the quarter with accurate scores so we can plan the next one well. A 0.6 honestly scored is more valuable than a 0.8 with weak evidence. I will challenge scores in both directions — if I think you undersold results, I will say so."

---

### KR-by-KR Review (0:05–0:50)

Work through each Objective and its KRs in order. For each KR:

1. **Lead reads the score and the measurement** (30 seconds max)
2. **Director confirms or challenges** (see challenge protocol below)
3. **Score is finalized** — write it in the shared doc in real time

Allow approximately 2–3 minutes per KR. For an org with 3 Objectives × 3 KRs = 9 KRs, that is 27 minutes. If you have multiple teams presenting, allocate proportionally.

#### Director Challenge Protocol

Use this sequence when a score seems off:

**If score seems too high:**
- "Walk me through the measurement. What was the target, and what is the final number?"
- "Is that measurement from a live dashboard, or is it reconstructed after the fact?"
- "What would need to be true for this to be a 0.9 instead of a 1.0?"

**If score seems too low:**
- "What did you actually deliver? Walk me through it."
- "The target was [X]. You're scoring this at 0.5 — what is the actual measured value?"
- "Is this a case where the KR was poorly written, so the real work doesn't show up in the score? If so, note that."

**If no measurement was provided:**
- "I don't have evidence for this score. What system do we pull the number from?"
- If no measurement exists: "The score for this KR is 0.0 until we can produce a measurement. What does that measurement require? Is that a corrective action for next quarter?"

---

### Calibration Check (0:50–0:55)

After all KRs are scored, review the portfolio:

- What is the average score across all KRs?
- Are any individual leads consistently scoring at extremes (all 0.8+ or all 0.4)?
- Are there KRs that scored 0.7+ that no one is proud of? (Signals wrong targets were set.)
- Are there KRs that scored below 0.5 where the team clearly outperformed the number? (Signals the measurement was wrong.)

State observations aloud. This is how the team learns to write better KRs next quarter.

---

### Closing and Next Quarter Input (0:55–1:00)

> "We have the final scores. Every KR below 0.5 has a root cause and a corrective action captured — those are inputs into next quarter's planning. I'll share the completed grading document with the VP by [date]. Anything I missed?"

---

## Evidence Standards

What counts as evidence for a KR score:

| Evidence Type | Acceptable? | Notes |
|---------------|------------|-------|
| Live dashboard screenshot with date | Yes | Strongest form of evidence |
| JIRA/ServiceNow/Linear ticket count or status | Yes | Include the query or filter |
| SLO/SLA report exported from monitoring system | Yes | Datadog, Splunk, Grafana — timestamp required |
| Incident count from ticketing system | Yes | With time range |
| Deployment frequency from CI/CD system | Yes | Pipeline report or chart |
| Customer survey score | Yes | With sample size and date |
| "We feel we achieved this" | No | Not evidence |
| "The team worked really hard on this" | No | Not evidence |
| "We completed most of the milestones" | No | Milestones are activities, not outcomes |
| Verbal assertion from the lead | No | Requires a backing source |

If a KR has no valid evidence source at grading time, one of two things is true: (a) the measurement system was never built — which is a 0.0 on the KR and a corrective action to instrument before next quarter; or (b) the KR was written in terms that cannot be measured — which is a design failure to fix in planning.

---

## Learning Capture

For every KR scored below 0.5, capture the following in the grading document before the session ends:

```
KR: [exact KR text]
Score: [0.0–0.4]
Root Cause: [one sentence — what specifically caused the miss?]
  - Was it: unclear target | under-resourced | external blocker | wrong assumption | measurement failure
Corrective Action: [one concrete change for next quarter]
  - Must be actionable by the team lead, not just "be better"
Owner: [who owns the corrective action]
```

Root causes fall into five categories. Identify which applies — this drives the corrective action:

| Root Cause Category | Corrective Action Direction |
|--------------------|----------------------------|
| Unclear target — we did not know what "done" looked like | Rewrite KR with explicit baseline and target at the start of next quarter |
| Under-resourced — the work was real but capacity was not there | Either reduce scope of KR or escalate resource need before committing |
| External blocker — another team or vendor did not deliver | Identify dependency earlier; use the misalignment intervention playbook |
| Wrong assumption — the plan was based on something that turned out to be false | Add assumption validation as the first KR milestone next quarter |
| Measurement failure — we did the work but cannot prove it | Build the measurement instrument before committing the KR |

---

## Output: What Leaves the Grading Session

The grading session is complete when the following exist:

- [ ] Graded OKR document: all KRs have a final numeric score (0.0–1.0) and a one-line evidence note
- [ ] Learning capture: every KR below 0.5 has a documented root cause and corrective action with owner
- [ ] Quarter retrospective summary: 3–5 bullets — what the team accomplished, what did not land, what changes next quarter
- [ ] VP review meeting scheduled within 5 business days
- [ ] Graded document shared in agreed tracking system before VP review

The quarter retrospective summary is separate from the graded OKR document. It is a narrative layer on top of the scores, written by the Director, that gives the VP a 2-minute read on the quarter without digging into individual KRs.

## Grading Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: Grade Inflation to Avoid Difficult Conversations

**What it looks like**: Scores cluster at 0.7–0.8 across the board regardless of actual delivery. Leads and Director are uncomfortable with lower scores.  
**Why it is harmful**: VP loses calibration signal. Next quarter's targets get set incorrectly. Sandbagging becomes the norm because 0.7 is achievable with soft targets.  
**Correction**: Director sets the tone at the opening — honest scores are valued more than high scores. Director must be willing to lower a score in the session, in front of the group.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Averaging Over Weak Evidence

**What it looks like**: A lead scores a KR at 0.6 and the Director accepts it because the effort was visible, even though no measurement exists.  
**Why it is harmful**: You have now established that evidence is optional.  
**Correction**: No evidence, no score above 0.0. Document the absence of measurement as a corrective action.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Retroactive KR Rewrite

**What it looks like**: A lead argues that the KR "meant something slightly different" than what is written, and the new interpretation makes the score higher.  
**Why it is harmful**: KRs are commitments. Reinterpreting them after the fact is not grading — it is revision.  
**Correction**: "We grade the KR as written. If the KR was written poorly, that is a learning we capture. The score stands against what was committed."

### Anti-Pattern 4: Ignoring 1.0 Scores

**What it looks like**: A lead scores 1.0 on multiple KRs and it is treated as success.  
**Why it is harmful**: Consistent 1.0 scores signal that targets are too easy. The OKR system stops creating pull toward better outcomes.  
**Correction**: Ask one question per 1.0 score: "If the target had been [X+20%], would we have achieved that?" If yes, the target was sandbagged. Address in next quarter's planning.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Conflating Score with Performance Rating

**What it looks like**: Leads feel that a 0.5 score means they personally underperformed.  
**Why it is harmful**: Leads start writing safe targets to protect their performance perception.  
**Correction**: State explicitly — and document it: "OKR scores assess plans, not people. A 0.5 on an ambitious KR is more valuable than a 0.9 on a sandbagged one. Your performance review and your OKR score are not the same conversation."
