# Quarterly OKR Planning Playbook
## Director / VP Platform Engineering — Operational Reference

**Audience**: Director-level leader running quarterly OKR planning with SRE or Platform Engineering teams  
**Format**: 2-week pre-work + 3-hour working session  
**Cadence**: Run once per quarter, 2–3 weeks before the new quarter begins  

---

## Pre-Work: 2 Weeks Before the Session

Complete all pre-work before the session. Walking into the room without it collapses the session into debate instead of decision-making.

### Week –2 (Two Weeks Out)

| Task | Owner | Output | Done? |
|------|-------|--------|-------|
| Obtain finalized company OKRs from your VP or Chief of Staff | Director | Company OKR deck or doc, shared with all leads | |
| Run last-quarter retrospective (async) — leads score their own OKRs 0.0–1.0 and submit with evidence | Each team lead | Completed grading sheet per lead | |
| Collect team input on next quarter priorities — async survey or 1:1 | Director | Priority list ranked by frequency; surface low-scoring but high-priority items | |
| Identify dependencies with adjacent teams (Security, App Dev, Data Platform) | Director | Dependency map: what you need from them, what they need from you | |
| Draft a straw-man Objective list (3 candidates max) based on company direction | Director | Internal draft — not shared yet; used to seed discussion, not to anchor it prematurely |  |

### Week –1 (One Week Out)

| Task | Owner | Output | Done? |
|------|-------|--------|-------|
| Distribute company OKRs to all leads with a 2-day read window | Director | Confirmed receipt and read | |
| Circulate prior quarter's graded OKRs to all attendees | Chief of Staff or Director | All leads have scores before the session | |
| Confirm session logistics: room, video bridge, shared doc ready (OKR canvas) | Chief of Staff | Calendar hold, shared doc link distributed | |
| Pre-brief any lead who is likely to show up with a list of 8 objectives | Director (1:1) | Expectation set: we are picking 3, not cataloguing work | |
| Prepare facilitation deck: 5 slides max — agenda, last quarter scores, company OKRs, blank OKR canvas, output template | Director | Facilitation deck ready | |

---

## Session: 3-Hour Working Session Agenda

**Participants**: Director + all engineering team leads (typically 3–6 people for a 15-person org)  
**Room setup**: Shared doc visible to all; OKR canvas projected or screen-shared  
**No laptops for side work** — enforce it at the start

---

### Block 1 — Retrospective (0:00–0:45)

**Purpose**: Close last quarter cleanly. No open scores going into planning.

#### Facilitation Steps

1. Pull up the last quarter's OKR grading sheet (leads submitted scores in pre-work).
2. Go objective by objective. For each one, have the lead who owns it read their score and one sentence of evidence.
3. Director confirms or adjusts the score. Do not debate at length — score is a diagnostic, not a performance review.
4. Capture learnings in the shared doc: one per KR that scored below 0.5.

#### Questions to Ask

- "What's the one thing that would have moved this KR from 0.4 to 0.7?"
- "Was this KR underfunded, under-understood, or blocked externally? Which?"
- "If we carry this work into next quarter, does it belong as a KR or as a health baseline?"

#### Common Failure Mode: Score Debate

If a lead argues strongly for a higher score, apply this test: "Show me the measurement that supports that score — dashboard, ticket count, or customer-facing metric." If they cannot produce evidence, the score stands at what is evidenced. Acknowledge the effort; do not inflate the score.

---

### Block 2 — Company Alignment Review (0:45–1:15)

**Purpose**: Establish the playing field. Team OKRs must trace to this.

#### Facilitation Steps

1. Display company OKRs (no more than 5 at the company level).
2. For each company OKR, ask: "Which of these does Platform/SRE own, enable, or directly support?"
3. Color-code in real time: **Own** (we are accountable), **Enable** (we unblock another team), **Support** (we contribute effort).
4. Identify any company OKRs where Platform/SRE has no connection — flag these explicitly. They are either not your domain or a gap in the company OKR set.
5. Note which company OKRs are not represented at all in team thinking — these are misalignment risks.

#### Questions to Ask

- "If this company OKR fails, is Platform on the critical path? Yes or no?"
- "Which of our planned work this quarter has zero connection to any of these company OKRs?"
- "Is there a company priority missing from this list that we know is real? If so, should we advocate for it or carry it as an implicit?"

#### Facilitation Note

Infrastructure and platform teams often find that company OKRs are product- and revenue-focused, leaving reliability and developer productivity unlabeled. Do not let this force the team into artificial connections. If you cannot trace to a company OKR, use the infrastructure exemption framing (see Misalignment Intervention Playbook). Flag it now; do not ignore it.

---

### Block 3 — Draft Objectives (1:15–2:00)

**Purpose**: Generate and narrow to 3 Objectives for the quarter.

#### Facilitation Steps

1. **Silent brainstorm (8 minutes)**: Each person writes objective candidates on sticky notes or in the shared doc. No filtering — all ideas go in.
2. **Read-out (10 minutes)**: Each person reads their candidates. No debate yet. Director types them into the shared doc.
3. **Affinity grouping (10 minutes)**: Group duplicate or overlapping candidates together. Label the group with a shared theme.
4. **Dot vote (5 minutes)**: Each lead gets 3 votes. Place them on themes, not individual candidates.
5. **Narrow to 3 (12 minutes)**: Director selects 3 based on votes + company alignment from Block 2. State the selection and the rationale aloud.

#### Questions to Ask During Narrowing

- "Does this objective, if achieved, move the needle for the company — or just for our team's comfort?"
- "Could we articulate this objective to our VP in one sentence? If not, it is not ready."
- "Is this an objective or a project? Objectives describe an outcome; projects describe activity."

#### How to Handle Disagreement on Objectives

When two leads advocate for competing objectives:

1. Ask each lead to state the outcome if their objective is achieved — not the work, the outcome.
2. Ask: "If we achieve the other objective instead, does the company still benefit? Does your team still benefit?"
3. If they are truly in conflict, choose based on company alignment score from Block 2, not team preference.
4. If they are compatible, test whether they can collapse into one broader objective with two Key Results sets.

Do not mediate indefinitely. Director has the call. Make it, state the reasoning, move on.

---

### Block 4 — Draft Key Results (2:00–2:45)

**Purpose**: For each of the 3 Objectives, draft 3 measurable Key Results.

#### Facilitation Steps

1. Assign one objective to each lead to draft KRs for (Director takes the third if needed).
2. Give 10 minutes for silent drafting.
3. Read out KR candidates per objective. Director challenges each one on measurability.
4. Refine language until each KR passes the measurement test (see below).

#### The KR Measurement Test

A KR is not ready unless you can answer all three:

- "What is the baseline today?" (If unknown, first KR is to establish the baseline.)
- "What is the target value at quarter end?"
- "Who produces the measurement, and from what system?" (Dashboard, ticket system, SLO report — not a subjective assessment.)

#### How to Push Back on Sandbagged KRs

Signs of sandbagging: targets that are already met, targets with no stretch from current state, vague language like "improve" or "increase" without a number.

Script: "What's the current value? [Lead answers.] And you're targeting [X]? If we start at [current] and your target is [X], what would 'great' look like — not 'safe'? Let's draft both, and you tell me which one requires the team to actually push."

Use this every time. Do not let sandbagged KRs pass. A 0.7 score on a real target is success; a 1.0 on a sandbagged target is noise.

---

### Block 5 — Alignment Check (2:45–3:00)

**Purpose**: Validate the full OKR set before it leaves the room.

#### Facilitation Steps

1. Display the 3 Objectives and their KRs side-by-side with the company OKR list.
2. For each team Objective, draw the trace line: "This maps to Company OKR [X] because..."
3. Identify any Objective with no trace line — it needs to be flagged as an infrastructure exemption or cut.
4. Check for internal conflicts: does achieving Objective 2 require work that blocks Objective 1?
5. Gut-check on capacity: given the team headcount and SAFe PI commitments, is this set achievable at 0.7 without heroics?

#### Questions to Ask

- "If we achieve only 2 of these 3 objectives, which one do we sacrifice? Is that acceptable?"
- "Does any KR depend on a dependency outside this team that is not confirmed?"
- "Would our VP look at this set and say 'yes, this is what I expected from this team'?"

---

## Output Checklist

An OKR set is ready for VP review when all of the following are true:

- [ ] 3 Objectives, each stated as an outcome (not an activity)
- [ ] 3 Key Results per Objective, each with baseline, target, and measurement source named
- [ ] Every Objective traces to at least one company OKR, or has a documented infrastructure exemption rationale
- [ ] No KR depends on an unconfirmed external dependency
- [ ] At least one lead has reviewed each KR and agreed the target is achievable at 0.7 with disciplined execution (not heroics)
- [ ] The grading retrospective from last quarter is closed — all scores final
- [ ] The OKR set is in the agreed tracking system (Confluence, Notion, JIRA — wherever your VP expects it)
- [ ] VP review meeting is on the calendar within 5 business days of the planning session

## Common Facilitation Failure Modes

### Failure Mode 1: The Session Becomes a Status Update

**Symptom**: Leads start presenting what they are working on instead of debating what the outcomes should be.  
**Recovery**: "I have context on the work. What I need from this session is the outcome — what does 'done' look like for the quarter? Let's stay in outcome language."

### Failure Mode 2: Too Many Objectives Survive the Narrowing

**Symptom**: After dot voting, the group has 5–6 "tied" objectives and no one wants to cut.  
**Recovery**: "We are choosing 3. I will make the call now. [Name Objective A, B, C.] The others are either health baselines we track on the side, or they go into the backlog for next quarter. Is anyone fundamentally opposed? If so, say it now — not after this meeting."

### Failure Mode 3: Leads Defer to the Director for All KR Content

**Symptom**: Director ends up writing the KRs while leads watch.  
**Recovery**: Stop writing. "This is your team's work. I can challenge your KRs but I cannot own them. What is the outcome your team is committing to?" Silence is acceptable. Wait.

### Failure Mode 4: KRs Are Activities Disguised as Outcomes

**Symptom**: KRs start with "Complete...", "Implement...", "Deploy...", "Launch..."  
**Recovery**: "That is a project milestone, not a Key Result. What changes for the customer or the business when that work is complete? State that instead."

### Failure Mode 5: The Session Runs Over Without Completing Block 5

**Symptom**: Blocks 3 and 4 consume all the time; alignment check is skipped.  
**Recovery**: Set a hard stop at 2:30 — tell the room at 2:00 that you have 30 minutes for KR drafting and the session ends at 3:00 regardless. An incomplete alignment check means the OKR set is not ready; schedule a 30-minute async review before VP submission rather than skipping it.
