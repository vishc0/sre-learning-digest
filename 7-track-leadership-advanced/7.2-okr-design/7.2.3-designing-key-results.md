# Chapter 3: Designing Key Results

## Bottom Line First

Key Results are the mechanism that makes OKRs falsifiable. Without them, an Objective is a preference. With them, it is a testable hypothesis. The failure mode at the Director level is not writing bad Key Results — it is writing Key Results that look good but measure the wrong thing. Output measures dressed as outcome measures. Task completions framed as metric improvements. Targets set low enough that the team will hit 100% every quarter regardless of ambition.

A Director who designs Key Results correctly treats 70% as the success signal, not the failure signal. That mental shift is where most leaders get stuck.

---

## SMART Key Results — With Director-Level Nuance

SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) is the right starting frame, but the Director-level application of "Achievable" is different from how most managers were taught it.

In most management training, "achievable" means "the team can realistically hit this." That interpretation produces sandbagged Key Results — targets set below what the team is capable of so that the quarterly review looks like a success story.

**At the Director level, "achievable" means: achievable if the team executes well and nothing catastrophic intervenes.** It should not mean "achievable even if we have a slow quarter." The distinction is between a confidence level of ~70% (right) and ~95% (sandbagging).

### The 70% Target Principle

Google's original OKR model was explicit about this: if you hit 100% of your Key Results every quarter, you are setting targets too low. The right target is one where hitting 70% represents meaningful progress and hitting 100% required real effort and some favorable conditions.

This serves two functions:

1. **It creates ambition headroom.** A team that consistently hits 70% of a stretch target is outperforming a team that consistently hits 100% of a safe target.
2. **It makes the quarterly review an honest conversation.** "We hit KR1 and KR3 fully, KR2 at 65%, and KR4 at 40% — let us talk about why KR4 fell short" is a more useful conversation than "we hit everything, here is the green dashboard."

The scoring convention: 0.7 (70%) is a good result. 1.0 (100%) signals the target was too easy. 0.4 or below signals either the target was unrealistic or something went wrong that deserves diagnosis.

**Interview application**: when asked how you set targets, say explicitly that you calibrate to 70% confidence, explain why, and be ready to describe a quarter where you missed a Key Result and what it taught you. Interviewers at the Director/VP level are testing whether you treat OKRs as learning tools or as performance theater.

## Outcome vs. Output: The Core Design Decision

This is the most common place Key Results fail. Output measures tell you what the team did. Outcome measures tell you what changed.

| Output KR (wrong) | Outcome KR (right) | Why it matters |
|-------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| Deploy distributed tracing across 12 services | Reduce mean time to diagnosis for P1 incidents by 60% | Tracing is the mechanism; diagnosis speed is the result |
| Conduct 4 quarterly reliability reviews with product teams | Reduce external escalations from product teams by 30% | Reviews are the activity; fewer escalations means the reviews worked |
| Implement automated runbook execution for top 10 alert types | Reduce on-call pages requiring human intervention by 50% | Automation is the work; the benefit is less human burden |
| Launch the internal developer platform | Reduce time from approved service to production-ready environment from 2 weeks to 4 hours | Launch is the milestone; the benefit is speed to production |
| Complete chaos engineering exercises for Tier 1 services | Achieve zero preventable repeat incidents in Tier 1 services for the quarter | Exercises are the practice; prevention is the outcome |

The test: can you hit the Key Result without the outcome actually materializing? If yes, it is an output measure.

You can deploy distributed tracing across 12 services and have engineers still spend 3 hours diagnosing incidents because the traces are not readable, the dashboards are not configured, or the on-call runbook does not reference them. The output happened. The outcome did not.

## Leading vs. Lagging Indicators for SRE Key Results

In SRE, most natural metrics are lagging indicators — they tell you what already happened. MTTR, incident count, error budget consumption, availability. These are important but they are backward-looking. By the time a lagging indicator signals a problem, the damage is done.

A well-designed Key Result set for a platform team includes both:

**Lagging indicators** (what happened):
- MTTR for P1 incidents
- Error budget consumption rate (percentage of error budget consumed)
- Availability percentage for Tier 1 services
- On-call escalation volume

**Leading indicators** (what predicts what will happen):
- Alert signal-to-noise ratio (percentage of pages that result in human action — a high ratio means noisy alerting, which predicts on-call burnout and missed real incidents)
- Runbook coverage rate (percentage of top-N alert types with current, verified runbooks — predicts MTTR)
- Deployment frequency and change failure rate (predicts future incident rate)
- Toil hours as a percentage of engineering capacity (predicts team sustainability and retention risk)

A Director who only uses lagging indicators is managing by looking in the rearview mirror. The interview question "how do you know your platform is getting more reliable before incidents tell you?" is answered by leading indicators.

### Recommended KR set structure for SRE/Platform

For a platform engineering department, a balanced set of 4 Key Results per Objective might look like:
- 1 lagging outcome metric (MTTR, availability, incident rate)
- 1 leading health metric (alert noise ratio, runbook coverage)
- 1 team-sustainability metric (toil percentage, on-call hours per engineer)
- 1 developer experience metric (time to production, internal NPS from product teams)

This spread prevents the failure mode of optimizing one dimension while degrading others.

## Writing Key Results That Are Falsifiable

Falsifiability means: at quarter end, there is no ambiguity about whether the Key Result was hit. Either the number is there or it is not.

### Tests for falsifiability

**Test 1 — The stranger test**: Could someone who was not on your team, with access to your metrics systems, determine whether this KR was hit? If the answer requires your interpretation, it is not falsifiable.

**Test 2 — The pre-mortem test**: Write out exactly how you would measure this at quarter end. What system? Which dashboard? What query? If you cannot answer those questions when writing the KR, you do not have a KR — you have an intention.

**Test 3 — The no-judgment test**: If hitting it requires a judgment call (e.g., "our platform is considered reliable by product teams"), it needs to be converted to a measurable proxy (e.g., "product teams file fewer than 5 reliability-related escalations per month, down from 18").

### Falsifiability failures and fixes

**Failing KR**: "Significantly improve on-call sustainability."

Problem: "Significantly" is a judgment call. No measurement is named. At quarter end, this can always be claimed as achieved.

**Fixed KR**: "Reduce average on-call hours per engineer per week from 6.2 to under 3.0, measured across the full quarter."

**Failing KR**: "Improve the relationship between platform and product engineering."

Problem: Relationship quality is not measurable as stated.

**Fixed KR**: "Achieve an internal NPS score of 40+ from product engineering teams on platform reliability and support, up from 12."

**Failing KR**: "Make meaningful progress on technical debt reduction."

Problem: "Meaningful progress" is defined by the person claiming it.

**Fixed KR**: "Reduce the time engineering teams spend on platform-related incident investigation from an average of 4.2 hours to under 90 minutes, measured by post-incident review data."

## The Director's Calibration Conversation

When reviewing your team's Key Results before finalizing them for the quarter, run this calibration:

1. **Confidence check**: "If we execute well and nothing catastrophic happens, what is our probability of hitting this at 100%?" If the answer is above 85%, the target is too easy.
2. **Outcome check**: "If we hit this KR but the underlying problem is not actually better, is that possible?" If yes, it is an output measure.
3. **Measurement check**: "What system do we query, and what query, to score this KR at quarter end?" If you cannot answer this on day one, the KR is not ready.
4. **Relevance check**: "If we hit this KR, does it directly demonstrate progress toward the Objective?" If it is tangentially related, cut it or rewrite the Objective.

Key Results that pass all four checks are ready to commit to. Key Results that fail any check need a rewrite before the quarter starts — mid-quarter rewrites signal poor planning and erode team trust in the OKR process.
