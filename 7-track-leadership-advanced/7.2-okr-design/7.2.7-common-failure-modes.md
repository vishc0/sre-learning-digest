# Chapter 7: Common OKR Failure Modes — What Directors Must Recognize and Correct

## Bottom Line First

OKRs degrade quietly. The failure modes listed here do not announce themselves. They accumulate over two or three quarters until the OKR program is a compliance exercise that no one believes in. A Director's job is not just to write good OKRs — it is to recognize the patterns that corrupt the system and intervene before the program loses credibility. Each failure mode below has a detection signal and a correction move.

---

## Failure Mode 1: Sandbagging

**What it is**: Setting targets that are easy enough to guarantee a 1.0 score. The team knows the target is safe before the quarter starts.

**Why it happens**: When OKR scores are tied to performance reviews, team leads rationally protect their people by setting targets they know they can hit. This is not dishonesty — it is rational self-defense in a poorly designed system.

**Detection signals**:
- Teams score 0.9–1.0 every quarter, consistently, across multiple quarters
- KR targets are flat or minimally improved from last quarter's actual results
- Targets are set at or below the current operating baseline

**Correction move**: In the OKR review session during the Set phase, for each KR ask: "What would it look like if this KR was 30% harder?" If the team cannot articulate a stretch version, the target is sandbagged. Push back explicitly: "This looks like where we already are. What would it mean to move the needle on this, not just maintain it?" Normalize scores of 0.6–0.8 as the signal of a well-calibrated program, not a sign of underperformance.

---

## Failure Mode 2: Stretch Theater

**What it is**: Setting targets so aggressive they are functionally impossible. Teams disengage because they know from week 2 that the quarter is already lost.

**Why it happens**: Usually top-down pressure from executives who conflate ambition with unreasonable targets. Sometimes it comes from leaders who read "moonshots" OKR philosophy without understanding the original context — Google's moonshot OKRs were a small subset of a much larger OKR portfolio.

**Detection signals**:
- Teams consistently score below 0.3 quarter after quarter on the same Objectives
- Engineers stop referencing OKRs in sprint planning because "they're not realistic anyway"
- Mid-quarter check-ins produce demoralized rather than energized conversations

**Correction move**: Introduce a calibration step at the end of the Set phase. For each KR, ask the team: "On a scale of 1–10, how confident are you that this is achievable with full effort and no major blockers?" A response below 3 is stretch theater. The target range for a healthy stretch OKR is a 4–6 confidence score — ambitious enough to be uncertain, grounded enough to be motivating.

---

## Failure Mode 3: Activity Masquerading as Outcomes

**What it is**: KRs that describe work performed rather than value delivered. The team can complete the KR even if nothing improved for the customer or end user.

**Why it happens**: Activities are easier to write, easier to track, and feel more concrete. Engineers and team leads who are new to OKRs default to project milestone language because that is what sprint tickets look like.

**Detection signals**: Apply the completion test — ask "could this KR be marked complete even if nothing improved for the people who depend on our platform?" If yes, it is an activity.

Examples:
- Activity KR: "Migrate 15 services to the new observability stack." (Complete even if those services are still blind after migration.)
- Outcome KR: "Reduce mean time to detect for the 15 migrated services from 47 minutes to under 8 minutes." (Requires actual improvement, not just migration.)

**Correction move**: For every KR written as a task or milestone, ask "So what?" What improves for whom if this is completed? The answer to that question is the real KR. Do not accept "we'll have better infrastructure" as an answer — that is not measurable.

---

## Failure Mode 4: OKR Fatigue

**What it is**: Too many OKRs. Teams are tracking so many KRs that no single KR gets real attention, and the quarterly review becomes a data-entry exercise.

**Why it happens**: Organizations that are new to OKRs often try to make every initiative an OKR. Every director wants their priorities represented. The result is a 40-KR portfolio that no engineer can hold in working memory.

**Detection signals**:
- Teams have more than 3 Objectives or more than 3 KRs per Objective
- Engineers cannot recite the team's top priority from memory
- OKR tracking spreadsheets require more than 10 minutes to update

**Correction move**: Enforce the 3×3 ceiling as a hard rule: maximum 3 Objectives, maximum 3 KRs per Objective. This is not a guideline — it is a forcing function for prioritization. If a team cannot reduce to 3×3, the real problem is that leadership has not made hard priority calls. That is a leadership accountability issue, not an OKR formatting issue. Force the conversation: "If you could only achieve one of these Objectives this quarter, which one would you choose?" That answer should be Objective 1.

---

## Failure Mode 5: Leadership Disconnection

**What it is**: OKRs are set at the start of the quarter and not reviewed by leadership until grading week. Teams receive no signal that the OKRs matter to anyone above them.

**Why it happens**: Directors have full calendars. The OKR check-in is the meeting that gets cancelled when a production incident, an executive request, or a planning conflict arises. Over time, the check-in disappears entirely.

**Detection signals**:
- Mid-quarter check-in meetings are cancelled or replaced with async score updates
- Teams update OKR scores without discussion
- Engineers express surprise when asked about OKR status, as if they forgot the OKRs existed

**Correction move**: Make the Director's attendance at the monthly OKR review non-negotiable. The signal that OKRs matter is the Director's calendar. If the check-in gets cancelled, the implicit message is "OKRs are not a priority." Beyond attendance, Directors should reference OKRs in weekly 1:1s with their Senior Managers: "Where are we on KR 2 for the platform reliability Objective?" This normalizes OKRs as a management tool, not a compliance form.

---

## Failure Mode 6: The Reporting Shell

**What it is**: Teams write OKRs that look correct and complete but bear no relationship to the work they are actually doing. The real work happens in sprints. The OKRs are a parallel document maintained for reporting purposes.

**Why it happens**: When OKRs are imposed top-down without team involvement in drafting, or when the OKR program is introduced as a compliance requirement rather than a strategic tool, teams learn to write OKRs that satisfy the form without internalizing the substance.

**Detection signals**:
- Teams cannot trace a specific sprint story to a specific OKR
- OKR scores are updated without any corresponding change in sprint work
- When asked "which OKR does this sprint priority connect to?" the answer is a long pause

**Correction move**: Require teams to run a traceability check at the start of each sprint: for every sprint commitment, identify which OKR it connects to. Anything with no OKR connection is either supporting a maintenance obligation (legitimate, but should be named as such) or is a priority that was not captured in the OKR (which means the OKR needs updating). This check exposes the shell within one sprint cycle.

---

## Failure Mode 7: OKR-Performance Review Conflation

**What it is**: Using OKR scores directly to determine performance ratings, bonuses, or promotion decisions. A 0.7 on a KR translates mechanically to a 70% performance score.

**Why it happens**: OKRs produce numbers, performance reviews need numbers, and conflating them seems efficient. It is not. It is structurally destructive.

**Why this is catastrophic**: The moment an individual's OKR score determines their compensation or promotion, every rational incentive shifts toward sandbagging. Engineers will write safe targets. They will avoid stretch OKRs. They will not update scores honestly mid-quarter when things go wrong, because an honest 0.3 at mid-quarter becomes evidence used against them in a performance conversation. The entire information value of the OKR system — which depends on honest scoring — is destroyed.

**The Google model**: Google explicitly separates OKR scores from performance reviews. OKRs assess goal achievement. Performance reviews assess behaviors, judgment, and impact. A person who scores 0.6 on a genuinely hard OKR while demonstrating excellent engineering judgment, leadership, and collaboration is a high performer. A person who scores 1.0 by sandbagging every quarter while blocking their teammates is a performance risk. OKR scores are one input into a broader conversation, not a formula.

**Correction move**: State the separation policy explicitly and publicly at the start of every OKR cycle: "OKR scores are not used directly in performance ratings. I want honest scores, which means I want you to stretch, score accurately, and tell me when targets need to be adjusted. Gaming the score helps no one." Then enforce this in practice by praising teams that score 0.6–0.7 on hard OKRs rather than rewarding 1.0s without asking why.

---

## The Director's Pattern Recognition Summary

| Failure Mode | Early Signal | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Sandbagging | Consistent 0.9–1.0 scores | 3×3 ceiling, normalize 0.6–0.8 as success |
| Stretch Theater | Consistent <0.3 scores, team disengagement | Calibration confidence check at Set phase |
| Activities as OKRs | KRs sound like tickets, completion test fails | "So what?" follow-up on every milestone KR |
| OKR Fatigue | >3 Objectives or >3 KRs per Objective | Hard 3×3 ceiling, force prioritization conversation |
| Leadership Disconnection | Check-ins cancelled, engineers unaware | Director calendar commitment, 1:1 OKR references |
| Reporting Shell | No sprint-to-OKR traceability | Sprint traceability check, bi-weekly alignment |
| Perf Review Conflation | Sandbagging follows comp cycle | Explicit stated separation, praise honest scoring |

Recognizing these patterns and naming them directly — without blame but with accountability — is what makes an OKR program durable. Directors who let failure modes accumulate silently eventually inherit a program that everyone tolerates and no one trusts.
