# Chapter 2: Writing Good Objectives

## Bottom Line First

An Objective that fails the three tests — memorable, directional, motivating — is not an OKR problem. It is a strategic clarity problem. If you cannot write an inspiring Objective for your platform team, it usually means one of two things: you do not know what the quarter is actually for, or you know what it is for but it is a maintenance quarter with no real change agenda. Both are valid situations. The answer in the first case is to do the strategic thinking before writing the OKR. The answer in the second is to name the maintenance agenda honestly rather than dress it up in OKR language.

---

## What Makes an Objective Work

An Objective is the qualitative half of an OKR. It does not contain numbers. It does not describe a project. It describes a state of the world that you want to exist at the end of the quarter that does not exist today.

### The Three Tests

**Test 1: Memorable**
Can someone on your team recite the Objective from memory two weeks after you announced it? If not, it is too long, too abstract, or too generic. A good Objective is one sentence, plain language, and specific enough to be distinguishable from last quarter's Objective.

Failing: "Improve operational excellence through proactive platform maturity initiatives and cross-functional reliability programs."
Passing: "Make our platform so reliable that product teams stop asking us permission before they ship."

**Test 2: Directional**
Does it tell you what changes, not what you do? An Objective is a direction, not a destination. It should make clear which way is forward for this quarter — what matters more this quarter than last quarter.

Failing: "Complete the observability platform rollout." (This is a project milestone, not a direction.)
Passing: "Give every engineering team full visibility into their service health before incidents find us."

**Test 3: Motivating**
Would a senior engineer on your team read this and feel the quarter has a point? Or would they shrug and keep doing what they were already doing? The Objective should create a sense of stakes — why does this quarter matter for the team's identity, the team's impact, or the business's trajectory.

Failing: "Reduce operational toil across platform services." (True, probably important, but generates no energy.)
Passing: "Free our engineers from the on-call grind so they can spend their edge on building, not firefighting."

## Bad Objectives and Corrected Versions

These are patterns that appear frequently in SRE and platform engineering contexts. Each one is wrong for a specific reason, not just generically.

### Pattern 1: The vague aspiration

**Bad**: "Improve platform reliability."

Why it fails: Improvement in which dimension? By how much? Compared to what baseline? This Objective tells you nothing about what changed or what the quarter is for. It could describe any quarter in any year.

**Corrected**: "Make our platform the reliability foundation that enables product to ship without asking us first."

Why it works: It describes a relationship change (product teams no longer need to check with platform), implies a reliability threshold (they ship autonomously because they trust the platform), and is specific enough to be wrong — if product teams are still coming to us for approvals at quarter end, we failed.

### Pattern 2: The task dressed as an Objective

**Bad**: "Deploy the new incident management toolchain."

Why it fails: This is a deliverable. You can deploy the toolchain on day one and have a worse quarter than before. The deployment is not the point — what the deployment produces is the point.

**Corrected**: "Cut the time between a production failure and a resolution in half."

Why it works: The toolchain is now a means to an end. If you deploy it and MTTR does not improve, you know the work is not done.

### Pattern 3: The sandbagged Objective

**Bad**: "Maintain current service reliability levels while onboarding three new services."

Why it fails: This is not ambition — it is a capacity plan dressed as an Objective. You are describing what you expect to happen anyway, not what you are driving toward. It fails the motivating test completely.

**Corrected**: "Scale our platform to 15 services without expanding the on-call rotation."

Why it works: It names the constraint (no headcount growth in on-call) and the ambition (15 services), which creates real tension and requires genuine problem-solving to achieve.

### Pattern 4: The internally-focused Objective

**Bad**: "Modernize the platform architecture."

Why it fails: Modernization for whom? This Objective is entirely self-referential — it describes what the platform team will work on but not why the business should care. An interviewer will ask "what business outcome does this drive?" and you will have no answer.

**Corrected**: "Make our architecture invisible to product teams — ship fast, break nothing, fix in minutes."

Why it works: The beneficiary is named (product teams), the business value is named (ship fast), and the operational excellence standard is named (fix in minutes). Architecture modernization becomes a visible contributor to this outcome.

## The Altitude Problem: Platform and SRE Objectives

The hardest challenge for SRE Managers moving into Director roles is connecting platform objectives to business outcomes. Most SRE and platform work operates in a technical register — availability, latency, deployment frequency, MTTR. These are real and important, but they are not business outcomes on their own.

A Director-level Objective must bridge the two. The bridge is always: *what does the business get to do because the platform does this well?*

| Technical State | Business Outcome |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| MTTR drops from 47 to 20 minutes | Product teams trust the platform enough to ship on Friday afternoons |
| Error budget consumption below 50% | Engineering leadership can commit delivery timelines to the board without hedging |
| Zero manual provisioning for new services | A new product idea can go from proposal to production environment in a day |
| On-call escalation rate drops 40% | Engineers stop leaving — retention improves because the job is sustainable |

The Director writes Objectives at the business-outcome row, not the technical-state row. The technical states become Key Results (Chapter 3).

---

## The Right Altitude: State of the World, Not Project Completion

An Objective should describe how the world is different at quarter end — what is true then that is not true now. This is not about what you shipped. It is about what changed as a result.

**Project-completion altitude** (wrong): "Complete the migration of 30 services to the new reliability stack."

**State-of-the-world altitude** (right): "Our reliability stack is the default infrastructure — teams choose it because it makes their lives easier, not because we required it."

The difference matters in practice. At project-completion altitude, the quarter is over when the migration is done, regardless of whether anything improved. At state-of-the-world altitude, the quarter is over when the change is real — when teams are actually choosing the stack and actually experiencing better reliability. The completion of the migration project is necessary but not sufficient.

This is a Director-level distinction. Individual contributors think in project completions. Directors think in changed states of the world.

---

## Practice Framework

After reading this chapter, write one Objective for your current or most recent team. Apply the three tests:

1. Say it out loud. Is it one sentence? Would a senior engineer on your team be able to repeat it tomorrow?
2. Does it describe a changed state of the world, or a project milestone?
3. Does it connect to a business outcome — something the business gets to do or know or trust as a result?

If it fails any of the three tests, rewrite it before moving to Key Results. A weak Objective produces weak Key Results by construction — the measurement system cannot save an unclear direction.
