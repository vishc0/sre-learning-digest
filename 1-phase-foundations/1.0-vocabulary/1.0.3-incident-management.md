# Incident Management and Blameless Postmortems

## Why this matters for top-paying Principal SRE roles

Principal SREs are expected to lead chaos calmly. Companies pay at the top end when you can reduce business impact during incidents, coordinate teams quickly, and ensure the same incident does not repeat.

## Simple explanation

Incident management is a structured way to respond when production is unhealthy.

Think of it in 4 phases:
1. **Detect** – notice user-impact fast.
2. **Triage** – understand impact and severity.
3. **Mitigate** – restore service quickly (rollback, failover, degrade gracefully).
4. **Learn** – run a blameless postmortem and fix systemic causes.

A blameless postmortem means: focus on **system gaps**, not person blame.

---

## Phase Breakdown for First-Time Learners

Each phase has a job. The phases run mostly in sequence, but overlap — you are always assessing spread while you mitigate, and you start documenting the timeline from the moment the alert fires, not after recovery.

The single most important rule across all four phases: **restore first, explain later.** The root cause can wait five minutes. The customer cannot.

---

### Phase 1 — Detect

**Objective**: Know there is a problem before a customer tells you.

Detection is the gap between when your system actually broke and when a human becomes aware of it. This gap is called MTTD (Mean Time to Detect). High MTTD means your monitoring is either not watching the right thing or not watching at the right granularity.

**What happens in this phase:**

**1a. Signal surfaces**
Something fires — a PagerDuty alert, a Slack message from a teammate, an anomaly in a dashboard, or a support ticket from a customer. Not every signal is real. The system generates noise constantly. The first job is to determine whether this signal represents actual user impact.

*What it looks like in practice*: At 02:14 UTC, an alert fires: "Payment gateway error rate > 5% for 2 consecutive minutes." PagerDuty pages the on-call SRE.

**1b. Signal validation (30–60 seconds)**
The on-call engineer acknowledges the alert immediately — this stops the MTTD clock — then opens the primary dashboard to confirm the metric is currently elevated, not just a historical spike that already resolved. Do not start reading logs yet. You are answering one question: is this still happening right now?

*Common mistake*: Spending 10 minutes reading logs before confirming whether the alert is real. You may be debugging a false alarm.

**1c. Corroboration (60–90 seconds)**
One data point is noise. Two correlated data points are a signal. Check for a second independent signal: is the error rate spike accompanied by a latency spike? Are two teams' services alerting at the same time? Is the error pattern matching a user journey, not just a random service? Corroborating signals give you confidence before you pull people off their work.

**1d. Human acknowledgment**
The engineer posts in the incident channel: "I'm looking at this." This one message matters because it prevents a second engineer from independently investigating the same thing, and it starts the coordination record.

**Detection quality questions to ask after the incident**:
- How long was the system broken before the alert fired?
- Did customers report it before we detected it internally?
- Would better alert thresholds or synthetic monitoring have caught it earlier?

### Phase 2 — Triage

**Objective**: In under five minutes, answer: *how bad is this, and who needs to know?*

Triage is not diagnosis. Do not try to find the root cause in this phase. Triage has one output: a declared severity level with a named Incident Commander.

**What happens in this phase:**

**2a. Scope assessment (90 seconds)**
Quickly answer three questions using dashboards, not logs:
- *How many users are affected?* (Not exact — order of magnitude: 100 users or 100,000?)
- *Which user journey is broken?* (Is this checkout, login, search, or an internal tool?)
- *Is it stable, improving, or spreading?* (Is the error rate climbing, flat, or already recovering?)

Use the fastest available number: current RPS × error rate = affected requests/sec. Multiply by average session duration / requests per session to estimate affected users. An approximate answer in 90 seconds beats an exact answer in 10 minutes.

**2b. Severity declaration**
Apply your severity criteria to what you just observed. Severity is driven by business impact — users affected, revenue exposure per minute, and cascade risk. When in doubt, declare higher and downgrade. A false SEV2 costs you 30 minutes of extra attention. A missed SEV1 costs you SLA credits, customer trust, and executive confidence.

*Decision rule*: classify by the highest dimension that matches. BRI score suggests SEV3 but revenue impact is $5,000/min? That's SEV2. Multi-dimension classification always resolves to the highest.

**2c. Role assignment**
The moment you declare a severity, assign the Incident Commander out loud. For SEV1/SEV2: the IC is a named senior engineer or SRE Manager. For SEV3/SEV4: the on-call can self-manage. The IC does NOT debug. Their job is to coordinate, decide, and communicate.

Assign the other roles explicitly: Technical Lead (owns the investigation), Comms Lead (owns status page and stakeholder updates), Scribe (records the timeline). These roles prevent duplicate actions and ensure nothing falls through the gaps.

**2d. Bridge and channel creation**
Open the incident bridge (video call or phone bridge). Create the incident Slack channel. Post the declaration message with: what is broken, who is affected, severity level, IC name, and first status update commitment. This one message is the coordination anchor for the entire incident.

**2e. Initial escalation and paging**
SEV1: page VP Engineering and Customer Success within 15 minutes of declaration. SEV2: notify Engineering Director. Do not wait to see if it resolves first. Leadership wants time to prepare, not surprises.

**Triage is complete when**: severity is declared, IC is named, bridge is open, and first update is posted. Total elapsed time: under 5 minutes.

### Phase 3 — Mitigate

**Objective**: Reduce customer impact to zero as fast as possible, using the least risky action available.

Mitigation and resolution are different jobs. **Mitigation** stops the bleeding — it may not fix the root cause, but it restores service. **Resolution** is the permanent fix. During an active incident, mitigation is your only goal.

**What happens in this phase:**

**3a. Hypothesis formation (5 minutes)**
The Technical Lead forms a working hypothesis. Not a confirmed diagnosis — a best guess. "The payment gateway started returning 502s 3 minutes after the 14:30 deploy. Hypothesis: the new connection pool configuration is exhausting the gateway connection limit." This hypothesis drives the first action. If the first action fails, you form a new hypothesis. You are not trying to be right — you are trying to be fast.

*Common mistake*: The engineer who refuses to act without certainty. Certainty takes 45 minutes. Mitigation takes 5. Form the best hypothesis with available information and act on it.

**3b. Mitigation action selection**
Choose the action that reduces impact fastest with the least risk of making things worse:

| Option | When to use | Risk |
|--------|-------------|------|
| Rollback the last deploy | Error appeared within 60 min of deploy, no database migration involved | Low — known-good state |
| Feature flag / kill switch | Failure isolated to a new feature, flag infrastructure is healthy | Low — instant toggle |
| Scale out horizontally | Capacity failure, more replicas absorb load | Low — additive change |
| Traffic routing / load shedding | Upstream overload, known-good region available | Medium — state must be stateless |
| Fix-forward (hotfix) | Rollback impossible (migration ran), fix is < 10 lines and testable | Medium — change under pressure |
| Manual data repair | Data corruption or processing failure with known scope | High — dry run required first |

**3c. IC authorization**
Every action in production during a SEV1 or SEV2 requires explicit IC authorization. The engineer says what they want to do. The IC says "authorized — proceed" or "hold, let's try X first." No one changes production silently on a bridge call. This prevents two engineers from making conflicting changes at the same time.

**3d. Action execution and effect verification**
Execute the action. Wait for it to propagate (usually 30–90 seconds for a config change, up to 5 minutes for a deploy rollback). Watch the metrics. You are looking for: error rate dropping, latency recovering, user session success rate climbing. If the metric does not move within 2 minutes, the action did not work. Do not keep waiting. Form a new hypothesis.

*What verification looks like*: "Rollback is deployed. Error rate was 94% — now watching. At T+45s it's at 87%. At T+90s it's at 23%. At T+2m it's at 1.4%. Recovery confirmed."

**3e. Sustained response (if not resolved in first action)**
If the first mitigation doesn't work, or if the incident requires more than one action, switch to a sustained response posture:
- Post internal status updates every 15 minutes (SEV1) or 30 minutes (SEV2)
- Rotate fresh engineers every 60–90 minutes if the incident runs long — tired engineers make mistakes
- Keep the Scribe recording every action and its outcome in the timeline
- Do not let the bridge accumulate silent observers — "go dark or go home" rule

**3f. Resolution declaration**
The incident is resolved when **customer-facing impact is zero** — not when the dashboard turns green. These are different. A rollback that fixes error rates may still leave 40,000 cached sessions in a broken state, or CDN edge nodes serving stale content, or pending transactions in a retry queue. Resolution is verified end-to-end: run the affected user journey manually, check customer support ticket volume, confirm error rate is at baseline.

The IC declares resolution out loud and posts the closure message to the incident channel with: time of resolution, total duration, total users affected, and next step (postmortem schedule).

### Phase 4 — Learn

**Objective**: Extract the maximum possible signal from this incident so that this class of failure cannot happen again.

Learning is the phase most organizations do badly. They write a postmortem document, file it, and move on. Three months later the same failure recurs. The problem is not the document — it is what happens after: action items left unowned, root causes stated as "human error," governance that never enforces completion.

**What happens in this phase:**

**4a. Timeline reconstruction**
Within 48 hours of a SEV1 (72 hours for SEV2), build the timeline from actual evidence — alert timestamps, deploy records, Slack messages, dashboard snapshots. Not from memory. Memory degrades within hours. The timeline is the factual record of what happened, in sequence, with sources cited for every entry.

*What a good timeline entry looks like*:
> `14:32:07` — Deployment of `payment-service v2.4.1` completed (Spinnaker deploy log)
> `14:33:41` — First 502 errors from payment gateway (Datadog error rate dashboard)
> `14:35:22` — First PagerDuty alert fires (PagerDuty alert history)

The 90-second gap between deploy completion and first errors is a critical data point — it tells you the failure mode had a warm-up period, which suggests connection pool exhaustion rather than immediate code failure.

**4b. Root cause analysis**
Use a framework — not free-form prose. The CDR model is the minimum:
- **Direct cause**: the proximate trigger (what actually broke the system today)
- **Contributing causes**: pre-existing conditions that allowed the direct cause to have impact
- **Root cause**: the systemic gap that allowed this class of failure to exist — the thing that, if fixed, prevents recurrence

"Human error" is never an acceptable root cause. Humans make errors constantly. The question is: what about the system made this particular human error consequential? That is your root cause.

**4c. Detection gap analysis**
Ask: *at what point could we have known sooner?* A deployment that degraded at 14:33 should not still be degrading at 14:47. What additional monitoring, synthetic testing, or alerting threshold would have fired 10 minutes earlier? The detection gap is often more valuable to fix than the failure itself, because it reduces MTTD for all future failures — not just this one.

**4d. What worked / what didn't**
Explicitly name what the team did well. This is not praise — it is identification of practices worth preserving. If the runbook had the right steps and saved 10 minutes, that runbook should be the template for five other services. If the IC's handoff at 60 minutes prevented engineer fatigue from causing a second failure, that rotation protocol should be documented.

Then name what broke down: was communication delayed? Did two engineers unknowingly push conflicting changes? Did the monitoring show a symptom but not the cause? These are the process gaps that live alongside the technical gaps.

**4e. Action items**
Every postmortem must close with a set of action items. Quality requirements for each action item:
- **Specific**: "Add circuit breaker on payment→fraud-check call with 500ms timeout" not "improve resilience"
- **Measurable**: "Alert fires when APR drops below 0.80" not "improve alerting"
- **Owner**: one named individual, not "the team"
- **Due date**: a specific date, not "next sprint"
- **Verification criteria**: how do you know when it is done? "Canary deployment shows zero 502s under 2× load in staging"

An action item without an owner and a due date is a wish, not a commitment.

**4f. Governance: tracking to closure**
The postmortem document is worthless without follow-through. Assign action items to Jira/Linear tickets linked to the incident. Review them in the weekly SRE standup. Escalate any item that slips past two sprints. Tie recurrence of the same failure class to a reliability sprint that blocks feature work until the root cause is resolved.

The learning phase is complete not when the document is filed but when the action items are closed and verified. One closed action item that prevents a recurrence is worth more than ten perfectly-written postmortems that never change anything.

**Summary: the four objectives**

| Phase | Objective | Done when |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Detect | Know before the customer does | Alert fires before support ticket arrives |
| Triage | Severity + IC declared in < 5 min | Bridge open, roles assigned, first update posted |
| Mitigate | Customer impact = zero | Journey verified end-to-end, not just dashboard green |
| Learn | This class of failure cannot recur | Action items closed and verified, SRMI trending up |

## Real-world examples

### Example 1: Checkout outage during peak traffic
- Symptom: spike in payment timeouts.
- Action: incident commander pauses rollout, enables safe fallback payment route.
- Outcome: conversion recovered in 12 minutes.

### Example 2: Kubernetes node pressure causes API latency
- Symptom: p99 latency jump + pod evictions.
- Action: reduce noisy background jobs, scale critical workload, tune requests/limits.
- Outcome: core APIs stabilized without full outage.

### Example 3: Third-party SMS provider fails
- Symptom: OTP delivery failures.
- Action: switch to secondary provider, update status page, add retry backoff.
- Outcome: auth flow restored with temporary delay.

### Example 4: Bad deploy introduces memory leak
- Symptom: rising restarts after release.
- Action: auto-rollback, freeze canary progression, start regression analysis.
- Outcome: blast radius limited to canary scope.

## Variations and tradeoffs

- **Centralized IC model vs rotating service owner IC**
  - Centralized = consistent process.
  - Service-owner IC = stronger domain depth.

- **Fast rollback vs fix-forward**
  - Rollback is safer under uncertainty.
  - Fix-forward may be better for schema/data migrations.

- **Public updates every 15 min vs 30 min**
  - Frequent updates increase trust.
  - Too frequent can distract responders.

## Best practices and guidelines

- Define severity levels with business impact criteria.
- Assign clear roles: IC, comms lead, ops lead, scribe.
- Keep one decision log and one timeline.
- Use prebuilt runbooks for top failure modes.
- Prefer restoring service first; root cause can follow.
- Require postmortem within 24–72 hours for Sev1/Sev2.
- Track action items with owner + due date + verification.
- Measure incident quality metrics: MTTA, MTTR, recurrence rate.

## Common mistakes

- Debugging silently without clear incident command.
- Too many people changing production at once.
- No status updates to stakeholders/customers.
- Postmortems with vague actions (“improve monitoring”).
- Closing incident before verifying customer impact is gone.

## Interview Q&A (strong answers)

### Q1: How do you run a Sev1 incident?
I establish an incident bridge, assign roles, and define impact/scope in the first 5 minutes. We prioritize mitigation over perfect diagnosis, issue updates on a fixed cadence, and log all decisions. Once stabilized, we run a blameless postmortem and track actions to closure.

### Q2: What do you include in a good postmortem?
A precise timeline, impact, root cause and contributing factors, detection gaps, what worked/failed, and corrective actions with owners and deadlines. The key is measurable prevention, not narrative perfection.

### Q3: Rollback or fix-forward?
Default rollback when user impact is active and confidence is low. Choose fix-forward only when rollback risk is higher (e.g., data migration side effects) and mitigation path is clear and tested.

### Q4: How do you reduce incident recurrence?
I trend recurrence by service/failure mode, prioritize top repeat patterns, and enforce preventive engineering work: stronger tests, better runbooks, and architecture hardening (timeouts, bulkheads, retries with jitter).

## 30-day mastery plan

- **Week 1:** Learn severity model, IC roles, and communication cadence.
- **Week 2:** Build/upgrade 5 runbooks for common failures.
- **Week 3:** Run tabletop exercises and a live game day.
- **Week 4:** Perform postmortem quality review and recurrence analysis.

By day 30, you should be able to lead a high-severity incident confidently and improve the system after recovery.

## The Two Dimensions of Incident Management

Before reading the frameworks below, anchor this distinction. Every framework covers one or both of these dimensions. Conflating them is the most common reason programs fail.

| Dimension | What it is | Timescale | Who does it | Output |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Incident Management Discipline** | What your organization builds, governs, and improves *between* incidents | Ongoing — weekly, quarterly, annually | SRE Managers, Principal SREs, Engineering Directors | Runbooks, alert policies, on-call health, postmortem culture, maturity improvement |
| **Incident Handling** | What your team executes *during* a live incident, from alert to resolution | Minutes to hours | On-call engineer, Incident Commander, Technical Lead | Service restored, customer impact zero, timeline recorded |

A team that is excellent at the discipline but weak at handling has beautifully organized chaos — great runbooks no one follows under pressure. A team excellent at handling but weak at the discipline firefights the same incidents forever because nothing systemic ever gets fixed. Both must be developed, but they are taught differently.

## Major Frameworks Reference

Eight frameworks shape how the industry thinks about incident management. Each was built for a specific purpose, at a specific time, for a specific audience. Knowing which dimension each covers — and what it was designed for — tells you when to reach for it and when to look elsewhere.

---

### 1. ITIL v4 (2019)

**What it is**: ITIL (IT Infrastructure Library) is the most widely adopted IT service management framework globally. Version 4 was released in 2019, replacing ITIL v3 (2007, refreshed 2011).

**What changed from v3 to v4**: ITIL v3 organized everything into a "Service Lifecycle" of five stages with rigid processes. ITIL v4 replaced this with a **Service Value System** (SVS) built around value streams and practices. The language shifted from "processes" to "practices" — a deliberate signal that each capability should adapt to context rather than follow a fixed script. ITIL v4 also explicitly embraced DevOps and Agile, which v3 treated as incompatible.

**Which dimension it covers**: Discipline (dominant). ITIL defines the organizational capability, not the real-time mechanics.

**The three practices you must distinguish** (ITIL separates these; most orgs collapse them):

| Practice | Triggered by | Closed when | Owner |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| **Incident Management** | Service disruption or degradation | Service restored to normal | On-call / SRE |
| **Problem Management** | Recurring incidents or unknown root cause | Root cause identified and fixed | Engineering / SRE |
| **Change Enablement** | Any modification to production | Change verified and documented | Change board / SRE |

**How to interpret today**: ITIL v4 is governance vocabulary. When a VP asks "what's your incident management process?", ITIL v4 gives you the language. The practices map: Incident → your SEV model and response protocol. Problem → your postmortem and root cause process. Change → your CAB/deployment governance. Use ITIL to *align language* with non-engineering stakeholders and auditors, not to design your real-time response.

**What to ignore**: ITIL's prescriptive role titles (Service Desk, 1st-level support, 2nd-level support). These are legacy ITSM constructs from the help desk world. In SRE contexts, replace with IC/TL/on-call structures.

### 2. NIST SP 800-61 Rev 2 (2012) — with Rev 3 coming

**What it is**: The NIST Computer Security Incident Handling Guide, published by the US National Institute of Standards and Technology. The most cited incident handling lifecycle in regulated industries (finance, healthcare, government). Rev 2 is the current published standard. **Rev 3 is in public draft as of August 2024** — final publication expected 2025.

**The lifecycle model**:

```
Preparation → Detection & Analysis → Containment/Eradication/Recovery → Post-Incident Activity
```

**Three terms NIST uses precisely** (most people treat these as synonyms — they are not):

| Term | NIST definition | SRE equivalent |
|------|----------------|---------------|
| **Containment** | Stop the incident from spreading further | Circuit breaker, traffic reroute, feature flag off |
| **Eradication** | Remove the root cause entirely | Rollback, hotfix, remove compromised component |
| **Recovery** | Restore and verify normal operations | Validate end-to-end user journey, confirm metrics at baseline |

**What's changing in Rev 3**: The draft shifts focus toward cloud-native architectures, AI/ML-involved incidents, and supply chain compromise. Rev 2 was written for on-premises enterprise environments. Rev 3 modernizes the language for containerized workloads, shared-responsibility cloud models, and events that involve third-party AI systems. If you work in cloud-native SRE, the Rev 3 conceptual model is more applicable even though it's not yet final.

**How to interpret today**: Use NIST 800-61 for the **Containment → Eradication → Recovery** vocabulary. These three words give precision to what "mitigation" actually means. "We contained the blast radius, eradicated the root cause, and verified recovery" is a more precise and auditor-friendly statement than "we fixed it." Especially important in regulated environments where you must document exactly what was done and when.

**What to ignore**: The organizational structure NIST prescribes (CIRT, CSIRT, SOC) is security-team-oriented. In SRE contexts, your IC/TL/on-call rotation maps to these roles implicitly.

### 3. SANS PICERL (Evolved — not versioned)

**What it is**: A six-phase incident response model from the SANS Institute, widely used in security operations. Not formally versioned — it evolved from the SANS GIAC security curriculum and has been stable for over a decade.

**The model**:

```
Preparation → Identification → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Lessons Learned
```

**What SANS adds that NIST doesn't make explicit**: The **Identification** phase — the explicit gate between "something fired" and "we have confirmed this is an actual incident with real impact." NIST's "Detection & Analysis" collapses this into one step. SANS separates them:

- **Detection**: the signal arrives (alert fires, user reports, SOC flags)
- **Identification**: you gather enough evidence to formally declare this is an incident, classify its type, and determine scope

This gate prevents two failure modes: (1) treating noise as an incident and pulling everyone off their work for a false alarm, and (2) dismissing a real incident as noise because the first signal looked ambiguous. The Identification phase is where you validate the alert, corroborate with a second signal, and make a deliberate "yes, this is real" determination before activating the full response.

**How to interpret today**: Use PICERL's Identification phase as your explicit validation gate during triage. Every experienced SRE does this implicitly — SANS gives it a name. In interviews, PICERL is a recognized vocabulary: saying "we had strong detection but our identification step was slow — the first signal was ambiguous and we didn't corroborate for 8 minutes" is a precise, mature observation.

**What to ignore**: The security-specific framing of eradication (malware removal, attacker eviction). Map it to production-incident equivalents: eradication = rollback or root cause fix.

### 4. ICS — Incident Command System (NIMS 2017)

**What it is**: The Incident Command System was developed in the 1970s after a series of catastrophic California wildfires where firefighters from different agencies could not coordinate, causing unnecessary deaths. ICS became the US national standard for emergency management under NIMS (National Incident Management System), last comprehensively updated in 2017.

**Why it matters for SRE**: Google adapted ICS for production incidents in the early 2000s. PagerDuty codified it publicly. The IC/TL/Comms Lead structure that is now the SRE industry standard is directly derived from ICS's Incident Commander / Operations Section Chief / Public Information Officer structure.

**ICS's core principle**: In a complex emergency, the most common failure mode is **command confusion, not resource shortage**. When multiple agencies respond independently with no unified command, they conflict, duplicate, and contradict each other. The solution is a single accountable decision-maker (the IC) with a defined span of control (3–7 direct reports) and a strict rule that every action goes through the IC.

**The five ICS functional sections**:

| ICS Section | SRE Equivalent | Function during incident |
|-------------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Incident Commander | IC | Single accountable authority — does NOT debug |
| Operations | Technical Lead (TL) | Executes technical response |
| Planning | Scribe | Tracks timeline, maintains situational awareness |
| Logistics | On-call coordinator | Gets people, tools, access that responders need |
| Finance/Admin | (Director level) | Tracks cost, communicates with executives |

**Current version (NIMS 2017)**: The 2017 revision added emphasis on pre-planned coordination protocols and interoperability with private-sector partners — directly relevant to cloud providers and third-party dependencies in production incidents.

**How to interpret today**: ICS is the foundation of how you *run a bridge call*. Three rules from ICS that apply verbatim to production incidents: (1) one IC only — no co-commanders, (2) no one takes action without IC authorization, (3) anyone can call a safety stop if they see something that will make the situation worse. Everything else in your incident handling protocol is detail built on top of these three rules.

**What to ignore**: ICS's physical logistics sections (shelter, food, transportation). Also ignore the rigid "strike team" and "task force" nomenclature — the functional section names (Operations, Planning) are the transferable concepts.

### 5. Google SRE Framework (2016 + 2018)

**What it is**: Two books published by Google define the de facto SRE standard: *Site Reliability Engineering* (2016) and *The Site Reliability Workbook* (2018). Neither is a formal standard — they are documented practices from Google's production operations, made public. A third book, *Building Secure and Reliable Systems* (2020), covers the intersection of security and reliability.

**What these books cover**:

| Google SRE component | Discipline or Handling | Core concept |
|---------------------|----------------------|--------------|
| SLO / error budget policy | Discipline | Error budget exhaustion → reliability sprint mandatory |
| Blameless postmortem culture | Discipline | System gaps, not human blame |
| Alert philosophy | Discipline | Every alert must require human action — noise is a bug |
| On-call health metrics | Discipline | Sustained on-call load above threshold = structural fix required |
| IC / Operations Lead / Comms Lead roles | Handling | ICS-derived command structure for production |
| "Restore first, explain later" | Handling | Mitigation before diagnosis — always |
| MTTD / MTTR measurement | Both | Discipline: track trends; Handling: know current urgency |

**How the two books differ**:
- *SRE Book (2016)*: Theory, principles, and Google-scale examples. Chapter 14 (Managing Incidents) and Chapter 15 (Postmortem Culture) are the most directly applicable for incident management.
- *SRE Workbook (2018)*: Practical implementation. Chapter 9 (Incident Management) gives a worked example of a real Google incident with timeline, bridge transcript, and postmortem. Read this chapter first if you want the "how" not just the "why."

**How to interpret today**: These books are not versioned — Google has not released a v2. Google's internal practices have evolved significantly since 2016, but the conceptual framework remains the industry reference. The key insight that has aged best: **error budget as a governance forcing function**. Every other framework identifies the problem (postmortem actions don't get implemented). Google is the only one that solves it with a mechanism that has teeth — when budget is gone, feature work stops by policy, not by negotiation.

**What to ignore**: Google-scale examples (fleets of thousands of machines, global traffic distribution). Anchor the principles to your system's scale. The IC role works whether you have 3 engineers or 300.

### 6. PagerDuty DOMM — Digital Operations Maturity Model (Current: v2023+)

**What it is**: PagerDuty's self-assessment model for measuring how mature an organization's incident management practice is. Updated annually on their website (not published as a formal version). The model has five levels.

**The five maturity levels**:

| Level | Label | What it looks like |
|-------|-------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Reactive** | No formal process. Heroes handle incidents. No runbooks. Alerts are noise. |
| 2 | **Aware** | Alerting infrastructure exists. Severity definitions drafted. On-call rotation informal. |
| 3 | **Defined** | Named IC role, formal severity model, runbooks for top 10 failure modes, basic postmortems filed. |
| 4 | **Measured** | MTTA/MTTR tracked and trending. Action items close within SLA. Recurrence rate declining quarter-over-quarter. |
| 5 | **Self-Optimizing** | Chaos engineering practiced. Automated remediation for known failure classes. Error budget policy enforced. Blameless culture embedded — postmortems are trusted and acted on. |

**How to use it**: Run the DOMM assessment on your team at the start of any reliability program. It tells you which investments to make first. A Level 1 team should not be learning chaos engineering — they need ICS fundamentals and runbook creation. A Level 4 team should not be re-reading ITIL basics — they should be investing in automated remediation and error budget governance.

**The Level 3 trap**: Most organizations reach Level 3 and stall. They have the artifacts (severity model, runbooks, postmortem template) but the artifacts are not enforced — postmortems are filed without action items, runbooks are outdated, severity is declared inconsistently. The difference between Level 3 and Level 4 is governance: someone is checking whether the process actually runs, and MTTA/MTTR numbers are used to make decisions.

**How to interpret today**: DOMM is a diagnostic, not a curriculum. Use it to answer "what should we build next?" not "what should we learn?" in the abstract. In interviews, DOMM gives you language to assess an organization during the offer process: "What does your incident response maturity look like? Are you tracking MTTA/MTTR trends?" maps to "Are you Level 3 or Level 4?"

### 7. AWS Well-Architected Framework — Operational Excellence Pillar (Updated 2023)

**What it is**: AWS's five-pillar framework for designing and operating cloud systems. The Operational Excellence pillar covers incident management within a broader operational posture. Updated continuously — the 2022–2023 revision added explicit guidance for event-driven and serverless architectures.

**The three-phase model**:

```
Prepare → Operate → Evolve
```

**What each phase covers**:

| Phase | Dimension | Key practices |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| **Prepare** | Discipline | Runbooks, on-call design, monitoring, game days, incident roles, communication plans |
| **Operate** | Handling | Execute runbooks, escalation paths, status communication, recovery verification |
| **Evolve** | Discipline | Post-incident analysis, action item tracking, process improvement, retrospectives |

**AWS's unique contribution — the Game Day**: A scheduled exercise where a failure is deliberately injected into production (or a production-mirror environment) to test whether the Prepare artifacts actually work under realistic conditions. Game Days are the bridge between the discipline and handling dimensions — they are organized as a discipline investment (scheduled, governed, with a defined scope) but executed as real incident handling. They reveal runbook gaps, alert blind spots, and role confusion before a real incident does.

**AWS Well-Architected Tool**: AWS provides this as a free service in the AWS console. It gives you a structured questionnaire against all five pillars and generates a remediation plan. Operational Excellence questions map directly to DOMM Level 3–4 behaviors. Running this assessment gives you an external benchmark of your operational maturity.

**How to interpret today**: Use the Prepare → Operate → Evolve model to structure your reliability program roadmap. Prepare is your pre-incident investment (runbooks, alerts, roles). Operate is your handling protocol. Evolve is your postmortem and action item system. When all three are healthy, incidents resolve faster and recur less. When Prepare is weak, Operate is chaotic. When Evolve is weak, Operate handles the same incident repeatedly.

**What to ignore**: AWS-specific service recommendations (CloudWatch Synthetics, AWS FIS). Extract the principles — these apply regardless of cloud provider or whether you're on-premises.

### 8. ISO/IEC 27035 — Information Security Incident Management (2023)

**What it is**: The international standard for managing information security incidents. Published in three parts, the most recent revision across all parts was completed in 2023:

| Part | Title | Status |
|------|-------|--------|
| ISO/IEC 27035-1 | Principles of incident management | **2023** (current) |
| ISO/IEC 27035-2 | Guidelines to plan and prepare for incident response | **2023** (current) |
| ISO/IEC 27035-3 | Guidelines for ICT incident response operations | 2020 (current for operational part) |

**The five-phase lifecycle**:

```
Plan & Prepare → Detect & Report → Assessment & Decision → Response → Post-Incident
```

**ISO's key concept — the Assessment & Decision gate**: Between detection and response, ISO 27035 mandates an explicit *decision step* where a designated person formally classifies the event, determines whether it meets the threshold for an incident, and authorizes the response. This is the same as SANS's Identification phase, but ISO makes it a governance artifact — the decision must be documented, and the person making it must have designated authority.

**Why this matters in regulated industries**: In financial services, healthcare, and government contexts, auditors will ask "who decided this was an incident and on what basis?" ISO 27035's Assessment & Decision gate answers that question with a paper trail. If you work in a regulated environment (PCI-DSS, HIPAA, SOX), your incident management process needs to satisfy this gate explicitly.

**Event vs. Incident distinction** (ISO is precise; SRE practice is often not):
- **Security event**: any observable occurrence in a system or network. Alerts, log entries, anomalies. Events are constant — every production system generates thousands per day.
- **Security incident**: an event that has been *assessed as having* actual or potential adverse impact on confidentiality, integrity, or availability. Incidents are declared, not detected.

This distinction maps directly to SRE practice: a PagerDuty alert is an event. The moment an engineer or IC says "I'm declaring SEV2" — that is the incident declaration. Keeping this distinction in your process prevents both alert fatigue (treating every event as an incident) and under-response (treating every incident as just another event).

**How to interpret today**: Use ISO 27035 when your work intersects compliance, audit, or regulated data handling. Its vocabulary — "assessment and decision," "incident record," "evidence preservation" — is what legal and compliance teams speak. If you're an SRE Manager building a program that must pass a SOC 2 Type II audit or a PCI-DSS assessment, ISO 27035 is the standard the auditors reference.

**What to ignore**: The security-team organizational model. ISO assumes a dedicated ISIRT (Information Security Incident Response Team). In SRE contexts, your on-call rotation and incident command structure serves this function.

## How to Talk About These Frameworks in an Interview

Senior SRE and Director-level interviews regularly probe framework awareness. The expectation is not that you have memorized the standards — it is that you can map your real experience to industry vocabulary and show you understand what each framework is designed to solve.

**Strong pattern**: Name the framework, state what it solves, describe how you applied it or why you chose not to.

> "We used an ICS-derived command structure — one IC, no co-commanders, all bridge actions authorized. That single rule eliminated the chaos we had in earlier incidents where three engineers were making production changes simultaneously without coordination."

> "We were at roughly DOMM Level 3 — we had the artifacts but no enforcement. The shift to Level 4 came when we started tracking MTTA/MTTR as a weekly review metric and the Engineering Director reviewed action item closure rates in the monthly ops review. The artifacts didn't change. The governance did."

> "ITIL v4 helped us communicate with the compliance team. We framed our work as three separate practices — Incident Management, Problem Management, and Change Enablement — rather than one blurry 'incidents and changes' process. That alignment got the CISO to sign off on our runbook policy."

**What not to say**: "We follow ITIL" (no one truly follows ITIL prescriptively — you borrow vocabulary from it). "We use the NIST framework" (be specific — 800-61? 800-53? SP 800-82?). Name the specific publication, not just the organization.
