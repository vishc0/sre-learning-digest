# Capacity Engineering, Performance, and Cost Optimization (FinOps for SRE)

## Why this matters for top-paying Principal SRE roles

At Principal level, you are expected to keep systems fast and reliable **without wasting money**. Top companies reward SREs who can balance performance, resilience, and cloud cost using data—not guesses.

## Simple explanation

This topic is about answering three questions continuously:
1. **Do we have enough capacity?** (avoid outages)
2. **Are we meeting performance goals?** (good user experience)
3. **Are we spending efficiently?** (avoid overprovisioning)

A practical way to think about it:
- Capacity protects availability.
- Performance protects user trust.
- Cost optimization protects business margin.

## Real-world examples

### Example 1: Seasonal traffic spike
- Problem: holiday traffic is 4x baseline.
- Solution: forecast demand, pre-scale critical tiers, and reserve headroom.
- Outcome: no saturation events during peak.

### Example 2: Latency regression after a new feature
- Problem: p99 API latency rises by 35%.
- Solution: profile hot path, cache expensive reads, and optimize DB query/index.
- Outcome: latency returns within SLO.

### Example 3: Cloud bill jumps 28% in one quarter
- Problem: oversized nodes + idle workloads in non-prod.
- Solution: right-size instances, schedule shutdowns, and enforce quotas.
- Outcome: spend reduced while SLOs remain stable.

### Example 4: Queue backlog causes delayed processing
- Problem: consumers cannot keep up during bursts.
- Solution: autoscale workers by lag + apply backpressure and priority queues.
- Outcome: stable processing latency under burst load.

## Variations and tradeoffs

- **Overprovision vs tight provisioning**
  - Overprovision reduces risk but wastes cost.
  - Tight provisioning lowers cost but risks saturation.

- **Vertical scaling vs horizontal scaling**
  - Vertical is simpler short-term.
  - Horizontal is resilient but can increase coordination complexity.

- **Reserved capacity vs on-demand/spot**
  - Reserved gives predictable cost.
  - Spot can cut cost but needs interruption-tolerant design.

- **Low latency vs low cost**
  - Lower latency often needs more compute/cache.
  - Cost optimization may require controlled latency tradeoffs for non-critical paths.

## Best practices and guidelines

- Define performance SLIs and SLOs (p95/p99 latency, throughput, saturation).
- Build demand forecasts from history + business events.
- Keep explicit headroom policy for Tier-1 services.
- Use load testing for known peaks and release readiness.
- Right-size CPU/memory regularly (not once a year).
- Track cost per request/transaction for business visibility.
- Segment dashboards by critical vs non-critical workloads.
- Set guardrails: quotas, budgets, anomaly alerts.

## Common mistakes

- Planning capacity using averages only.
- Ignoring tail latency (p99).
- Scaling blindly without bottleneck profiling.
- Cost cutting that harms user-critical services.
- No ownership for cost-performance decisions.

## Interview Q&A (strong answers)

### Q1: How do you do capacity planning for critical services?
I combine historical traffic, seasonality, and business event multipliers, then test with stress/load scenarios. I define headroom targets by service tier and track saturation leading indicators so we scale proactively.

### Q2: What metrics matter most for performance tuning?
User-facing latency (p95/p99), error rate, throughput, and saturation. Then I drill into dependency timings, queue lag, and resource contention to locate bottlenecks.

### Q3: How do you optimize cost without hurting reliability?
I start with workload classification. For critical paths, reliability SLOs are non-negotiable. For lower tiers, I apply aggressive right-sizing and scheduling. I monitor cost per transaction alongside SLO attainment.

### Q4: What is your FinOps approach as an SRE leader?
Joint ownership with engineering and finance, shared KPIs, monthly optimization reviews, and automated guardrails (budgets, anomaly detection, and policy checks).

## 30-day mastery plan

- **Week 1:** Audit top services for saturation, p99 latency, and spend.
- **Week 2:** Build demand forecast and headroom model for Tier-1 systems.
- **Week 3:** Run performance profiling + focused optimization changes.
- **Week 4:** Implement cost guardrails and publish cost-per-service dashboard.

By day 30, you should be able to present a clear performance-and-cost strategy with measurable reliability impact.
