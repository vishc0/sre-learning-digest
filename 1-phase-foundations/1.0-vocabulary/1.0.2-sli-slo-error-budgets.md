# SLI, SLO, and Error Budgets

## Why this matters for high-paying Principal SRE roles

At Principal level, your value comes from making reliability objective, measurable, and aligned with business priorities. SLI/SLO/error budget frameworks give you a common language that connects engineering, product, and leadership.

Without this framework, teams often:
- Argue based on opinion. no focus on what and why. just random discussions. 
- Over-alert/over telemetry results in More noise than actionable tasks.
- Pursue unrealistic “five nines” goals for low-impact services. cost of time and effort to the result ratio becomes bad. the not important and non urgent things takeup most of time and effort resulting in business failure as critical and important items never get addressed. 
- Sacrifice delivery speed due to fear. if there are no metrics to track, every change has probablity of failure, and not delivering of which keeps stacking up changes and hence the probablity of failure for those accumulative changes will cause 100% failure.

With this framework, you can:
- Define what “good enough reliability” means for delivery team, that is tracable, measurable, Agreeable and gives ability to decide(Go/NoGo).
- Prioritize work based on budget burn, not politics.Delivery has to be done within confined limits of Error budget and the burn rates. Focus on agreed reliability increases, which if breaks, everyone is on same page that iot has to be rolled back and reworked. 
- Balance innovation and stability transparently. concistent reliable deliveries of new features is the contribution to busines and inturn contribute to either value to customer or increase revenue to business. 

This is a core competency for Principal SRE interviews, especially when discussing service ownership, governance, and org-level reliability strategy.

## Simple explanation

### SLI (Service Level Indicator)
A metric that reflects user experience. this is the thing that we want to measure and track. 
- Examples: successful request rate, p95 latency, fresh data availability.

### SLO (Service Level Objective)
A target for an SLI over a time window. this is the thresholds that tells us the metrics are good vs bad, determines wether the service is good or bad. metrics with a value over a period of time. when translates to a rate for the team to track. 
- Example: “99.9% of checkout requests succeed over 30 days.”

### Error budget
The allowed unreliability derived from the SLO. this is the 100% - the SLO : this is the scope of Errors the Service can actually have and (100%-SLO) / Time == the Rate of burn a service can have. in the error rates.

this inturn means that if the Change request can be delivered with in Error burn rate or error budget, then no one should object. features can be quickly and reliably delivered in the fastest way possible but when the needle goes high on either error budget or error burn rate, then team has to slow down and reengineer to decrease the burn rate and error budget consumption and then redeliver the feature. rollback is evident in these cases with redelivery of the same feature. 
- If SLO is 99.9%, budget is 0.1% failed/slow requests in that window.
- Budget is a decision tool:
  - Healthy budget: ship features faster.
  - Burned budget: pause risky changes and improve reliability.

Quick formula:
$$
\text{Error Budget} = 1 - \text{SLO}
$$

If SLO is $99.95\%$, budget is $0.05\%$.

## Real-world examples

### 1) Checkout API for online retail
- **SLI**: successful checkout requests / total checkout requests.
- **SLO**: 99.95% monthly success.
- **Budget use**:
  - A risky release is allowed only if budget burn is below threshold.
  - If burn spikes after deploy, rollout halts and rollback triggers.
- Outcome: protects revenue-critical flow while enabling controlled velocity.

### 2) Internal analytics dashboard
- **SLI**: p95 query latency.
- **SLO**: p95 < 2 seconds for 99% of 5-minute windows during business hours.
- **Budget use**:
  - Accept slightly lower reliability than customer-facing checkout.
  - More relaxed target avoids overinvestment in low-criticality workload.
- Outcome: practical reliability target matched to business impact.

### 3) Mobile push notification pipeline
- **SLI**: delivery within 2 minutes for time-sensitive campaigns.
- **SLO**: 98% within 2 minutes in rolling 7 days.
- **Budget use**:
  - If budget consumption rises, throttle non-urgent campaigns.
  - Prioritize queue and worker stability work.
- Outcome: avoids silent degradation and improves campaign trust.

### 4) Platform auth service
- **SLI**: authentication success rate and p99 latency.
- **SLO**: 99.99% success and p99 < 300ms.
- **Budget use**:
  - Very strict because blast radius is platform-wide.
  - Aggressive change control and multi-step canarying.
- Outcome: protects all downstream products from cascading login failures.

## Variations/tradeoffs

- **High SLO vs engineering cost**
  - 99.99% can be much harder than 99.9%.
  - Last “nine” often requires disproportionate complexity and cost.

- **User-journey SLIs vs component SLIs**
  - User-journey SLIs map better to customer impact.
  - Component SLIs are easier to instrument but can miss real UX issues.

- **Short windows vs long windows**
  - Short windows catch acute incidents faster. alerts for e.g. must be for 15 min interval. if they are for 1-2 hrs then the issue already happened and surfaced to impact and blast radius is already impacted. but long ranges must be used to understand patterns like traffic patterns, anomaly detection, or comparative study hourly, daily weekly or monthly.can detect more complex problems. 
  - Long windows smooth noise but may hide short severe outages.

- **Uniform SLOs vs service-tiered SLOs**
  - Uniform targets are simple.
  - Tiered targets better reflect criticality and cost efficiency.
  - if there are complex tiers involved in the user joueneys, then tier aproach must be implemented to calculate SLA, SLI and SLO. that will result in calucation of Error budgets and error rates and response times fo each underlying services. and more tight and tight controls over the last tiers. 
  but if the architecture is simple then a broader and simple and uniform SLO/SLA/SLI can be imposed on all Services orr api calls and the Error budgets need not be shared. 

- **Strict burn policies vs flexible governance**
  - Strict policies improve discipline.
  - Flexible policies enable pragmatic exceptions for strategic launches.
  - a clear example is that we dont have time and need an exception ==> indicates that SRE folks had to be involved in planning and execution and delivery of the features from day 1. this often goes without saying that next time we will do it, and so the same problem cannot be repeated. NFRs are for a reason and then have to be addressed. this compromizes security , user experience and quality of delivery and later will impact reputation of the company. better later than never so have process for teams to follow if it cannot be implemented right now. SRE COE must have fallback excetions to be rework items. when everything is said and done , before celebrations begin certain FNRs must be taken and must be incorporated into the delivery. 

## Best practices and guidelines

- Start with critical user journeys, not internal metrics.
  - Identify Journeys, Systems, Applications, ==> Define metrics, measure and then deifne SLO for each layer and app. and then plan to improve. 
  - this makes measurable metrics to work towards for each developer and each team to target. 
- Keep SLIs small in number and high in relevance. follow 1-Success Rates, 2-Latency, 3-Thresold & Capacity.
- Make SLOs realistic and evidence-based. there must be a org standard to reflect onto and measure the journey against. and thereby determine the SLO for journey, platforms, and apps in the flow. ==> that shall determine SLO at each app level, and error burn rate for each app in the flow. 
- Use percentile latency (p95/p99), not just averages.
- Exclude planned maintenance and clearly define valid events.
- Establish burn-rate alerts (fast-burn + slow-burn) for each events.(deployments)
- Tie release gates to error budget policy.(release gating must include SLo and Error budgets and Error burn rates expected and avaiable.)
- Segment by region, tenant, or feature if behavior differs significantly.
- Review SLOs quarterly with product + engineering together.
  - a service may be part of multiple journeys, the lowest SLo muys tbe the baseline for each service or platfrom level or atleast deliniated as haveing multiple SLO for different functionality in the same service. 
- Document what happens when budget is exhausted.
  - there will be no burn period if the erro rbudgets are reached completely. 
  - due to certain windows and sensitivity of the business , there are soft and hard freeze dates for CRs to go in and in these days the Error budget and Error rates must be aligned to be zero. ensure this is par tof planning like NPI , holidays period, intervals after major incidents.

## Interview questions with strong sample answers

### Q1) "How would you define SLIs for a new service?"
**Strong answer:**
I start from user journeys and identify measurable outcomes users care about: success, latency, and freshness/completeness where relevant. I avoid proxy metrics first. Then I validate data quality, define collection boundaries, and ensure segmentation by high-impact dimensions like region and client type. I choose a minimal set of SLIs that are actionable and map directly to reliability decisions.==> this is the top bottom approach, can be simple uniform standards, or complex and multi flows oriented and Diff SLo for different functionality. no one size fits all, this can also be tiered depending on the call depth.

### Q2) "What happens when error budget is exhausted?"
**Strong answer:**
Error budget exhaustion triggers a policy shift: reduce change risk and prioritize reliability work. Typical actions include pausing high-risk feature rollouts, requiring stronger release approvals, and focusing engineering capacity on incident drivers. The exact policy is pre-agreed to avoid ad hoc debates during stress. Once burn stabilizes, normal delivery pace resumes.

### Q3) "How do you avoid setting bad SLOs?"
**Strong answer:**
I use historical performance data, customer impact analysis, and service criticality tiers. I also run a calibration period before enforcing policy. A good SLO should be ambitious but attainable, and it should influence behavior. If an SLO never breaches or always breaches, it is poorly tuned. I iterate quarterly with stakeholders.

### Q4) "How are SLOs different for critical vs non-critical systems?"
**Strong answer:**
For critical systems like auth or payments, I set tighter objectives, stricter burn policies, and stronger release controls because blast radius is large. For non-critical systems, I set more cost-effective targets and allow more flexibility. Reliability targets should reflect business value and failure impact, not a one-size-fits-all standard.

### Q5) "What alerts would you configure from SLOs?"
**Strong answer:**
I use multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerts: a fast-burn alert for acute incidents and a slow-burn alert for chronic degradation. This catches both severe outages and gradual reliability drift while reducing alert fatigue. I tune thresholds to trigger action, not noise, and tie each alert to a runbook.

## 30-day mastery plan

### Days 1-7: Core understanding
- Study SLI/SLO/error budget definitions and anti-patterns.
- Review 2-3 public SRE case studies on objective setting.
- Pick one service and draft candidate user-journey SLIs.
- Create a glossary for SLA vs SLO vs OLA.

### Days 8-14: Instrumentation and baselining
- Validate telemetry quality for selected SLIs.
- Build baseline graphs (30-90 days if available).
- Draft initial SLOs with rationale and confidence level.
- Identify segmentation needs (region, endpoint, customer tier).

### Days 15-21: Governance and operations
- Define error budget policy with concrete actions.
- Configure burn-rate alerts and escalation paths.
- Write runbooks for top SLO failure scenarios.
- Simulate breaches and test response workflow.

### Days 22-30: Principal-level rollout
- Present SLO framework to product and engineering leads.
- Pilot release gating using budget status.
- Review early outcomes: alert quality, deployment behavior, incident trends.
- Refine targets and policies based on observed impact.

### Outcomes by day 30
You should be able to:
- Build SLI/SLOs tied to user value.
- Use error budgets for objective delivery decisions.
- Implement burn-rate alerting with actionable runbooks.
- Communicate reliability posture to both technical and business stakeholders.
