# Enterprise Architecture Patterns for SRE Leaders

This chapter is designed for professionals who operate as both **Solution Architect** and **Enterprise Architect**, while driving reliability outcomes.

## 1) Why this matters at principal level

At enterprise scale, your impact is no longer at single-service level only. You shape:
- Portfolio-wide architecture direction
- Platform standards and guardrails
- Cross-domain risk and resilience posture
- Business continuity and regulatory confidence

A senior SRE with architecture depth is expected to answer:
- Which pattern fits this business capability?
- What are the tradeoffs in risk, cost, latency, and compliance?
- How do we standardize without slowing product teams?

## 2) Fundamentals you must know deeply

## 2.1 Capability-first architecture

Start with business capabilities (billing, identity, fulfillment, risk, customer support), then map systems to capabilities.

Why this matters:
- Reduces architecture by org chart
- Improves ownership clarity
- Makes resilience investments business-relevant

## 2.2 Domain boundaries and bounded contexts

Use clear domain boundaries to reduce coupling and blast radius.

Signals of good boundaries:
- Clear API contracts
- Independent deployment capability
- Local data ownership

## 2.3 Reliability tiers

Not all systems deserve identical reliability targets.

Typical tiering:
- Tier 0/1: revenue and customer-critical
- Tier 2: important but tolerates degraded operation
- Tier 3: internal/non-critical

Map each tier to SLOs, RTO/RPO, and release controls.

## 2.4 Failure-domain design

Architect around failure domains:
- Process/pod
- Node
- Availability zone
- Region
- Provider

Principle: failure in one domain should not collapse unrelated capabilities.

## 2.5 Control plane vs data plane

Many architectures protect data plane services but ignore control-plane dependencies (CI/CD, IAM, DNS, PKI, secrets, artifact repositories).

Control-plane outages frequently create broad enterprise impact.

## 2.6 Architecture decision records (ADR)

ADRs capture why a pattern was chosen and what was rejected.

Strong ADR includes:
- Context and constraints
- Decision and rationale
- Tradeoffs and risks
- Review trigger conditions

## 3) Advanced concepts and strategic patterns

## 3.1 Modular monolith -> domain services (evolutionary architecture)

Pattern: evolve incrementally instead of forced big-bang microservices.

Use when:
- Team maturity is uneven
- Domain boundaries are still emerging

## 3.2 Strangler pattern for modernization

Pattern: route selected capabilities from legacy systems to new services over time.

Benefits:
- Controlled migration risk
- Business continuity during transition

## 3.3 Event-driven architecture with replay safety

Pattern: decouple systems using events and asynchronous processing.

Critical safeguards:
- Idempotency keys
- Exactly-once expectations replaced with effectively-once design
- Dead-letter and replay workflows

## 3.4 Cell-based architecture

Pattern: partition architecture into semi-independent “cells” (tenant/region/domain slices) to limit blast radius.

Tradeoff: operational complexity rises, but fault isolation improves dramatically.

## 3.5 Service mesh with policy and resilience controls

Pattern: platform-level traffic policy for retries, timeouts, mTLS, and observability.

Risk: hidden complexity if teams do not understand policy inheritance.

## 3.6 Multi-region active-active / active-passive

Decision framework:
- Business downtime tolerance
- Data consistency constraints
- Regulatory locality requirements
- Team operational maturity

## 3.7 CQRS + read-model optimization

Pattern: separate write and read paths for high-scale or latency-sensitive systems.

Useful when read patterns dominate and strict transactional coupling creates bottlenecks.

## 3.8 API gateway + backend-for-frontend (BFF)

Pattern: gateway for cross-cutting concerns; BFF for client-specific optimization.

Benefits:
- Better security and contract governance
- Better user experience per channel

## 3.9 Platform APIs and internal products

Treat platform capabilities (build, deploy, telemetry, secrets, policy) as products.

Metrics:
- Time-to-first-deploy
- Lead time for safe change
- Adoption and developer satisfaction

## 3.10 Policy-as-code architecture

Pattern: compliance and reliability controls codified and enforced automatically.

Examples:
- Must-have probes and resource limits
- Restricted public exposure paths
- Mandatory telemetry labels

## 4) Enterprise pattern catalog (what to apply where)

## 4.1 Integration patterns

- **Synchronous request/response:** simple, low latency, tightly coupled
- **Async messaging:** decoupled, resilient, eventual consistency
- **Batch exchange:** high throughput, delayed freshness
- **CDC (change data capture):** event propagation from transactional stores

Use a hybrid model; one style rarely fits all.

## 4.2 Data patterns

- Polyglot persistence by capability
- Data mesh governance where domain ownership is mature
- Caching hierarchy (edge/app/data)
- Archive and retention patterns by compliance class

## 4.3 Security patterns

- Zero trust service-to-service identity
- Token exchange and short-lived credentials
- Secrets broker pattern
- Segmentation by trust zones

## 4.4 Resilience patterns

- Timeouts + bounded retries + jitter
- Circuit breaker and bulkhead
- Graceful degradation
- Shed-load for non-critical operations

## 4.5 Delivery patterns

- Trunk-based development + feature flags
- Canary and blue/green rollouts
- Expand/contract DB migration
- Automated rollback with health gates

## 4.6 Governance patterns

- Reliability review board
- Architecture runway planning
- Exception process with expiration dates
- Portfolio risk heatmap reviews

## 5) How architecture connects to programs, projects, platforms, products

## 5.1 Program level (strategy)

You set architectural guardrails, service tiering, funding priorities, and risk acceptance policy.

## 5.2 Project level (execution)

You influence delivery sequencing, dependency management, and release risk controls.

## 5.3 Platform level (shared capability)

You ensure reusable reliability and security defaults across teams.

## 5.4 Product level (customer value)

You align architecture choices to customer journeys and revenue-critical paths.

## 5.5 Operational level (runtime)

You connect design assumptions to production reality via SLOs, incidents, and postmortem loops.

## 6) Enterprise anti-patterns to watch for

- Architecture by org chart instead of domain
- Shared databases across unrelated domains
- No versioning strategy for APIs/events
- “One-size-fits-all” reliability and security controls
- Central review bottlenecks with no automation
- Ignoring control-plane dependencies
- Exception approvals with no expiry

## 7) Decision frameworks you should practice

## 7.1 Risk-impact-cost triangle

For each architecture decision, evaluate:
- Risk reduced/increased
- Business impact protected/exposed
- Build and run cost delta

## 7.2 Reversibility test

Prefer decisions that are reversible early in uncertainty.

## 7.3 Blast-radius-first design

If a change fails, how much can break? choose the design with smaller blast radius where possible.

## 7.4 Operability test

Can on-call teams debug and recover this architecture at 2 AM? If not, redesign.

## 8) How to present as experienced in enterprise interviews

Demonstrate you can:
- Draw target + transition architecture (not only end-state)
- Explain patterns and anti-patterns with tradeoffs
- Tie technical decisions to business and compliance outcomes
- Define governance that enables speed with safety
- Show measurable outcomes from past architecture changes

## 9) 30-day deepening plan

- **Week 1:** Build capability map and tiering model for one enterprise domain.
- **Week 2:** Produce pattern selection matrix (integration, data, resilience, security).
- **Week 3:** Draft ADRs for 3 major architecture decisions.
- **Week 4:** Build executive-ready architecture narrative: risk, investment, expected outcomes.

If you can do this repeatedly, you operate at enterprise-architect + principal-SRE level.
