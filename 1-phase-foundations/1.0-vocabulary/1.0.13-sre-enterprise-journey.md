# New SRE in a Real Enterprise: 90-Day Journey Playbook

This chapter simulates what a new SRE might experience at large enterprises (for example: telecom, gaming, banking, BPO, energy, regulated sectors).

## What day 1 feels like

You do not start by “fixing everything.” You start by building context.

### What you usually see first

- Multiple environments, clouds, and legacy dependencies
- Several ticketing and alerting systems
- Competing priorities from product, security, and operations
- Existing incidents recurring in patterns

### Your immediate goal

Understand **how the business makes money** and **which customer journeys cannot fail**.

## Days 1–30: orient, observe, and map risk

### What to do

- Learn service catalog and ownership map
- Identify Tier-1 services and critical customer journeys
- Read the last 10 major incidents and postmortems
- Review current SLOs, if any
- Join incident bridges silently first, then actively

### What to produce

- Dependency map for 2–3 critical flows
- Top 10 operational risks with likely business impact
- Alert quality summary (noise vs signal)
- Runbook gap list

### Example

In a telecom-like environment, your Tier-1 flow may be:
`Login -> Plan Selection -> Billing -> Provisioning`

If billing is slow, conversion and support calls both rise.

## Days 31–60: stabilize and standardize

### What to do

- Tune alerting for actionability
- Build/rebuild golden dashboards
- Introduce burn-rate alerts for critical SLOs
- Improve top runbooks and escalation paths
- Implement safer release gates for high-risk services

### What to produce

- MTTA/MTTR baseline and improvement plan
- Incident severity matrix with clear criteria
- Service health review template
- Weekly reliability review cadence

### Example

In a gaming platform, matchmaker latency spikes during events. You may:
- Add queue-depth autoscaling
- Prioritize matchmaker over non-critical analytics
- Set event-day operational playbook

## Days 61–90: scale improvements and build trust

### What to do

- Partner with product and platform leads on reliability roadmap
- Introduce reliability scorecards by service tier
- Run one game day for a known high-risk dependency
- Track action-item closure from postmortems

### What to produce

- Quarterly reliability priorities tied to business outcomes
- Reliability investment proposals (effort vs impact)
- Cross-team ownership model (RACI)

### Example

In a banking-like environment, auth and transaction paths may require tighter SLOs and stricter release controls than internal reporting systems.

## Day-to-day routine of an effective enterprise SRE

A realistic daily rhythm:

1. Review overnight incidents and error-budget burn
2. Validate top alerts and operational noise
3. Join release/change reviews for risk-sensitive services
4. Support active incidents or reliability debt work
5. Update runbooks, dashboards, or automation
6. End day with reliability checkpoint and handoff notes

## Skills you develop that are rarely taught explicitly

- Stakeholder communication under uncertainty
- Prioritizing by business impact, not technical elegance
- Negotiating tradeoffs between delivery speed and reliability risk
- Saying “not now” with data and alternatives
- Converting incident lessons into durable engineering changes

## How to perform like an experienced SRE faster

- Learn the business terms used in your company
- Speak in customer impact and risk language
- Be consistent in incident process and communication
- Build small wins early (alert cleanup, runbook clarity, dashboard accuracy)
- Avoid heroics; build repeatable mechanisms

## Common traps for new enterprise SREs

- Trying to redesign architecture before understanding constraints
- Focusing only on tooling and ignoring people/process
- Treating every alert as equally important
- Solving one incident without fixing recurrence patterns

## Practical checklist to “look senior” quickly

- Can you explain top 5 business-critical flows?
- Can you map each to SLOs and dependencies?
- Can you lead a Sev2 bridge confidently?
- Can you propose a 90-day reliability roadmap with metrics?

If yes, you are already operating near experienced enterprise expectations.
