# Platform Engineering | Week 6 Day 3

## Why This Matters (Interview Relevance + Business Impact)

At Staff and Principal SRE level, interviewers stop asking "can you operate a platform?" and start asking "can you build one that a hundred teams use without calling you?" Self-service infrastructure is the answer to that question.

**Interview signals this topic unlocks:**
- "How do you scale your SRE team's impact beyond headcount?"
- "How do you enforce guardrails without blocking developers?"
- "What's your philosophy on platform engineering vs. traditional ops?"
- "How do you measure the ROI of an internal developer platform?"

**Business impact at T-Mobile scale:**
- 25M msg/day platform, 15-person SRE team. If every app team opens a ticket to get a new K8s namespace + IAM role, your team spends 40% of sprint capacity on toil. Golden paths eliminate that toil structurally, not by adding headcount.
- The Netflix/Spotify model: SRE teams shrink (in ratio) as platform matures. A Director-level hire is expected to know how to build that leverage, not just manage on-call rotas.

## Analogy — The Airport Security Lane

Think about how T-Mobile rolls out a new device at retail. Corporate does not hand each store a toolbox and say "build your own display case." They ship a **planogram** — a pre-engineered, pre-approved shelf layout. Store managers execute it. They can request exceptions, but the default is pre-built.

Golden paths are the planogram for your engineering org.

- **Golden path** = the planogram. Pre-built, pre-approved, works out of the box.
- **Crossplane** = the warehouse that auto-ships the display case components when you submit the order form.
- **Port (or Backstage)** = the order form system — the UI/API where teams say "I need a new microservice."
- **Tech radar** = the catalog of approved display fixtures. Items in ADOPT are planogram-approved. Items in HOLD are legacy parts you're discontinuing.
- **Golden cage** = when you lock the store so tightly that managers can't move a shelf even when the store layout demands it.

## 30-Second Version

A golden path is a pre-built, pre-approved way for a developer to get from "I have an idea" to "I have a running service in production" without opening a ticket. Crossplane lets you define that path in Kubernetes-native YAML, so one manifest apply creates a GitHub repo, a namespace, a ServiceAccount, and an AWS IAM role simultaneously. The goal is to make the right thing the easy thing — not the only thing.

---

## 2-Minute Version

**The problem golden paths solve:** In a large org, every new microservice needs 12 things: namespace, service account, IAM role, ECR repo, GitHub repo, Datadog monitor, PagerDuty rotation, VPN policy... Each is owned by a different team. Without a golden path, a developer waits two weeks for tickets. With a golden path, they fill out one form and get all 12 in 20 minutes.

**Crossplane's role:** Crossplane runs inside your K8s cluster as a set of controllers. You define a Composition (the recipe) and a CompositeResourceDefinition (the schema). When a developer submits a CompositeResource manifest, Crossplane's controllers call the AWS API, GitHub API, and whatever else you wired up — all declaratively, all reconciled like any K8s object. Drift gets corrected automatically. This is Terraform's job, but done the Kubernetes way: always-on reconciliation loop, not a one-shot `plan/apply`.

**Port vs. Backstage:** Backstage (Spotify) is the market leader but requires significant engineering investment to self-host, maintain plugins, and keep catalogs current — it's a platform product you have to build. Port is a SaaS-first alternative with a visual workflow builder. If you have a platform team of 3 engineers, Port is faster to value. If you have a platform team of 10 and want full ownership, Backstage scales better.

**IDP KPIs that matter to a Director:**
- Onboarding time (P95): days from "approved new service" to first deploy in prod
- Ticket deflection rate: % of infra requests handled via self-service vs. tickets to SRE
- Golden path adoption rate: % of services using the standard stack vs. custom builds
- DORA metrics (deploy frequency, MTTR, change failure rate) — the platform's job is to move all four in the right direction

## 5-Minute Version — Tradeoffs, Failure Modes, Design Decisions

### Golden Path vs. Paved Road — the philosophy split

There are two schools of thought in platform engineering:

**Golden path (opinionated):** "Here is the one way we do this. It is well-lit, well-maintained, and fast. You can go off-path but you own that maintenance." Netflix coined this model. Works well when: org is large, security/compliance requirements are uniform, and you have a dedicated platform team to maintain the path.

**Paved road (permissive):** "Here is the highway, here are the on-ramps. The highway has guardrails. You can take a dirt road, but the highway has the speed cameras and rest stops." Works better in organizations with many distinct product verticals that have legitimately different infrastructure needs.

**Your call as a Director:** Start with paved road philosophy, implement it via golden path tooling. This means: the golden path is the default, escape hatches are documented and supportable, and choosing to go off-path triggers a lightweight architecture review (not a ticket queue).

### Crossplane vs. Terraform — when to use which

| Dimension | Crossplane | Terraform |
|---|---|---|
| Reconciliation | Continuous — drift corrected automatically | One-shot — requires re-plan/apply |
| Runtime | Runs inside K8s cluster | CLI or CI/CD runner |
| State management | K8s etcd (no separate state file) | Remote state (S3 + DynamoDB) |
| Developer UX | kubectl apply (familiar to K8s teams) | terraform apply (separate toolchain) |
| Maturity | Younger, fewer providers | Mature, 3000+ providers |
| Best for | K8s-native platforms, self-service portals | Foundational infra, multi-cloud, team not K8s-first |

**Recommendation for a Director:** Do not pick one and mandate it org-wide. Use Terraform for foundational infrastructure (VPCs, EKS clusters, accounts). Use Crossplane for developer-facing self-service within those foundations. Layer, don't replace.

### How Golden Paths Become Golden Cages — and how to prevent it

**Signs your golden path became a cage:**
1. Teams fork the golden path template rather than contributing changes back
2. Exception request queue is longer than 5 items per sprint
3. "Platform-compliant" services are slower to deploy than non-compliant ones
4. Security reviews catch issues that the golden path should have caught at template level

**Prevention mechanisms:**
- **Tech radar governance cadence:** Quarterly review. Any tool in HOLD for 3 quarters either gets deprecated or gets a migration path. The catalog never stagnates.
- **Escape hatch SLA:** A team that needs to go off-path gets a documented, time-bounded exception — not a permanent fork. If 3 teams request the same exception, you add it to the golden path.
- **Platform as a product:** Treat your internal platform like an external SaaS. Run office hours. Have a product roadmap. Collect NPS from app teams quarterly. If your NPS is below 30, you have a cage problem.
- **Contribution model:** App teams can submit PRs to the golden path. Platform team reviews within 5 business days. This is the key to preventing the platform team from becoming a bottleneck.

### Failure Modes

**Failure Mode 1 — Platform team becomes the new tickets team.** Golden path requires so much customization per team that SRE engineers spend all sprint customizing templates instead of maintaining them. Fix: hard limit on per-team customizations; if it needs custom logic, it goes into the composition, not the request form.

**Failure Mode 2 — Composition sprawl.** You create a separate Composition for every team's requirements. 6 months in, you have 40 Compositions, 3 of which are actively maintained. Fix: Composition versioning + deprecation policy from Day 1.

**Failure Mode 3 — Crossplane provider outage blocks all self-service.** If your Crossplane AWS provider loses connectivity to AWS, no new provisioning works. Fix: circuit-breaker pattern — manual provisioning runbook always exists and is tested quarterly, just like a failover drill.

## Director/VP Lens

**How a VP governs golden paths (not just implements them):**

The IC question is "does the Composition work?" The Director question is "does the platform reduce our risk profile while accelerating delivery?"

**Governance model you own:**
1. **Platform product owner role** — assign one person who is accountable for IDP NPS, onboarding time SLA, and tech radar cadence. This is not the SRE manager. It is a dedicated role.
2. **Quarterly tech radar review** — formalize the process. Invite architects, security leads, and 2-3 rotating app team leads. The output is a published decision, not a private Slack thread.
3. **Budget justification** — platform team is overhead until you measure ticket deflection. Track it from week one. "Our golden path deflected 340 tickets last quarter" is the ROI story that keeps your platform team headcount in the budget cycle.
4. **AI augmentation angle** — GitHub Copilot + golden path templates = developers write less boilerplate AND deploy into a compliant stack. The combination is the story you tell in 2026 interviews. "We reduced mean time to first production deploy from 14 days to 4 hours, measured it, and attributed 60% of the gain to the golden path + AI-assisted scaffolding."

**The key Director framing for interviews:**
> "My job is to make the right thing the easy thing, not the only thing. A golden path that developers bypass isn't a path — it's a wall. I measure success by adoption rate and NPS, not by compliance mandates."

## Comprehension Check

Answer these before the lab. You need 2 of 3 correct.

**Q1.** Your Crossplane Composition provisions a K8s namespace + an AWS S3 bucket. A developer applies a CompositeResource YAML and the namespace gets created but the S3 bucket fails because the IAM role attached to the Crossplane provider lacks `s3:CreateBucket` permission. What does Crossplane do — does it roll back the namespace? Why or why not?

**Q2.** You have a golden path for deploying a new microservice. Three teams this quarter have submitted exceptions to use a different secrets management tool than the standard (Vault). What is the right platform engineering response, and what governance process does this trigger?

**Q3.** An app team lead tells you: "The golden path takes 3 days to provision a new service because the JIRA approval workflow is baked into the template." This is a golden cage symptom. Name two specific changes you would make to the platform design, and what metric would tell you the fix worked?

---

## ANSWER KEY — Comprehension Check

**A1.** Crossplane does NOT roll back the namespace. Crossplane's reconciliation model is eventually consistent, not transactional. Each managed resource in a Composition is reconciled independently. The namespace will stay in place; the S3 bucket will show a `Synced: False` / `Ready: False` condition on the CompositeResource status. Crossplane will keep retrying the S3 bucket creation until you fix the IAM permission — at which point it self-heals. This is the key difference from Terraform: Terraform has no persistent reconciliation loop, so partial failures stay partial until you re-run `apply`. Crossplane keeps trying. The operational implication: you need alerting on `Synced: False` CompositeResources, just like you alert on K8s pods in `CrashLoopBackOff`.

**A2.** Three exception requests for the same item is the threshold to review the golden path. The governance response is: (1) open a tech radar review item for the alternative secrets tool, (2) evaluate it against the security and operational criteria that Vault currently meets, (3) either adopt it into the golden path, add it as an approved alternative, or document a clear reason for rejection with a migration path offered to the three teams. The wrong response is silently granting three exceptions without a documented decision — that creates undocumented platform fragmentation.

**A3.** Two changes: (1) Replace the JIRA approval gate with an automated policy check — OPA/Gatekeeper or Crossplane composition-level validation that enforces the same rules the JIRA review checked, but without human latency. (2) If human approval is genuinely required (e.g., cost threshold), make it asynchronous and non-blocking for low-risk requests, triggered only above a cost or risk threshold. The metric that tells you it worked: P95 onboarding time drops below your SLA (e.g., 4 hours for standard services). Secondary metric: ticket deflection rate increases, meaning fewer teams bypass the golden path to get faster provisioning.

### Step 5 — Write the CompositeResourceDefinition (XRD)

The XRD defines the schema for your golden path resource. Think of it as the API contract — the form a developer fills out.

```bash
mkdir -p ~/platform-lab
cat <<'EOF' > ~/platform-lab/xrd.yaml
# XRD = the API contract for your golden path
# When a developer says "I need a new service", this is what they fill in.
apiVersion: apiextensions.crossplane.io/v1
kind: CompositeResourceDefinition
metadata:
  # This name becomes the CRD name in your cluster
  name: xservices.platform.example.com
spec:
  # The API group developers use when they kubectl apply their request
  group: platform.example.com

  names:
    # XService = the resource type developers submit
    kind: XService
    plural: xservices

  # Allow namespace-scoped Claims (what developers actually apply)
  claimNames:
    kind: Service
    plural: services

  versions:
    - name: v1alpha1
      served: true
      referenceable: true
      schema:
        openAPIV3Schema:
          type: object
          properties:
            spec:
              type: object
              properties:
                # The only inputs a developer needs to provide
                serviceName:
                  type: string
                  description: "Short name for the service, e.g. payment-api"
                ownerTeam:
                  type: string
                  description: "Team slug, e.g. platform-sre"
                awsAccountId:
                  type: string
                  description: "AWS account ID for IRSA trust policy"
              required:
                - serviceName
                - ownerTeam
                - awsAccountId
EOF

kubectl apply -f ~/platform-lab/xrd.yaml
```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT:**
```
compositeresourcedefinition.apiextensions.crossplane.io/xservices.platform.example.com created
```

**WHY:** The XRD is your golden path's contract. You are publishing an API that says "give me a service name and a team, and I will provision everything underneath." App teams never see the underlying IAM roles or namespace YAML — they see only the form. This is the abstraction layer that prevents teams from needing to know AWS IAM syntax.

### Step 6 — Write the Composition

The Composition is the recipe — it defines what gets created when a developer submits an XService claim.

```bash
cat <<'EOF' > ~/platform-lab/composition.yaml
# Composition = the recipe that runs when a developer submits a Service claim
# Think of this as the planogram — the pre-built layout that gets deployed
apiVersion: apiextensions.crossplane.io/v1
kind: Composition
metadata:
  name: xservice-composition
  labels:
    crossplane.io/xrd: xservices.platform.example.com
spec:
  # Which XRD this Composition satisfies
  compositeTypeRef:
    apiVersion: platform.example.com/v1alpha1
    kind: XService

  # The pipeline of resources to create
  # Crossplane creates ALL of these in parallel when you apply a Service claim
  resources:

    # ── Resource 1: Kubernetes Namespace ─────────────────────────────────────
    # Every service gets its own namespace for isolation
    - name: namespace
      base:
        apiVersion: kubernetes.crossplane.io/v1alpha1
        kind: Object
        spec:
          forProvider:
            manifest:
              apiVersion: v1
              kind: Namespace
              metadata:
                labels:
                  # These labels are used by network policies and cost allocation
                  managed-by: crossplane
      patches:
        # Pull the serviceName from the developer's claim and use it as the namespace name
        - type: FromCompositeFieldPath
          fromFieldPath: spec.serviceName
          toFieldPath: spec.forProvider.manifest.metadata.name
        - type: FromCompositeFieldPath
          fromFieldPath: spec.ownerTeam
          toFieldPath: spec.forProvider.manifest.metadata.labels.team

    # ── Resource 2: ServiceAccount ────────────────────────────────────────────
    # The K8s identity that pods in this namespace will run as
    # The IRSA annotation links it to the AWS IAM role below
    - name: serviceaccount
      base:
        apiVersion: kubernetes.crossplane.io/v1alpha1
        kind: Object
        spec:
          forProvider:
            manifest:
              apiVersion: v1
              kind: ServiceAccount
              metadata:
                annotations:
                  # This annotation is what IRSA reads to find the IAM role
                  # The role ARN is built from account ID + service name
                  eks.amazonaws.com/role-arn: "placeholder"
      patches:
        - type: FromCompositeFieldPath
          fromFieldPath: spec.serviceName
          toFieldPath: spec.forProvider.manifest.metadata.namespace
        - type: FromCompositeFieldPath
          fromFieldPath: spec.serviceName
          toFieldPath: spec.forProvider.manifest.metadata.name
          transforms:
            # Append "-sa" so it's clear this is a service account
            - type: string
              string:
                type: Format
                fmt: "%s-sa"
        # Build the IAM role ARN from account ID and service name
        - type: CombineFromComposite
          combine:
            variables:
              - fromFieldPath: spec.awsAccountId
              - fromFieldPath: spec.serviceName
            strategy: string
            string:
              fmt: "arn:aws:iam::%s:role/%s-irsa-role"
          toFieldPath: spec.forProvider.manifest.metadata.annotations["eks.amazonaws.com/role-arn"]

    # ── Resource 3: AWS IAM Role (IRSA) ────────────────────────────────────────
    # The AWS identity. The trust policy allows the K8s ServiceAccount above to assume it.
    # This is the IRSA pattern: K8s workload identity → AWS IAM role, no static keys.
    - name: irsa-role
      base:
        apiVersion: iam.aws.upbound.io/v1beta1
        kind: Role
        spec:
          providerConfigRef:
            name: default
          forProvider:
            # Region where the role metadata lives — IAM is global but API calls need a region
            description: "IRSA role provisioned by Crossplane golden path"
            # Trust policy: allows EKS OIDC provider to assume this role
            # The condition locks it to the specific service account — least privilege
            assumeRolePolicyDocument: |
              {
                "Version": "2012-10-17",
                "Statement": [{
                  "Effect": "Allow",
                  "Principal": {
                    "Federated": "arn:aws:iam::ACCOUNT_ID:oidc-provider/OIDC_PROVIDER"
                  },
                  "Action": "sts:AssumeRoleWithWebIdentity",
                  "Condition": {
                    "StringEquals": {
                      "OIDC_PROVIDER:sub": "system:serviceaccount:SERVICE_NAME:SERVICE_NAME-sa"
                    }
                  }
                }]
              }
      patches:
        # Build the role name from the service name
        - type: FromCompositeFieldPath
          fromFieldPath: spec.serviceName
          toFieldPath: metadata.name
          transforms:
            - type: string
              string:
                type: Format
                fmt: "%s-irsa-role"
        # Tag the role with the owning team for cost allocation and access reviews
        - type: FromCompositeFieldPath
          fromFieldPath: spec.ownerTeam
          toFieldPath: spec.forProvider.tags.team
        - type: FromCompositeFieldPath
          fromFieldPath: spec.serviceName
          toFieldPath: spec.forProvider.tags.service
EOF

kubectl apply -f ~/platform-lab/composition.yaml
```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT:**
```
composition.apiextensions.crossplane.io/xservice-composition created
```

**WHY:** The Composition is where your platform engineering decisions live. You decided: every service gets a dedicated namespace (isolation), every service gets an IRSA-annotated ServiceAccount (no static AWS keys ever), and every service gets a tagged IAM role (cost allocation, security audit). These are baked in. A developer cannot accidentally skip them. This is the "right thing is the easy thing" principle made concrete.

## Tradeoffs and Failure Modes — Top 3

**Tradeoff 1 — Crossplane vs. Terraform for platform teams**

Crossplane gives you continuous reconciliation and a K8s-native developer experience. The cost is higher operational complexity: you need to maintain providers, manage provider credentials inside the cluster, and debug Crossplane-specific error conditions. Terraform is simpler to operate and has far more mature providers. The right answer for most orgs in 2026: use Terraform for foundational infra (accounts, VPCs, EKS clusters), and Crossplane for the developer-facing self-service layer on top. Not either/or.

**Tradeoff 2 — Port vs. Backstage for the portal layer**

Backstage gives you full control and an enormous plugin ecosystem. It costs you a platform team investment of 2-3 engineers just to keep it current. Port is SaaS — you pay per user but the operational overhead is near zero. For a platform team of fewer than 5 engineers, Port's operational simplicity wins. For organizations that need deep catalog integration, custom plugins, and want to avoid SaaS dependency, Backstage wins at scale. The trap to avoid: starting with Backstage when you have 2 platform engineers, spending 70% of sprint capacity on Backstage maintenance, and never building the actual golden paths.

**Tradeoff 3 — Abstraction depth vs. developer autonomy**

The deeper your golden path abstraction — the less a developer sees of the underlying infrastructure — the faster onboarding becomes. But deep abstraction creates a ceiling: when something breaks at the IAM level, the developer has no mental model of what went wrong. The right balance: abstract away the provisioning (developers never write IAM JSON), but surface the resource graph in your developer portal so developers can see "my ServiceAccount is linked to this IAM role with these permissions." Transparency without complexity. This maps exactly to how you'd design an observability tool: developers should not need to understand Splunk internals, but they should be able to read the dashboard.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Golden paths are leverage.** One hour of platform engineering work (the Composition) enables N developers to self-serve instead of opening N tickets.
2. **Crossplane's drift-correction model is operationally superior to Terraform for always-on platforms.** Terraform is still the right choice for foundational infra.
3. **Platform adoption measures the real success, not compliance.** If adoption is below 70%, your golden path is too opinionated or too slow.
4. **Escape hatch governance is critical.** Three exception requests for the same thing means your golden path is wrong, not that your teams are wrong.
5. **Tech radar cadence prevents stagnation.** Quarterly reviews keep the catalog current and prevent the "golden cage" problem.
