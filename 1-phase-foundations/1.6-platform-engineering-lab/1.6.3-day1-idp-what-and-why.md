# Platform Engineering — What is an IDP and Why It Matters | Week 6 Day 1

## Why This Matters (Interview Relevance + Business Impact)

Platform Engineering is the fastest-growing SRE/DevOps specialization in 2025-2026. At Staff and Principal level, interviewers probe whether you can articulate:

- The business case for a platform team (not just the technology)
- How you measure ROI on a platform investment
- The organizational model that makes platforms succeed or fail
- Where Backstage, golden paths, and software templates fit in a real org

At Director/VP level, the question is harder: "You just spent 6 months and 4 engineers building an IDP. How do you prove it paid off?"

**Business impact (directly relevant to T-Mobile SRE interview stories):**
- Teams that adopt IDPs reduce new-service onboarding from weeks to hours
- Cognitive load reduction translates directly to fewer human errors under incident pressure
- Platform teams absorb toil centrally so stream-aligned teams can stay in flow
- DORA metrics (deployment frequency, lead time) improve measurably when golden paths exist

## Analogy

**The T-Mobile Notification Portal Analogy:**

At T-Mobile, when a new squad needs a push notification queue for a new campaign flow, today they file a JIRA ticket, wait 2-3 days for SRE to provision RabbitMQ bindings, request a Splunk dashboard, ask Infosec for namespace permissions, then chase DevOps for a Helm chart. Each dependency is a handoff. Each handoff is a context switch for the receiving team.

An Internal Developer Platform is the portal that replaces every one of those tickets with a single button. A developer fills in a form: service name, team, tier (critical / standard / batch). They click "Create Service." Behind the button:
- A GitHub repo appears, pre-configured with your org's CI template
- A Kubernetes namespace is created with RBAC already applied
- A Grafana dashboard is provisioned with the four golden signals
- A Backstage entry is created in the service catalog so anyone can find it

The SRE platform team built the button. The SRE team maintains the button. Stream-aligned product squads just push it.

**The airport analogy (secondary):** An IDP is the check-in kiosk. The airline staff (platform engineers) designed the kiosk and keep it running. Passengers (developers) don't need to know about baggage routing systems — they just scan their passport and get a boarding pass. The value is in removing expert knowledge from the critical path.

## Vocabulary Translation Table

| Their Term | Your Existing Mental Model |
|---|---|
| Internal Developer Platform (IDP) | Self-service portal: like T-Mobile's internal tooling for queue provisioning, but for every infra resource |
| Golden Path | Your org's blessed Helm chart + CI template + naming convention — the approved, pre-paved route so engineers don't have to invent the road |
| Backstage | The front-end of the IDP — a service catalog + software template runner, open-sourced by Spotify |
| Software Template (Scaffolder) | Like a Jenkins shared library or a Helm chart starter — a parameterized blueprint that generates real repos + K8s config on demand |
| Team Topologies | Organizational design model: defines 4 team types and how they interact. Think of it as your incident escalation matrix but for everyday team interactions |
| Stream-Aligned Team | Your product squads — they own end-to-end delivery of a feature stream (like the T-Mobile notification team for a specific channel) |
| Platform Team | Your SRE team — provides self-service capabilities to stream-aligned teams, absorbs toil centrally |
| Enabling Team | Embedded consultants or CoE — they work alongside stream-aligned teams temporarily to level them up (like a DevSecOps guild) |
| Complicated-Subsystem Team | Owns deeply specialized systems (like a Cassandra DBA team or ML model serving layer) — too complex to self-serve |
| Cognitive Load | Mental bandwidth a team must spend on infra/tooling instead of their actual product. You reduce this by building golden paths |
| Platform as a Product | Treating the IDP like a customer-facing product — with roadmap, SLOs, user research, and adoption metrics |
| Paved Road | Same as golden path — the well-maintained route vs. the off-road option that's unsupported |
| DORA Metrics | Deployment Frequency, Lead Time for Changes, Change Failure Rate, Time to Restore — the 4 KPIs that measure delivery health |

## 30-Second Version

An IDP is a self-service layer on top of your Kubernetes clusters, CI/CD pipelines, and cloud accounts. Developers fill in a form, click create, and get a fully configured service — repo, namespace, dashboard, secrets — without filing tickets. Backstage is the most common open-source framework for building that layer. Golden paths are the pre-approved configurations that make the self-service safe and consistent.

## 2-Minute Version

Before platform engineering, every new service required a developer to know: which Helm chart to use, how to configure RBAC, where to store secrets in Vault, what the Grafana dashboard template is, how to wire up the alerting channel. That knowledge lived in your SRE team's heads and in tribal Confluence pages. Every onboarding was a custom engagement.

Platform engineering centralizes that knowledge into automation. You build a software template once — parameterized, tested, compliant — and then any developer can instantiate it in 5 minutes.

The organizational model that makes this work is called Team Topologies (from the 2019 book by Skelton and Pais). It defines four team types:

1. **Stream-Aligned Teams**: move fast, own a product surface, minimize dependencies
2. **Platform Teams**: provide self-service infra capabilities, eliminate repetitive handoffs
3. **Enabling Teams**: embed temporarily to level up stream-aligned teams (CoE / guild model)
4. **Complicated-Subsystem Teams**: own deep specialist systems too complex to self-serve

The interaction mode between platform teams and stream-aligned teams is deliberately "X-as-a-Service" — the consumer doesn't need to understand internals, only the interface.

Backstage, open-sourced by Spotify in 2020 and now a CNCF incubating project, is the most widely adopted framework for building the IDP front-end. It provides:
- **Service Catalog**: searchable registry of every service, owner, dependencies, docs
- **Software Templates (Scaffolder)**: form-driven workflows that create repos, configure CI, provision infra
- **TechDocs**: auto-generated documentation from markdown in repos
- **Plugins**: extensible integration layer for Grafana, PagerDuty, Kubernetes, Vault, etc.

## 5-Minute Version (Tradeoffs, Failure Modes, Design Decisions)

### The Full Picture

An IDP is not a product you buy — it is a product you build and operate. That distinction is important in interviews because it shifts the conversation from "what tool do I install" to "what organizational commitment am I making."

**IDP vs CI/CD vs Kubernetes Cluster — what lives where:**

| Layer | What It Does | Owned By |
|---|---|---|
| Kubernetes Cluster | Workload runtime: pods, namespaces, networking | Platform team — infrastructure layer |
| CI/CD Pipeline (ArgoCD, GitHub Actions) | Automates build → test → deploy | Platform team supplies templates; dev teams own their pipeline |
| IDP (Backstage + scaffolding) | Self-service interface over everything above | Platform team — experience layer |

The K8s cluster is the engine. CI/CD is the transmission. The IDP is the dashboard and ignition key — the part the driver actually touches.

### Golden Path Design Decisions

A golden path is not a mandate — it is the path of least resistance that is also safe and compliant. Key design decisions:

1. **Breadth vs. depth**: Start with one golden path done well (e.g., "new microservice") rather than trying to cover every pattern at launch
2. **Escape hatches**: Engineers must be able to go off-path for legitimate reasons — document the cost and accept that it requires explicit justification
3. **Drift detection**: Golden path instances can drift from the template over time. You need tooling (OPA/Kyverno policies, Renovate bot, GitOps reconciliation) to detect and alert on drift
4. **Versioning**: Templates must be versioned. When you update the golden path, existing services should not break. Think of it like a shared library with semantic versioning

### Backstage Architecture (what interviewers probe)

Backstage is a React/Node.js monorepo. Key components:

- **Catalog**: driven by YAML descriptor files (`catalog-info.yaml`) checked into each repo — no central database of truth, the source repos ARE the truth
- **Scaffolder**: workflow engine that executes a series of actions (fetch template, create repo, publish to GitHub, register in catalog, create Grafana dashboard)
- **Software Templates**: YAML-defined, live in a `templates/` directory, parameterized with form fields
- **Backend plugins**: where integrations live — AWS, GitHub, Grafana, PagerDuty, Vault all have plugins

**What Backstage is not:** It is not a CI/CD engine. It is not a secrets manager. It is not a policy enforcement system. It is the portal. Each of those functions lives in the appropriate backing tool; Backstage surfaces them through its plugin model.

### Team Topologies: The Org Design Behind the Tech

The reason most platform engineering efforts fail is not technology — it is organization. Team Topologies defines interaction modes:

- **Collaboration**: two teams work closely together for a bounded period (high bandwidth, temporary)
- **X-as-a-Service**: consumer uses the platform without deep interaction (low bandwidth, scalable)
- **Facilitating**: enabling team helps a stream-aligned team level up, then withdraws

Platform teams should operate in X-as-a-Service mode with stream-aligned teams at scale. If your platform team is constantly pulled into "help me use the platform" requests, you have a UX problem, not a staffing problem.

**Cognitive Load Reduction as a KPI:**
Team Topologies defines three types of cognitive load:
- *Intrinsic*: the complexity of the problem domain itself (you cannot reduce this)
- *Extraneous*: cognitive load from poor tooling, unclear processes, undiscoverable systems (this is what IDPs target)
- *Germane*: learning that builds useful mental models (you want to increase this)

Platform engineering specifically attacks extraneous cognitive load.

### Failure Modes

1. **The "museum exhibit" IDP**: Built once, never updated. Templates go stale. Developers stop trusting it and go back to tickets. Requires dedicated platform team ownership — not a side project.
2. **Mandate without adoption**: Forcing all teams onto the golden path without earning trust destroys morale. Adoption must be pulled (developer delight) not pushed (policy).
3. **Platform team becomes a bottleneck**: If every template change requires a platform team engineer to approve and deploy, you replicated the JIRA ticket problem in a new costume. Self-service must be truly self-service.

## Director/VP Lens

### Leader Governs vs IC Implements

**As an IC (Principal SRE):**
- Design software templates: parameterize, test, document escape hatches
- Define the catalog-info.yaml standard and enforce it via CI lint
- Build the Backstage plugin integrations (Grafana, PagerDuty, AWS)
- Instrument template usage with OpenTelemetry — who is using what
- On-call for the platform itself — IDP is a production service

**As a Director/VP:**
- Own the "platform as a product" roadmap — quarterly planning, backlog prioritization
- Establish the adoption metric baseline: what percentage of new services use the golden path?
- Commission user research: what is the developer NPS on the platform? What are the top friction points?
- Make the build vs. buy decision (Backstage vs. Cortex vs. Port vs. OpsLevel)
- Justify headcount: "A 4-person platform team eliminates 30 hours/week of toil across 12 product teams"

### How to Measure IDP ROI (Director-Level Framing)

This is a frequent senior interview question. Frame it in three buckets:

**Adoption metrics:**
- % of new services created via golden path (target: >80% within 6 months of launch)
- Time to first deployment for a new service (baseline vs. post-IDP)
- Number of active catalog entries vs. total known services (catalog coverage %)

**Toil reduction metrics:**
- Manual JIRA tickets for infra provisioning (before vs. after — measure the drop)
- Mean time to onboard a new engineer to their first production deployment
- Hours/week platform team spends on reactive "how do I provision X" support

**Quality/reliability metrics:**
- DORA metrics delta post-IDP adoption (deployment frequency up, lead time down)
- Misconfiguration incidents attributable to drift from golden path (should trend to zero with policy enforcement)
- Security findings from golden path services vs. off-path services (golden path should have fewer findings)

**Developer satisfaction:**
- Quarterly developer NPS survey: "How easy is it to create and deploy a new service?"
- Qualitative feedback: "What is the biggest thing that slows you down?"

### The "Platform as a Product" Mindset

A platform team that does not treat their platform as a product will eventually be dismantled. Product mindset means:
- Real users (developers) with real user research
- A backlog prioritized by user impact, not platform team preferences
- SLOs for the platform itself (Backstage uptime, template execution success rate, catalog freshness)
- A roadmap communicated to stakeholders with clear ROI milestones
- Deprecation strategy for legacy scaffolding — you cannot maintain every version forever

## Comprehension Check

Pass 2 out of 3 to unlock the lab.

**Q1.** A developer at your org needs to create a new microservice. Without an IDP, they must: clone a starter repo, request a K8s namespace from SRE, configure RBAC manually, ask SecOps for Vault access, and set up a Grafana dashboard. With the IDP golden path, this takes 5 minutes. What specific type of cognitive load are you reducing, and why does this matter operationally — not just for developer happiness?

**Q2.** A stream-aligned product team wants to run their own PostgreSQL cluster with a custom operator because they have low-latency requirements the platform's shared RDS setup cannot meet. Using Team Topologies vocabulary, what team type should own this PostgreSQL operator, and what interaction mode should they have with the stream-aligned team?

**Q3.** Your VP asks: "We spent 5 months building Backstage. How do I know it was worth it?" Name three concrete metrics you would bring to that conversation, explain the data source for each, and identify which one is most likely to be challenged by a skeptical CFO and why.

---

## ANSWER KEY — Comprehension Check

**A1.** You are reducing *extraneous cognitive load* — the overhead from poor tooling and undiscoverable processes, not the inherent complexity of writing a microservice. Operationally this matters because cognitive overhead compounds during incidents: an engineer who spent mental energy navigating provisioning is less effective during a P1. Reducing extraneous load keeps engineers in a problem-solving state rather than a process-navigation state. The T-Mobile parallel: your zero Sev1s over 36 months are partly attributable to ops processes that reduce the surface area for human error under pressure.

**A2.** A dedicated PostgreSQL operator with custom latency characteristics is a *complicated-subsystem team* — the internals are too specialized for stream-aligned teams to maintain, but the interface should be self-service. The interaction mode is *X-as-a-Service*: the complicated-subsystem team exposes a CRD or API; the stream-aligned team consumes it without needing to understand the operator internals. If the complicated-subsystem team is constantly embedded in the stream-aligned team's standups, that is the wrong interaction mode — it defeats the purpose.

**A3.** Three metrics:
1. **Time to first deployment for new services** (data source: JIRA/ServiceNow ticket timestamps before vs. GitHub Actions first-deploy timestamps after — measures the actual business outcome of faster onboarding)
2. **Toil ticket volume** (data source: JIRA label "infra-provisioning-request" — before/after count shows direct workload impact on SRE team; translates to FTE hours saved)
3. **Developer NPS** (data source: quarterly survey — measures whether developers are actually adopting vs. working around)

The metric most challenged by a skeptical CFO: developer NPS (qualitative, subjective, hard to translate to dollars). The CFO will want FTE hours saved. Translate: "We ran 40 fewer provisioning tickets per month at 2hrs each = 80 eng-hours/month = 1 FTE-month per year." At $200K fully-loaded eng cost, that is $200K annual ROI on a 4-person platform team investment. That framing lands.

## Lab: Install Backstage Locally (Docker), Add a Software Template

**Cost: $0. Runtime: ~45 minutes. Platform: WSL2 Ubuntu.**

This lab does not use real AWS (there is no K8s cluster yet). It provisions Backstage locally via Docker Compose, adds a software template, and observes what scaffolding generates. The GitHub integration uses a real GitHub PAT so the scaffold actually creates a repository.

**Prerequisites before starting:**
- Docker Desktop running with WSL2 backend enabled
- A GitHub account (free tier is fine)
- Node.js 18+ installed in WSL2 (check: `node --version`)
- A GitHub Personal Access Token with `repo` and `workflow` scopes

### Step 1 — Create a GitHub Personal Access Token

Go to: https://github.com/settings/tokens/new

Settings:
- Token name: `backstage-lab`
- Expiration: 7 days (this is a lab token — never commit tokens to git)
- Scopes: check `repo` (all), `workflow`, `read:org`

Click "Generate token." Copy it — you cannot see it again.

```bash
# Store it in your shell session (never commit this to a file)
export GITHUB_TOKEN=ghp_YOUR_TOKEN_HERE

# Verify it works
curl -s -H "Authorization: token $GITHUB_TOKEN" https://api.github.com/user | grep login
```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT:**
```json
  "login": "your-github-username",
```

**WHY:** Backstage's scaffolder will call the GitHub API to create repos on your behalf. The token is what authenticates those API calls. In production, you use a GitHub App (not a PAT) — but for a lab, a PAT is the fastest path.

### Step 3 — Configure GitHub Integration

```bash
# Edit the Backstage app config
cd ~/backstage-lab/my-idp
nano app-config.yaml
```

Find the `integrations:` section (around line 20) and replace it with:

```yaml
integrations:
  github:
    - host: github.com
      token: ${GITHUB_TOKEN}
```

Find the `catalog:` section and confirm it has at least:

```yaml
catalog:
  import:
    entityFilename: catalog-info.yaml
    pullRequestBranchName: backstage-integration
  rules:
    - allow: [Component, System, API, Resource, Location, Template]
  locations:
    - type: url
      target: https://github.com/backstage/software-templates/blob/main/scaffolder-templates/react-ssr-template/template.yaml
    - type: url
      target: https://github.com/backstage/software-templates/blob/main/scaffolder-templates/springboot-grpc-template/template.yaml
    - type: url
      target: https://github.com/backstage/software-templates/blob/main/scaffolder-templates/docs-template/template.yaml
```

Save and exit (Ctrl+O, Enter, Ctrl+X in nano).

**WHY:** `app-config.yaml` is Backstage's central configuration file. The `${GITHUB_TOKEN}` syntax tells Backstage to read the token from the environment variable you set in Step 1 — this is the correct pattern. Never hardcode tokens in config files.

### Step 6 — Create a Software Template

You will now create a minimal software template that, when executed, creates a GitHub repository with a pre-configured `catalog-info.yaml`.

```bash
# Leave the running Backstage (keep that terminal open)
# Open a new WSL2 terminal tab/window

mkdir -p ~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice
cd ~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice
```

Create the template definition file:

```bash
cat > template.yaml << 'EOF'
apiVersion: scaffolder.backstage.io/v1beta3
kind: Template
metadata:
  name: new-microservice
  title: New Microservice
  description: Creates a new microservice repo with catalog registration, Dockerfile stub, and Kubernetes namespace manifest.
  tags:
    - recommended
    - microservice
spec:
  owner: platform-team
  type: service

  parameters:
    - title: Service Details
      required:
        - name
        - owner
        - tier
      properties:
        name:
          title: Service Name
          type: string
          description: Lowercase, hyphens only. Example: payment-processor
          pattern: '^[a-z][a-z0-9-]*$'
          ui:autofocus: true
        owner:
          title: Team Owner
          type: string
          description: Your team name in Backstage. Example: notifications-team
        tier:
          title: Service Tier
          type: string
          enum:
            - critical
            - standard
            - batch
          enumNames:
            - 'Critical (P1 on-call, 99.99% SLO)'
            - 'Standard (P2 on-call, 99.9% SLO)'
            - 'Batch (no on-call, best-effort)'
          default: standard
    - title: Repository Location
      required:
        - repoUrl
      properties:
        repoUrl:
          title: GitHub Repository
          type: string
          ui:field: RepoUrlPicker
          ui:options:
            allowedHosts:
              - github.com

  steps:
    - id: fetch-base
      name: Fetch Base Template Files
      action: fetch:template
      input:
        url: ./skeleton
        values:
          name: ${{ parameters.name }}
          owner: ${{ parameters.owner }}
          tier: ${{ parameters.tier }}

    - id: publish
      name: Publish to GitHub
      action: publish:github
      input:
        allowedHosts: ['github.com']
        description: "Microservice: ${{ parameters.name }} owned by ${{ parameters.owner }}"
        repoUrl: ${{ parameters.repoUrl }}
        defaultBranch: main

    - id: register
      name: Register in Backstage Catalog
      action: catalog:register
      input:
        repoContentsUrl: ${{ steps['publish'].output.repoContentsUrl }}
        catalogInfoPath: '/catalog-info.yaml'

  output:
    links:
      - title: Repository
        url: ${{ steps['publish'].output.remoteUrl }}
      - title: Open in catalog
        entityRef: ${{ steps['register'].output.entityRef }}
EOF
```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT:**
```
(no output — file written successfully)
```

Now create the skeleton files that the template will instantiate:

```bash
mkdir -p ~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice/skeleton

cat > ~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice/skeleton/catalog-info.yaml << 'EOF'
apiVersion: backstage.io/v1alpha1
kind: Component
metadata:
  name: ${{ values.name }}
  description: "${{ values.name }} microservice — tier: ${{ values.tier }}"
  annotations:
    github.com/project-slug: ${{ values.repoUrl | parseRepoUrl | pick('owner') }}/${{ values.name }}
  tags:
    - ${{ values.tier }}
spec:
  type: service
  owner: ${{ values.owner }}
  lifecycle: experimental
EOF

cat > ~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice/skeleton/Dockerfile << 'EOF'
# Base image — replace with your org's approved base image
FROM node:20-alpine

WORKDIR /app

# Copy application files
COPY package*.json ./
RUN npm ci --only=production

COPY . .

EXPOSE 8080

CMD ["node", "src/index.js"]
EOF

cat > ~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice/skeleton/k8s-namespace.yaml << 'EOF'
apiVersion: v1
kind: Namespace
metadata:
  name: ${{ values.name }}
  labels:
    team: ${{ values.owner }}
    tier: ${{ values.tier }}
    managed-by: backstage-scaffolder
EOF
```

**WHY:** The skeleton directory contains the files that will be copied into the generated GitHub repo, with Mustache-style template variables substituted. When a developer runs this template, they get a repo with a `catalog-info.yaml` (so Backstage auto-discovers it), a `Dockerfile` starter, and a Kubernetes namespace manifest. This is the golden path in action — one form, three artifacts, zero manual steps.

### Step 7 — Register the Template in Backstage

Now tell Backstage about your local template by adding it to `app-config.yaml`:

```bash
# Back to the backstage app directory
cd ~/backstage-lab/my-idp
nano app-config.yaml
```

Add this entry under `catalog.locations`:

```yaml
    - type: file
      target: ../../templates/new-microservice/template.yaml
      rules:
        - allow: [Template]
```

Save and exit. Backstage hot-reloads config — watch the terminal where `npm run dev` is running.

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT (in the npm run dev terminal):**
```
[1] Reloading backend...
[1] Backend started successfully
```

**WHY:** Backstage's catalog supports multiple location types. `type: file` lets you point at a local template during development. In production, you would use `type: url` pointing at a GitHub-hosted template. This is how you iterate on templates without a deploy cycle.

## Lab ANSWER KEY — Diagnostics Per Step

**Step 0 — Node not found:**
Run `nvm install 20 && nvm use 20` if using nvm, or the curl install command in Step 0.

**Step 2 — npx @backstage/create-app fails with network error:**
Check proxy settings in WSL2. Try: `npm config set registry https://registry.npmjs.org/` and retry.

**Step 2 — npm install runs for 15+ minutes and hangs:**
Normal on first run on slow connections. Check with `npm install --verbose` if truly stuck. Alternatively, kill and retry — npm will resume from cache.

**Step 4 — Backend fails to start (port 7007 in use):**
```bash
sudo lsof -i :7007
kill -9 <PID>
```
Then retry `npm run dev`.

**Step 4 — "Cannot read GitHub token" error in backend logs:**
Verify `echo $GITHUB_TOKEN` in the same terminal where you ran `npm run dev`. Environment variables set with `export` do not survive to new terminal sessions. Re-export and restart.

**Step 8 — Template card does not appear in Create:**
1. Check the `npm run dev` terminal for config reload errors
2. Verify the file path in `app-config.yaml` is correct relative to the app root
3. Check `~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice/template.yaml` exists: `ls ~/backstage-lab/templates/new-microservice/`

**Step 8 — Publish step fails (401 Unauthorized):**
Your GitHub PAT expired or has insufficient scopes. Generate a new one with `repo` + `workflow` + `read:org` and re-export `GITHUB_TOKEN`.

**Step 9 — Service does not appear in catalog after 5 minutes:**
Navigate to Backstage Settings → Catalog → Locations and check if your file location shows an error. Common cause: YAML syntax error in `catalog-info.yaml` in the generated repo.

## STAR Anchor (T-Mobile Framing Per Question)

**Q1 — Incremental catalog adoption:**
**S:** T-Mobile notification platform had 25+ services across 3 teams, no centralized ownership registry. During an incident, finding who owned a specific consumer group took 15 minutes of Slack threads.
**T:** As SRE Manager, I needed a way to establish service ownership without a 3-month migration project.
**A:** We implemented a lightweight `catalog-info.yaml` standard. Required it only for new services and any service that had an incident. Within 90 days, 70% of critical-path services had ownership documented.
**R:** Mean time to identify blast radius owner during incidents dropped from 15 minutes to under 2 minutes. We used this as the foundation for our IDP roadmap pitch to leadership.

**Q2 — Platform escape hatch policy:**
**S:** At T-Mobile, one team wanted to use Pulumi instead of our Terraform golden path because they had a developer who was a Pulumi expert.
**T:** I needed to balance developer autonomy with platform supportability and security compliance.
**A:** We created a formal "off-path request" process: document the business case, confirm security controls are equivalent (SAST on IaC, state stored in approved backend), accept that platform team will not provide first-line support. Approved the Pulumi request under those conditions.
**R:** Team shipped faster, we maintained security posture, and the off-path process became the policy we use for all platform divergence requests.

**Q3 — Backstage blast radius:**
Blast radius of Backstage being down: developers cannot create new services via golden path, catalog is not browsable, TechDocs unavailable. However, existing services continue to run — Backstage is not in the request path for production traffic. It is a developer productivity tool, not a production dependency.
Incident response: P3 severity (developer experience degraded, no production impact). SLO: 99.5% availability (lower than production APIs). On-call: platform team, not production SRE rotation.

## Tradeoffs and Failure Modes (Top 3)

**1. Backstage maintenance cost vs. build-from-scratch:**
Backstage is powerful but has a steep learning curve. The plugin ecosystem is rich but inconsistent in quality. The monorepo architecture means dependency upgrades are complex. Alternative: commercial IDPs (Port, Cortex, OpsLevel) have lower operational overhead but less flexibility and vendor lock-in. Decision frame: if your platform team has fewer than 3 dedicated engineers, a commercial IDP may be the right trade.

**2. Golden path adoption cliff:**
Teams adopt golden paths when the path is measurably easier than the alternative. If the golden path adds friction (longer forms, more approval steps, slower feedback), developers will route around it. The failure mode: your IDP becomes a compliance checkbox that nobody actually uses, while real provisioning happens through direct Slack messages to SRE. Measure this with template usage metrics from day one.

**3. Org structure mismatch (Team Topologies violations):**
Platform engineering fails when the platform team is embedded into product team standups, pulled into product feature delivery, or structured as a shared services team that takes tickets. The platform team must be funded and protected to work on the platform, not on individual team requests. If your platform engineers spend more than 20% of their time on one-off "help me use the platform" requests, you have an adoption/UX problem that more staffing will not solve.
