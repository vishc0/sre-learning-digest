## Enterprise IT teams

This document maps the main teams that typically enable IT in an enterprise and shows a practical people tree. It is intentionally generic so it can be adapted to a specific company, size, or operating model.

### 1) Leadership and governance

- **Board / Executive leadership** — sets enterprise direction and budget.
- **CEO / COO** — owns business outcomes and operating model.
- **CIO / CTO** — owns technology strategy and delivery.
- **Enterprise governance** — architecture review, risk, compliance, investment control, portfolio governance.

### 2) Strategy and planning teams

- **Enterprise architecture** — target-state architecture, standards, principles.
- **IT strategy and transformation** — roadmap, modernization, operating-model changes.
- **Portfolio management / PMO** — prioritization, funding, milestones, dependency tracking.
- **Financial management / FinOps** — cost control, budgeting, chargeback, forecasting.

### 3) Product and business-facing teams

- **Product management** — product vision, roadmap, business value, backlog priorities.
- **Business analysis / requirements** — process analysis, requirement definition, user stories.
- **Customer / stakeholder management** — business relationship management, demand intake.
- **Change management / release adoption** — adoption plans, communications, training, readiness.

### 4) Platform and engineering teams

- **Platform engineering** — internal platforms, developer experience, reusable capabilities.
- **Application engineering / development** — builds business applications and services.
- **DevOps / SRE** — CI/CD, environments, observability, reliability, deployment automation.
- **Integration engineering** — APIs, middleware, event streams, system-to-system connectivity.
- **Data engineering / analytics engineering** — pipelines, reporting foundations, data products.
- **QA / test engineering** — test strategy, automation, regression, quality gates.

### 5) Infrastructure and operations teams

- **Cloud / infrastructure engineering** — compute, network, storage, platform hosting.
- **End-user computing / workplace IT** — devices, identity on endpoints, collaboration tools.
- **IT operations / command center** — monitoring, incident coordination, operational control.
- **Service desk / L1 support** — ticket intake, password resets, standard support.
- **L2 / application support** — functional and technical triage, deeper diagnostics.
- **L3 / engineering support** — root-cause fixes, code/config changes, major incident support.

### 6) Security, risk, and compliance teams

- **CISO / security leadership** — security strategy and risk posture.
- **Security architecture** — secure design patterns, threat modeling, control design.
- **Identity and access management** — authentication, authorization, privileged access.
- **Security operations (SOC)** — detection, monitoring, response, threat hunting.
- **GRC / compliance** — policies, audits, controls, regulatory obligations.
- **Privacy / legal / records management** — data handling, retention, and legal compliance.

### 7) Service and support enablement teams

- **Incident management** — restores service quickly during outages.
- **Problem management** — identifies root causes and permanent fixes.
- **Change management** — controls risk for production changes.
- **Release management** — coordinates deployment windows and release readiness.
- **Knowledge management** — runbooks, knowledge articles, operational documentation.
- **Vendor management** — manages third-party contracts, performance, and delivery.

### 8) Specialized capability teams

- **ERP / enterprise apps** — finance, HR, procurement, CRM, SCM systems.
- **Database administration** — database platforms, performance, backup, recovery.
- **Network engineering** — connectivity, routing, firewalls, connectivity resilience.
- **Automation / platform tooling** — workflow automation, scripts, developer tools.
- **AI / data science / ML engineering** — models, experimentation, AI products.

### 9) Adjacent enterprise functions that enable IT

These teams may sit outside IT but are important for IT execution and control.

- **Finance** — budgeting, invoicing, investment approvals, cost allocation.
- **Procurement / sourcing** — vendor selection, contracts, renewals, purchase orders.
- **Human resources** — hiring, onboarding, workforce planning, role management.
- **Legal** — contracts, IP, privacy, regulatory review.
- **Enterprise risk / internal audit** — controls, audits, risk oversight.
- **Facilities / workplace services** — offices, physical access, site readiness.
- **Communications / change office** — employee communications and adoption support.
- **Program / transformation office** — cross-functional change coordination.

## People tree structure

The following is a simple people hierarchy that can be used for most enterprise IT organizations.

```text
Board / Executive Leadership
└── CEO / COO
	└── CIO / CTO
		├── Technology Strategy & Governance
		│   ├── Enterprise Architects
		│   ├── Portfolio / PMO
		│   ├── FinOps / IT Financial Management
		│   └── Transformation Leads
		├── Product Organization
		│   ├── Product Directors / Heads of Product
		│   ├── Product Managers
		│   ├── Business Analysts / Product Owners
		│   └── Change / Adoption Managers
		├── Platform Engineering
		│   ├── Platform Manager / Platform Lead
		│   ├── Platform Engineers
		│   ├── DevOps / SRE Engineers
		│   └── Integration Engineers
		├── Application Engineering
		│   ├── Engineering Managers / Tech Leads
		│   ├── Software Engineers
		│   ├── QA / Test Engineers
		│   └── L3 Application Support
		├── Infrastructure & Operations
		│   ├── Infrastructure Leads
		│   ├── Cloud / Network / Workplace Engineers
		│   ├── NOC / Operations Analysts
		│   ├── Service Desk / L1 Support
		│   └── L2 Support Analysts
		├── Security, Risk & Compliance
		│   ├── CISO / Security Manager
		│   ├── Security Architects
		│   ├── SOC Analysts
		│   ├── IAM Specialists
		│   └── GRC / Compliance Analysts
		└── Shared Enablement
			├── Vendor Managers
			├── Knowledge Managers
			├── Training / Enablement Leads
			└── Operations Coordinators
```

### Optional overlay for non-IT enablement

If you want the full enterprise picture, the CIO/CTO node often works in parallel with these functions:

```text
CEO / COO
├── CIO / CTO
├── CFO
├── CHRO
├── CPO / Head of Procurement
├── General Counsel
└── CRO / Internal Audit
```

## How the teams usually interact

- **Business asks** enter through product, BA, or stakeholder management.
- **Prioritization** happens through portfolio, product, and governance.
- **Solution design** is owned by architecture, security, and platform teams.
- **Build and release** is executed by engineering, DevOps, QA, and operations.
- **Run and support** is handled by service desk, support, SRE, and operations.
- **Risk and control** are managed by security, compliance, change, and governance.

## Practical rule of thumb

If you are mapping humans to AI agents later, treat each box in the tree as a role cluster with:

- a clear purpose,
- explicit inputs,
- explicit outputs,
- decision rights,
- escalation path,
- and measurable service levels.

That makes it much easier to replace repetitive work with agents without losing accountability.
