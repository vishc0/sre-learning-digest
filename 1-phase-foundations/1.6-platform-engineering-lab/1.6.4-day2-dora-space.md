## Analogy

**Your SLO system, but for the delivery pipeline itself.**

You already run SLOs for your 25M msg/day notification platform:
- SLI = actual delivery success rate
- SLO = target (e.g., 99.9%)
- Error budget = how much failure is allowed before you freeze deploys

DORA works exactly the same way — just measuring your *pipeline* instead of your *product*:

| SRE/SLO Concept | DORA Equivalent |
|---|---|
| SLI (measured signal) | Deployment Frequency, Lead Time, CFR, MTTR |
| SLO target | Elite/High/Medium/Low benchmarks |
| Error budget burn | Rising CFR + falling DF = pipeline burning budget |
| Burn rate alert | "We've had 4 rollbacks in 2 weeks — freeze new features" |
| Postmortem → SLO fix | Incident → pipeline improvement → new DORA baseline |

You already know how to set targets, measure actuals, and use the gap to drive engineering behavior. DORA just extends that instinct upstream into the delivery pipeline.

## 30-Second Version

DORA gives you four numbers that predict whether an engineering org is high-performing: how often they deploy, how fast code flows to prod, how often deploys break things, and how fast they recover. Elite performers score well on all four simultaneously — high throughput AND high stability. SPACE adds human/team dimensions DORA misses: developer satisfaction, collaboration health, and cognitive load. Together they let a Director answer: "Is this platform getting better or worse?"

---

## 2-Minute Version

**The Four DORA Keys:**

1. **Deployment Frequency (DF)** — How often does your team deploy to production? Elite = multiple times per day. This is your pipeline throughput signal. Low DF usually means large batches, which means higher risk per deploy.

2. **Lead Time for Changes (LT)** — From first commit to production running that commit, how long? Elite = less than 1 hour. Long lead times = slow feedback loops = bugs compound.

3. **Change Failure Rate (CFR)** — Of all deploys, what % required a rollback, hotfix, or caused an incident? Elite = 0–5%. High CFR means your pipeline has poor gates — missing tests, missing canary rollout, missing feature flags.

4. **Mean Time to Restore (MTTR)** — When a deploy does break prod, how fast do you recover? Elite = less than 1 hour. This is your incident command effectiveness metric, mapped directly to your existing SRE practice.

**The key insight**: DF and LT measure *throughput*. CFR and MTTR measure *stability*. The research finding that shook the industry: **you don't have to trade them off**. Elite orgs have BOTH high throughput AND high stability. The teams with low DF trying to "be careful" actually have HIGHER CFR because batch sizes are huge.

**SPACE** extends this into human territory: Satisfaction (are devs burnt out?), Performance (are outcomes being achieved?), Activity (commit/PR volume — a leading not lagging indicator), Communication/Collaboration (PR review time, async health), Efficiency (toil ratio, flow state time).

## 5-Minute Version (Tradeoffs, Failure Modes, Design Decisions)

### Calculating Each Metric

**Deployment Frequency:**
```
DF = total_prod_deploys / time_window_days
```
Unit: deploys per day (or week). Normalize by team size for cross-team comparison.

Watch out: "deploy" must mean *production*. Staging pushes don't count. This definition fight is common in orgs with murky environments.

**Lead Time for Changes:**
```
LT = median(prod_deploy_timestamp - first_commit_timestamp) across all deploys in window
```
Use median not mean — outlier PRs (large refactors, dependency upgrades) will skew mean badly. Same statistical reasoning as your SLO p99 vs mean latency decision.

**Change Failure Rate:**
```
CFR = incidents_caused_by_deploy / total_deploys
```
"Incident" threshold matters enormously. Elite orgs count anything that required a rollback or hotfix. Orgs gaming the metric count only Sev1s. Set this definition at the Director level and enforce it — otherwise Goodhart's Law destroys the signal.

**MTTR:**
```
MTTR = mean(restore_timestamp - incident_open_timestamp)
```
Restore = "platform confirmed healthy at target SLO." Not "deploy rolled back." This is important — a rollback is a tool, not a restore. Restore is when your SLO returns to green.

### SPACE Framework Deep Dive

SPACE (from Microsoft Research, 2021) was designed to correct the failure mode where orgs over-index on Activity metrics (commits, PRs, lines of code) and miss the human dimensions. The five dimensions:

| Dimension | What It Measures | How to Instrument |
|---|---|---|
| **Satisfaction** | Developer happiness, burnout risk, intent to stay | Quarterly pulse (simple 5-question eNPS-style) |
| **Performance** | Outcomes delivered vs goals | OKR completion rate, feature adoption, reliability targets hit |
| **Activity** | Work volume — commits, PRs, reviews, deploys | Git + JIRA telemetry; use as leading indicator only |
| **Communication/Collaboration** | Async health, PR review latency, knowledge silos | PR cycle time, cross-team dependency latency |
| **Efficiency** | Toil ratio, CI wait time, context switching overhead | % time on toil vs feature work; measured via time-tracking or survey |

**The critical rule**: Never use SPACE dimensions in isolation. An Activity metric spike (high commits) during an Efficiency crash (CI is slow) means your team is thrashing, not shipping. SPACE is a dashboard, not a leaderboard.

### Toil Measurement

Toil = manual, repetitive, automatable work that scales with service load. Not all ops work is toil — postmortems, architecture review, mentoring are not toil.

```
toil_ratio = toil_hours / total_eng_hours_per_week
```

Google SRE target: toil < 50% of any SRE's time. Above 50% = org is consuming its own engineering capacity. This is a Director-level governance metric: if your team's toil ratio is rising, you're not scaling your platform, you're scaling your headcount treadmill.

### Failure Modes

1. **Goodhart's Law corruption**: When DF becomes a target, teams split PRs to hit 10 deploys/day artificially. Mitigation: track DF alongside CFR. You can't fake both simultaneously.

2. **Survivorship bias in CFR**: Teams stop counting rollbacks as "failures" because rollbacks are "the right behavior." This silently inflates CFR. Define: any deploy that required intervention within 24 hours = failure.

3. **MTTR denominator gaming**: Measuring only Sev1 MTTR while Sev2s linger for 8 hours. Mitigation: MTTR should include all customer-impacting events, consistent with your SLO breach definition.

### Design Decisions at Director Level

- **Tooling**: GitHub Actions + custom Python dashboards (this lab), or commercial platforms (LinearB, Haystack, Cortex). Open-source first, commercial if team > 50 engineers.
- **Cadence**: Weekly trending report (engineering team), Monthly aggregate (CTO), Quarterly narrative (CFO).
- **Normalization**: Teams vary by domain risk. A payments team with DF = 2/week and CFR = 2% may be Elite for that risk profile. A frontend team at 2/week is Low. Document the adjustment.
- **Leading vs lagging**: DORA keys are all lagging indicators. Use PR cycle time and CI pass rate as leading indicators to predict DORA degradation before it hits the scoreboard.

## ANSWER KEY — Comprehension Check

**Q1 Answer:**
- CFR = 4 failures / 40 deploys = **10%** → Medium performer tier (Elite is 0–5%, High is 5–10%, technically at the boundary — call it High/Medium boundary, most frameworks say >10% is Low, 5–10% is Medium)
- MTTR = mean(45, 90, 30, 75) = 240/4 = **60 minutes** → Elite tier (Elite is < 1 hour — you're right at the boundary; use median: median(45, 75) = 60 min, same result)
- Interpretation: You have Elite MTTR (great incident response) but degraded CFR (pipeline gates need improvement). Classic pattern for teams with strong oncall culture but missing pre-prod validation.

**Q2 Answer:**
You say: "Splitting PRs to hit DF is Goodhart's Law — the metric becomes the target and loses its signal. DF only means something if CFR holds steady or drops. I'd rather see DF at 6 with CFR at 3% than DF at 10 with CFR at 12%. Let's track both together and set a joint target."

**Q3 Answer:**
"Every month, roughly 3 deploys cause customer-impacting incidents, each costing us 6+ engineer-hours to resolve. Reducing our Change Failure Rate from 10% to Elite tier (under 5%) eliminates half of those incidents — that's 9 engineer-days per month returned to feature work. The CI rebuild is the enabler: faster feedback loops mean developers catch failures before they reach prod."

### Step 2 — Write the DORA calculator script

```bash
cat > dora_calculator.py << 'PYEOF'
#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
DORA Metrics Calculator
-----------------------
Input:  deployments.csv  (30 deployments over ~5 weeks)
Output: DORA report printed to stdout

DORA Four Keys:
  1. Deployment Frequency (DF)   — how often we ship to prod
  2. Lead Time for Changes (LT)  — commit to prod time (median)
  3. Change Failure Rate (CFR)   — % deploys that caused incidents
  4. MTTR                        — mean time to restore after incident

Elite benchmarks (DORA 2023):
  DF   : multiple deploys per day  (> 1.0/day)
  LT   : < 1 hour                  (< 60 min)
  CFR  : 0-5%                      (< 5.0%)
  MTTR : < 1 hour                  (< 60 min)
"""

import csv
from datetime import datetime
import statistics

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# CONFIGURATION: benchmark thresholds
# Source: DORA 2023 State of DevOps Report
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
BENCHMARKS = {
    "DF": {
        "Elite":  {"min": 1.0,    "label": "> 1 deploy/day"},
        "High":   {"min": 0.143,  "label": "> 1/week"},     # ~1/week
        "Medium": {"min": 0.033,  "label": "> 1/month"},    # ~1/month
        "Low":    {"min": 0,      "label": "< 1/month"},
    },
    "LT_hours": {
        "Elite":  {"max": 1,      "label": "< 1 hour"},
        "High":   {"max": 24,     "label": "< 1 day"},
        "Medium": {"max": 168,    "label": "< 1 week"},
        "Low":    {"max": 9999,   "label": "> 1 week"},
    },
    "CFR_pct": {
        "Elite":  {"max": 5,      "label": "0-5%"},
        "High":   {"max": 10,     "label": "5-10%"},
        "Medium": {"max": 15,     "label": "10-15%"},
        "Low":    {"max": 100,    "label": "> 15%"},
    },
    "MTTR_hours": {
        "Elite":  {"max": 1,      "label": "< 1 hour"},
        "High":   {"max": 24,     "label": "< 1 day"},
        "Medium": {"max": 168,    "label": "< 1 week"},
        "Low":    {"max": 9999,   "label": "> 1 week"},
    },
}

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# HELPERS
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
TIMESTAMP_FMT = "%Y-%m-%d %H:%M:%S"

def parse_ts(s):
    """Parse a timestamp string. Return None if empty."""
    # WHY: CSV cells for non-incident rows are empty strings
    if not s or not s.strip():
        return None
    return datetime.strptime(s.strip(), TIMESTAMP_FMT)

def hours_between(start, end):
    """Return float hours between two datetime objects."""
    # WHY: using total_seconds() / 3600 avoids integer truncation
    return (end - start).total_seconds() / 3600

def tier_for_df(value):
    """Map a DF value (deploys/day) to an Elite/High/Medium/Low tier."""
    if value >= BENCHMARKS["DF"]["Elite"]["min"]:
        return "Elite"
    elif value >= BENCHMARKS["DF"]["High"]["min"]:
        return "High"
    elif value >= BENCHMARKS["DF"]["Medium"]["min"]:
        return "Medium"
    else:
        return "Low"

def tier_for_max(metric_key, value):
    """
    Map a metric value to a tier for metrics where LOWER is better.
    Checks Elite first (tightest threshold), then High, Medium, Low.
    """
    for tier in ["Elite", "High", "Medium", "Low"]:
        if value <= BENCHMARKS[metric_key][tier]["max"]:
            return tier
    return "Low"

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# LOAD DATA
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
deployments = []

with open("deployments.csv", newline="") as f:
    reader = csv.DictReader(f)
    for row in reader:
        deployments.append({
            "id":              row["deploy_id"],
            "deploy_ts":       parse_ts(row["deploy_timestamp"]),
            "commit_ts":       parse_ts(row["first_commit_timestamp"]),
            "is_incident":     row["resulted_in_incident"].strip().lower() == "true",
            "incident_open":   parse_ts(row.get("incident_open_timestamp", "")),
            "incident_closed": parse_ts(row.get("incident_resolved_timestamp", "")),
        })

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# METRIC 1: DEPLOYMENT FREQUENCY
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# WHY: measure the span of the actual data window, not a fixed 30 days.
# Using the real first-to-last deploy window gives a more honest DF.
deploy_timestamps = [d["deploy_ts"] for d in deployments]
window_start = min(deploy_timestamps)
window_end   = max(deploy_timestamps)
window_days  = (window_end - window_start).days + 1  # +1 to include both endpoints

total_deploys = len(deployments)
df_per_day = total_deploys / window_days

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# METRIC 2: LEAD TIME FOR CHANGES (median, in hours)
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# WHY: median, not mean — large outlier PRs (refactors) would skew mean.
# Same logic as using p99 latency over average for SLO measurement.
lead_times_hours = []
for d in deployments:
    if d["commit_ts"] and d["deploy_ts"]:
        lt = hours_between(d["commit_ts"], d["deploy_ts"])
        lead_times_hours.append(lt)

median_lt_hours = statistics.median(lead_times_hours)
mean_lt_hours   = statistics.mean(lead_times_hours)

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# METRIC 3: CHANGE FAILURE RATE
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# WHY: CFR = incidents caused BY deploys / total deploys.
# This is NOT the same as all incidents — only deploy-triggered ones.
failed_deploys = [d for d in deployments if d["is_incident"]]
cfr_pct = (len(failed_deploys) / total_deploys) * 100

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# METRIC 4: MEAN TIME TO RESTORE (MTTR)
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# WHY: only calculated over deploys that actually caused incidents.
# Mean used here (vs median) because each incident is a discrete event
# and outlier incidents are operationally important, not noise to remove.
mttr_values_hours = []
for d in failed_deploys:
    if d["incident_open"] and d["incident_closed"]:
        mttr = hours_between(d["incident_open"], d["incident_closed"])
        mttr_values_hours.append(mttr)

mean_mttr_hours = statistics.mean(mttr_values_hours) if mttr_values_hours else 0

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# TIER CLASSIFICATION
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
df_tier   = tier_for_df(df_per_day)
lt_tier   = tier_for_max("LT_hours",   median_lt_hours)
cfr_tier  = tier_for_max("CFR_pct",    cfr_pct)
mttr_tier = tier_for_max("MTTR_hours", mean_mttr_hours)

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# TOIL PROXY (bonus metric)
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# WHY: Incidents from deploys require manual intervention.
# A rough toil signal: incident_hours / window_days.
# Real toil measurement needs time-tracking, but this is a proxy.
total_incident_hours = sum(mttr_values_hours)
daily_toil_proxy_hours = total_incident_hours / window_days

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# REPORT OUTPUT
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
TIER_ICONS = {
    "Elite":  "[ELITE  ]",
    "High":   "[HIGH   ]",
    "Medium": "[MEDIUM ]",
    "Low":    "[LOW    ]",
}

separator = "=" * 60

print(separator)
print("  DORA METRICS REPORT")
print(f"  Dataset: {total_deploys} deployments")
print(f"  Window:  {window_start.date()} to {window_end.date()} ({window_days} days)")
print(separator)

print(f"\n1. DEPLOYMENT FREQUENCY")
print(f"   Measured : {df_per_day:.2f} deploys/day  ({total_deploys} deploys / {window_days} days)")
print(f"   Tier     : {TIER_ICONS[df_tier]} {df_tier}")
print(f"   Elite    : {BENCHMARKS['DF']['Elite']['label']}")

print(f"\n2. LEAD TIME FOR CHANGES")
print(f"   Median   : {median_lt_hours:.1f} hours")
print(f"   Mean     : {mean_lt_hours:.1f} hours  (shown for reference; median is canonical)")
print(f"   Tier     : {TIER_ICONS[lt_tier]} {lt_tier}")
print(f"   Elite    : {BENCHMARKS['LT_hours']['Elite']['label']}")

print(f"\n3. CHANGE FAILURE RATE")
print(f"   Measured : {cfr_pct:.1f}%  ({len(failed_deploys)} incidents / {total_deploys} deploys)")
print(f"   Tier     : {TIER_ICONS[cfr_tier]} {cfr_tier}")
print(f"   Elite    : {BENCHMARKS['CFR_pct']['Elite']['label']}")

print(f"\n4. MEAN TIME TO RESTORE (MTTR)")
print(f"   Measured : {mean_mttr_hours:.1f} hours  (across {len(mttr_values_hours)} incidents)")
print(f"   Tier     : {TIER_ICONS[mttr_tier]} {mttr_tier}")
print(f"   Elite    : {BENCHMARKS['MTTR_hours']['Elite']['label']}")

print(f"\n--- INCIDENT DETAIL ---")
print(f"  {'ID':<6} {'Deploy Time':<22} {'MTTR (hrs)':<12}")
print(f"  {'-'*40}")
for d in failed_deploys:
    if d["incident_open"] and d["incident_closed"]:
        mttr_h = hours_between(d["incident_open"], d["incident_closed"])
        print(f"  {d['id']:<6} {str(d['deploy_ts']):<22} {mttr_h:.2f}")

print(f"\n--- TOIL PROXY ---")
print(f"  Total incident-recovery hours in window : {total_incident_hours:.1f} hrs")
print(f"  Daily toil proxy (incident hours/day)   : {daily_toil_proxy_hours:.2f} hrs/day")
print(f"  Note: Real toil = this + manual runbook + oncall overhead")

print(f"\n--- SUMMARY SCORECARD ---")
```

```bash

# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# BONUS: WEEK-OVER-WEEK CFR TREND
# ------------------------------------------------------------------
# WHY: A single CFR number can hide an improving or worsening trend.
# Directors need to know: "Is this getting better or worse?"
# This splits deploys into ISO calendar weeks and calculates CFR per week.

from collections import defaultdict

print("\n--- CFR TREND (week-over-week) ---")
print(f"  {'Week':<8} {'Deploys':<10} {'Failures':<10} {'CFR %':<10} {'Trend'}")
print(f"  {'-'*50}")

weekly = defaultdict(lambda: {"deploys": 0, "failures": 0})
for d in deployments:
    # ISO week gives consistent Monday-Sunday buckets
    week_key = d["deploy_ts"].strftime("%Y-W%W")
    weekly[week_key]["deploys"] += 1
    if d["is_incident"]:
        weekly[week_key]["failures"] += 1

prev_cfr = None
for week in sorted(weekly.keys()):
    w = weekly[week]
    cfr = (w["failures"] / w["deploys"]) * 100 if w["deploys"] > 0 else 0
    if prev_cfr is None:
        trend = "  (baseline)"
    elif cfr < prev_cfr:
        trend = f"  v DOWN {prev_cfr - cfr:.0f}pp (improving)"
    elif cfr > prev_cfr:
        trend = f"  ^ UP   {cfr - prev_cfr:.0f}pp (worsening)"
    else:
        trend = "  = FLAT"
    print(f"  {week:<8} {w['deploys']:<10} {w['failures']:<10} {cfr:<10.1f} {trend}")
    prev_cfr = cfr
PYEOF
```

## Tradeoffs and Failure Modes

**1. Goodhart's Law at scale**

The moment DORA metrics become executive OKRs, teams optimize for the metric, not the outcome. DF goes up because PRs are artificially split. CFR goes down because "rollback" is redefined as "planned maintenance." MTTR goes down because incident tickets are closed prematurely. Mitigation: always track metrics in pairs (DF+CFR), audit the definitions annually, and tie metrics to business outcomes (SLO compliance, customer ticket volume) not just their own targets.

**2. Context collapse in cross-team comparison**

DORA is calibrated against self-reported industry data. A regulated fintech team deploying to prod once per week after mandatory 4-eyes approval may be operating at the appropriate risk posture for their domain — they are not a "Low performer," they are constrained. Publishing raw DORA scores across all teams creates destructive competition. Mitigation: tier teams by regulatory/risk context before benchmarking. Your T-Mobile notification platform has different risk tolerance than a payments API — document that explicitly.

**3. Missing the human layer (SPACE gap)**

DORA can be green while your team is burning out. If CFR is 3% because your best IC is reviewing every PR before merge, you have a single-point-of-failure in your pipeline that DORA cannot see. SPACE's Satisfaction and Efficiency dimensions surface this. The failure mode: an org declares itself Elite on DORA, skips the SPACE survey, and loses the senior IC who was the hidden load-bearing wall. Mitigation: run SPACE quarterly alongside DORA. Pay specific attention to Efficiency (toil ratio) and Satisfaction (engineering NPS) as leading indicators of future DORA degradation.
