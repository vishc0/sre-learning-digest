# Supply Chain Security — SBOM, SCA, and Why It Matters | Week 2 Day 1

## Why This Matters

Supply chain attacks are now the #1 vector for nation-state and ransomware intrusion — SolarWinds compromised 18,000 organizations including the US Treasury without ever touching the target's own code. OWASP Top 10:2025 lists Supply Chain Failures as A03, and White House Executive Order 14028 (May 2021) mandates SBOM generation for any software sold to the US federal government. Senior SRE and DevSecOps interviewers will probe whether you can distinguish between "we have SCA" and "we have a governed supply chain posture" — the gap between those two positions is the Director-level conversation.

---

## Analogy

Think of your application's dependencies the way a food supplier thinks about ingredient traceability. When there is an E. coli outbreak in romaine lettuce, the FDA doesn't just test the finished salad — they trace every batch of lettuce across every supplier, farm, and shipping route. An SBOM is your ingredient label and traceability manifest. SCA is the food safety inspector who checks every ingredient against the recall database. Without the SBOM, you cannot run the inspection — you don't even know what's in the dish.

The SolarWinds attack is the equivalent of a contaminated ingredient being added at the supplier's factory before it ever reached your kitchen. Your own security team never touched the compromised code. That is why perimeter security and SAST (scanning your own code) are insufficient — you also need to scan everything you pulled from external suppliers.

## 30-Second Version

A software supply chain attack exploits the trust you place in open-source libraries, build tools, and third-party vendors — not your own code. An SBOM is a machine-readable manifest of every component in your software artifact. SCA tools scan that manifest against vulnerability databases to find known-bad versions. EO 14028 made SBOM mandatory for federal software vendors in 2021, and the industry followed. In 2025, if you cannot generate an SBOM from your CI pipeline and automatically block deployments with critical CVEs, you do not have a supply chain posture — you have wishful thinking.

## 2-Minute Version

### The Three Attack Patterns to Name in Interviews

**SolarWinds (2020)**: Attackers compromised SolarWinds' build pipeline and injected malicious code into a digitally signed software update (Orion platform). 18,000+ customers installed a backdoor thinking it was a legitimate patch. The lesson: your vendor's signed artifact is not automatically trustworthy.

**XZ Utils (2024)**: A patient, multi-year social engineering campaign compromised the maintainer of `xz`, a ubiquitous Linux compression library. The attacker inserted a backdoor into versions 5.6.0 and 5.6.1 that would have allowed unauthorized SSH access on most Linux distributions. Caught only days before reaching stable Debian/Ubuntu. The lesson: transitive dependencies in foundational OS packages are a critical attack surface.

**Log4Shell (2021)**: A remote code execution vulnerability (CVE-2021-44228) in Apache Log4j — a logging library embedded in thousands of Java applications, often as a transitive dependency. Many organizations didn't even know they were using it. The lesson: you cannot patch what you cannot inventory.

### SBOM: The Inventory Mandate

An SBOM answers: what software components are in this artifact, what versions, and where did they come from? EO 14028 (White House, May 2021) requires federal software vendors to provide SBOMs. NTIA published minimum elements: supplier name, component name, version, dependency relationships, timestamp, and author.

**SPDX** (Software Package Data Exchange): Linux Foundation standard, ISO/IEC 5962:2021, older and more widely implemented in tooling. Format: tag-value, JSON, or YAML.

**CycloneDX**: OWASP standard, purpose-built for security use cases. Better support for vulnerability exploitability exchange (VEX). Preferred by Grype and many modern tools.

### SCA: What SAST Misses

SAST (Static Application Security Testing) analyzes YOUR code for vulnerabilities you wrote. SCA analyzes your DEPENDENCIES — including transitive ones (dependencies of dependencies). A typical Node.js app with 30 direct dependencies may have 300+ transitive dependencies. SCA catches CVEs in that entire tree. Log4Shell would have been caught by SCA; it would NOT have been caught by SAST because the vulnerability was in the library, not in your code.

## 5-Minute Version

### The Full Attack Surface Model

Supply chain attacks target five insertion points:

1. **Source code compromise** — attacker commits malicious code to an open-source repo (XZ Utils)
2. **Build system compromise** — attacker injects code during CI/CD build (SolarWinds)
3. **Dependency confusion** — attacker publishes a malicious package with a name matching an internal private package (exploits npm/PyPI resolution order)
4. **Registry poisoning** — compromising a package registry (npm, PyPI, Maven Central) directly
5. **Update mechanism abuse** — compromising the software update channel post-deployment

### SBOM Formats: When to Choose Which

| Decision | Choose SPDX | Choose CycloneDX |
|---|---|---|
| Regulatory compliance (NTIA, EO 14028) | Yes — ISO standard | Also accepted |
| Vulnerability workflow integration | Adequate | Better (VEX support) |
| Tool ecosystem (2025) | Broad | Broader for security tools |
| License compliance tracking | Stronger | Adequate |
| T-Mobile notification platform context | Either | CycloneDX preferred for Grype integration |

### Transitive Dependency Problem: The Math

If your service depends on 10 libraries, and each of those depends on 10 libraries, and each of those depends on 10 more — you have a dependency tree with potentially 1,000+ components. Log4Shell was a transitive dependency in most affected systems. The only way to govern this at scale is: SBOM generation in CI (artifact-level inventory) + automated SCA scanning + CVE policy gates (block deploy if CVSS >= 9.0 unpatched).

### Grype vs Trivy: The Design Philosophy Difference

**Grype** (Anchore): SBOM-first scanner. Takes an SBOM as input and outputs vulnerabilities. Clean separation of concerns — generate inventory first, then scan. Better for organizations that want SBOM as an artifact they store and re-scan as new CVEs emerge without rebuilding the image.

**Trivy** (Aqua Security): All-in-one Swiss Army knife. Scans images, filesystems, git repos, Kubernetes clusters, IaC files, and SBOMs in one tool. Easier to adopt, slightly less flexible for advanced SBOM workflows. In 2025, Trivy is the more common entry point because it reduces tool sprawl.

**The failure modes to know**:
- **False negatives**: Both tools miss vulnerabilities in statically linked binaries where the library version is not detectable from package metadata
- **False positives**: Both tools flag CVEs in libraries that your code never exercises the vulnerable code path of — this is where VEX (Vulnerability Exploitability Exchange) metadata helps
- **Database lag**: Both rely on NVD, OSV, and vendor advisories — a zero-day is invisible until it's reported
- **SBOM coverage gap**: Syft/Trivy can only inventory what's declared in package manifests — manually vendored code (copy-pasted library code) is invisible

### OWASP Top 10:2025 A03 — Supply Chain Failures

Previously called "Injection" in older OWASP Top 10 versions. Renamed and re-scoped in 2025 to explicitly cover:
- Use of components with known vulnerabilities
- Lack of software composition analysis
- Missing SBOM generation
- Unsigned or unverified artifacts
- Dependency confusion attacks

Interview signal: If you mention OWASP A03 and can explain it was A06 (Components with Known Vulnerabilities) in 2021 and was elevated to A03 in 2025 reflecting real-world attack trends, you demonstrate current awareness.

## Director/VP Lens

**How an IC implements this**: Adds Syft to the Dockerfile build step, adds Grype to the CI pipeline with `--fail-on critical`, reviews CVE reports in the security dashboard, files tickets to upgrade vulnerable dependencies.

**How a Director/VP governs this**:

1. **Policy definition**: Sets the CVE threshold for deploy blocking (typically: block on CVSS >= 9.0 critical with known exploits; warn on 7.0–8.9 high; log and track 4.0–6.9 medium). This threshold is a business risk decision, not a tool configuration decision.

2. **SBOM as artifact lifecycle**: Mandates that every container image published to ECR includes a co-located SBOM artifact in CycloneDX format. SBOM is re-scanned nightly against updated CVE databases — not just at build time.

3. **Exception process**: Establishes a formal waiver process for teams that cannot immediately patch a critical CVE (dependency has no fix yet). Waiver requires: exploitability assessment, compensating control, owner acknowledgment, 30-day expiry.

4. **Vendor SBOM requirements**: Includes SBOM delivery requirements in vendor contracts and SaaS agreements. For EO 14028 compliance: federal customers require SBOM delivery as part of software procurement.

5. **AI lens**: GitHub Advanced Security and Snyk now use LLM-assisted reachability analysis — they determine whether your code actually calls the vulnerable function, reducing false positives by 40-60%. A Director evaluates these tools not just as scanners but as signals for prioritization.

**The interview answer a Director gives**: "We govern supply chain security at three levels: policy (what CVE threshold blocks deployment), process (SBOM as a living artifact re-scanned on a schedule), and toolchain (Grype in CI, Trivy in cluster-side scanning, CycloneDX SBOMs stored alongside images in ECR). The hardest operational problem is not generating SBOMs — it's what you do when a critical CVE appears in a library that has no patch yet and 40 services depend on it. We solved that with a formal compensating control waiver process and automated quarantine tagging in our image registry."

## ANSWER KEY — Comprehension Check

**A1**:
- **SolarWinds**: Build system compromise — attackers injected malicious code into SolarWinds' CI/CD pipeline so the signed, distributed binary itself contained the backdoor. Your perimeter never saw the attack.
- **XZ Utils**: Source code compromise — a patient attacker socially engineered the open-source maintainer and directly committed malicious code into the upstream repository. The backdoor was in the source before it reached any build system.
- **Log4Shell**: Vulnerable component in production — no attacker action was required to introduce it; it was a pre-existing critical vulnerability in a widely-used library (Apache Log4j) that millions of systems were already running as a transitive dependency.

**A2**: No — SAST would NOT catch this. SAST analyzes the code YOU write for vulnerabilities in your logic. Apache Commons Text is a third-party library your code imports; SAST does not analyze its internals. SCA (Software Composition Analysis) would catch it — it compares the full dependency tree (including transitive deps) against CVE databases. If you had a Grype or Trivy scan in your CI pipeline, it would flag the vulnerable version of Apache Commons Text the moment the CVE was published and your database was updated.

**A3**: Build-time SBOM generation is insufficient when:
- A new CVE is published after the image was built and before the next build. The SBOM exists but was never re-scanned against the new CVE. Mitigation: nightly re-scan of stored SBOMs against updated vulnerability databases.
- Other valid answers: the image is never rebuilt (long-lived base images), or a dependency is vendored manually (copy-pasted) and not declared in package manifests so Syft never inventoried it.

### Step 3: Generate an SBOM for nginx:latest in CycloneDX Format


### Step 6: Scan the SBOM with Grype — Observe CVE Findings

```bash
cd ~/supply-chain-lab

# Scan the SBOM file — this is the SBOM-first workflow
grype sbom:nginx-sbom.cdx.json

# Capture output for comparison later
grype sbom:nginx-sbom.cdx.json 2>&1 | tee grype-results.txt

# Count findings by severity
echo ""
echo "=== CVE Count by Severity ==="
grype sbom:nginx-sbom.cdx.json --output table 2>/dev/null | grep -E "Critical|High|Medium|Low|Negligible" | \

```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT** (representative — your exact CVEs will vary by nginx version and Grype DB date):

### Step 10: Generate a CI-Gate-Style Severity Check

```bash
cd ~/supply-chain-lab

# This is what you would put in your CI pipeline to gate deployments
echo "=== SIMULATING CI PIPELINE GATE ==="
echo "Policy: Block deployment if any CRITICAL CVEs found"
echo ""

CRITICAL_COUNT=$(grype sbom:nginx-sbom.cdx.json --fail-on critical 2>&1; echo $?)

# Alternative: use exit code behavior
grype sbom:nginx-sbom.cdx.json --fail-on critical > /dev/null 2>&1
EXIT_CODE=$?

if [ $EXIT_CODE -ne 0 ]; then
    echo "DEPLOY BLOCKED: Critical CVEs found. Exit code: $EXIT_CODE"
    echo "Action required: Review grype-results.txt for CRITICAL severity findings"
    echo "If no patch exists: open compensating control waiver with security team"
else
    echo "DEPLOY ALLOWED: No critical CVEs found at this time"
fi

echo ""
echo "=== SBOM Artifacts Generated ==="
ls -lh ~/supply-chain-lab/*.json ~/supply-chain-lab/*.txt 2>/dev/null
```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT**:
```
=== SIMULATING CI PIPELINE GATE ===
Policy: Block deployment if any CRITICAL CVEs found

DEPLOY ALLOWED: No critical CVEs found at this time
  (or)
DEPLOY BLOCKED: Critical CVEs found. Exit code: 1
Action required: Review grype-results.txt for CRITICAL severity findings
If no patch exists: open compensating control waiver with security team

=== SBOM Artifacts Generated ===
-rw-r--r-- 1 user user 180K Jun 11 10:15 nginx-sbom.cdx.json
-rw-r--r-- 1 user user  95K Jun 11 10:15 nginx-trivy-sbom.cdx.json
-rw-r--r-- 1 user user  12K Jun 11 10:15 grype-results.txt
-rw-r--r-- 1 user user  15K Jun 11 10:15 trivy-results.txt
```

**WHY**: The `--fail-on critical` flag is the integration point between your security scanner and your CI pipeline (GitHub Actions, Jenkins, GitLab CI). A non-zero exit code causes the pipeline to fail, blocking the deployment. This is what "shift-left security" means operationally — the policy enforcement happens in the pipeline, not after a human reviews a report.

## Tradeoffs & Failure Modes

These three are interview gold — interviewers probe beyond "we scan our images."

**Tradeoff 1: SBOM-first (Grype) vs All-in-One (Trivy)**

Grype's SBOM-first model means you pay the scan cost once (at build time) and can re-interrogate the SBOM as new CVEs emerge without rebuilding the image. Trivy is faster to adopt (one tool, one command) but re-scanning requires access to the live image or a separately stored SBOM. At scale (200+ microservices), Grype's model is more efficient for ongoing governance; Trivy's model is better for teams starting out or for ad-hoc scanning. The risk of choosing Trivy only: you may not store SBOMs as artifacts, losing the ability to prove historical compliance posture to auditors.

**Tradeoff 2: Blocking CI on High vs Critical CVEs**

Blocking deploys on CRITICAL CVEs (CVSS >= 9.0) is generally accepted. Blocking on HIGH (CVSS 7.0–8.9) generates so many false positives — especially for CVEs with no available fix — that teams begin disabling the gate entirely. This is the most common failure mode: over-tuned gates lead to bypass culture where developers add `continue-on-error: true` to security steps. The correct governance model is: Critical = hard block; High with known exploit = block; High without exploit = warn + ticket; Medium = log only. The thresholds must be revisited quarterly as the threat landscape changes.

**Tradeoff 3: Build-time Scanning vs Runtime Detection**

SBOM scanning catches known vulnerabilities at build time against known CVEs. It does not detect: zero-days (not yet in CVE databases), behavioral attacks (malicious code that has no CVE because it was intentionally written, like XZ Utils), or credential theft at runtime. Runtime security (Falco, eBPF-based tooling) is the complement to SCA — you need both. The common interview mistake is treating SCA as a complete supply chain security solution. The Director answer: "SCA is the inventory and known-CVE layer. Runtime behavioral monitoring is the unknown-threat layer. Both are required, and they operate on different data sources."
