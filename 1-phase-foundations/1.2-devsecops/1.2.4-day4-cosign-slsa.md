# Container Image Signing: Cosign, Sigstore, SLSA | Week 2 Day 4

## Why This Matters

At T-Mobile scale — 25M messages/day — a compromised container image reaching production is a P0 incident that bypasses every upstream control you built. Container image signing is the supply chain control that answers: "Is the image running in my cluster the exact same artifact that was scanned, built, and approved?" This topic appears in 80% of Staff/Principal SRE and DevSecOps interviews as a direct follow-up to "how do you secure your CI/CD pipeline" — and most candidates cannot explain the difference between key-based and keyless signing.

## Analogy

Think of Kubernetes image signing exactly like tamper-evident seals on pharmaceutical shipments in a regulated supply chain.

When Pfizer ships vials from a manufacturing plant to a hospital:
1. The plant applies a tamper-evident seal at the moment of packaging (the **signature**)
2. A third-party auditor (like the FDA) records the seal ID in a public ledger (the **Rekor transparency log**)
3. The hospital pharmacist checks the seal before administering (the **verification at admission**)
4. If the seal is broken or missing, the vial is quarantined — the hospital doesn't guess (the **policy enforcement**)

Without this: a counterfeit vial (a maliciously modified image) with the same label (`nginx:latest`) looks identical until it harms someone. "The label said nginx" is not a defense.

The Rekor transparency log is like a blockchain-style public ledger at the FDA: nobody can claim they signed something without it appearing there, and nobody can hide a signing event. This is what makes keyless signing auditable without a private key you have to protect.

## 30-Second Version

Container image signing proves that the image your Kubernetes pod runs is byte-for-byte identical to the image that was scanned and approved in your pipeline. Cosign is the tool; Sigstore is the trust infrastructure (Rekor log + Fulcio CA) that makes keyless signing work without managing private keys. SLSA is the maturity framework that defines what "trustworthy build" means at four escalating levels. Together, these three things close the gap between "we scanned the image" and "the image running in prod is the one we scanned."

## 2-Minute Version

**The core problem:** Docker image tags are mutable. `nginx:latest` today is not `nginx:latest` tomorrow. An attacker who compromises your registry can replace an image under the same tag. Your vulnerability scanner approved the Monday image; your Wednesday deployment runs the Tuesday replacement. Tag-based references give you zero assurance.

**Digest pinning (first defense):** Using `image: nginx@sha256:abc123...` instead of `image: nginx:latest` pins to an immutable content hash. If even one byte changes, the hash changes. This is necessary but not sufficient — it proves the bits are the same, but not that the image was built the way you think.

**Cosign + Sigstore (second defense):** Cosign attaches a cryptographic signature to the image in the OCI registry — stored alongside the image as an OCI artifact, not as a separate sidecar file. Sigstore's Fulcio CA issues a short-lived X.509 certificate tied to your OIDC identity (your GitHub Actions job, your Google account). The signing event is recorded in Rekor (a public, append-only, Merkle-tree log). Verification checks: did the signature come from a trusted identity, and is it recorded in Rekor?

**Keyless vs key-based:**
- Key-based: generate a keypair, protect the private key forever, rotate it, audit its access. Familiar. Operationally expensive. Single point of failure.
- Keyless: no long-lived private key. Identity comes from your CI/CD OIDC token (GitHub Actions, GCP Workload Identity). Fulcio issues a cert valid for ~10 minutes. Rekor records it. Verification uses Rekor + the OIDC issuer as the trust anchor. Operationally simpler at scale — no secret rotation.

**SLSA levels (simplified):**
- Level 1: Build script exists, basic provenance generated
- Level 2: Signed provenance from a hosted build service (GitHub Actions counts)
- Level 3: Hardened build environment, two-person review enforced
- Level 4: Hermetic/reproducible builds (hardest, rare in practice)

Most organizations target SLSA Level 2 — it's the pragmatic security-vs-effort crossover point.

## 5-Minute Version

**How Cosign signature storage works:**

When you `cosign sign`, the signature is stored in the same OCI registry as the image, under a derived tag based on the image's digest. If your image digest is `sha256:abc123`, the signature is stored at a reference like `<registry>/<repo>:sha256-abc123.sig`. This is important: the signature travels with the image through registries. You don't need a separate signature database.

**Keyless signing flow (step by step):**
1. Your GitHub Actions job runs. GHA generates an OIDC token with claims: `sub: repo:org/repo:ref:refs/heads/main`
2. Cosign presents this token to Fulcio CA
3. Fulcio verifies the OIDC token with GitHub's OIDC endpoint, issues a short-lived X.509 cert (10-minute TTL)
4. Cosign signs the image digest using an ephemeral private key, generates the signature
5. The signing event (cert + signature) is recorded in Rekor
6. Cosign uploads the signature to the OCI registry
7. The ephemeral private key is discarded — it was only needed for those seconds

**Verification flow:**
1. `cosign verify` fetches the signature from the OCI registry
2. Checks Rekor: does this signing event appear in the public log?
3. Checks the cert: does the OIDC identity match your policy (e.g., `--certificate-identity-regexp "github.com/myorg/myrepo"`)?
4. Checks the cert chain: does it chain to Fulcio's root CA?
5. Verifies the signature cryptographically against the image digest

**Provenance attestation (SLSA Level 2):**
A signature proves WHO signed WHAT. Provenance proves HOW it was built. A SLSA provenance attestation is a signed JSON document (in-toto format) containing:
- The builder identity (GitHub Actions run ID)
- The source commit SHA
- The build invocation parameters
- The output artifact digest

This lets you answer: "Did this image come from a protected branch, run through our approved pipeline, from an unmodified commit?" Signature alone cannot answer that.

**Tradeoff: Sigstore public log vs. private Rekor:**
The public Rekor log (rekor.sigstore.dev) is operated by the Linux Foundation. It works immediately, no setup. Tradeoff: your signing events are public — anyone can see that you signed `mycompany/myapp:1.2.3` at timestamp X. For regulated industries (fintech, telecom), this may be a compliance concern. Solution: run your own Rekor instance (self-hosted Sigstore) — adds operational complexity but keeps signing events private.

**Digest pinning failure mode:**
The most common misunderstanding: "We pin digests so we're safe." Pinning prevents drift but not a compromised build pipeline. If your CI/CD is compromised at build time, the malicious image gets a legitimate digest, gets scanned (possibly passing, if the malicious payload evades the scanner), and gets signed. Defense in depth requires pinning AND signing AND admission control. The pinned digest prevents tag mutation; the signature proves build provenance; the admission controller enforces at deployment time.

**SLSA Level 3 requirements (what's hard):**
- Hardened build platform: no way for the build job to influence its own provenance (prevents "lying build" attacks)
- Two-person review enforced at the platform level, not just policy
- Source integrity: hermetic inputs (all dependencies pinned, no network fetches during build)
- This is why most teams are at SLSA Level 2 — Level 3 requires redesigning how builds work, not just adding signing

## Director/VP Lens

**How an IC implements this:**
- Installs Cosign, wires signing into the CI/CD pipeline after the build step
- Configures `cosign verify` in a Kyverno/Gatekeeper policy
- Pins image digests in Kubernetes manifests
- Generates SLSA provenance with `slsa-github-generator` action

**How a Director/VP governs this:**
- Sets the policy: "No unsigned image reaches staging or production" — enforced by admission controller, not by trusting engineers to remember
- Defines SLSA Level 2 as the baseline for all production workloads; Level 1 acceptable for dev/test
- Mandates digest pinning in all Kubernetes manifests via a linting gate in CI (if the manifest has `:latest`, the pipeline fails)
- Requires provenance attestation for all images in the production ECR registry, with weekly audit reports
- Evaluates self-hosted Rekor for regulated workloads (fintech compliance, telecom data residency)
- Tracks signing coverage as a security KPI on the engineering scorecard: "% of production images with verified signatures" — target 100%
- AI tooling angle: LLM-assisted policy generation for Kyverno rules; automated provenance analysis during incident investigation ("which commit triggered this bad image build?")

**The interview version of the leadership answer:**
"At T-Mobile, I would make image signing non-negotiable at the admission controller level — it's not a recommendation, it's a gate. I'd treat an unsigned image reaching production the same way we treat a deploy without a change ticket: automatic rollback, P2 incident, postmortem. The signing coverage metric belongs on my team's weekly OKR scorecard, not in a security audit that happens quarterly."

## ANSWER KEY — Comprehension Check

**A1.** Digest pinning prevents tag mutation — it ensures the image that runs tomorrow has the same bytes as today. It does NOT prove how the image was built. If an attacker compromises your CI/CD pipeline and injects malicious code during the build step, the resulting image gets a legitimate digest, passes scanning (possibly), and gets pinned. Signing with provenance attestation proves the image was built by a specific pipeline from a specific commit using a specific builder identity. Pinning = content integrity. Signing = build provenance. Both are required; neither replaces the other.

**A2.** Flow: (1) GHA job generates an OIDC token with identity claims (`repo:org/repo:ref:...`). (2) **Cosign** (the tool) presents this token to **Fulcio** (the CA). (3) Fulcio verifies the token with GitHub's OIDC endpoint, issues a short-lived X.509 cert (~10 min TTL). (4) Cosign uses an ephemeral keypair to sign the image digest, generating the signature. (5) The signing event (cert + signature) is recorded in **Rekor** (the public transparency log). (6) Cosign uploads the signature OCI artifact to ECR alongside the image. The ephemeral private key is then discarded — it has no value after the signing event is in Rekor.

**A3.** Decision: run a self-hosted Rekor instance (private Sigstore deployment) within your infrastructure. This keeps all signing events internal, satisfying data residency and compliance requirements. Operational complexity introduced: (a) you must operate and maintain the Rekor service (availability, backup, upgrade); (b) verification configuration must point to your private Rekor endpoint, not rekor.sigstore.dev; (c) your Fulcio CA is also self-hosted, so you manage the root CA cert distribution; (d) engineers must configure their local Cosign to use your private Sigstore endpoints. Kubernetes admission controllers (Kyverno/Gatekeeper) must also be configured with your private Rekor URL and Fulcio root for verification.

### Step 1: Install Cosign in WSL2

```bash
# Download the latest Cosign binary for Linux amd64
COSIGN_VERSION=$(curl -s https://api.github.com/repos/sigstore/cosign/releases/latest | grep '"tag_name"' | cut -d'"' -f4)
echo "Installing Cosign version: $COSIGN_VERSION"

curl -Lo cosign "https://github.com/sigstore/cosign/releases/download/${COSIGN_VERSION}/cosign-linux-amd64"

# Verify the download checksum (security practice: verify the verifier)
curl -Lo cosign-linux-amd64.sig "https://github.com/sigstore/cosign/releases/download/${COSIGN_VERSION}/cosign-linux-amd64.sig"

# Make it executable and install
chmod +x cosign
sudo mv cosign /usr/local/bin/cosign

# Verify installation
cosign version
```

### Step 3: Check if nginx:latest Has a Sigstore Signature


### Step 5: Push the Image to a Public Ephemeral Registry (ttl.sh)

```bash
# ttl.sh is a free ephemeral registry — images expire after 1 hour
# No account required. Perfect for signing labs without setting up ECR.

# Tag the image for ttl.sh — the tag format includes the TTL
# Use a unique name to avoid collisions
UNIQUE_TAG="ttl.sh/vchippa-cosign-lab-$(date +%s):1h"

docker tag training-lab/cosign-demo:v1.0.0 "$UNIQUE_TAG"

# Push to ttl.sh (no login required)
docker push "$UNIQUE_TAG"

# Save the tag for subsequent steps
echo "export DEMO_IMAGE=$UNIQUE_TAG" > ~/cosign-lab/env.sh
source ~/cosign-lab/env.sh
echo "Image pushed: $DEMO_IMAGE"
```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT:**

### Step 8: Look Up Your Signing Event in Rekor (Public Ledger)

```bash
source ~/cosign-lab/env.sh

# Get the image digest (needed for Rekor lookup)
IMAGE_DIGEST=$(docker inspect "$DEMO_IMAGE" --format='{{index .RepoDigests 0}}' | cut -d'@' -f2 2>/dev/null || \
  crane digest "$DEMO_IMAGE" 2>/dev/null || \
  cosign triangulate "$DEMO_IMAGE" 2>/dev/null | head -1)

# Search Rekor for signing events on your image
# Install rekor-cli if needed
REKOR_VERSION=$(curl -s https://api.github.com/repos/sigstore/rekor/releases/latest | grep '"tag_name"' | cut -d'"' -f4)
curl -Lo rekor-cli "https://github.com/sigstore/rekor/releases/download/${REKOR_VERSION}/rekor-cli-linux-amd64"
chmod +x rekor-cli && sudo mv rekor-cli /usr/local/bin/rekor-cli

rekor-cli search --artifact "$DEMO_IMAGE" --rekor_server https://rekor.sigstore.dev
```

### Step 10: Generate a SLSA Provenance Attestation (Simulation)


### Step 11: Demonstrate Digest Pinning in Kubernetes Manifests

```bash
# Get your actual image digest
source ~/cosign-lab/env.sh

# Get the full digest reference
PINNED_REF=$(docker inspect "$DEMO_IMAGE" \
  --format='{{index .RepoDigests 0}}' 2>/dev/null || echo "ttl.sh/vchippa-cosign-lab:sha256:<your-digest>")
echo "Pinned reference: $PINNED_REF"

# Create a Kubernetes manifest with BAD practice (tag-based reference)
cat > ~/cosign-lab/pod-bad.yaml << 'EOF'
# BAD: tag-based reference — nginx:latest tomorrow is not nginx:latest today
# An attacker who pushes to this tag controls what runs in your cluster
apiVersion: v1
kind: Pod
metadata:
  name: nginx-bad-practice
  labels:
    signing-policy: "none"
spec:
  containers:
  - name: nginx
    image: nginx:latest   # DANGER: mutable tag, no signature verification possible
EOF

# Create a Kubernetes manifest with GOOD practice (digest-pinned + signed)
cat > ~/cosign-lab/pod-good.yaml << EOF
# GOOD: digest-pinned reference — bytes are guaranteed immutable
# Combined with admission controller signature verification = full supply chain integrity
apiVersion: v1
kind: Pod
metadata:
  name: demo-signed-pod
  labels:
    signing-policy: "enforced"
    slsa-level: "2"
spec:
  containers:
  - name: demo
    # Digest-pinned: exact sha256 means this pod spec is immutable
    # Admission controller (Kyverno) will also verify Cosign signature on this digest
    image: ${PINNED_REF}
    resources:
      requests:
        memory: "32Mi"
        cpu: "10m"
      limits:
        memory: "64Mi"
        cpu: "50m"
EOF

echo "=== BAD manifest (tag-based) ==="
cat ~/cosign-lab/pod-bad.yaml

echo ""
echo "=== GOOD manifest (digest-pinned) ==="
cat ~/cosign-lab/pod-good.yaml

# In a cluster with Kyverno signature policy, pod-bad.yaml would be REJECTED
# pod-good.yaml would be ADMITTED only if signature verification passes
echo ""
echo "In a cluster with Kyverno image verification:"
echo "  pod-bad.yaml  --> REJECTED (no signature possible on mutable tag)"
echo "  pod-good.yaml --> ADMITTED (if cosign verify passes for this digest)"
```

**EXPECTED_OUTPUT:**
```
Pinned reference: ttl.sh/vchippa-cosign-lab-<timestamp>@sha256:<digest>

=== BAD manifest (tag-based) ===
... (pod-bad.yaml content) ...

=== GOOD manifest (digest-pinned) ===
... (pod-good.yaml content with sha256 pinned image) ...

In a cluster with Kyverno image verification:
  pod-bad.yaml  --> REJECTED (no signature possible on mutable tag)
  pod-good.yaml --> ADMITTED (if cosign verify passes for this digest)
```

**WHY:** This is what a PR review should catch — any Kubernetes manifest with a tag-based image reference instead of a digest-pinned reference. Add a CI gate (conftest + OPA) that fails the pipeline if `image:` field contains a tag without `@sha256:`. This is the lint rule that prevents the class of attacks that digest pinning solves.

## Lab ANSWER KEY — Diagnostics Per Step

**Step 1 fails (Cosign install):** Check if the GitHub releases API is accessible: `curl -s https://api.github.com/repos/sigstore/cosign/releases/latest | grep tag_name`. If rate-limited, go to https://github.com/sigstore/cosign/releases directly and hardcode the version: `COSIGN_VERSION=v2.4.0`.

**Step 2 fails (docker pull):** Ensure Docker daemon is running: `sudo service docker start` (in WSL2). Then retry.

**Step 3 — if nginx shows a signature:** Some versions of nginx on Docker Hub may have been signed by Docker's own tooling. The error text may differ. The principle holds: if the identity doesn't match your policy, the verification fails.

**Step 5 fails (push to ttl.sh):** ttl.sh is an ephemeral public service — occasionally unavailable. Alternative: use Docker Hub (create free account: `docker login`, push to `docker.io/yourusername/cosign-demo:v1.0.0`). Update `$DEMO_IMAGE` accordingly.

**Step 6 — browser doesn't open in WSL2:** WSL2 may not auto-open browsers. Cosign prints the full URL — copy it and paste into your Windows browser manually. The flow works across OS boundaries.

**Step 6 — "Error: failed to get token":** Your OIDC provider session may have expired. Re-run `cosign sign` — it will open the browser auth flow again.

**Step 7 — "no matching signatures" error:** Check that the `--certificate-identity` flag exactly matches the email shown in the Rekor entry from Step 8. Email must be exact, not regex, unless you use `--certificate-identity-regexp ".*@gmail.com"`.

**Step 8 — rekor-cli not found or download fails:** Use the Rekor Web UI instead: https://search.sigstore.dev — enter your image digest in the search box. Same data, browser interface.

**Step 10 — attest command asks for browser auth again:** This is normal — each signing/attesting operation requires a fresh Fulcio cert. Authenticate again in the browser.

**Step 11 — Kubernetes manifests:** If you have a running cluster, apply `pod-good.yaml` with `kubectl apply -f ~/cosign-lab/pod-good.yaml`. Without Kyverno image verification policy installed, it will be admitted regardless — the admission controller is the enforcement layer.

## STAR Anchor

**T-Mobile framing for each question:**

**Q1 — Enforcement mechanism:**
"S: At T-Mobile, our 25M/day notification platform runs on EKS with images deployed from our internal ECR registry. We had no enforcement — it relied on engineers following policy. T: I identified that a compromised build could reach production without detection. A: I proposed adopting Kyverno with a ClusterPolicy requiring `cosign verify` to pass for all images in production namespaces. For key compromise: keyless signing means there's no long-lived key to compromise — we use GitHub Actions OIDC. If our Fulcio trust is compromised, we update the Fulcio root in our Kyverno policy and re-sign all images. R: We moved from a honor-system to an enforced gate — unsigned images are automatically rejected with a clear error."

**Q2 — Tag migration plan:**
"S: Same context — 200 microservices with `:latest` tags. T: We needed to migrate without breaking services. A: Three-phase approach: (1) Audit phase — CI gate that flags (but doesn't fail) tag-based references. (2) Freeze phase — new PRs must use digests; old services have 30-day sunset. (3) Enforce phase — CI fails on any tag-based image ref. Used a conftest OPA policy as the gate. R: Over 6 weeks, migrated all 200 services. Zero production incidents because the old pods kept running; only new deployments were blocked."

**Q3 — Keyless vs key-based:**
"For most enterprise use cases I recommend keyless — identity comes from the CI/CD OIDC token, which you're already managing. No private key to rotate, lose, or compromise. The tradeoff: your signing events appear in a public Rekor log. For regulated environments (we have some CPNI-sensitive workloads at T-Mobile), I would run self-hosted Sigstore to keep the log private. Key-based makes sense if: you're signing artifacts that must be verified in air-gapped environments with no Rekor access, or if your signing authority is an HSM (hardware security module) managed by a security team."

**Q4 — SLSA Level 2 gap:**
"SLSA Level 2 requires: (1) version-controlled build scripts, (2) signed provenance generated by a hosted build service (GitHub Actions qualifies out of the box with slsa-github-generator), (3) the provenance is authenticated. From Level 1: the gap is usually just wiring slsa-github-generator into your pipeline and attaching the provenance attestation to the OCI image. That's a two-sprint effort for an experienced team — one sprint for the pipeline changes, one sprint for verification tooling."

**Q5 — Signing without enforcement:**
"This is a policy-versus-enforcement gap — a very common one. You have the artifact (signed images) but not the enforcement (admission control). Root cause: signing was added as a CI pipeline step, but nobody closed the loop by adding the Kyverno/Gatekeeper policy. Fix without production incident: (1) deploy Kyverno in audit mode first (logs violations, does not block), (2) run for two weeks to identify which workloads would fail, (3) fix the gaps (unsigned images, missing digest pins), (4) switch to enforce mode in non-production first, then production with a maintenance window. Never flip to enforce mode cold — audit mode is your dry run."

## Tradeoffs and Failure Modes

**Top 3 — these are interview gold:**

**1. Keyless signing: public Rekor log vs. operational simplicity**
- Pro: No private key management, no key rotation, no single point of failure. Identity is ephemeral — tied to your CI/CD job, not a person.
- Con: Signing events (your image digests, your organization's signing timestamps) are publicly visible in Rekor. For telecom/fintech, this may expose deployment cadences and product identifiers to competitors.
- Mitigation: Self-hosted Sigstore (private Rekor + private Fulcio). Adds ~2 weeks of platform engineering effort to stand up and maintain.
- Interview signal: mentioning self-hosted Sigstore shows you understand the architecture, not just the happy path.

**2. Admission controller enforcement: gate vs. audit mode**
- Pro: Admission control (Kyverno/OPA Gatekeeper) makes signing enforcement automatic — no human in the loop, no exception creep.
- Con: Flipping to enforce mode cold breaks workloads with unsigned images or missing digest pins. Production incident guaranteed if not staged.
- The failure mode: "We added Kyverno but kept it in audit mode because we had exceptions to clean up. Three months later, we're still in audit mode." This is the security theater anti-pattern.
- Interview signal: describing a staged rollout (audit → warn → enforce per namespace) shows operational maturity.

**3. Digest pinning: operational overhead vs. image freshness**
- Pro: Pinning to `sha256:abc123` guarantees the image never changes unexpectedly.
- Con: When the base image has a CVE patch, the pin is now pointing at a vulnerable image. You must update every manifest that references the old digest. At 200 services, this is a significant toil burden without automation.
- Mitigation: Use Renovate Bot or Dependabot for automated digest bump PRs. The bot detects upstream digest changes, opens a PR with the new digest, your CI scans and signs the new image, the PR is auto-merged if clean. Full automation closes this toil loop.
- Interview signal: knowing that digest pinning without automated refresh creates a security debt (stale, vulnerable pinned images) shows you've operated this at scale.
