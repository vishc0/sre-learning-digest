# Week 5, Day 2: SLOs, Error Budgets, and Burn Rate Math

## Why This Matters

At T-Mobile, you're responsible for a 25M msg/day notification platform. Your SRE team gets paged at 2 AM. The question isn't "are we down?" — it's "**should we be paged right now, or can this wait for the morning standup?**"

Error budgets answer that. An SLO (Service Level Objective) is a promise you make to your product team: "99.9% of requests will succeed." The error budget is **how much failure you can afford** before you breach that promise. When you burn through your budget too fast (high burn rate), you need immediate action. When you're burning slowly, you have time to fix it properly without heroics.

Senior SREs and DevSecOps leads govern this rigorously — they write policies that prevent teams from gaming the budget. Interviewers probe here because it separates **chaos reactivity** from **disciplined reliability engineering**.

---

## Real-World Analogy: The On-Call Shift Budget

Imagine you're a Fire Chief with 1,000 on-duty firefighters across a city of 10 million people. Your SLO is:
- **99.9% of emergencies** get a fire truck within 5 minutes.
- Over a 28-day month, **0.1% failure is acceptable** = max 0.1 failures per 100 calls = 100,000 emergencies × 0.001 = **~100 failures allowed**.
- Each failure costs you ~$50,000 (lawsuit, injuries). Your error budget = $5 million in expected losses per month.

Now, a massive wildfire depletes 300 trucks over 2 days. You've burned **30% of your monthly budget in 2 days** → **14x normal burn rate**. Your director makes hard calls:
- **Slow down non-emergency dispatch** → defer vehicle maintenance calls, inspections.
- **Page senior captains** to orchestrate emergency protocols.
- **Hire temp contractors** (expensive) to stabilize.
- **Freeze feature work** — only bug fixes for 2 weeks.

That's burn rate governance. By Day 2 (out of 28), you know you're in trouble. You don't wait until Day 27.

## Analogy: The On-Call Shift Budget

Think about how you manage your on-call shift budget at T-Mobile. Your team has roughly 8,760 hours in a year. Leadership has agreed that no more than 43.8 of those hours (0.5% of the year) should be spent in P1 incident command — if you burn more than that, you're failing your SLA with the business. Now imagine you could see in real time how fast you're burning through those 43.8 hours: if you're burning at 14x the normal rate, you'll exhaust your annual incident budget in 26 days instead of a year. That's a page-right-now emergency. If you're burning at 2x the normal rate, you'll exhaust it in 6 months — still bad, but you have time to triage and fix. **Error budget is that on-call hour budget, measured in service reliability instead of human time.** Burn rate is the speedometer showing how fast you're consuming it.

The multi-window/multi-burn-rate alerting pattern is like having two different alarm systems in an incident bridge: one alarm fires if your P1 commander has been on a single call for more than 2 hours (fast burn, urgent), and a second alarm fires if your team has had more than 5 P1s this month (slow burn, trend). You need both because a single massive outage looks different from a death-by-a-thousand-cuts pattern.

## Vocabulary Translation Table

| SRE/DevSecOps Term | What It Means (Your World) |
|---|---|
| **SLO** (Service Level Objective) | A promise: "99.9% uptime" → measured in monthly error budget |
| **Error Budget** | Total **time you can afford to be broken** without breaking your SLO promise |
| **Burn Rate** | **How fast** you're consuming your error budget; ratio to normal rate |
| **Fast-Burn Alert** | Page the on-call (severity: we're losing budget too fast; probably customer-visible) |
| **Slow-Burn Alert** | Ticket the incident (severity: we're hemorrhaging budget slowly; fix in next sprint) |
| **SLI** (Service Level Indicator) | The **metric you measure** to prove you hit the SLO (e.g., "% of reqs with latency <100ms") |
| **Alert Windows** | Time periods used to detect abnormal burn rate (e.g., 1-hour rolling window) |
| **Exhaustion Time** | Days until error budget = $0 at current burn rate |

## 2-Minute Version

An SLO is a product promise with a dollar sign attached. 99.9% uptime over 28 days:

**Math:**
```
Monthly error budget (minutes) = (1 - SLO%) × days × 24 × 60
99.9% SLO → (1 - 0.999) × 28 × 24 × 60 = 0.001 × 40,320 = 40.32 minutes/month
```

**Example**: You use up 10 minutes in the first 7 days.
- Normal burn = 40.32 ÷ 28 = 1.44 min/day.
- You burned 10 min in 7 days = 1.43 min/day → still on track.
- Burn rate = 1.43 / 1.44 = **0.99x** (slightly under budget).

**Now a bad deployment breaks your service for 1 hour** (60 minutes downtime).
- New budget spent: 40.32 − 10 − 60 = **NEGATIVE 29.68** minutes.
- You've **exceeded your monthly SLO 4 weeks early** → breach.
- Burn rate during that outage = 60 / 1.44 ≈ **41.6x**.

**Decision:**
- At **14x+ burn rate** (multiple 1-hour windows), you page the on-call → fast-burn alert.
- At **6x burn rate** (slow degradation over hours), you file a ticket → slow-burn alert.
- At **2x burn rate**, you just monitor.

## 5-Minute Version (The Deep Dive)

### Core Formula and Calendar Effect

Error budget depends on your calendar window. SREs use **rolling 28 days** (not calendar months) for consistency:

```
Error Budget (minutes) = (1 - SLO) × 28 days × 1440 min/day

99.0% SLO:   (1 - 0.990) × 28 × 1440 = 0.01 × 40,320   = 403.2 min   (~6.7 hrs/month)
99.5% SLO:   (1 - 0.995) × 28 × 1440 = 0.005 × 40,320  = 201.6 min   (~3.4 hrs/month)
99.9% SLO:   (1 - 0.999) × 28 × 1440 = 0.001 × 40,320  = 40.32 min   (~40 min/month)
99.95% SLO:  (1 - 0.99950) × 28 × 1440 = 0.0005 × 40,320 = 20.16 min (~20 min/month)
99.99% SLO:  (1 - 0.9999) × 28 × 1440 = 0.0001 × 40,320 = 4.03 min   (~4 min/month)
```

At T-Mobile's scale (25M msg/day), a 99.9% SLO means **you can lose ~1,200 messages across the month**.

### Burn Rate and Time-to-Exhaustion

**Burn Rate** = (error budget consumed during window) / (error budget that would be normal for that window length).

**Example**: Your normal rate over 1 hour is `40.32 min / (28 × 24) hours = 0.0597 min/hr`.  
If you're losing **1 minute per hour**, your burn rate = `1.0 / 0.0597 = 16.75x`.

**Exhaustion time** (days until budget = 0 at current burn rate):
```
Days to Exhaustion = 28 days / Burn Rate

Burn Rate 14x   → 28 / 14 = 2 days      → FAST-BURN: page now
Burn Rate 6x    → 28 / 6  = 4.67 days  → SLOW-BURN: escalate to ticket
Burn Rate 3x    → 28 / 3  = 9.33 days  → Yellow alert; monitor
Burn Rate 1x    → 28 / 1  = 28 days    → Normal; no alert
```

### Google's Multi-Window Alert Logic (Best Practice)

Google SRE Workbook defines burn rate alerts with **two windows to reduce false positives**:

| Alert | Condition | Window Pair | Exhaustion Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fast-Burn (Page)** | Burn rate > 14x for 1 hour AND burn rate > 5x for 5 min | Both must fire | < 2 days |
| **Slow-Burn (Ticket)** | Burn rate > 6x for 6 hours AND burn rate > 3x for 30 min | Both must fire | 4.67 days |

**Why two windows?** A single spike (e.g., 1 sec of outage) shouldn't page you. But a 5-minute window + 1-hour window both firing = systematic problem.

### The Edge Case: What About Burn Rate = 10x?

Scenario: You have a **2-hour outage** (120 minutes).
- Burn rate = `120 / 1.44 = 83x` (over 1 minute window) → **fast-burn fires immediately**.
- Over 5 minutes, still 83x → **fast-burn confirmed, page now**.

Now a different scenario: **0.6 min/hr degradation for 8 hours** (no complete outage, just slow errors).
- 8 hours × 0.6 min/hr = **4.8 minutes lost**.
- Burn rate = `4.8 / 1.44 = 3.33x` (over 1 hour, averaged).
- Over 6 hours, burn rate = `(6 × 0.6) / (6 × 1.44 / 28) = 3.6 / 0.309 = 11.6x`.
- **Slow-burn fires** (6x threshold met for 6h), but **fast-burn does not** (1h window < 14x).
- Exhaustion time = 28 / 11.6 ≈ **2.4 days** → urgent, page on next shift start (ticket for night).

## Director / VP Lens: Governance and Policy

As an SRE Manager or Principal, you write the **error budget policy** — how teams use theirs.

**Three Governance Decisions:**

1. **SLO Target Trade-Off**: Tighter SLO = more ambitious, harder to hit, forces faster MTTR.
   - 99.9% (43 min/month) → sustainable, realistic for most services.
   - 99.99% (4 min/month) → only for critical systems; kills velocity (every bug costs big).
   - Product VP + Tech Lead must co-sign; this is a **business contract**.

2. **Burn Rate Gates**: Hard stops prevent teams from gaming the budget.
   - If burn rate > 6x for 6h, **freeze deploys** (except critical fixes).
   - If burn rate > 2x for 24h, **mandatory postmortem within 48h**.
   - If burn rate exhausts budget before next sprint, **no new feature work** for 2 weeks.

3. **Alert Escalation**: Define who gets paged and when.
   - Fast-burn (14x): wake up on-call immediately.
   - Slow-burn (6x): async ticket; discuss in standup.
   - Forecast alert: if trend predicts exhaustion by end of week, let team know Monday.

**Example policy excerpt** (T-Mobile):
```
SLO: 99.9% (notification delivery within 2s)
Error Budget: 40.32 min/month (rolling 28 days)

Alert Thresholds:
- Fast-Burn: >14x for 1h AND >5x for 5m → page on-call
- Slow-Burn: >6x for 6h AND >3x for 30m → escalate ticket
- Freeze Deploy: when remaining budget < 2× monthly burn rate

Co-Sign Requirement: Product Manager + Tech Lead approve SLO change
```

### Step 1: Define the SLI (Service Level Indicator)

An SLI is the metric you measure. For a notification platform:

```yaml
# File: notification-slo.yaml
# sloth format (https://github.com/slok/sloth)

serviceLevel:
  name: notification-platform-slo
  description: "T-Mobile notification delivery SLO"
  
  objectives:
    - name: delivery-latency
      description: "95% of notification deliveries complete within 2 seconds"
      sli:
        type: "ratio"
        ratio:
          good: 'sum(rate(notifications_delivered_seconds_bucket{le="2"}[5m]))'
          total: 'sum(rate(notifications_total[5m]))'
      objective: 99.9
      window: 28d
      
    - name: error-rate
      description: "Error rate < 0.1% across all delivery attempts"
      sli:
        type: "ratio"
        ratio:
          good: 'sum(rate(notifications_success_total[5m]))'
          total: 'sum(rate(notifications_total[5m]))'
      objective: 99.9
      window: 28d
```

**Why 99.9%?** At 25M msg/day:
- 0.1% error = 25,000 messages fail per day.
- Over 28 days = 700,000 failing messages allowed (acceptable loss).
- Tighter (99.95%) = only 175k failures allowed; forces constant on-call heroics.
- Looser (99%) = 250k failures per day; users riot.

### Step 2: Calculate Your Error Budget

Create a Python script to make this a team artifact (reference for meetings).

```python
# File: error-budget-calculator.py

def calculate_error_budget(slo_percent, days=28):
    """
    Calculate error budget in minutes and percentage.
    
    Args:
        slo_percent: SLO target (e.g., 99.9)
        days: rolling window (default 28)
    
    Returns:
        dict with minutes, percentage, hours, days allowed
    """
    total_minutes = days * 24 * 60
    error_rate = (100 - slo_percent) / 100
    error_budget_minutes = error_rate * total_minutes
    
    return {
        "slo": slo_percent,
        "error_budget_minutes": round(error_budget_minutes, 2),
        "error_budget_hours": round(error_budget_minutes / 60, 2),
        "error_budget_percentage": error_rate * 100,
        "normal_burn_rate_per_hour": round(error_budget_minutes / (days * 24), 4),
        "days_window": days,
    }
```

### Step 3: Write PromQL for Burn Rate Alerts

```yaml
# File: burn-rate-alerts.yaml
# Prometheus AlertManager rules

groups:
  - name: slo-burn-rate
    interval: 30s
    rules:
      # Fast-Burn Alert: page the on-call
      # Condition: burn rate > 14x for 1 hour AND > 5x for 5 min
      # Exhaustion: ~2 days
      - alert: FastBurnRateNotificationPlatform
        expr: |
          # 1-hour window: burn rate > 14x
          (
            sum(rate(notifications_total[1h])) - sum(rate(notifications_success_total[1h]))
          ) / sum(rate(notifications_total[1h]))
          >
          (0.001 * 14)  # 0.001 = 1 - 99.9%; multiply by 14x threshold
          and
          # 5-minute window: burn rate > 5x (short-term confirmation)
          (
            sum(rate(notifications_total[5m])) - sum(rate(notifications_success_total[5m]))
          ) / sum(rate(notifications_total[5m]))
          >
          (0.001 * 5)
        for: 1m
        labels:
          severity: critical
          slo: "99.9"
          platform: notification
        annotations:
          summary: "FAST BURN: Notification platform error rate critical"
          description: |
            Error rate {{ $value | humanizePercentage }} (SLO target: 0.1%)
            Estimated budget exhaustion: ~2 days
            Action: Page on-call, declare SEV-1 incident, roll back recent deploys
      
      # Slow-Burn Alert: escalate to ticket
      # Condition: burn rate > 6x for 6 hours AND > 3x for 30 min
      # Exhaustion: ~4.7 days
      - alert: SlowBurnRateNotificationPlatform
        expr: |
          # 6-hour window: burn rate > 6x
          (
            sum(rate(notifications_total[6h])) - sum(rate(notifications_success_total[6h]))
          ) / sum(rate(notifications_total[6h]))
          >
          (0.001 * 6)
          and
          # 30-minute window: burn rate > 3x (confirmation)
          (
            sum(rate(notifications_total[30m])) - sum(rate(notifications_success_total[30m]))
          ) / sum(rate(notifications_total[30m]))
          >
          (0.001 * 3)
        for: 15m
        labels:
          severity: warning
          slo: "99.9"
          platform: notification
        annotations:
          summary: "SLOW BURN: Notification platform error rate elevated"
          description: |
            Error rate {{ $value | humanizePercentage }} over 6h
            Estimated budget exhaustion: ~4.7 days
            Action: Escalate to ticket, prioritize in next sprint, NO new features
      
      # Budget Forecast Alert (optional, for planning)
      - alert: BudgetExhaustionForecast
        expr: |
          # If current burn rate continues, will budget be exhausted by end of sprint?
          # Rolling 7-day burn rate * (28 - days_elapsed) > remaining_budget
          # Simplified: alert if weekly burn > 1/4 of total monthly budget
          (
            sum(rate(notifications_total[7d])) - sum(rate(notifications_success_total[7d]))
          ) / sum(rate(notifications_total[7d]))
          >
          (0.001 * 7)  # 7x normal for 7 days = on track to exhaust in 4 weeks
        for: 10m
        labels:
          severity: info
          slo: "99.9"
          platform: notification
        annotations:
          summary: "SLO Budget Forecast: exhaustion likely within 4 weeks"
          description: |
            At current burn rate, error budget will be exhausted by {{ $externalLabels.alerting_date | addDate "4 weeks" }}
            Action: Discuss in weekly planning, consider prioritizing reliability work
```

**Key insight for PromQL**:
- `notifications_success_total` = successful deliveries (counter).
- `notifications_total` = all delivery attempts (counter).
- Error rate = `(total - success) / total = 1 - (success / total)`.
- Burn rate threshold for 99.9% SLO: `0.001 × multiplier` (e.g., 14x = 0.014).

```python
# File: burn-simulation.py
# Simulate 5 real-world scenarios and show which alerts fire

import math

class SLOMonitor:
    def __init__(self, slo_percent=99.9, days=28):
        self.slo = slo_percent
        self.days = days
        self.error_rate = (100 - slo_percent) / 100
        self.total_budget_min = self.error_rate * days * 24 * 60
        self.normal_rate_per_hour = self.total_budget_min / (days * 24)
    
    def burn_rate(self, actual_error_rate):
        """Calculate burn rate multiplier."""
        return actual_error_rate / self.error_rate
    
    def exhaustion_days(self, burn_rate_mult):
        """Days until budget exhaustion at this burn rate."""
        return self.days / burn_rate_mult
    
    def alert_decision(self, hour_window_rate, short_window_rate):
        """
        Determine alert type based on multi-window logic.
        
        Args:
            hour_window_rate: error rate over 1h
            short_window_rate: error rate over 5m
        
        Returns:
            ("alert_type", burn_rate_mult, exhaustion_days)
        """
        br_hour = self.burn_rate(hour_window_rate)
        br_short = self.burn_rate(short_window_rate)
        
        # Fast-Burn: both > 14x and > 5x
        if br_hour > 14 and br_short > 5:
            return ("FAST_BURN_PAGE", br_hour, self.exhaustion_days(br_hour))
        
        # Slow-Burn: 6h window > 6x and 30m window > 3x

        if br_hour > 6 and br_short > 3:
            return ("SLOW_BURN_TICKET", br_hour, self.exhaustion_days(br_hour))
        
        # Monitor (2x < rate < 6x)
        if br_hour > 2:
            return ("MONITOR_WATCH", br_hour, self.exhaustion_days(br_hour))
        
        return ("NORMAL", br_hour, self.exhaustion_days(br_hour))
```

```markdown
**Effective Date**: 2026-06-11  
**Owner**: SRE Manager (Vishweshwar Chippa)  
**Co-Sign**: Product VP, Tech Lead  

### 1. SLO Definition and Budget

| Metric | Value | Notes |

| Error Budget | 40.32 minutes | Per rolling 28-day window |
| Monthly Burn Rate (Normal) | 1.44 min/day | Budget / 28 days |

**Rationale**: 99.9% = 25M msg/day × 0.001 = 25k acceptable failures/day. Tighter SLO (99.95%) would block all feature work; looser (99%) causes customer churn. **VP and Tech Lead co-signed** on 2026-06-11.

### 2. Alert and Escalation Rules

#### Fast-Burn Alert (Critical — Page On-Call)
- **Condition**: Burn rate > 14x for 1 hour AND > 5x for 5 minutes
- **Exhaustion Time**: ~2 days
- **Action**:
  - Wake on-call immediately (page through PagerDuty)
  - Declare SEV-1 incident in Slack #incidents
  - Form war room (SRE, backend, platform leads)
  - Investigate and roll back recent deploys or config changes
  - Post incident summary within 4 hours (even if not fully resolved)
  - **Mandatory post-mortem within 48 hours**

#### Slow-Burn Alert (Warning — Escalate to Ticket)
- **Condition**: Burn rate > 6x for 6 hours AND > 3x for 30 minutes
- **Exhaustion Time**: ~4.7 days
- **Action**:
  - Create HIGH priority ticket (not P0; P1)
  - Assign to SRE rotation lead for next business day review
  - **Freeze new feature deploys** until root cause identified
  - Add to sprint planning as top priority bug fix
  - Notify product PM of budget impact (e.g., "We have 4.7 days of budget remaining")

#### Monitor Alert (Information — Watch, No Page)
- **Condition**: Burn rate > 2x for 1 hour (not > 6x)
- **Exhaustion Time**: ~14 days
- **Action**:
  - Log metric in team dashboard
  - Discuss in daily standup ("health check")
  - Plan mitigation (no emergency, can defer to next sprint if low risk)

### 3. Deploy Freeze Rules (Hard Gates)

**Automatic freeze triggers** — no new feature deploys allowed until condition clears:

1. **Active Fast-Burn**: Any fast-burn alert in last 1 hour
   - Exception: Critical security patch (requires SRE Manager + VP approval)
   - Resume: Only after root cause fix deployed + no fast-burn for 2 consecutive hours

2. **Slow-Burn + Low Budget**: Slow-burn alert AND remaining budget < 5 minutes
   - Block all feature commits; bug fixes + rollbacks only
   - Resume: When budget > 10 minutes and burn rate drops to < 3x

3. **Monthly Budget Exhaustion Forecast**: If weekly burn rate suggests exhaustion by end of month
   - Require product VP + tech lead approval for any new feature
   - Pivot team to reliability debt (test coverage, monitoring, runbooks)

### 4. Error Budget Replenishment (New Sprint Cycle)

- Error budget resets at **Monday 00:00 UTC** (start of new week).
- Rolling 28-day window means budget "renews" 1/4 each week if burn rate stays at 1x normal.
- **If you overspend in week 1**, week 2 budget is reduced proportionally (no "bailout").

**Example**:
- Week 1: Spend 15 min of 40.32 min budget → 25.32 remaining
- Week 2: Earn ~10 min (1 week's worth) → 35.32 remaining
- Week 3: Earn ~10 min → 45.32 (capped at 40.32; can't "save" extra)
```

```
- Learning: "Add canary testing guide to runbooks; teach team in next Friday session"
```

### Step B: Create the sloth SLO definition file

```bash
mkdir -p ~/slo-lab && cd ~/slo-lab

cat > notification-slo.yaml << 'EOF'
version: "prometheus/v1"
service: "notification-platform"
labels:
  team: sre
  env: production
slos:
  # SLO 1: Message delivery success rate — 99.9% over 28 days
  - name: "message-delivery-availability"
    objective: 99.9
    description: >
      99.9% of notification delivery attempts must succeed (non-5xx response
      from the delivery service) over a 28-day rolling window.
    sli:
      events:
        error_query: sum(rate(notification_delivery_requests_total{code=~"5.."}[{{.window}}]))
        total_query: sum(rate(notification_delivery_requests_total[{{.window}}]))
    alerting:
      name: NotificationDeliveryBurnRate
      labels:
        severity: critical
      annotations:
        summary: "Notification delivery error budget burning too fast"
        description: >
          The notification platform is consuming its error budget at {{ $value }}x
          the normal rate. At this pace, the 28-day budget will be exhausted in
          {{ humanizeDuration (mul (div 1 $value) (mul 28 86400)) }}.
      page_alert:
        labels:
          severity: critical
          pagerduty_policy: sre-oncall-p1
      ticket_alert:
        labels:
          severity: warning
          pagerduty_policy: sre-oncall-p2
EOF

echo "SLO definition written."
```

**WHY:** This YAML is the "SLO contract in code." The `{{.window}}` template variables are how sloth generates rules for multiple time windows simultaneously — sloth substitutes these with 1h, 6h, 24h, 72h windows when it generates Prometheus rules.

## STAR Stories (T-Mobile Notification Platform)

### STAR 1: "Tell me about a time you had to make a tough call on SLO vs. velocity."

**Task**: Decide: do we slip the release to fix reliability gaps first, or ship features and accept the risk?

**Action**:
1. **Quantified the gap**: Ran our error-budget-calculator — at 99.85%, we had 60.48 min/month budget. At 99.9%, we'd have 40.32 min. We were hemorrhaging ~20 extra minutes per month due to infrastructure issues (uncached database queries, no circuit breakers on Redis timeouts).
2. **Socialized the tradeoff with VP**: "If we ship 3 features now, we add complexity, which increases failure modes. At our current reliability level, we'll breach SLO by week 3 of the sprint. Then we'll be in reactive mode: hot-fixes, on-call heroics, no time for new features."
3. **Proposed the compromise**: "Ship 2 features (recommend + retry). Defer delivery tracking. Allocate 1 sprint (2 weeks) to reliability: circuit breaker implementation, caching strategy, and observability improvements. This gets us to 99.9% by end of Q3, and we ship delivery tracking in Q4 with better velocity."

### STAR 2: "Tell me about a time you had to respond to a high burn rate incident."

**Situation**: 3 AM on a Wednesday. Fast-burn alert fires: 50% error rate on notification delivery for 45 minutes (burn rate ~500x). Error budget bleeding out fast.

### STAR 3: "Tell me about a time you designed SLO governance for your team."

**Situation**: I was promoted to SRE Manager. The team had monitors and on-call, but no formal SLO governance. We had no consistent policy for when to page vs. ticket vs. ignore. Different services had different reliability targets, leading to confusion.

**Task**: Design and implement a unified SLO and burn rate governance model.

**Action**:
1. **Research phase**: Studied Google SRE Workbook and Betsy Beyer's multi-window alert model. Looked at Uber and Meta's public postmortems to see how they handle SLO breaches.

2. **Defined SLOs**:
   - Notification platform: 99.9% (40 min/month error budget).
   - Orchestration (internal): 99.5% (200 min/month).
   - Metrics/monitoring (internal): 99% (403 min/month).
   - Rationale: based on customer impact, not just technical feasibility.

3. **Built alert thresholds**:
   - Fast-burn (14x, 2-day exhaustion) → page.
   - Slow-burn (6x, 4.7-day exhaustion) → ticket + feature freeze.
   - Monitor (2x, 14-day exhaustion) → standup discussion.
   - Created PromQL rules with multi-window logic.

4. **Wrote the governance doc**:
   - Explicit escalation rules (decision tree).
   - Deploy freeze criteria.
   - Postmortem SLA (48 hours for root cause).
   - VP + Tech Lead co-sign.

5. **Socialized and trained**:
   - Presented to engineering leadership; got buy-in.
   - Trained the team: "Error budgets are how we decide to page vs. sleep. Here's the math."
   - Created weekly ritual: 30 min on Monday, review error budget and burn rate; discuss anything above 2x.

6. **Iterated**: After 3 months, we found the 14x threshold was too aggressive (false positives during traffic spikes). Adjusted to require 1-hour + 5-minute multi-window (not just 1-hour). Reduced false pages by 60%.

**Result**: By month 6, the team had high confidence in SLO governance. Incident response became data-driven, not gut-driven. We breached SLO zero times in Q3 (vs. 3 times in Q2 before governance). On-call team morale improved (fewer bogus pages).

**Why it matters**: Shows I can design systems, build buy-in across orgs (product, eng, leadership), and iterate on policy based on real-world feedback.

## Tradeoffs and Interview Follow-Ups

### Tradeoff 1: Tight SLO vs. Feature Velocity

**The tension**: Tighter SLO (99.95% vs. 99.9%) forces more testing, slower deploys, more on-call burden. Slower velocity = delayed features.

**Strong answer**:
"You can't have both. Pick one:
- **Tight SLO (99.95%)**: Expect 1/4 sprint capacity on reliability work (testing, runbooks, observability). New features take longer. Good for mission-critical systems.
- **Loose SLO (99.5%)**: Faster feature velocity, but more customer complaints and SRE pager storms. Acceptable for internal tools.
- **Hybrid**: Most services live at 99.9%. We allocate 10% of sprint time to reliability (small but consistent). For new high-risk features, we do 1-2 week hardening sprints before general availability.

At T-Mobile, we chose the hybrid: 99.9% for notification platform, 99.5% for internal tools. Lets us move fast where it matters, bulletproof where it doesn't."

---

### Tradeoff 2: Multi-Window Complexity vs. Alert Quality

**The tension**: Simple single-window alerts (just "1-hour burn rate") are easy to build but noisy (5-sec spike triggers alerts). Multi-window (require 1h + 5m to both fire) is more accurate but harder to reason about.

**Strong answer**:
"Single-window is tempting — simpler logic, faster to build. But it pages on blips: a 5-second outage during one 1-hour window causes a false fast-burn alert. The team gets paged at midnight for something that self-healed.

Multi-window is worth the complexity. The rule 'both 1h AND 5m must fire' filters noise without missing real problems. A real failure is sustained; it shows up in both windows. A blip shows up in 5m but not 1h.

Trade-off**: You need better observability (rolling windows in Prometheus, PromQL skill in team). But it's worth it. We reduced false fast-burn pages by 60% after implementing multi-window.

At scale (25M msg/day), even a 1% false alert rate = 250/day pages. Multi-window is a must-have."

---

### Tradeoff 3: Automatic Policy Gates vs. Team Autonomy

**The tension**: Hard rules ('if burn rate > 6x, freeze deploys immediately') reduce chaos but feel heavy-handed. Soft guidance ('please consider pausing features') respects team autonomy but lets teams ignore reliability signals.

**Strong answer**:
"Hard gates scale better. A 'soft guidance' culture relies on consistent good judgment from every developer. At a big team, someone will ignore the signal, push a risky feature, and cause an incident.

I prefer **explicit, written policy with exceptions**:
- Hard rule: 'Deploy freeze when burn rate > 6x for 6h.'
- Exception: 'VP + SRE Manager can approve feature deploys during freeze if it's a revenue-blocking bug.'
- This is enforceable (CI/CD gate checks the SLO, blocks merges) and not arbitrary.

The tradeoff: teams lose autonomy in a crisis, but they gain confidence in normal times. They know the rules upfront and can plan around them."
