# Teams, Roles, and Responsibilities in a Typical Technology Organization

## Why this matters for Principal SRE and Director-level roles

At Director level, you are expected to know not just your own team's scope but every team your platform depends on, competes with for resources, or hands work off to. Incidents, platform gaps, and delivery failures almost always happen at the seams between teams — not inside them. Understanding who owns what, and where the handoff lines are, is a leadership competency, not an org-chart curiosity.

---

## How to Read This Document

Teams are grouped into **organizational layers** — the concentric rings of how a technology organization is structured around the business.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    Business / Product Layer                     │
│         (teams that build what customers interact with)         │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                    Platform Engineering Layer                   │
│     (teams that build and run what product teams run on)        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                    Infrastructure Layer                         │
│         (teams that manage compute, network, storage)           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                    Security and Compliance Layer                │
│    (teams that protect, audit, and govern the above layers)     │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                    Data and Intelligence Layer                  │
│        (teams that move, store, and make sense of data)         │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                    Operations and Support Layer                 │
│    (teams that keep everything running and resolve issues)      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Layer 1 — Business and Product Teams

These teams build the features and flows that customers directly use. They are the **consumers** of everything the platform, infrastructure, and operations teams provide.

---

### Product Engineering (Feature Teams / Squad Teams)

**What they own**: Application code for customer-facing products — web apps, mobile apps, APIs, business logic.

**What they need from other teams**: Compute (Kubernetes namespaces, cloud accounts), CI/CD pipelines, secrets management, observability tooling, on-call support from SRE.

**What they are responsible for**: Feature delivery, service-level instrumentation of their own applications, owning their own SLOs (in mature orgs), and responding to application-layer incidents.

**Common sub-teams inside Product Engineering**:

| Sub-team | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| Frontend Engineering | Web UI, mobile app, design system |
| Backend Engineering | APIs, microservices, business logic |
| Full-stack / Feature Squad | End-to-end feature delivery per product area |
| Growth Engineering | A/B testing, experimentation platform, onboarding flows |
| Commerce / Payments Engineering | Checkout, billing, subscription flows |
| Notification Engineering | Email, SMS, push notification pipelines |

**Interaction with SRE**: SRE embeds with or supports product engineering during incident response, capacity planning, and reliability reviews. The handoff question is always: "Who pages when this breaks?"

---

### QA / Test Engineering

**What they own**: Test strategy, test infrastructure, automated test suites (unit, integration, end-to-end), performance testing, chaos injection testing.

**What they need**: Test environments, realistic data sets, CI integration, observability into test runs.

**Common models**:
- Embedded QA per squad (most common in modern orgs)
- Central QA platform team that builds shared test tooling
- Shift-left model — QA engineers pair with developers before code is written

**Key outputs**: Test coverage reports, release readiness gates, regression suites, performance benchmarks.

---

### Product Management

**What they own**: Roadmap, prioritization, feature requirements, user research, business case for technical investment.

**Why SREs need to understand this team**: Reliability investment (SLO targets, error budget policy, toil reduction) must be justified in business terms that Product Management speaks. Director-level SREs need to negotiate reliability work into the product roadmap — not just operate in parallel to it.

---

## Layer 2 — Platform Engineering Teams

These teams build and operate the **internal platforms** that product engineering runs on. Their customers are other engineers, not end users. Their product is developer experience, deployment velocity, and reliability primitives.

---

### Site Reliability Engineering (SRE)

**What they own**: Production reliability, SLO framework, incident management, on-call rotation design, toil reduction, chaos engineering, postmortem process, capacity planning.

**Operating model**: Embedded SREs (within product teams) or centralized SRE team that supports multiple products via an engagement model.

**What distinguishes SRE from DevOps**: SRE has an explicit error budget model — when error budget is consumed, SREs can stop new feature work and require reliability investment. This is a governance mechanism, not just a practice.

**Key outputs**: SLO definitions, runbooks, postmortems, reliability scorecards, incident response automation, chaos experiment results.

**Interaction surface**: Every other team in this document. SRE sits at the intersection of all of them.

---

### Platform Engineering / Internal Developer Platform (IDP)

**What they own**: The "golden path" — the opinionated, supported way for product engineers to build, deploy, and run services. This includes developer portals, self-service infrastructure provisioning, standard application templates, and developer tooling.

**Goal**: Reduce cognitive load on product engineers so they don't need to be infrastructure experts to ship software safely.

**Key tools**: Backstage (developer portal), Crossplane (self-service Kubernetes provisioning), ArgoCD (GitOps delivery), Port (developer portal alternative).

**Interaction with SRE**: Platform Engineering builds the deployment substrate; SRE defines the reliability contracts that run on top of it. Failure to coordinate produces platforms that are easy to deploy to but impossible to operate in production.

| What Platform Engineering builds | What SRE governs |
|----------------------------------|-----------------|
| Deployment pipelines | Deployment gates (test coverage, SLO compliance) |
| Service templates | Observability requirements baked into templates |
| Self-service environment provisioning | Capacity limits and cost guardrails |
| Developer portal (Backstage) | Runbook links, SLO dashboards per service |

---

### DevOps / DevSecOps Engineering

**What they own**: CI/CD pipeline infrastructure, build systems, artifact registries, release orchestration, pipeline security (SAST/DAST/SCA integration), supply chain security.

**What they build**: Jenkins/GitHub Actions/GitLab CI pipelines, ArgoCD/Flux GitOps workflows, Nexus/Artifactory artifact management, container image signing (Cosign/Sigstore), SBOM generation.

**Interaction with SRE**: DevOps owns the pipe; SRE owns production. The handoff is at the deployment gate — DevOps delivers a validated artifact, SRE accepts it into production via a canary or blue/green strategy.

**Common sub-teams**:

| Sub-team | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| Build Engineering | CI pipeline infrastructure, build tooling |
| Release Engineering | Release orchestration, version management, changelog automation |
| Artifact Management | Container registries, Helm chart repos, binary artifact stores |
| Supply Chain Security | SBOM, Sigstore, SLSA compliance, dependency scanning |

---

### Kubernetes / Container Platform Team

**What they own**: The Kubernetes fleet — cluster provisioning, upgrades, node management, namespace governance, RBAC policies, admission webhooks, cluster autoscaling, multi-tenancy model.

**What they do NOT own**: What runs inside the cluster (that's product engineering + SRE).

**Key responsibilities**:
- Cluster lifecycle (version upgrades, node pool management, Karpenter or Cluster Autoscaler)
- Multi-tenant isolation (namespace quotas, network policies, PSA/PSP)
- Admission control (OPA/Gatekeeper or Kyverno policies)
- Service mesh management (Istio, Linkerd — if centrally managed)
- Cluster observability (kube-state-metrics, node-exporter, control plane metrics)

**Interaction with SRE**: SRE depends on this team for stable cluster primitives. Kubernetes platform issues (etcd performance, API server degradation, node pressure) are escalated from SRE to the platform team.

---

### Observability / Monitoring Platform Team

**What they own**: The centralized telemetry infrastructure — the tools and pipelines that collect, store, query, and alert on metrics, logs, and traces from all services.

**What they build and manage**:

| Component | Common tools |
|-----------|-------------|
| Metrics pipeline | Prometheus, Thanos/Cortex (long-term), VictoriaMetrics |
| Log pipeline | Fluent Bit, Fluentd, Loki, Elasticsearch/OpenSearch, Splunk |
| Trace pipeline | Jaeger, Tempo, Zipkin, Honeycomb |
| Collector / agent | OTel Collector (DaemonSet), Datadog Agent, ADOT |
| Dashboards | Grafana, Kibana, Datadog, Splunk dashboards |
| Alerting | Alertmanager, PagerDuty, OpsGenie, VictoriaMetrics alerting |
| AIOps | Splunk MLTK, Dynatrace Davis, Datadog Watchdog |

**What they do NOT own**: Defining SLOs, writing runbooks, managing on-call — those belong to SRE. The observability platform team owns the pipes; SRE owns what flows through them.

**Interaction with SRE**: The observability team is SRE's most critical dependency. If the monitoring platform is down, the SRE team is flying blind.

---

### API Gateway / API Management Team

**What they own**: The edge layer — the gateway through which all external and internal API traffic flows. Rate limiting, authentication/authorization at the edge, traffic routing, API versioning, developer-facing API portal.

**Key tools**: Kong, AWS API Gateway, Apigee, Azure API Management, Traefik, Envoy (as gateway).

**Key responsibilities**:
- Rate limiting and throttling policies
- mTLS termination at the edge
- API key management and OAuth token validation
- Traffic routing (blue/green, canary via gateway weights)
- API catalog and documentation portal
- DDoS protection at the gateway layer

**Interaction with SRE**: API Gateway is a shared critical dependency — degradation here affects every service simultaneously. SRE needs gateway-level metrics (request rate, error rate by upstream, latency per route) as Layer 2 customer impact signals.

---

### Middleware / Integration Engineering

**What they own**: Message brokers, event buses, ESBs, and integration platforms that carry data between systems.

**Common systems owned**:

| System type | Examples |
|-------------|---------|
| Message queues | RabbitMQ, ActiveMQ, TIBCO EMS |
| Streaming platforms | Kafka, AWS Kinesis, Azure Event Hubs |
| Enterprise Service Bus | MuleSoft, TIBCO BusinessWorks, IBM MQ |
| iPaaS / Integration | Boomi, Informatica, Azure Logic Apps |
| CDC (Change Data Capture) | Debezium, AWS DMS |

**Interaction with SRE**: In organizations with high-volume messaging (like T-Mobile DND at 25M msg/day), the middleware team and SRE share the most operational surface. Queue depth, consumer lag, DLQ growth, and retry storms are joint concerns — middleware team owns the broker; SRE owns the consumer reliability and SLOs.

---

### Identity and Access Management (IAM) / Identity Engineering

**What they own**: Authentication and authorization infrastructure — who can log in, what they can do, and how identities flow across services.

**Key systems**:
- Identity providers (Okta, Azure AD / Entra ID, Ping Identity, Keycloak)
- Service-to-service auth (OAuth2, mTLS, SPIFFE/SPIRE, AWS IRSA)
- Secrets management (HashiCorp Vault, AWS Secrets Manager, CyberArk)
- Privileged access management (CyberArk PAM, BeyondTrust)
- Certificate lifecycle management

**Interaction with SRE**: Certificate expiry and key rotation are some of the most common causes of sudden, hard-to-diagnose production incidents. SRE needs IAM team coordination for any changes to service identities, token expiry windows, or certificate rotation schedules.

---

### Release Management / Change Management

**What they own**: The governance process for what changes go to production, when, and with what approval gates. In SAFe organizations, this overlaps with PI Planning and ART release trains.

**Key responsibilities**:
- Change Advisory Board (CAB) process
- Change freeze windows (holiday, launch, regulatory blackout)
- Release calendar and deployment windows
- Rollback coordination for failed changes
- Post-release validation gates

**Interaction with SRE**: Every deployment is a change event that the observability platform must correlate to anomalies (Layer 4 AI-era signal). SRE depends on Release Management to surface the change feed that AI/ML models use for correlation.

---

## Layer 3 — Infrastructure Teams

These teams manage the physical and virtual compute, network, and storage that everything runs on.

---

### Cloud Engineering / Cloud Platform Team

**What they own**: Cloud account structure, landing zones, VPC/network architecture, cloud service guardrails, cost governance, cloud provider relationships.

**Key responsibilities**:
- AWS Organizations / Azure Management Groups (account hierarchy)
- Service Control Policies (SCPs) — what services can be used in which accounts
- Landing zone automation (AWS Control Tower, Azure Landing Zones)
- FinOps — cost allocation, reserved instance strategy, savings plans
- Cloud security baseline (CloudTrail, Config Rules, SecurityHub)

**Interaction with SRE**: Cloud Engineering defines what the Kubernetes platform and product teams can provision. SRE depends on this team for account-level blast radius isolation — a misconfigured SCP can silently block a service from calling another AWS service, causing production failures with no obvious error.

---

### Network Engineering

**What they own**: Physical and logical network — data center switching and routing, WAN/MPLS connectivity, SD-WAN, DNS, CDN, load balancers (hardware and cloud), firewall rules, network segmentation.

**Key systems**: Cisco/Arista switching, F5 / AWS ALB / NLB, Cloudflare / Akamai CDN, Route53 / CoreDNS, Palo Alto / Fortinet firewalls.

**Interaction with SRE**: Network issues are among the hardest to diagnose because they appear as latency spikes or connection timeouts in application metrics, not network metrics. SRE needs relationships with Network Engineering for any incident involving connectivity symptoms — DNS misconfiguration, TLS termination issues, load balancer health check failures.

---

### Database Administration (DBA) / Data Infrastructure

**What they own**: Database server provisioning, performance tuning, backup/restore, replication configuration, schema migration governance, connection pool management.

**Common databases**: PostgreSQL, MySQL, Oracle, SQL Server, Cassandra, MongoDB, Redis, DynamoDB.

**Interaction with SRE**: Database degradation is the most common root cause in Layer 1 technical incidents. Lock waits, slow queries, connection pool exhaustion, and replication lag all surface as application errors first. SRE needs DBA team involvement in any P1 with database symptoms and in capacity planning for high-growth services.

---

### Storage Engineering

**What they own**: Object storage, block storage, file storage, backup infrastructure, data lifecycle policies.

**Common systems**: NetApp, Pure Storage, AWS S3, Azure Blob, NFS/SMB file shares, tape/archive.

**Interaction with SRE**: Storage is most visible when it fails — disk full events, IOPS saturation, and object store throttling all produce application failures. SRE monitors storage utilization as a USE-framework saturation metric.

---

### Datacenter Operations (DCOps)

**What they own**: Physical hardware lifecycle, colocation facilities, power and cooling, physical security, hardware provisioning and decommission.

**Interaction with SRE**: Less direct in cloud-native organizations. In hybrid or on-prem environments, DCOps handles the physical infrastructure that cloud engineering manages virtually. SRE escalates to DCOps for hardware failures, power events, and physical rack-level incidents.

---

## Layer 4 — Security and Compliance Teams

These teams protect the organization and ensure it meets regulatory obligations. They are a cross-cutting layer — their policies apply to every team above.

---

### Security Operations Center (SOC)

**What they own**: 24/7 threat monitoring, detection, and incident response for security events. SIEM platform (Splunk, Sentinel, QRadar), EDR tools, threat intelligence feeds.

**Key responsibilities**:
- Alert triage from SIEM rules
- Threat hunting
- Security incident response (separate process from operational incidents)
- Forensics and evidence collection
- Vulnerability management coordination

**Interaction with SRE**: Security incidents can masquerade as reliability incidents (DDoS looks like traffic surge; credential stuffing looks like error spike). SRE and SOC need a clear escalation protocol: if an operational anomaly has security indicators, SOC takes the lead; SRE supports with telemetry.

---

### Application Security (AppSec)

**What they own**: Security testing integration into CI/CD (SAST, DAST, SCA), threat modeling, security code review, penetration testing coordination, vulnerability disclosure program.

**Key tools**: Snyk, Checkmarx, Veracode (SAST); OWASP ZAP, Burp Suite (DAST); Dependabot, Black Duck (SCA).

**Interaction with SRE**: AppSec provides the security gates in the CI/CD pipeline that DevSecOps enforces. SRE cares about AppSec findings that affect production reliability — for example, a Snyk critical CVE in a library used by a core service is both a security and a reliability risk.

---

### Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)

**What they own**: Regulatory compliance (SOC2, PCI-DSS, HIPAA, GDPR, CCPA, FCC/TCPA for telecom), audit management, risk register, policy framework, vendor risk management.

**Interaction with SRE**: GRC translates regulatory requirements into technical controls. TCPA (for notification delivery timing), PCI-DSS (for payment service availability), and SOC2 (for evidence of controls) all create hard requirements on SLOs, log retention, and access governance. SRE must understand which SLOs have regulatory backing — those are non-negotiable error budgets.

---

### Vulnerability Management

**What they own**: Continuous scanning of infrastructure and application components for known CVEs, patch coordination, SLA enforcement for patching timelines (Critical: 24hr, High: 7 days, etc.).

**Key tools**: Qualys, Tenable, AWS Inspector, Prisma Cloud.

**Interaction with SRE**: Emergency patching of a critical CVE (log4shell-scale events) is a forced change event. SRE must coordinate patch deployment through the change management process without normal lead times — requires pre-established emergency change procedures.

---

### Cloud Security / CSPM Team

**What they own**: Cloud security posture — are cloud accounts configured securely? Misconfiguration detection, drift remediation, cloud-native security service management (AWS GuardDuty, SecurityHub, Azure Defender).

**Interaction with SRE**: CSPM tools scan for over-permissioned IAM roles, publicly exposed storage buckets, and unencrypted data stores — all of which are both security risks and potential reliability risks (an over-permissioned role that gets compromised can cause data deletion, which SRE then responds to as a production incident).

---

## Layer 5 — Data and Intelligence Teams

These teams move, store, transform, and make sense of data — both operational data (telemetry) and business data (analytics, ML).

---

### Data Engineering / Data Platform

**What they own**: Data pipelines, ETL/ELT processes, data lake, data warehouse, streaming data infrastructure, data quality frameworks.

**Key tools**: Spark, dbt, Airflow, AWS Glue, Snowflake, Databricks, Kafka Streams, Flink.

**Interaction with SRE**: Data pipelines are services — they have SLOs (freshness, completeness, latency). SRE increasingly supports data platform reliability. A broken Airflow DAG that delays a nightly financial report is a business-impact reliability event, not just a data problem.

---

### Machine Learning / AI Platform

**What they own**: Model training infrastructure, feature stores, model serving platforms, ML pipeline orchestration, model monitoring (drift detection).

**Key tools**: SageMaker, Vertex AI, MLflow, Kubeflow, Ray, Seldon, BentoML.

**Interaction with SRE**: ML models in production have reliability characteristics — latency, error rate, prediction quality (a form of SLI). SRE is increasingly expected to apply observability to ML systems: not just "is the model serving?" but "is the model producing good predictions?"

---

### Business Intelligence (BI) and Analytics

**What they own**: Business dashboards, self-service reporting, data mart management, KPI definitions, executive reporting.

**Interaction with SRE**: BI teams consume operational data (incident counts, MTTR, deployment frequency, SLO compliance) to build leadership dashboards. This is the consumer of your Layer 4 financial impact metrics from the 4-Impact Model.

---

### Data Science

**What they own**: Statistical analysis, forecasting models, experimentation (A/B testing framework), anomaly detection models, customer segmentation.

**Interaction with SRE**: Data Science and AIOps overlap. Data Science teams sometimes build the anomaly detection models that the Observability Platform team operationalizes. In mature organizations, SRE and Data Science co-own the feedback loop that improves alert accuracy over time.

---

## Layer 6 — Operations and Support Teams

These teams keep the daily operational machine running — they handle end-user issues, service desk, IT operations, and process governance.

---

### IT Operations (IT Ops)

**What they own**: End-user computing, device management, corporate network, on-premises application management, IT asset lifecycle, service desk escalation.

**Common activities**: Laptop provisioning, VPN management, printer/meeting room AV, software license management, vendor IT contracts.

**Interaction with SRE**: IT Ops and SRE are distinct. IT Ops serves internal employees; SRE serves production systems serving customers. However, in incidents, IT Ops sometimes surfaces the first signal ("engineers can't connect to VPN") that turns out to be a production network issue.

---

### Network Operations Center (NOC)

**What they own**: 24/7 monitoring of network health, first-line triage for network alarms, circuit provider coordination.

**Interaction with SRE**: In large enterprises and telecom (T-Mobile context), the NOC handles network-layer events and escalates to SRE when the symptom crosses into application territory. NOC sees physical layer issues; SRE sees service layer impact. The handoff protocol between NOC and SRE must be written, rehearsed, and unambiguous.

---

### IT Service Management (ITSM)

**What they own**: The process framework for IT operations — Incident Management, Problem Management, Change Management, Configuration Management, and Service Request processes. Often implemented in ServiceNow, Jira Service Management, or Remedy.

**Key ITSM concepts relevant to SRE**:

| ITSM Process | SRE Equivalent | Relationship |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| Incident Management | SRE on-call response | ITSM tracks tickets; SRE owns technical resolution |
| Problem Management | Postmortem process | ITSM tracks recurring patterns; SRE drives root cause elimination |
| Change Management | Deployment pipeline + CAB | ITSM governs the change window; DevOps executes the deployment |
| CMDB (Config Mgmt DB) | Service catalog / topology | ITSM tracks assets; SRE needs this for blast radius mapping |
| Service Request | Self-service developer portal | ITSM handles employee requests; Platform Engineering automates them |

**Interaction with SRE**: SRE often inherits ITSM incident management processes and must either align with them or explicitly negotiate where they diverge (e.g., SRE postmortems are blameless; ITSM RCA often is not).

---

### Help Desk / Service Desk

**What they own**: Tier 1 support for internal employees and sometimes external customers — password resets, access requests, software installation, basic troubleshooting.

**Interaction with SRE**: Service Desk is the canary for customer-facing incidents. If the service desk receives a spike in "I can't log in" tickets, that is a leading indicator of an authentication service degradation — and it arrives at the service desk 5–15 minutes before the metrics trip alert thresholds.

---

### Incident Command / Major Incident Management

**What they own**: The process and role structure for major incidents (P0/P1) — declaring incidents, assigning Incident Commanders, coordinating war rooms, managing customer communication, driving escalation decisions.

**Key roles in a major incident**:

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Incident Commander (IC) | Controls the incident — who does what, what is communicated, when to escalate |
| Technical Lead | Drives technical diagnosis and remediation |
| Communications Lead | Manages status page, customer communication, executive updates |
| Scribe | Documents timeline in real time |
| Subject Matter Experts (SMEs) | Domain experts brought in to investigate specific system areas |

**Interaction with SRE**: In many organizations, the Incident Commander role is staffed by SRE or a dedicated Incident Management team. Either way, SRE engineers operate within the incident command structure — they do not freelance during major incidents.

---

## Cross-Cutting Teams — Exist Across All Layers

---

### Architecture Review Board (ARB) / Technical Steering Committee

**What they own**: Technology standards, architectural decision records (ADRs), technology radar (what is approved, trial, hold, deprecated), design review gates for major initiatives.

**Interaction with SRE**: New services and platforms must pass architecture review before production deployment. SRE input into architecture reviews — reliability patterns, observability requirements, deployment strategy — is how reliability is designed in rather than bolted on.

---

### FinOps / Cloud Cost Management

**What they own**: Cloud spend visibility, cost allocation by team/product, reserved instance and savings plan strategy, commitment management, rightsizing recommendations, showback/chargeback model.

**Interaction with SRE**: FinOps and SRE intersect at capacity planning and observability cost. Over-provisioning for reliability (extra capacity buffers) has a cost; under-provisioning increases incident risk. SRE must balance these — and justify the cost of observability tooling (often the 2nd or 3rd largest cloud line item after compute and storage) in terms of MTTR reduction and incident prevention.

---

### Chaos Engineering / Resilience Engineering

**What they own**: Systematic failure injection — Game Days, chaos experiments, disaster recovery testing, failover validation. May be a dedicated team or a function within SRE.

**Key tools**: Chaos Monkey, Gremlin, AWS Fault Injection Simulator, LitmusChaos, Steadybit.

**Interaction with SRE**: Chaos experiments reveal reliability gaps before incidents do. SRE owns the findings and drives remediation. Without SRE partnership, chaos experiments produce findings that go unaddressed.

---

### Developer Experience (DevEx) Team

**What they own**: The ergonomics of the engineering environment — IDE tooling, local development environment, inner dev loop speed, documentation quality, onboarding experience for new engineers.

**Interaction with SRE**: DevEx and Platform Engineering overlap significantly in modern organizations. Both measure developer productivity (SPACE framework). SRE contributes to DevEx by making observability tooling accessible and the on-call experience less miserable.

---

## Part II — Roles, Responsibilities, and SRE Audience Classification

---

## How Teams Are Classified for SRE Purposes

Not every team interacts with SRE the same way. The classification below groups teams by **how SRE relates to them** — this determines communication style, escalation protocols, SLO ownership boundaries, and who gets which level of observability signal (maps directly to the 5-tier audience model in the observability document).

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Audience Set A — SRE Core Partners     (daily operational work)     │
│  Audience Set B — SRE Service Consumers (teams SRE serves)          │
│  Audience Set C — SRE Infrastructure Dependencies (SRE escalates to)│
│  Audience Set D — SRE Governance Layer  (sets rules SRE operates in) │
│  Audience Set E — SRE Business Stakeholders (consume SRE outputs)   │
│  Audience Set F — SRE Operational Co-owners (shared process owners) │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

## Audience Set A — SRE Core Partners

**Definition**: Teams that work alongside SRE daily on shared technical problems. Neither team fully owns the problem without the other. Failure at the boundary between SRE and these teams is the most common source of production incidents.

**SRE relationship**: Peer collaboration. Joint ownership of reliability primitives. Regular sync cadence (weekly minimum). SRE has veto input into their technical decisions that affect production.

---

### Platform Engineering / IDP

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|----------------|
| Staff Platform Engineer | Designs golden-path templates, developer portal architecture, self-service provisioning workflows |
| Senior Platform Engineer | Builds and maintains IDP tooling (Backstage, Crossplane, ArgoCD); defines service scaffolding standards |
| Platform Engineer | Implements service templates, onboards new teams to the platform, maintains pipeline integrations |
| Platform Architect | Sets technical direction for the IDP; defines the boundary between platform services and product services |
| Developer Advocate (internal) | Drives adoption of platform capabilities; gathers developer feedback; maintains documentation |

**Responsibilities owned by this team**:
- Golden-path deployment templates (service mesh config, resource quotas, health checks baked in)
- Developer portal (Backstage) — service catalog, runbook links, SLO widget integration
- Self-service namespace and environment provisioning
- Standardized Helm chart library
- Internal platform SLAs (what uptime does the IDP itself guarantee?)

**What SRE needs from them**: Reliability primitives embedded in templates (readiness probes, resource limits, PodDisruptionBudgets). Observability hooks in every template. Canary/blue-green deployment capability.

**What they need from SRE**: SLO definitions to display in the developer portal. Reliability review of new platform capabilities before general availability. On-call contact when the platform itself degrades.

---

### Kubernetes / Container Platform Team

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|----------------|

### Expanded Profiles — Teams New to the Generic Framework

These teams exist in your org but were not in the generic framework. Each is defined below with SRE context.

---

#### Env Teams (Environment Engineering)

**What they own**: Non-production environment lifecycle — dev, integration, QA, performance, staging, UAT environments. Provisioning, refresh cadence, data masking for lower environments, environment parity with production.

**Why they matter for SRE**: Production incidents are often traceable to "it worked in staging." Environment Teams are responsible for closing the production parity gap — if staging doesn't mirror production's config, scale, and data shape, every test result is suspect.

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Environment Manager | Owns environment strategy, refresh calendar, cost management for non-prod |
| Senior Env Engineer | Configures environment topology; manages IaC for env provisioning; coordinates data masking |
| Env Engineer | Day-to-day environment support; breaks/fixes in lower envs; user access for test teams |

**SRE needs from Env Teams**: Production-parity environments for pre-launch chaos and performance testing. Reliable environment availability (if QA env is flaky, QA results are meaningless). Change notification when environment configs are updated (prevents "but it worked in staging" failures).

**SRE escalation trigger**: A staging or QA environment difference that silently allowed a bug to reach production — SRE raises this as a parity gap finding requiring remediation.

#### Perf Testing Teams (Performance / Load Engineering)

**What they own**: Load test design, execution, and analysis. Capacity baseline establishment. SLO stress validation before major launches or scaling events.

**Why they matter for SRE**: SLOs are only credible if they have been stress-tested. Perf Testing Teams are the pre-production validators of SRE's capacity assumptions. They answer: "How many requests per second can this service handle before latency SLO breaches?"

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Perf Test Lead | Designs load test strategy; prioritizes services by business risk; owns tooling (k6, Gatling, JMeter, Locust) |
| Senior Perf Engineer | Writes realistic load profiles based on production traffic patterns; analyzes bottlenecks |
| Perf Engineer | Runs tests; monitors system behavior during load; reports results with SLO pass/fail verdict |

**SRE needs from Perf Teams**: Baseline throughput per service (what is normal max capacity?). Pre-launch load test results before any major release. Identification of resource saturation points before they appear in production.

**Perf Teams need from SRE**: Production traffic profiles and growth projections. Access to production-like environments. SLO targets to test against (without them, perf teams have no pass/fail criterion).

**SRE contribution**: SRE defines the SLO thresholds that perf tests validate against. Without SRE-defined targets, perf testing produces data with no clear success criterion.

#### BVT Team (Build Verification Testing)

**What they own**: Automated smoke and sanity test suites that run immediately after every deployment to validate the build is functionally alive before it progresses to deeper testing or production acceptance.

**Why they matter for SRE**: BVT is the fastest post-deployment signal. If BVT fails, the deployment is bad — and SRE needs that signal in under 5 minutes, not after a full test suite run that takes an hour.

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| BVT Lead | Owns BVT suite coverage; ensures BVT tests match production critical flows |
| BVT Engineer | Writes and maintains BVT test cases; integrates BVT results into deployment pipeline |

**SRE needs from BVT**: Pass/fail signal within 5 minutes of deployment. BVT result as a deployment gate input into the pipeline — a BVT failure should automatically trigger a rollback or deployment halt. BVT results as the "change verification" signal in the observability layer (deployment happened, BVT passed = known good state).

**BVT needs from SRE**: Which endpoints and flows are critical (so BVT covers them). SLO-aligned latency thresholds in BVT assertions (not just "returns 200" but "returns 200 in under 300ms").

**SRE boundary**: BVT is not a replacement for SLO monitoring. BVT validates the deployment artifact. SRE monitors production behavior over time. Both are required.

#### SRE COE (Center of Excellence)

**What they own**: Enterprise-wide SRE standards — SLO framework definition, observability standards, on-call practice governance, reliability tooling selection, SRE training and maturity assessment, cross-team postmortem reviews.

**Why this is special**: The SRE COE is not a consumer, dependency, or stakeholder. It is the standard-setting body that makes SRE practice coherent across multiple product teams or business units. In organizations with embedded SREs per product team, the COE is the backbone that prevents every team from inventing its own definition of "reliable."

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| SRE Principal / Staff SRE | Defines reliability standards; creates reference implementations; reviews team SLOs for consistency |
| SRE Practice Lead | Drives SRE adoption across teams; owns the SRE maturity model; trains new embedded SREs |

**What the COE produces**:
- SLO framework: standard SLI types, SLO target guidance by criticality tier
- Observability standards: telemetry schema contract, mandatory context fields, golden dashboard templates
- On-call standards: rotation design, escalation protocols, postmortem template
- Error budget policy: what actions trigger when budget is consumed
- Reliability maturity model: L1–L5 assessment criteria per team

**SRE COE — Observability Tier**: The COE itself consumes all tiers — it reads Tier 3 (engineer detail) to assess team maturity and produces Tier 4 (leadership) reliability scorecards aggregated across all teams.

#### SNOW Team (ServiceNow Platform Team)

If SNOW is down during an incident, SRE loses incident documentation, change tracking, and CMDB lookups simultaneously.

#### Illumio Team (Zero Trust Segmentation)

**What they own**: Micro-segmentation policies across all workloads using the Illumio platform. They define which services are allowed to communicate with which other services, enforced at the workload level (not just at the network perimeter).

**Why they matter for SRE**: Illumio policies can silently break service-to-service communication without any obvious network error. A service gets a connection refused or timeout — and it looks like the downstream service is down, when in reality the Illumio policy is blocking the traffic. This is one of the hardest-to-diagnose failure modes because the observability signal points to the wrong cause.

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|

**SRE escalation trigger**: New service deployment that can't reach its dependencies despite DNS resolving correctly and no application error. **SRE needs from Illumio Team**: Clear process for requesting policy exceptions or new policy rules when deploying new services. Visibility into the policy map for blast radius analysis. Fast-track approval process for emergency policy changes during P1 incidents.

**What Illumio Team needs from SRE**: Advance notification of new services before they deploy to production (so policies can be pre-approved). Architecture diagrams showing intended service communication paths. Incident data when policy-blocked traffic is suspected.

#### Bar Raiser Call (Quality Gate Review)

**What they own**: A structured review process — similar to a design review or CAB but focused on raising quality, reliability, and security standards before a new service, platform, or major change goes to production. The "Bar Raiser" concept comes from Amazon's hiring process — someone whose job is to ask the hard questions that the team may be glossing over in their enthusiasm to ship.

**Why they matter for SRE**: The Bar Raiser Call is where SRE has the highest-leverage input. A reliability concern raised at the Bar Raiser Call costs days of design rework. The same concern raised after production launch costs weeks of incident response and remediation.

| SRE Input at Bar Raiser Call | What to ask |
|-----------------------------|-------------|
| SLO definition | "What is the SLO for this service? What SLI measures it? Is it measurable from day one?" |
| Failure mode | "What happens when the downstream dependency this service calls goes down?" |
| Observability | "How will on-call know this service is degraded at 2am without customer calls?" |
| Blast radius | "If this service fails, what other services fail with it?" |
| Rollback | "How do we roll this back within 5 minutes if the canary degrades?" |
| Toil | "What manual work will SRE inherit when this goes to production?" |

**SRE relationship to Bar Raiser**: At Principal/Director level, you should be a standing Bar Raiser participant for any service or platform that will require SRE support. Your questions are the reliability gate. If a team can't answer the six questions above, the Bar Raiser Call should not produce a green light.

These teams are cross-cutting — they audit services, review changes, and govern data across every other team in the organization. They are not a single layer; they span governance (D), operational co-ownership (F), and infrastructure dependency (C) depending on which function they are performing at a given moment.

### Cybersecurity / Information Security Team

**What they own**: The enterprise security posture — security policy framework, security architecture standards, threat and vulnerability management, security tool ownership, and security incident response process. This is the parent function from which SOC, AppSec, and Vulnerability Management typically report.

**Three modes they operate in — and how SRE relates to each**:

```
Mode 1 — Policy and Governance
  Cybersecurity defines the rules.
  SRE must comply with them and provide evidence of compliance.
  
Mode 2 — Architecture Review
  Cybersecurity reviews new system designs for security risk.
  SRE provides reliability context; Cybersecurity provides threat model.
  Both must sign off before production.
  
Mode 3 — Active Security Incident
  Cybersecurity leads the incident.
  SRE supports with telemetry, production access, and system context.
  SRE does not act without Cybersecurity direction.
```

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| CISO | Owns enterprise security strategy; leads board-level security reporting; manages security budget |
| VP / Director of Cybersecurity | Owns security operations, AppSec, and vulnerability management; leads security incident response |
| Security Architect | Reviews new architecture for security risk; defines security reference architectures |
| Security Engineer | Implements security tooling; manages EDR, SIEM, DLP, CASB deployments |
| Penetration Tester | Conducts authorized attacks to find exploitable weaknesses before adversaries do |
| Threat Intelligence Analyst | Monitors threat landscape; maps adversary TTPs to your environment; updates detection rules |

**What SRE provides to Cybersecurity**:
- Production telemetry access — the observability stack is the richest source of evidence during a security investigation
- System architecture knowledge — which services communicate with which, what data they handle, where secrets are stored
- Fast access to production systems during forensic investigation
- Postmortem timelines that establish what was normal before an incident (baseline for anomaly detection)

**What Cybersecurity provides to SRE**:
- Security control requirements that shape SRE's operational standards (e.g., all secrets must be in Vault, all access must be logged, all remote access through PAM)
- Threat model context that improves reliability design (knowing your threat actors helps you design against both adversarial failures and accidental ones)
- Advance notice of penetration tests and red team exercises (so SRE doesn't respond to a pen test as a production incident)
- Security incident declaration authority — SRE defers to Cybersecurity on whether an anomaly is a security incident

**SRE escalation trigger**: Any operational anomaly with security characteristics — unusual auth failure patterns, unexpected data volume leaving a service, privilege escalation events, unexpected process execution on production nodes.

**Critical boundary**: During a security incident, SRE operates under Cybersecurity's direction. This includes:
- Do not patch or restart compromised systems before forensic evidence is collected (restarting wipes memory)
- Do not communicate about the incident outside of approved channels (evidence preservation, legal hold)
- Do not make infrastructure changes without explicit approval (you might destroy the attacker's persistence mechanism — before it's documented)

### Data Security / Data Privacy Team

**What they own**: Data classification policy, data handling requirements, PII (Personally Identifiable Information) controls, DLP (Data Loss Prevention) tooling, data retention and deletion policies, privacy impact assessments, and regulatory compliance for data-specific regulations (GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA, TCPA for telecom).

**Why they matter for SRE more than most engineers realize**:

1. **Log data contains PII** — phone numbers, customer IDs, message content. What SRE logs to Splunk must comply with data classification policy. Logging the wrong field can create a regulatory violation.
2. **DLP tools can break services** — DLP agents inspect data in transit. In aggressive enforcement mode, they can block data from leaving a service, creating what looks like a network connectivity failure. SRE needs to know DLP is in the chain.
3. **Retention policies constrain observability** — Data Security sets how long logs, traces, and metrics can be retained. A 90-day retention policy means SRE cannot do long-term trend analysis without a separate archive tier.
4. **Incident response involves customer data** — Every P1 that touches customer data requires Data Security involvement to assess breach notification obligations.

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Chief Privacy Officer / VP Data Security | Owns data privacy strategy; leads regulatory data breach notification; manages data governance policy |
| Data Privacy Officer (DPO) | Required by GDPR; oversees data processing activities; handles subject access requests |
| Data Classification Analyst | Defines what data is classified at which tier (public, internal, confidential, restricted) |
| DLP Engineer | Configures and manages DLP tooling; tunes policies to reduce false positives; responds to DLP incidents |
| Data Retention Analyst | Defines retention schedules by data type; manages deletion workflows; ensures regulatory compliance |

**What SRE provides to Data Security**:
- Log pipeline architecture — where logs go, what fields are captured, how long they are retained
- Incident timelines for breach notification assessment (did customer data leave the system? when? how much?)
- Access controls for production data (who can query logs, who can access databases)
- Evidence of PII controls in telemetry (proof that logs do not capture prohibited fields)

**What Data Security provides to SRE**:
- Data classification for every field SRE logs — SRE cannot make safe logging decisions without knowing which fields are restricted
- Approved log retention tiers and deletion schedules — SRE's retention architecture must comply
- DLP policy documentation — SRE needs to know what DLP rules are active so they can diagnose DLP-caused connectivity failures
- Breach notification thresholds — if a security incident involves customer data, SRE's incident timeline feeds the breach notification assessment

**SRE-specific data security requirements**:

| Requirement | What SRE must do |
|-------------|-----------------|
| No PII in logs | Never log phone numbers, email, SSN, account numbers, auth tokens in plain text |
| No secrets in logs | Never log passwords, API keys, Vault tokens, certificate private keys |
| Log access control | Splunk/logging platform access must be role-based; production log access requires justification |
| Retention compliance | Logs must be deleted (not just archived) at the end of the retention window |
| Incident data handling | During a breach investigation, SRE preserves data in coordination with Data Security — no deletion without approval |
| PII in metrics | Metric label values must not contain user-identifying information (see cardinality section in observability doc) |

### How Cybersecurity and Data Security Teams Appear in the 4-Impact Model

The security layer adds a fifth dimension to the 4-Impact Model that is unique to security events — **Regulatory and Legal Impact** — which sits above all four technical layers and can override normal incident priorities.

```
Raw Signal (security anomaly in telemetry)
        ↓
[Layer 1] Technical Impact    — Credential compromise? Service behavior changed?
        ↓
[Layer 2] Customer Impact     — Is customer data exposed? Is service availability affected?
        ↓
[Layer 3] Business Impact     — Which SLAs are breached? Which regulatory windows are triggered?
        ↓
[Layer 4] Financial Impact    — Breach notification cost, regulatory fine exposure, legal hold cost
        ↓
[Layer 5] Regulatory/Legal    — Breach notification obligation (GDPR: 72hr, CCPA, TCPA)
                                Evidence preservation, legal hold, external counsel
                                (Cybersecurity and Legal own this layer — SRE supports)
```

**SRE's role at Layer 5**: Provide the telemetry timeline that establishes when the breach occurred, what data was accessed, and how. SRE does not make notification decisions — that is Cybersecurity, Legal, and the DPO. SRE makes the timeline credible and defensible.

## Part V — The Reliability Participation Model

---

### The Core Reframe

SRE does not own reliability. SRE **governs** reliability — and every other team **participates** in it.

This distinction matters enormously at Director level. An SRE team that tries to own reliability unilaterally becomes a bottleneck and a blame target. A team that governs reliability — setting contracts, measuring participation, holding teams accountable — scales infinitely.

Every team in this document introduces reliability risk through its actions and reduces reliability risk through its responsibilities. SRE's job is to make that visible, define the contract explicitly, and measure compliance.

```
Traditional view:         Reliability Participation view:
                  
SRE → Production          Every team has a reliability role.
  (SRE is responsible)    SRE defines and governs those roles.
                          Production reliability is the aggregate output
                          of all teams doing their reliability job.
```

When a production incident happens, the postmortem question is never "which team failed?" — it is "which reliability contract was not honored, by which team, and why?"

---

### Reliability Risk Categories Every Team Introduces

Before defining each team's reliability role, understand the four categories of reliability risk that non-SRE teams introduce:

| Risk Category | Description | Most Common Source Teams |
|--------------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Change-induced failure** | A team makes a change that breaks production | GitLab Team, Backend Teams, TKe Team, Firewall Team, Illumio Team, SNOW Team |
| **Dependency failure** | A team's system goes down and takes dependent services with it | SNOW Team, GitLab Team, MS Office Tools, DB Team, Middleware Layer |
| **Policy-induced failure** | A team enforces a policy that silently breaks service communication or data flow | Cybersecurity, Data Security/DLP, Governance, Firewall Team, Illumio Team |
| **Process failure** | A team's process breaks down during an incident, slowing resolution | MIM Team, L1/L2 Triage, Release Management/CAB, SNOW Team |

---

### The Reliability Participation Contract — Per Team

For each team: **what they must do, what they must not do, and what signal SRE uses to verify they are doing their part.**

---

#### QA Teams

**Reliability role**: Gate keeper for known defects. QA prevents known bad code from reaching production.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Define and run regression suites covering SLI-critical flows | Ship a release that failed tests under "time pressure" | Test pass/fail rate per deployment in CI pipeline |
| Report performance regressions before production | Test only happy-path flows, ignoring failure scenarios | Performance delta between builds (latency, throughput) |
| Maintain test environments that mirror production configuration | Approve a release with known open P1/P2 severity defects | Defect escape rate — bugs found in production vs. in test |
| Validate BVT gates are covering actual user-journey SLIs | Treat test coverage as a checkbox, not a reliability signal | SLO coverage percentage of user journeys in automated tests |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Defect escape — bugs reach production that QA was supposed to catch. Every defect escape is a potential SLO breach.

#### Env Teams

**Reliability role**: Production parity guarantors. If staging doesn't mirror production, every test is a lie.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Maintain production-parity configuration in staging (same versions, same config structure) | Run staging on under-provisioned resources that can't reveal performance issues | Configuration drift delta between staging and production |
| Coordinate environment refresh cadence with SRE capacity planning | Make environment changes without a change record | Environment uptime SLO (test teams are users of Env Teams' service) |
| Notify SRE when environment topology changes (new services added, network changes) | Ignore environment degradation tickets that block QA | Incident-to-environment-parity gap rate |
| Pre-provision environments for major load tests in advance | Reuse shared environments across teams with conflicting test schedules | Load test environment readiness lead time |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Production surprises — failures that would have been caught in staging were invisible because staging didn't resemble production.

---

#### Perf Testing Teams

**Reliability role**: Capacity baseline setters and SLO stress validators. They prove (or disprove) that the system can handle its expected load before production sees it.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Run load tests using realistic traffic profiles from production data | Run synthetic traffic that doesn't reflect actual usage patterns | Correlation between perf test results and production latency under load |
| Produce pass/fail verdicts against SRE-defined SLO thresholds | Deliver a "results report" with no pass/fail conclusion | % of SLO targets validated by perf test before each major release |
| Flag saturation points (where does the system start to degrade?) | Declare success because average latency is fine (ignoring p99) | Saturation points mapped to capacity headroom in production |
| Re-run tests after architecture changes, not just before launch | Test once at launch and never again as the system evolves | Perf test frequency per service per quarter |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Capacity surprises in production — Black Friday traffic, marketing campaigns, or organic growth hits a saturation point that was never discovered because nobody ran the right test.

#### Governance Team

**Reliability role**: Constraint setters whose decisions shape the reliability envelope SRE operates within.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Provide clear, stable compliance requirements with sufficient lead time | Issue compliance mandates 30 days before an audit that require architectural changes | Change request lead time for compliance-driven requirements |
| Define which SLOs have regulatory backing (non-negotiable error budget floors) | Interpret regulations in ways that make the system unreliable without providing a path forward | Number of compliance-driven emergency changes per quarter |
| Participate in architecture reviews when regulatory implications exist | Treat reliability and compliance as competing concerns (they are complementary) | Compliance-related incident rate |
| Communicate audit windows to SRE in advance | Surprise SRE with audit evidence requests with 24-hour notice | Evidence production time per audit request |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Compliance-driven emergency changes — last-minute mandates that force architectural changes without testing, leading to production incidents.

#### GitLab Team (CICD / Vault / Config Ownership)

**Reliability role**: The delivery infrastructure owner. GitLab is the deployment system; Vault is the secrets system; config management is the configuration system. If any of these fail, the ability to deploy, rollback, or change configuration in production is lost.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Treat GitLab CI/CD, Vault, and config pipelines as production-grade services with SLOs | Run GitLab or Vault as "best effort" internal tools without SLOs | GitLab availability SLO, Vault availability SLO |
| Coordinate GitLab upgrades and maintenance windows with SRE (avoid deployment-window conflicts) | Perform GitLab maintenance during peak deployment periods or active incidents | Maintenance window conflict rate with SRE deployments |
| Ensure Vault HA is configured so a single node failure does not block secrets retrieval | Run Vault in single-node mode in production | Vault seal/unseal events per month |
| Manage certificate expiry for GitLab and Vault proactively (automated rotation) | Let certificates expire silently — both GitLab and Vault will stop working | Cert expiry lead time monitoring |
| Provide SRE with emergency break-glass access to deploy without CI/CD during a P1 | Make GitLab the only path to production with no manual override | Break-glass deployment procedure tested quarterly |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Loss of deployment capability during a crisis — when SRE needs to push an emergency hotfix, the deployment system is unavailable. Also Vault outages that prevent services from retrieving secrets at startup.

---

#### BVT Team

**Reliability role**: First post-deployment reliability signal. BVT is the fastest gate between deployment and "this broke production."

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Cover all SLI-critical user flows in BVT (not just "service is up") | Limit BVT to "does the homepage load?" — that is not sufficient reliability validation | % of SLO-critical flows covered in BVT suite |
| Complete BVT within 5 minutes of deployment (SRE needs fast signal) | Run BVT suites that take 45 minutes — they cannot gate rollback decisions | BVT execution time per deployment |
| Include latency assertions in BVT (not just correctness) | Test only functional correctness, ignoring performance regression | BVT latency threshold coverage |
| Produce a pass/fail verdict that the deployment pipeline can act on automatically | Produce results that require human interpretation before a decision can be made | Automated rollback trigger rate from BVT failures |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Slow or missing deployment validation — bad deployments reach production before anyone knows, making rollbacks harder and MTTR longer.

---

#### Release Management / CAB Team

**Reliability role**: Change risk governance. CAB's reliability job is to ensure every change to production has been evaluated for risk, has a rollback plan, and happens in a window that SRE can support.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Require rollback plans for every change (a change without rollback is not approved) | Approve changes with "rollback: not applicable" as an acceptable answer | % of changes with documented and tested rollback plans |
| Publish change freeze windows with sufficient advance notice (minimum 2 weeks) | Issue emergency freeze windows with less than 24 hours notice | Change freeze advance notice lead time |
| Produce the change event feed that observability AI uses for incident correlation | Allow undocumented changes — shadow changes are reliability's worst enemy | % of production changes with matching SNOW tickets |
| Define an emergency change process that is pre-approved and rehearsed | Make emergency changes require a 2-hour CAB convening (creates dangerous pressure to skip CAB entirely) | Emergency change approval time |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Undocumented changes — when something breaks in production and there is no change record, SRE cannot correlate the incident to a cause, MTTD increases dramatically.

---

#### MIM Team (Major Incident Management)

**Reliability role**: Incident coordination efficiency. MIM's reliability job is to ensure that when a P1 happens, the human coordination around it does not make the incident worse or longer.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Assign an Incident Commander within 5 minutes of P1 declaration | Leave the war room uncoordinated while SRE engineers freelance in different directions | IC assignment time from P1 declaration |
| Manage stakeholder and executive communication so SRE engineers can focus on technical work | Pull SRE engineers off technical work to update executives directly | % of P1s where IC handled stakeholder comms (not SRE engineer) |
| Produce a complete incident timeline in real time via a dedicated scribe | Reconstruct the timeline from memory after the incident (always incomplete) | Incident timeline completeness score |
| Drive postmortem completion within 5 business days | Allow postmortems to stay open indefinitely | Postmortem completion rate within SLA |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Coordination chaos — a P1 without a functioning incident command structure adds 30–60 minutes to MTTR as engineers talk over each other, duplicate work, and field executive calls simultaneously.

---

#### SNOW Team (ServiceNow)

**Reliability role**: ITSM platform reliability. SNOW is the system of record for incidents, changes, and problems. If SNOW is unreliable, incident tracking, change governance, and audit evidence all degrade simultaneously.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Run SNOW as a production-grade service with its own SLO (availability, API response time) | Treat SNOW as an "internal tool" exempt from reliability standards | SNOW API availability SLO, SNOW page load time SLO |
| Provide API access for SRE automation (auto-create incidents, update CMDB from deployment events) | Require all incident creation to be manual web form entry | % of P1 incidents auto-created vs. manually created |
| Coordinate SNOW maintenance windows with SRE (avoid overlap with peak deployment or incident periods) | Perform SNOW upgrades during deployment windows or business-critical hours | SNOW maintenance conflict rate with incident events |
| Maintain CMDB accuracy (stale CMDB makes blast radius assessment wrong) | Allow CMDB to drift — teams that deploy services but do not update the CMDB | CMDB staleness rate (% of services with last-updated > 30 days) |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: SNOW outage during an active P1 — no incident ticket, no change record lookup, no CMDB reference. The incident becomes undocumented and harder to manage.

---

#### Firewall Team

**Reliability role**: Controlled network change execution. Every firewall rule change is a potential connectivity break for a production service.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Test firewall rule changes in non-production before production, even for "minor" changes | Push firewall changes directly to production because "it's just an ACL" | Firewall-change-to-incident correlation rate |
| Notify SRE before any rule change affecting services with production traffic | Make firewall changes silently during business hours without a change ticket | Firewall change advance notification rate |
| Maintain a rollback procedure for every firewall change (deny → permit revert) | Assume firewall changes are safe because they are additive | Firewall change rollback exercise frequency |
| Provide SRE with read access to firewall rule state for incident investigation | Treat firewall rules as security-sensitive information that SRE cannot see | SRE incident investigation blocked by firewall visibility gap rate |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Silent connectivity failures — a firewall rule blocks service-to-service traffic, the service gets connection refused errors, and SRE spends 45 minutes investigating the wrong layer before escalating to the Firewall team.

#### Illumio Team

**Reliability role**: Micro-segmentation policy lifecycle management. Illumio policies control which workloads can talk to which. A wrong policy is an invisible outage.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Onboard new services to Illumio policies before they go to production (not after) | Switch a new service to enforcement mode without validating its communication flows first | New service policy approval lead time |
| Run Illumio in "test mode" for new policies before switching to enforcement | Go directly from "no policy" to "enforcement mode" with no test period | Policy test-to-enforcement promotion rate |
| Provide SRE with Illumio Explorer access to visualize traffic flows during incident investigation | Treat policy maps as configuration-only data that SRE cannot access | SRE Illumio Explorer access availability |
| Notify SRE before any policy change affecting high-traffic services | Change policies during peak traffic periods without a change window | Policy change advance notification rate |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Silent segmentation-induced outages — the hardest class of connectivity failures to diagnose because they look identical to a downstream service being down, but the downstream service is healthy and the policy is the actual blocker.

#### TKe Team (Kubernetes Platform)

**Reliability role**: Platform substrate stability. The Kubernetes platform is the foundation everything runs on. TKe team reliability failures affect all services simultaneously — they have the highest blast radius of any team in the organization.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Communicate cluster maintenance windows (node drains, upgrades) at least 2 weeks in advance | Drain nodes during business hours without advance SRE coordination | Planned maintenance advance notice lead time |
| Maintain cluster version N-2 compatibility so workloads survive cluster upgrades without modification | Force all workloads to upgrade simultaneously during a cluster version upgrade | Workload compatibility failure rate during cluster upgrades |
| Run cluster upgrades using a canary approach (one node pool at a time, validate, then roll forward) | Upgrade the entire cluster in one maintenance window with no validation gates | Cluster upgrade incident rate |
| Provide SRE with cluster-level observability (etcd health, API server latency, node pressure taints) | Treat cluster internals as opaque to SRE | SRE visibility into control plane health |
| Define and test a cluster failure scenario (etcd quorum loss, API server unavailability) at least annually | Assume the control plane is never the root cause | Cluster-level Game Day frequency |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Platform-level blast radius events — a cluster upgrade that breaks pod scheduling or a node drain that evicts workloads without waiting for graceful termination. These affect every service simultaneously.

#### DB Team

**Reliability role**: Data layer stability. Databases are the most common root cause of production incidents at the application layer.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Perform schema migrations using backwards-compatible patterns (additive changes, not breaking changes) | Drop a column or rename a table without a blue/green migration strategy | Schema migration incident rate |
| Coordinate maintenance windows (vacuum runs, index rebuilds, stats updates) with SRE | Run heavy maintenance operations during peak traffic hours | DB maintenance-to-latency spike correlation rate |
| Ensure automated failover is tested quarterly (not just configured) | Assume failover works because it is configured | Failover test success rate |
| Monitor and alert on leading indicators (replication lag, connection pool exhaustion, lock wait rate) | Alert only on failure (when it is already too late) | DB leading indicator alert coverage |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Application-layer latency spikes and error rate increases that trace to the database — lock contention, connection pool exhaustion, slow queries, or failed failover.

#### Cybersecurity Team (Reliability Lens Only)

**Reliability role**: Coordinated change execution for security-mandated changes. Security requirements are non-negotiable; how they are implemented determines whether they cause outages.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Provide 72-hour minimum notice for non-critical security changes (cert rotation, key rotation, policy updates) | Push security changes to production without SRE coordination because "security can't wait" | Security change advance notification rate |
| Test security changes (key rotations, cert renewals) in staging before production | Skip staging for security changes because "urgency" | Security change staging validation rate |
| Define emergency change procedures for critical CVE patches that include rollback plans | Issue "patch immediately" mandates with no rollback guidance | Emergency security change rollback plan coverage |
| Coordinate penetration test schedules with SRE so alerts are not false incidents | Run red team exercises without notifying SRE (triggers false P1 incident response) | Pen test notification lead time |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Security-change-induced outages — the most ironic failure category: a change made in the name of security causes a production reliability incident. Log4shell-era emergency patches, cert rotations that expire 30 seconds before the new cert is loaded, key rotations that leave services unable to decrypt data — all common patterns.

#### Data Security / DLP Team (Reliability Lens Only)

**Reliability role**: Policy change coordination. DLP policies that block data movement can break service communication in ways that look like network failures.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Test DLP policy changes in test mode before enforcement mode | Switch from test mode to enforcement mode on high-traffic services without validation | DLP-policy-change-to-incident correlation rate |
| Coordinate enforcement mode changes with SRE change management | Treat DLP as a security-only concern that doesn't need operational coordination | DLP change advance notification rate |
| Document which services have DLP agents active (for SRE incident investigation reference) | Leave SRE without visibility into DLP deployment map | SRE access to DLP agent inventory |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: Silent DLP-induced connectivity failures — among the most difficult incidents to diagnose because the service is healthy, the network is healthy, but data cannot flow between services.

#### L1 / L2 Triage Team

**Reliability role**: First signal amplifiers and filter. L1/L2 are often the first humans to know something is wrong, either from customer calls or monitoring alerts. Their quality of triage determines whether SRE is engaged at the right time with the right context.

| Must DO | Must NOT DO | SRE Verification Signal |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| Escalate to SRE with a structured handoff: symptom, customer impact scope, timeline, steps already taken | Escalate to SRE with "something seems wrong, can you check?" | Escalation quality score (does the handoff contain required fields?) |
| Execute known-issue runbooks independently for recurring conditions | Call SRE for every alert, including those with documented L1 resolution steps | % of alerts resolved by L1/L2 without SRE escalation |
| Document the incident timeline from first customer contact before SRE engages | Hand off verbally with no written record | Timeline documentation completeness at handoff |
| Filter noise — not every alert is an incident | Escalate every alert to SRE regardless of severity | False escalation rate (escalations that required no SRE action) |

**Reliability risk they introduce if they fail their contract**: SRE alert fatigue and delayed response — if L1/L2 escalate everything without triage, SRE desensitizes to escalations and misses real incidents. Conversely, if L1/L2 hold incidents too long trying to solve them, SRE engages too late.

### The Shared Reliability Model — One-Page Summary

```
Every team has three reliability obligations:

  1. OWN YOUR CHANGES
     Any change you make to production (code, config, policy, network rule)
     must have a test plan, a change record, and a rollback path.
     "It's just a small change" does not exist. Every change is a potential incident.

  2. OWN YOUR SERVICE
     If a system you operate is a dependency for production services,
     it must be treated as a production service — with an SLO, monitoring,
     maintenance windows that don't break others, and an on-call contact.

  3. OWN YOUR PART OF INCIDENT RESPONSE
     When an incident involves your domain, you respond in your role —
     not as a bystander, not as a blocker, and not by delegating back to SRE.
     SRE governs the process. You execute your part.
```

### How SRE Governs Reliability Participation

SRE does not enforce these contracts through authority alone — it enforces them through visibility. If every team's reliability participation is measured and reported, non-participation becomes visible to leadership.

**The Reliability Participation Scorecard** — produced monthly by SRE COE, consumed by Leadership Team (Tier 4/5 audience):

| Team | Change Records Complete | Advance Notification Rate | Rollback Plan Coverage | SLO Defined for their system | Incidents they introduced last 30 days |
|------|------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| GitLab Team | 98% | 95% | 100% | Yes (99.5%) | 0 |
| Firewall Team | 87% | 72% | 91% | No | 2 |
| Illumio Team | 79% | 61% | 85% | No | 3 |
| DB Team | 95% | 88% | 97% | Yes (99.9%) | 1 |
| TKe Team | 99% | 99% | 100% | Yes (99.99%) | 0 |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

**The conversation this creates**: A Firewall team with 72% advance notification and 2 incidents it introduced last month gets a conversation with their manager and SRE leadership — backed by data, not opinion. This is reliability governance, not reliability blame.

**The goal**: Every team reaches the point where they do not need SRE to remind them of their reliability obligations — because those obligations are defined, measured, and visible to everyone.
