# 05 — Financial Acumen for SRE Directors

## Why Financial Fluency Is Non-Negotiable at Director Level

SMs can often defer financial conversations to their managers or finance partners. Directors cannot. You will own a budget. You will defend headcount requests. You will be asked to justify infrastructure spend. You will have to translate reliability investment into business value language.

This module gives you the frameworks, formulas, and language to operate confidently in financial conversations — without an MBA.

---

## Concept 1: The Budget Architecture

### Three Types of Budget

**OpEx (Operating Expenditure)**: Day-to-day running costs. Expensed in the year incurred.
- Salaries, benefits, contractors
- Cloud compute, SaaS licenses
- Training, travel
- On-call compensation

**CapEx (Capital Expenditure)**: Investment in long-term assets. Depreciated over time.
- Data center hardware (if on-prem)
- Software licenses with multi-year terms
- Some cloud reserved instances (accounting treatment varies)

**Director's practical rule**: Cloud is mostly OpEx. This is strategically important — it means your cost base is variable, not fixed. You can scale it. But VPs will also ask why your OpEx is growing without CapEx investment to show.

### The Budget Line Items You Own

| Category | What's In It | Your Lever |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Headcount (HC) | Salary, benefits (~1.3× salary in total comp cost) | Hiring, attrition management, HC mix (FTE vs. contractor) |
| Cloud/Infrastructure | Compute, storage, network, managed services | Right-sizing, reserved instances, waste elimination |
| Tooling & Licensing | Splunk, Datadog, PagerDuty, etc. | Contract negotiation, consolidation |
| Training & Certifications | Courses, certifications, conference attendance | ROI case: certs reduce attrition risk |
| Contractor/Vendor | Staff augmentation, professional services | When vs. FTE tradeoffs |

## Concept 2: Headcount Planning

### The HC Model

**FTE (Full-Time Employee)**: Core team. High continuity, higher initial cost (recruiting + ramp), lower long-term cost per hour. Use for: steady-state work, domain expertise, leadership roles.

**Contractor/Staff Aug**: Variable capacity. Pay premium (1.3-1.7× FTE hourly rate) but no benefits overhead, flexible duration. Use for: surge capacity, specialized skills you need for < 12 months, backfill during recruiting lag.

**Managed Service / Outsourcing**: Operational work packaged as a service. Use for: well-defined, process-heavy operational tasks (L1 monitoring, routine patching). Risk: knowledge loss, SLA friction.

### The Headcount Justification Formula

When requesting HC, never lead with "the team is overwhelmed." Lead with a business case:

**Step 1: Quantify current demand**
"Our team handles X incidents/week averaging Y hours each = Z engineer-hours/week on reactive work."

**Step 2: Quantify toil**
"Toil is 35% of current engineering capacity = A hours/week not available for strategic work."

**Step 3: Quantify backlog**
"We have a reliability improvement backlog of B engineer-weeks that reduces future incident load by C%."

**Step 4: ROI**
"Adding 1 FTE (D annual cost) delivers:
- E hours/week freed from toil via automation
- F% reduction in incident frequency = G engineering-hours saved/year
- Payback period: H months"

**Step 5: Risk of not hiring**
"Without this HC: current team is at N% utilization. Attrition risk is elevated. One departure creates a critical single-expert gap in [domain]."

### FTE vs. Contractor Decision Framework

| Factor | Prefer FTE | Prefer Contractor |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| Duration of need | > 18 months | < 12 months |
| Domain expertise required | Deep, proprietary | Commodity skill |
| Budget flexibility | Stable base | Need variable |
| Recruiting timeline | Can wait 60-90 days | Need in 2-4 weeks |
| Knowledge retention | Critical | Documented/procedural |
| Regulatory/compliance sensitivity | High (PII, security) | Low |

## Concept 3: Cloud Cost Management (FinOps)

### The Three FinOps Phases

| Phase | Focus | Director Role |
|-------|-------|--------------|
| **Inform** | Visibility into spend | Ensure teams have cost dashboards. No surprises. |
| **Optimize** | Eliminate waste | Right-size instances, reserved capacity, delete unused resources |
| **Operate** | Continuous efficiency | Unit economics, cost-per-transaction, chargeback models |

### Unit Economics — The Director's Superpower

Instead of "our cloud bill is $500K/month" (which sounds big and hard to judge), frame as:
- **Cost per message processed**: $0.0003/message (RabbitMQ + K8s overhead)
- **Cost per deployment**: $12 (compute + pipeline time)
- **Cost per on-call hour**: $180 (fully loaded, including opportunity cost)
- **Cost per SLO point**: How much does each 9 of reliability cost to achieve?

Unit economics let you make *proportional* decisions: "Can we reduce cost-per-message by 30% by moving to Kafka on MSK? What's the migration cost vs. 3-year savings?"

### The Cloud Cost Conversation with Finance

Finance sees cloud bills as a cost center problem. Directors reframe as an *investment efficiency* problem:

**Reframe 1**: "Our cloud bill grew 22% but message throughput grew 40% — cost per message went *down* 13%. This is good."

**Reframe 2**: "We're proposing to increase cloud spend by $80K to add reserved capacity. This eliminates $200K of over-provisioning during peak events."

**Reframe 3**: "The cost of our current observability stack ($240K/year) prevented 3 major incidents estimated at $1.8M MTTR cost. 7.5× ROI."

## Concept 4: The Cost of Downtime

### The Formula

```
Cost of downtime = (Revenue at risk per hour)
                 + (Engineering cost to respond)
                 + (Customer recovery cost)
                 + (Reputational/contractual penalty)
```

**For a telecom platform processing 25M messages/day**:
- Revenue at risk: $X/hour (get this from your finance partner — often surprisingly high)
- Engineering cost: (# engineers on incident) × (hours) × (fully-loaded rate ~$150/hr for senior SRE)
- Customer recovery: Potential SLA credits, support case volume
- Reputational: Brand cost (harder to quantify — use "1 SLO violation = X% increase in churn risk")

### Why Directors Need This Number

When you ask for funding to improve reliability, you need to answer: "What is the cost of NOT doing this?"

Example:
- Current state: 4 P1 incidents/quarter averaging 2h each
- Average downtime cost: $180K/incident (revenue + engineering + SLA credits)
- Total annual cost of current reliability: 16 incidents × $180K = $2.88M
- Proposed investment: $400K (HC + tooling) to reduce P1 frequency by 75%
- Expected savings: $2.16M/year
- ROI: 540% year 1

This is the language of a Director making a VP-level business case.

## Concept 5: Budget Presentation Framework

### The One-Page Budget Ask

When presenting to your VP or CFO, use this structure:

**Situation** (1 paragraph): "In FY2026, our platform processed X events with Y% SLA achievement. We managed Z incidents at a total cost of $A."

**Recommendation** (table form):

| Investment | Amount | Expected Return | Payback |
|-----------|--------|----------------|---------|
| +2 FTE SRE | $420K | Reduce toil 40%, enable H2 platform work | 18 months |
| AIOps tooling | $120K | Reduce P1 MTTR by 35%, save 180 eng-hrs/yr | 9 months |
| Observability consolidation | ($80K) savings | Eliminate redundant tools | Immediate |
| **Net ask** | **$460K** | | |

**Risk of not acting** (1-2 bullets):
- Current team at 87% utilization — any departure creates critical coverage gap
- Without toil reduction, H2 roadmap (self-healing platform) has no capacity to execute

**Ask**: "Approve $460K increase to FY2026 OpEx budget."

### The Budget Negotiation Mindset

VPs rarely approve the full ask on first pass. Anticipate:
1. "What's the minimum viable version of this?" → Pre-prepare a Phase 1 (smaller ask that still delivers core value)
2. "Can we do this with contractors?" → Have the FTE vs. contractor tradeoff pre-calculated
3. "Where can you cut to fund this?" → Know your own budget's discretionary vs. non-discretionary split
4. "What happens if we wait until next year?" → Have the opportunity cost/risk of delay articulated

## Concept 6: CapEx vs. OpEx in Cloud Decisions

### Reserved Instances / Savings Plans (the CapEx equivalent in cloud)

Most cloud providers offer significant discounts (30-60%) for committing to compute capacity:

| Commitment | Discount | Risk |
|-----------|---------|------|
| On-demand | 0% | None — pay as you go |
| 1-year reserved | ~30% | Risk: capacity you bought goes unused if workload changes |
| 3-year reserved | ~50-60% | Risk: locked in for 3 years — significant |
| Savings Plan (AWS) | ~20-30% | More flexible than reserved instances |

**Director decision rule**:
- Baseline steady-state load → 1-year reserved or savings plan
- Variable/growth workloads → on-demand or spot instances
- Never commit 100% of compute as reserved — keep 20-30% on-demand buffer

### Presenting to Finance

Finance teams often have CapEx budgets separate from OpEx. Reserved instance purchases sometimes qualify as CapEx (check with your finance partner — accounting treatment varies by organization).

**The play**: If OpEx budget is constrained but CapEx is available, structuring a 3-year compute commitment as CapEx can fund infrastructure you couldn't afford in OpEx.
