# 08 — Executive Communication & Influence

## Why This Is a Skill, Not a Talent

Directors who cannot communicate upward effectively are invisible. They do great work that no one knows about. They make solid decisions that get overridden because they couldn't explain the reasoning. They lose budget battles to less capable Directors who can tell a clearer story.

This module gives you the structural tools — not charisma tricks — to communicate with clarity and influence at VP and above levels.

---

## Tool 1: The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)

### The Core Idea

Most people communicate bottom-up: "Here's the context, here's the data, here's my analysis, here's what it means... therefore here's my recommendation."

Executives don't have time for that. They interrupt after the context slide. They check email. They want the answer first, then the support.

The Pyramid Principle says: **Lead with the conclusion. Then support it.**

```
         ┌─────────────────────┐
         │   RECOMMENDATION    │  ← Say this FIRST
         └────────┬────────────┘
          ┌───────┴────────┐
          │                │
     ┌────┴───┐       ┌────┴───┐
     │ Reason │       │ Reason │  ← Then this
     │   1    │       │   2    │
     └────────┘       └─────┬──┘
                            │
                    ┌───────┴──────┐
                    │ Data/Evidence│  ← Then this (on demand)
                    └──────────────┘
```

### Structuring an Executive Update — The SCQ Framework

**Situation**: The stable facts everyone in the room already agrees on.
**Complication**: The thing that changed or the tension that makes the situation a problem.
**Question**: The question the situation + complication creates.
**Answer**: Your recommendation (this is the Pyramid top).

**Example: Requesting budget for AIOps tooling**

> **Situation**: Our platform processes 25M messages/day with 4 engineers on rotating on-call.
> 
> **Complication**: Alert volume has grown 60% in 6 months due to new services. The alert-to-engineer ratio is now unsustainable — MTTR is trending up despite team skill levels staying constant.
> 
> **Question**: How do we maintain our SLO without scaling headcount linearly?
> 
> **Answer**: I recommend a $120K investment in AIOps tooling that reduces alert noise by an estimated 40%, freeing 6 engineer-hours/week and reducing MTTR by 35%.

**Notice**: You haven't said "I need budget" yet. You've made the business case so compelling that the request feels obvious. *Then* you say the number.

## Tool 2: BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

BLUF comes from the US military intelligence community. Every briefing starts with the most important information first. The detail follows for those who need it.

### The Format

```
BLUF: [What I need you to know / decide / do — in 1-2 sentences]

[Supporting detail for those who want to go deeper]
```

### BLUF in Practice

**Email subject line**: Never "Re: Platform discussion" — always "Decision needed: RabbitMQ upgrade scope by June 30"

**Slack message**: Never 3 paragraphs of context before "so I wanted to ask..." — always "Can you approve the DevSecOps team attending KubeCon? ($3K, direct business value: X). Details below."

**Status update to VP**:

> **BLUF**: We are on track for Q2 SLO. One risk: the Terraform migration timeline is slipping — I need a decision on de-scoping Module 3 by Thursday.
>
> Detail: Platform team completed migration of 60% of infra. Module 3 (networking configs) has a dependency on the Security team's baseline policy review which is delayed 2 weeks. Options: (1) de-scope Module 3 to Q3, (2) bring in contractor to unblock Security team, (3) extend Q2 milestone by 2 weeks. My recommendation: Option 1.

A VP who reads this knows the status, the risk, what they need to do, and when — in 5 seconds.

## Tool 3: The Executive Presentation Structure

### The 3-Act Structure for Director-Level Decks

**Act 1 — Context (1-2 slides max)**
- Where we are. What we agreed in the last conversation.
- Never more than 2 slides. Executives lived through the context. They don't need a history lesson.

**Act 2 — The Problem or Opportunity (2-3 slides)**
- What changed? What's the tension or the opportunity?
- Data-driven. One insight per slide.
- "This chart says one thing and I'm going to tell you exactly what it means for us."

**Act 3 — Recommendation and Ask (1-2 slides)**
- Clear recommendation. Clear ask. Clear timeline.
- What happens if you approve? What happens if you don't?
- What do you need from this room, and by when?

**Total**: 6-8 slides max for a Director-to-VP presentation. If you have 20 slides, you haven't done the work of simplifying.

### The Backup Slide Principle

Anticipate the 3-4 questions most likely to come from your VP or CFO. Prepare backup slides for each. Have them ready — don't put them in the main deck. When the question comes ("What's the cost basis for that estimate?"), pull the backup slide. You look prepared. You don't derail the meeting with a long verbal explanation.

## Tool 4: Managing Up — The Director's Protocols

### The No-Surprise Protocol

The single most important rule in managing your VP relationship: **No surprises.**

Your VP should never hear bad news from someone other than you. Before an incident is escalated to your VP from another leader, they should have heard it from you. Before a missed SLA appears in a business review, you should have flagged it.

**Practical application**: Set a mental threshold. Anything with potential VP visibility → brief them proactively in your weekly sync or a Slack message. The format: "Heads up: [issue]. Here's what we're doing. You don't need to act — I just don't want you surprised."

### The Decision Request Framework

When you need a VP decision:

1. **Frame the decision**: "I need a decision on X."
2. **Give options**: Always at least 2 (never make them choose between "your option" and "the void")
3. **Give your recommendation**: "I recommend Option A because..."
4. **State the deadline**: "I need this by Thursday to keep the project on track."
5. **State the consequence of no decision**: "If we don't decide, the default outcome is Y."

**Never bring a problem without a recommendation.** VPs who consistently get "here's the problem, what should we do?" from a Director start to wonder if the Director is leading or just reporting.

### The Weekly VP Update

A standing async update (Slack or email) that takes 2 minutes to write and prevents 90% of unnecessary meetings:

```
Week of [date]:
✅ [What's going well — 1-2 bullets]
⚠️ [What needs attention — 1-2 bullets with brief context]
🔴 [What's at risk — 1 bullet, what I'm doing about it]
📋 [What I need from you — specific, by when]
```

If the VP has no red items and no asks from them, they stay confident and informed without needing to check in.

## Tool 5: Stakeholder Mapping and Influence Strategy

### The Power/Interest Grid

Map stakeholders on two axes:
- **Power**: Their ability to affect your work (budget, decisions, escalations)
- **Interest**: How much they care about your domain/initiative

```
         POWER
         High
          │
Keep    │ Manage
Satisfied│ Closely
(partner)│ (champion)
         │──────────────── INTEREST
Monitor  │ Keep
(low    │ Informed
effort) │
         Low
```

**Manage Closely** (High Power, High Interest): These are your champions and your threats. VP of Engineering who cares about your platform. Meet regularly. Share information early. Build relationship intentionally.

**Keep Satisfied** (High Power, Low Interest): The CFO who controls budget but doesn't care about RabbitMQ. Don't bore them with detail. Give them what they need to stay comfortable: cost stability, ROI, risk-managed decisions.

**Keep Informed** (Low Power, High Interest): Product managers, architects who care. Update them regularly. They become informal advocates.

**Monitor** (Low Power, Low Interest): Periodic check-ins. Don't over-invest.

### The Influence Strategy

For each "Manage Closely" stakeholder:

1. **What do they care about?** (Their OKRs, their political pressures, their career trajectory)
2. **What's my value to them?** (What do I give them that helps with what they care about?)
3. **What do I need from them?** (Budget approval, resource allocation, air cover)
4. **What's the relationship health?** (Trust, frequency, history)
5. **What's my engagement plan?** (How often, what channel, what I'll share)

## Tool 6: The Cross-Functional Alignment Meeting

Directors run cross-functional alignment. This is not a status update — it's a governance checkpoint for shared work.

### The Meeting Structure (60 min, bi-weekly for major programs)

**10 min — State of the program**: BLUF from the program lead. RAID updates. Key metrics.

**20 min — Decision items**: 1-2 decisions that require cross-team agreement. Use the Decision Request Framework. Make a decision in the room. Document it.

**20 min — Risk and dependency review**: Walk the RAID log. Flag any items that crossed threshold since last meeting. Assign owners.

**10 min — Commitments**: Each team lead states one commitment for the next two weeks. Captured visibly.

**Anti-pattern**: 60-minute alignment meetings that are entirely status updates. If there's no decision made and no commitment taken, you just had an expensive read-aloud of a slide deck.

## Tool 7: The Communication Style Adaptation Model (DISC Lite)

Directors adapt their communication style to their audience. Not inauthentically — but strategically.

### Four Communication Profiles

| Type | Values | Under Pressure | Communicate by... |
|------|--------|---------------|------------------|
| **D — Driver** | Results, speed, control | Becomes dictatorial, impatient | Lead with bottom line. Be direct. No small talk. Give options. |
| **I — Influencer** | Recognition, enthusiasm, people | Becomes impulsive, talks over | Acknowledge their ideas. Be energetic. Connect to vision and people. |
| **S — Steady** | Stability, process, harmony | Becomes passive-resistant | Be patient. Provide structure. Don't ambush with change. |
| **C — Conscientious** | Accuracy, quality, logic | Becomes withdrawn, over-analytical | Provide data. Don't rush. Acknowledge their expertise. |

**Your VP profile**: Learn it. If your VP is a D (driver), don't bring them a 20-slide deck with historical context — bring them a 3-slide recommendation with the answer first. If your VP is a C (conscientious), bring the data. Both styles, wrong room = friction.
