# 10 — Cross-Functional Leadership

## Why This Is Hard — and Why It Matters

Cross-functional leadership is leadership without authority. You need outcomes from teams you don't manage, from leaders who have their own priorities and their own VPs. This is where many technically strong Senior Managers fail to make the Director leap — not because they lack domain knowledge, but because they don't know how to operate in a matrix organization.

---

## Concept 1: The Matrix Organization

### What It Is

A matrix org is one where people belong to both a **functional group** (SRE, Engineering, Security, Data) *and* a **product/delivery unit** (Consumer Platform, B2B, Network). People have two chains of authority: their functional manager and their delivery lead.

**T-Mobile equivalent**: An SRE embedded in the DND Platform team has a dotted line to the Platform Product VP and a solid line to the SRE Director. Both parties have legitimate claims on their time.

### The Director's Role in a Matrix

You are the **functional authority** — you set standards, growth paths, tooling, culture for your function across all embedded assignments. The delivery team sets the priorities.

**The tension you manage daily**: "Our SRE in Team X wants to spend 40% on reliability improvements. Team X's Product VP wants them at 80% on feature delivery." Your job is to negotiate the right balance — and to set the baseline policy (e.g., "embedded SREs maintain no less than 30% time on reliability engineering — this is non-negotiable") that protects your function's health across the org.

### The Dotted-Line Negotiation

When an SRE is embedded, negotiate a "dotted-line charter" with the delivery team lead:
- What percentage of the SRE's time is allocated to reliability work vs. feature delivery?
- Who has authority to assign P1 incident response (answer: the SRE Director's escalation path always wins)?
- What reporting does the SRE provide back to the SRE function?
- What are the SRE's growth criteria — who evaluates them and how?

Document this. Verbal agreements decay.

## Concept 2: The Stakeholder Influence Cycle

For any cross-functional initiative, influence follows a cycle. Skip a step and it collapses.

### The 5-Step Cycle

**1. Understand their world first**

Before you ask for anything, understand what your cross-functional partner is dealing with. Their OKRs. Their pressures. Their wins. Their risks. If you walk into a conversation about what *you* need without showing you understand what *they* need — you're starting in a deficit.

"I know you're in the middle of your Q2 delivery crunch. I want to understand what the reliability risks look like from your perspective before we talk about what I'm proposing."

**2. Find the shared interest**

There's always a shared interest — even between teams that seem in conflict. Security and DevOps both care about stability. Finance and Engineering both care about ROI. Find that intersection and anchor there.

"You care about deployment speed. I care about deployment reliability. Those aren't in conflict — they're both solved by automated, tested deployment pipelines. That's actually the same problem."

**3. Co-create the solution**

Don't arrive with a fully-formed proposal. Arrive with a framing and invite them to build it with you. People support what they help create.

"I have a rough idea for how we could solve the shared monitoring gap between our teams. Would you be willing to spend 45 minutes co-designing this? I want your fingerprints on the solution."

**4. Small commitments → big commitments**

Cialdini's commitment principle: people are more likely to agree to large requests if they've already made small ones. Get a small yes (attend a working session, review a doc, pilot a tool) before asking for a large yes (fund a program, provide headcount, change their team's process).

**5. Deliver and acknowledge**

When the collaboration produces a win, attribute it generously. "The DevSecOps team built the security gate that made this zero-trust deployment possible — this is their win as much as ours." Future collaboration becomes easier after public acknowledgement.

## Concept 3: Managing Vendor Relationships at Director Level

### The Director-Vendor Dynamic

SMs interact with vendor technical contacts. Directors interact with vendor account executives and sometimes executives. The dynamic is different — and the leverage is different.

### Vendor Relationship Tiers

| Tier | Type | Director Engagement |
|------|------|-------------------|
| **Strategic Partner** | Core to your platform. Switching is a major event. | Executive relationship. QBR participation. Product roadmap input. |
| **Preferred Vendor** | Significant but switchable with effort. | Account Manager relationship. Annual contract review. SLA monitoring. |
| **Transactional** | Commodity, easy to replace. | Procurement-led. Price negotiation. No executive relationship needed. |

**T-Mobile examples**:
- Splunk: Strategic — switching is a multi-year migration. You need an executive relationship and roadmap influence.
- PagerDuty: Preferred — significant but alternatives exist. Annual renegotiation.
- Monitoring tools: Transactional — commoditized, price-competitive.

### The Vendor QBR (Quarterly Business Review)

For strategic vendors, drive a QBR every quarter. Your agenda:

1. **Usage and value delivered**: Are you getting the value you contracted for? Usage data.
2. **SLA performance**: Did they meet SLAs? Document any misses for contract leverage.
3. **Roadmap alignment**: What are they building? How does it align with your H2/H3 needs?
4. **Your roadmap**: What are you building that might require their support?
5. **Commercial discussion**: Are you approaching renewal? Now is when to negotiate, not at renewal.
6. **Escalation resolution**: Any unresolved issues? Get their executive's attention in the room.

### Vendor Contract Negotiation Principles

**Know your leverage before you negotiate**:
- Renewal = leverage (they want to keep you)
- Multi-year = you give something (lower rate), they give something (stability, features)
- Volume commitment = discount, but verify the volume is real before committing
- Competitive alternative = biggest leverage. Even if you'd never switch, knowing the alternative price is power.

**Director-level negotiation moves**:
- Lead with relationship: "We've been partners for X years and we want to stay. Help us make the renewal easy to justify internally."
- Introduce a champion: "Our VP has the final say on any contract above $500K. I need you to help me make this an easy yes for them."
- Bundle and commit: "If we commit to a 3-year term, what can you do on the total cost?"
- Escalate for stuck negotiations: "I appreciate the effort, but we're not getting there on price. Can we involve your Account Director?"

## Concept 4: Leading Programs Across Teams

### The Program vs. Project Distinction

**Project**: Defined scope, defined deliverable, defined end date. One team, one manager.

**Program**: A collection of related projects that together deliver a strategic outcome. Multiple teams, multiple managers, cross-functional dependencies, longer horizon.

Directors lead programs, not individual projects.

### The Program Governance Model

| Element | What It Is | Who Owns It |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| **Program Charter** | Scope, goals, success criteria, stakeholders | Director (you) |
| **Steering Committee** | Executive-level oversight. Major decisions and escalations. | VP + cross-functional peers |
| **Working Group** | Cross-team operational delivery team | Program Manager + tech leads |
| **RAID Log** | Active risk/issue management | Program Manager |
| **Status Report** | Weekly health update to Steering | Program Manager → Director review |

### The Program Status Report (Traffic Light Format)

```
Program: [Name]
Week: [Date]
Program Manager: [Name]  Director: [Name]

Overall Status: 🟡 AT RISK

Schedule:         🟢 On Track

KEY ACCOMPLISHMENTS THIS WEEK:
- Completed EKS cluster migration for Teams A and B
- DevSecOps gate integrated for 80% of pipelines

KEY RISKS/ISSUES:
- [R01] Security baseline delay: Decision needed by [date]. Owner: [Director]
- [I02] Splunk capacity: Emergency license purchased. Monitor cost impact.

- Approve Module 3 de-scope to Q3 — Owner: [VP], needed by: [Thursday]

- Complete Module 2 final testing
- Finalize vendor contract for AIOps tooling
```

## Concept 5: The Director's Cross-Functional Credibility Stack

Cross-functional influence is built on credibility — and credibility is built over time through specific behaviors:

| Credibility Dimension | What It Means | How to Build It |
|----------------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Technical credibility** | Peer leaders know you understand the domain | Share informed technical views in cross-functional forums. Don't be the one who defers on everything. |
| **Delivery credibility** | You do what you say, when you say | Keep commitments. When you can't, communicate early. |
| **Judgment credibility** | You make good calls under uncertainty | Articulate your reasoning when you make decisions. Be right more than wrong. |
| **People credibility** | You're known as someone who develops others | Be seen investing in your managers. Speak well of your team publicly. |
| **Domain credibility** | You're the go-to person on your topic | Publish internal articles, run lunch-and-learns, present at all-hands on your domain. |

**The new Director trap**: Arriving at a new org and trying to demonstrate credibility by making changes quickly. Credibility in a new role comes from listening first, delivering small wins, and earning trust — not from showing how much you know in week 2.

## Concept 6: Conflict Resolution Between Teams

### The Conflict Escalation Ladder

Conflicts between teams should be resolved at the lowest possible level. As a Director, you intervene only when the team-to-team resolution has failed.

| Level | Who resolves | When |
|-------|-------------|------|
| 1 | ICs from each team | First attempt — within a sprint |
| 2 | Managers from each team | If ICs can't align — within a week |
| 3 | Directors from each domain | If managers can't align — within 2 weeks |
| 4 | VP (or mutual VP) | If Directors can't align — with full context and proposed resolution |

**Director role in Level 3 resolution**:
1. Hear both sides separately first. "Tell me the situation from your team's perspective."
2. Find the shared interest: "What outcome do both teams actually agree on?"
3. Propose a resolution that acknowledges both constraints.
4. Make a decision if needed — don't defer indefinitely. Unresolved conflict costs more than an imperfect decision.
5. Document the resolution. RAID it. Prevent recurrence.

**The worst pattern**: Directors who avoid cross-functional conflict by deferring everything to the VP. This costs political capital, trains your teams to escalate instead of resolve, and signals to your VP that you're not operating at Director level.
