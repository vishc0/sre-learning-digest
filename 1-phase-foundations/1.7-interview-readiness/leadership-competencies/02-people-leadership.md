# People Leadership: Managing Every Type of Person | Director Leadership Track

---

## Why This Separates Directors from ICs (What Interviewers Are Actually Testing)

Directors are not judged on their own output — they are judged on whether they can make a team of diverse humans produce at a level none of those humans would achieve alone. Interviewers probe this by asking about specific people: the difficult one, the quiet one, the one who left. They are testing whether you have frameworks or just instincts, whether you stay in the manager role or collapse back into IC under pressure, and whether you can distinguish between a people problem and a systems problem. The answer "I had a great team" disqualifies you. The answer "Here is exactly how I diagnosed and intervened in three specific situations" gets you the offer.

---

## The Mental Model: The Four Levers

Every people challenge you will face as a Director reduces to one or more of four levers being broken:

**CRED: Clarity, Relationship, Environment, Diagnosis**

- **Clarity**: Does the person know exactly what winning looks like — in their role, on this team, for their career?
- **Relationship**: Do they believe you are on their side? Trust precedes change.
- **Environment**: Is the org, the process, or the structure making the desired behavior impossible or irrational?
- **Diagnosis**: Have you correctly identified root cause — or are you treating a symptom?

When an intervention fails, one of these four was missing. Before every difficult conversation, run this check. The conversation that follows will be completely different depending on which lever is broken.

---

## The Framework in Practice

**Before any difficult people conversation:**

1. Name the behavior — specific, observable, timestamped
2. Name the impact — on the team, on the platform, on the org
3. Run CRED: which lever is likely broken?
4. Choose the intervention level: structural change, direct feedback, escalation, or exit path
5. Prepare the opening sentence — this is the single most important preparation step

**The escalation model:**

```
Level 1: Structural — fix the environment before blaming the person
Level 2: Direct conversation — SBI + So What, no hedging
Level 3: Named pattern conversation — "This is the third time"
Level 4: Stakes conversation — "Here is what happens if this continues"
Level 5: Formal process — PIP, documented, HR looped in
Level 6: Separation
```

Most managers live at Level 1 or oscillate between Level 1 and Level 6 without going through Levels 2-5. That oscillation is what creates wrongful termination exposure and team trust damage.

---

## What Good Looks Like at Director Level

- You can name the root cause of each person's dysfunction before prescribing the intervention
- You give feedback within 48 hours of the incident, not at the next scheduled 1:1
- Your team brings you bad news early because they have learned your first response is "tell me more" not "who's fault is this"
- Your high performers feel challenged and protected simultaneously
- Your underperformers know where they stand — you have not left anyone in ambiguity about their standing
- You have a succession plan for every critical capability on your team and can show it on a slide
- When someone leaves, you know exactly why — because you asked

---

## What Bad Looks Like (How Directors Fail)

- **The avoider**: Lets performance drift for 6+ months, then tries to PIP someone who has no documented history — legally exposed, team demoralized
- **The overrider**: Cannot tolerate disagreement from their technical leads; drives the capable ones out
- **The rescuer**: Creates learned helplessness by solving every problem instead of coaching through it
- **The universalizer**: Uses the same management style on every person — extroverted feedback with introverts, collaborative style with rogues
- **The credit-hoarder**: Takes visibility from reports in exec meetings — the fastest way to lose your best people
- **The delayed truth-teller**: Waits until annual review to give feedback that should have been given in real time — surprises in annual reviews are a management failure, not a performance failure

## The Rigid Person: Won't Change, Argues Every Decision

### Diagnosis First — Three Root Causes, Three Interventions

Rigidity is not one thing. Get the root cause wrong and the intervention backfires.

| Root Cause | Observable Signal | Correct Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Prior trauma** — "I've seen this fail" | Arguments come with historical references, specific incidents | Extract the legitimate lesson; separate the lesson from the battle |
| **Low psychological safety** | Argues with you, compliant with peers; or argues privately, never publicly | Create safety explicitly; separate pattern from person |
| **Genuine expertise + poor framing** | Technically right, organizationally wrong; frustrates peers | Teach advocacy mechanics, not compliance |
| **Entitlement** | Arguments are about status, not substance; position shifts when you push back | This is a fit/values conversation, not a skills conversation |

**The diagnostic question to ask in the 1:1:**

> "Help me understand what outcome you're protecting against when you push back on this direction."

If they give you a real answer — specific risk, specific historical failure — there is signal in their objection. Engage it seriously. If they can't articulate a concrete outcome, the rigidity is about control or status, not substance. The distinction determines everything that follows.

### The Conversation That Works — Separating Concern from Resistance

**Opening:**

> "I want to understand something before I respond to your pushback. Are you seeing a risk that I'm not seeing — something specific that will go wrong if we go this direction? Or is this a preference difference — you'd do it differently, but you're not predicting a specific failure? I'm asking because those are two completely different conversations and I want to have the right one."

This question does two things: it forces them to be specific, and it legitimizes the distinction between substantive disagreement and stylistic preference. Vague resistance is harder to sustain when you demand specificity.

**If they give you a real risk:**

> "That's a legitimate concern. Walk me through the failure scenario in detail — timeline, blast radius, detectability. I want to understand whether this is a showstopper or something we can mitigate. If it's real, I want to change the plan. If we can build a mitigation, I want to do that and proceed."

Then genuinely engage the risk. If you dismiss it, you've confirmed their belief that you don't actually want input.

**If the risk doesn't hold up under scrutiny:**

> "I've gone through the failure scenario you described. Here's where I think it doesn't hold — [specific rebuttal]. I'm not dismissing the instinct, but I'm not seeing the specific failure path. I'm going to move forward. I need you to execute on this even though you'd have decided differently. Can you do that?"

**If you've heard the objection and are moving forward anyway:**

> "I've heard your concern. I understand it. I'm going to overrule it this time — here's why: [one specific reason, not a list]. I want you to hold me accountable: if this goes the way you predicted, come back and tell me directly. I'll want to learn from it. But we're moving. I need you with me on execution."

### Escalation Ladder When the Pattern Continues

**First recurrence:**

> "This is the second time we've had this pattern. I made a decision, you objected, I explained my reasoning, we moved forward — and you've continued to surface objections in execution. I need to name what I'm seeing: disagreement in design is healthy. Continuing to re-litigate after we've decided is a different thing. Which one is this?"

**Third recurrence — the pattern conversation:**

> "We've disagreed on four of the last six decisions. That's a pattern I have to take seriously. I'm not asking you to become a yes-person — I'm asking you to tell me whether you trust my judgment enough to execute on decisions you disagree with. Because if the answer is no, that's a real problem. Not a you're-in-trouble problem yet — but a real problem that I can't let sit. What's actually going on here?"

This opens the door to what is often the real issue: a trust breakdown, a past decision that went wrong and was never processed, or genuine misalignment on direction. Listen to what comes back.

**When to stop trying and make the decision:**

The rule is: unlimited disagreement before the decision, zero tolerance for sabotage after the decision. Disagreement that becomes work-to-rule, slow-rolling, or hallway-lobbying against the decision crosses a line. When you see that pattern:

> "I want to be explicit. We've moved past the decision phase. What I'm seeing now — [specific behavior] — is not disagreement, it's interference with execution. I can't have that. If you disagree with the direction of this team at a fundamental level, the right move is to tell me directly and we talk about whether this is still the right place for you. What I can't have is passive resistance. I need a direct answer: are you in on this or not?"

## The Timid Person: Capable, Won't Speak Up

### Diagnosis — Three Different Problems, Three Different Interventions

Do not treat timidity as a personality trait. In capable people, it is almost always an environmental response.

| Root Cause | Observable Signal | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Previous environment punished mistakes** | Speaks confidently in 1:1s, shuts down in groups; hedges everything publicly | Safety-building; model your own mistakes publicly |
| **Cultural background** | Deferential to hierarchy; disagrees privately, never in the room | Structural formats that don't require public challenge; explicit invitation |
| **Impostor syndrome** | Diminishes their own contributions ("this is probably obvious but..."); defers to others even when they know more | Attribution — name their expertise publicly and specifically |
| **Past dismissal** | Has tried and been talked over; stopped trying | Create a moment where their idea wins, visibly |

**The diagnostic question in 1:1:**

> "I hear strong opinions from you here. I almost never hear them in the room. I want to understand what's making that hard — not to push you, but because your career depends on people seeing your thinking and I want to help. What's actually getting in the way?"

Listen for the specific answer. "I don't want to say something wrong" is impostor syndrome. "I've tried and people talk over me" is an environmental problem you can fix structurally. "It doesn't feel like my place" is cultural background requiring a different approach.

### The Question That Draws Out Quiet People in Meetings

The worst question: "Does anyone have thoughts?" They will not raise their hand.

The best question: **"Before we move on — [name], you've been closest to [specific thing]. What's your read on this?"**

Specificity is the key. It signals: your opinion is expected, it's legitimate, and I've already established it has value by referencing what you know. This is not putting someone on the spot — it is a prepared invitation. The difference is whether you've given them signal in advance that the question is coming.

**The pre-seeding protocol:**

Before any meeting where you want them to speak, send a message 2-3 hours before:

> "In today's design review I'm going to ask you about the on-call burden on the alerting stack. Think through your take — I want to hear it in the room."

This converts a high-stakes public moment into execution of a prepared plan. The psychological experience is completely different.

### The 90-Day 1:1 Confidence Build

**Month 1 — Rehearsal:**
Use the first 10 minutes of every 1:1 to rehearse something they are going to say publicly. "Tell me your read on the incident before you write it up." "Walk me through the architecture decision you're going to present next week." Make the 1:1 a practice space, not just a status report.

**Month 2 — Attribution:**
In every meeting where their work comes up, name them. Not "the team did X" — "[Name] built the detection framework that caught this." Do this consistently and publicly. Make their expertise visible to the people they are afraid to speak in front of.

**Month 3 — Expand the invitation:**
Ask them to lead a specific agenda item in a team meeting. Frame it not as exposure therapy but as recognition: "You know this best. I want the team to hear it from you." Give feedback after privately — what landed, what to strengthen.

**The direct conversation at the 30-day mark:**

> "I want to be straight with you about something. You have sharp thinking and I see it in our 1:1s. I almost never see it in the room. That gap is costing you — not in terms of performance right now, but in terms of how senior people perceive your potential. I'm not going to just tell you to speak up more, because I know that's not useful. What I'm going to do is create specific openings and tell you in advance when they're coming. Your job is to take them when I create them. Does that work?"

## The Rogue: Good Work, Ignores Process, Creates Chaos

### Why Rogues Exist — The Systems Diagnosis First

Before you intervene on a person, ask: is the process they're bypassing actually good? Rogues are often efficiency-hunters operating in environments where the process is genuinely broken, slow, or theater. If you crack down without examining the process, you suppress the signal and keep the dysfunction.

Ask your team in a 1:1: "Is there a reason someone would skip this process other than laziness or arrogance?" If the honest answer is "the process takes 3 days and doesn't catch anything," fix the process first. Then the behavior becomes unjustifiable.

**Diagram the blast radius, not just the violation:**

The problem with rogues is not the process skip. It is the asymmetric risk: their shortcut works 19 times and on the 20th it takes down production at 2am on a Saturday — and the blast radius extends to the whole team, your SLA, and your reputation. That's the conversation to have, not a lecture on compliance.

### The First Conversation

> "I want to name something directly. The work you produce is strong — I'm not here about output quality. The way you get to the work is creating risk that I have to carry and that the team has to carry. The last [specific incident] — if that had gone wrong at 2am on a Friday, it wouldn't just be your problem. Walk me through your thinking on why you skipped [specific process]."

Then genuinely listen. If they tell you the process is broken, say: "That's useful. If the process is broken, I want to fix it — and I want you to help me do that. But the way to fix a broken process is not to bypass it unilaterally; it's to tell me it's broken. Can we agree on that?"

If they say "I forgot" or "it was faster": "I hear that. Here's what I need you to understand about why that can't be my problem to carry. [Specific blast radius scenario]. I'm not asking you to be slower. I'm asking you to tell me when you're about to take a shortcut so I can make an informed call about the risk."

### The Deal: Autonomy in Exchange for Transparency

This is the offer you make to rogues who are worth keeping:

> "Here's the deal I want to make with you. I'm not going to manage your methods if you give me visibility into your decisions. If you're about to do something outside the standard path, tell me — 30 seconds, a Slack message, whatever. Tell me what you're doing and why. I'll either say 'go ahead' or 'wait, let's talk.' What I cannot have is finding out after the fact when the blast radius has already happened. Can you commit to that?"

Most rogues will accept this. It preserves their autonomy and self-image while giving you the oversight you need.

### Containment Without Destroying Capability

**Targeted autonomy — explicit scope:**

> "You have full autonomy within your dev environment and your individual service boundary. Outside that — anything that touches shared infrastructure, shared state, or prod — requires a check-in before, not after. That's the line. I'm not drawing it because I don't trust your judgment. I'm drawing it because the blast radius of those environments extends past you."

**Turn them into the process owner:**

> "You've told me the change review process is broken. I believe you. Here's what I want to do: I'm giving you 90 days and the authority to redesign it. You own the outcome. If the new process is better, you'll follow it because you built it — and so will everyone else. I'll fund your time on it. What do you need to start?"

This is not a trap or a trick. It is genuine. It channels the instinct productively and typically produces real process improvement.

### When Rogue Becomes a Liability You Cannot Carry

The line is a production incident caused by a known process bypass where the person was explicitly told not to bypass it.

> "What happened last [date] was a production incident that was directly caused by bypassing the process we had an explicit conversation about. I need you to understand that I cannot continue to absorb that risk. We are at the point where if this happens again, I cannot protect the employment relationship. I'm not saying this to threaten you — I'm saying it because you deserve to know exactly where we stand. What's going to be different?"

If it happens again after that conversation, the separation decision is defensible, documented, and honestly given fair warning.

## The Disengaged Person: Was Great, Now Coasting

### The CAMP Diagnostic Model

Before any intervention, run this diagnostic:

| Factor | Diagnostic Question | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Do they know what winning looks like right now, in this role, in this season? | Vague on goals, doesn't self-report progress |
| **Autonomy** | Are they being micromanaged, blocked, or told how to do work they mastered years ago? | Disengages in meetings where direction is handed down |
| **Meaning** | Do they believe the work matters? Have they lost the connection between their work and real impact? | Goes through motions; stopped suggesting improvements |
| **Progress** | Are they learning anything? Is there growth available here? | Stopped asking for feedback; has mastered everything in their current scope |

Engagement drops when one or more CAMP factors breaks. The intervention is completely different depending on which factor is failing. Do not skip the diagnosis.

### The Root Cause Conversation

> "I want to check in at a different level than we usually do. On a scale of 1 to 10, how energized are you by the work right now? I'm not asking about performance — I know you're capable. I'm asking whether you're actually in it."

Whatever number they give, ask: "What would make it a [current number + 2]?"

This forces them to articulate the gap and surfaces what they actually need. Their answer tells you which CAMP factor is broken.

### Re-Engagement Scripts by Root Cause

**If the answer is Clarity:**

> "I think part of what's happening is that the target has been unclear. Let me fix that. In the next 90 days, I need you to own [specific outcome] end to end. Here's what done looks like: [specific, measurable]. That's your north star. Everything else is secondary."

**If the answer is Autonomy:**

> "I want to check in on something. Am I or the process getting in the way of you doing your best work? I'm asking directly because I'd rather hear it from you than watch you get smaller over time. What would you need to operate with more ownership?"

**If the answer is Meaning:**

> "I think you've lost the thread between what you do daily and why it matters. Let me pull it back up. [Specific impact of their work — specific customer outcomes, team dependencies, business value]. You're the reason [specific thing] works. I'm not sure that's been said clearly enough."

**If the answer is Progress:**

> "Your skills have outgrown this scope. I know it. I think you know it. Here's what I can offer: [specific stretch opportunity, new problem, adjacent scope]. The alternative is I watch you get more disengaged and eventually lose you. I'd rather have the harder conversation now. Is there a path here that re-engages you, or are we past that?"

**If there's an outside pull (competing offer, life event):**

> "Is there anything outside work that I should factor in to what you need right now? You don't have to tell me — but if there is, it might change what I do. I'm asking because I want to support you, not because I need information."

### When Re-Engagement Has Failed

You will know re-engagement has failed when: the stretch opportunity produced no change in energy, the direct conversation produced short-term improvement followed by regression, and the CAMP diagnosis found no fixable factor.

At that point:

> "I want to have an honest conversation. I've tried a few things to re-engage you here — [specific things you tried]. I haven't seen a sustained change. I'm not going to pretend I'm not noticing, and I don't think it's fair to you to let this continue without naming it. I think the honest question is: is this still the right place for you? Not as a threat — as a real question. What's your read?"

This opens the exit conversation with dignity intact. Most disengaged employees know the answer to that question. Giving them permission to say it out loud is often a relief.

## The Person Who Won't Listen: Hears Feedback, Doesn't Change

### Why People Don't Change After Feedback — The Real List

1. They don't believe you'll actually do anything (test this with explicit stakes language)
2. They don't know HOW to change — they know what to change but not the mechanics
3. They disagree it's a problem (this is a values/fit issue, not a skills issue)
4. The feedback was not specific enough to act on (examine your own delivery)
5. The environment immediately after the feedback failed to reinforce the change

Most managers who complain that someone "doesn't take feedback" have delivered feedback that fails criteria 4, never tested criteria 1, and never diagnosed criteria 2.

### The Feedback Model Directors Use: SBI + So What

Not the sandwich. The sandwich teaches people to discount the opening positive and brace for the negative. It teaches you to soften necessary messages.

**SBI + So What:**

- **Situation**: Specific, observable, dated. No editorializing.
- **Behavior**: What they did. Not their character. Not their intent.
- **Impact**: What actually happened as a result — on the team, on the output, on the relationship.
- **So What**: What you need to be different, and why it matters at the level of team or org function — not just your preference.

**Example:**

> "In the incident review last Tuesday [Situation], you interrupted the on-call engineer twice and dismissed their root cause analysis before they'd finished explaining it [Behavior]. Two things happened: they stopped contributing for the rest of the session, and two other engineers told me afterward they will not speak up in incident reviews if that's how it goes [Impact]. I need interruption-free analysis space in that room — not as a courtesy norm, but because our incident quality degrades when people shut down. I need this to change [So What]."

### Documenting Feedback in a Way That Protects You

After every Level 2+ feedback conversation (see escalation ladder above), send this:

> "Per our conversation today, I wanted to document what we discussed: [specific behavior], [specific impact], [what I asked you to do differently]. Let me know if I've captured it wrong."

This email serves three functions: it closes the loop, it gives them a chance to dispute the record (which is healthy), and it creates legal documentation without feeling like a formal process.

**Do not wait until it matters to start documenting.** By the time you are considering a PIP, you should have 3+ documented instances. If you don't, you were at Level 1 too long.

### The Escalation Ladder for Repeated Non-Change

**Level 2 — Explicit behavioral feedback:**

> "In [specific situation], you [specific behavior]. The impact was [specific outcome]. That is the second time I have seen this pattern in 30 days. I need this to change. Here is specifically what different looks like: [behavioral description]."

**Level 3 — Named pattern + stakes:**

> "I've given you feedback on [behavior] three times. I've been specific each time. I've documented it. I'm still seeing it. I need to understand what's getting in the way — do you understand why this matters, and do you know how to change it? Because if you do and you're choosing not to — that's a different conversation than if you don't know how."

Then: "I want you to hear something clearly. If this pattern continues, it is going to affect your standing here. I'm not saying that to frighten you — I'm saying it because you deserve to know what's at stake before we get to a formal process."

**Level 4 — The pre-PIP conversation:**

> "We are in a different conversation now. I've been clear about this behavior multiple times, I've documented those conversations, and the pattern has continued. Here's where we are: if I don't see concrete change in the next 30 days, I have to initiate a formal performance process. That is not what I want. What I want is to hear from you specifically — not generally — what is going to be different starting tomorrow."

Ask them to send you a follow-up email after the conversation with their commitment. This is not a gotcha — it gives them ownership and gives you a record.

**When the PIP becomes necessary:** Run it as described in the Tools and Templates section below.

## Performance and Promotion

### The Criteria Directors Actually Use for Promotion Decisions

HR criteria: demonstrated performance at the next level, technical skills, cross-functional impact. Those are the official criteria. Here is what Directors actually decide on:

1. **Can I give them this title without undermining my own credibility?** The title is a public signal to the rest of the team. If your team knows someone is not performing at that level and you promote them anyway, you damage your judgment and demoralize everyone who was actually operating at that level.

2. **Scope readiness, not just skill readiness.** Can they own a problem end-to-end — including the ambiguous, uncomfortable parts? Technical skill is necessary, not sufficient. The question is: can they function at the next level of autonomy?

3. **Calibration against external bar.** The question to ask yourself privately: "If I were hiring for this role externally today, would this person get through the interview?" If the honest answer is no, do not promote.

4. **Team multiplier effect.** Individual contributors get promoted on personal output. Senior ICs and above get promoted on how much they raise the floor around them.

5. **The retention test.** Am I promoting this person because they're ready, or because they have an offer and I'm matching? If it's the second, the rest of your team will eventually know, and it tells everyone that you need an offer to advance. Be honest with yourself.

**On equity:** Women and underrepresented engineers are statistically promoted on proven past performance; majority-group peers get promoted on demonstrated potential. The bar is applied asymmetrically. Catch yourself in calibration sessions when the language shifts from evidence to instinct.

### How to Advocate in a Calibration Meeting

Come in with a story, not just a rating.

**Do not say:** "She's performing well at senior level."

**Say:** "In the last two quarters, she owned the SLO redesign end to end — got buy-in from three product teams without escalating to me once, reduced page rate by 40%, and trained two junior engineers on the methodology. That's scope, judgment, and team multiplier. I'm promoting her to Staff."

When someone challenges with "I'm not sure she's ready": ask them to name a specific gap. "What would she need to show you?" Vague impressions are not a basis for a hold decision. Force specificity.

When you disagree with a hold decision: "I want to record that I'm advocating for this promotion and I disagree with the hold. What is the specific, measurable criterion that needs to be met, and in what timeframe? I want to be able to tell her exactly what she needs to do next."

### The Conversation When Someone Is Not Getting Promoted

> "I want to give you a direct answer rather than let you find out another way. I'm not putting you up for promotion this cycle. Here's specifically what that's based on: [three concrete examples — not traits, behaviors]. Here's what ready looks like: [specific, measurable description of what different looks like]. I want to make a commitment to you: if you close these gaps, I will go into the calibration meeting with a strong case. This is not me giving up on you — it is me being direct about what needs to be different. What questions do you have?"

Do not soften the "not this cycle" into ambiguity. People hear ambiguity as "maybe if I wait." Be clear: this cycle, no. Next cycle, possible if specific things change.

## Managing Offshore and Distributed Teams

### What Specifically Changes With Offshore

| Factor | Co-located | Distributed |
|---|---|---|
| Trust | Accrues through visible presence, hallway conversations | Must be manufactured through structured touchpoints — it does not accrue passively |
| Miscommunication | Corrected informally and quickly | Compounds over async cycles; shows up as "misalignment" that feels like a people problem but is a communication structure problem |
| Career visibility | Natural — they're in the room | Requires active sponsorship; out of sight is out of mind in promo cycles |
| Accountability | Social pressure is ambient | Must be explicit, documented, public within team |
| Feedback delivery | Informal correction is available | Everything must be deliberate and more frequent |

What does not change: the feedback model, the performance management standard, the promotion criteria. If you apply a lower standard because someone is offshore, you are managing two teams with two cultures, and the offshore team will know it.

### Time Zone Management That Doesn't Burn People Out

**The principle:** You cannot ask people to permanently live in overlap hours. You can ask them to share the sacrifice.

**Structure:**
- Define a protected overlap window — the minimum intersection of both time zones. This is the sacred synchronous window. Use it only for decisions, design reviews, and relationship-building. Never for status updates.
- Rotate who takes the off-hours burden. If the India team takes 7:30am calls daily to meet US time, you should have US team members taking 7:30am calls for India-led reviews on a rotating basis. Unilateral time zone burden is a structural power imbalance that drives attrition.
- Document asynchronously by default. Every decision made in a synchronous call gets a written summary within 24 hours. This is not optional — writing is the communication, not a record of it.

**The meeting that kills offshore engagement:** long US-timezone calls where offshore participants are observers for 50 of 60 minutes and then asked "any questions?" at the end. Redesign those meetings.

### Building Trust With People You Rarely See

**The trust deficit with offshore is real and structural.** You fix it by being more visible and more explicit about the relationship, not by hoping it develops over time.

1. **Individual 1:1s with offshore leads — weekly, video, non-negotiable.** The offshore lead needs to know you as a person, not just a manager. Ask about their work, their career, their concerns. Do not let these 1:1s be dominated by status updates.

2. **Rotate who presents to leadership.** If the US team always presents the work to VP-level audiences, you have created a permanent visibility gap. Make offshore leads present their own work upstream. Prepare them for it.

3. **Name them in executive forums.** When offshore work is recognized, use their name — not "the team" or "India."

4. **Onboarding script for accountability with offshore team members:**

> "I want to be explicit about how I work with everyone on my team, regardless of where they are. I will give you direct feedback — not filtered, not softened. I'll do it in a way that respects you, but I won't hold back because of distance. I expect the same: if something here isn't working, I want to hear it from you directly. Distance is not a reason to be indirect. Does that work for you?"

This conversation, had once clearly, sets the tone for everything that follows.

## Tools and Templates

### 1:1 Question Library — 20 Questions That Surface Real Issues

**On current state:**
1. "What's the hardest thing about your work right now — not the most time-consuming, the hardest?"
2. "What decision are you waiting on that's slowing you down?"
3. "Where are you getting the least help you actually need?"
4. "What would you stop doing entirely if you could?"
5. "What have you tried to tell me that you feel like I haven't fully heard?"

**On engagement and direction:**
6. "On a 1-10, how energized are you by what you're working on? What would move it two points up?"
7. "Do you know what winning looks like for you in the next 90 days? Say it back to me."
8. "What are you learning right now? What do you want to be learning?"
9. "What part of your job feels like a waste of your skills?"
10. "What would make you more excited to come to work on Monday?"

**On team and peers:**
11. "Is there someone on the team who's making your work harder? I don't need names — I want to understand the dynamic."
12. "Who on the team do you learn the most from?"
13. "Is there a decision the team made recently that you disagreed with but didn't say?"
14. "What's a norm on the team that you think we should change?"

**On career:**
15. "Where do you want to be in 18 months? Not here — your career overall."
16. "What's the gap between where you are now and where you want to be?"
17. "What are you afraid people at work don't know about your capability?"
18. "What feedback have you gotten from me that you actually found useful? What feedback have you ignored and why?"

**On you as a manager:**
19. "What's the most frustrating thing I do as a manager?"
20. "What could I do differently in the next 30 days that would make your work significantly better?"

Questions 19 and 20 take at least three months of relationship before you get real answers. Ask them anyway. The willingness to ask is itself a signal.

### Feedback Model Template

```
SBI + SO WHAT

SITUATION (specific, dated, observable):
"In [meeting/incident/interaction] on [date]..."

BEHAVIOR (what they did, not who they are):
"You [specific action]..."

IMPACT (concrete outcome, not your feeling):
"As a result, [specific thing that happened]..."

SO WHAT (what needs to change and why it matters at org level):
"I need [specific behavioral change] because [team/org function reason, not personal preference]..."

FOLLOW-UP:
Send written summary within 24 hours.
Next 1:1: explicitly check back on this item.
```

### PIP Structure — Fair, Legal, and Honest

**Before you write the PIP, answer these questions:**

1. Is this a genuine rehabilitation PIP (you believe they can change) or an exit PIP (you've decided and need documentation)? Be honest. Running a fake rehabilitation PIP is cruel.
2. Have you given explicit feedback at Levels 2-4 of the escalation ladder? If not, the PIP has no foundation.
3. Is HR looped in? Do not write a PIP without HR review.

**PIP Document Structure:**

```
EMPLOYEE: [Name]
ROLE: [Title]
PIP PERIOD: [30/60/90 days from date]
MANAGER: [Your name]
HR PARTNER: [HR contact]

BACKGROUND:
Summary of feedback conversations that preceded this plan.
Reference specific dates and documented conversations.

PERFORMANCE GAPS:
Gap 1: [Specific behavior] — [3 specific observed instances with dates]
Gap 2: [Specific behavior] — [3 specific observed instances with dates]

SUCCESS CRITERIA:
[Gap 1]: [Measurable, observable outcome by Day X]
[Gap 2]: [Measurable, observable outcome by Day X]
Not "show improvement" — specific, binary-testable criteria.

SUPPORT PROVIDED:
[Coaching session with X, scheduled Y]
[Training resource provided: Z]
[Reduced meeting load to focus on core deliverables]

CHECK-IN SCHEDULE:
Week 1: [Date]
Week 2: [Date]
[Weekly cadence throughout PIP period]

OUTCOMES:
If criteria are met by [date]: PIP closes, return to standard performance management
If criteria are not met: employment decision will be made

SIGNATURES: [Employee + Manager + HR]
```

**After every PIP check-in:** Send a written summary within 24 hours. This is your documentation. It also gives the employee a real-time read on where they stand.

## Decision Matrix

| Situation | Do This | Not This |
|---|---|---|
| Technical disagreement with a stronger IC | Ask for the failure mode analysis; decide on reversibility, not elegance | Override with authority; pretend to understand; credit-take |
| Rigid person with a legitimate concern | Extract the specific risk; engage it seriously; credit them if they're right | Dismiss the concern because the style was abrasive |
| Rigid person with no legitimate concern | Force specificity; move forward; name the pattern if it recurs | Re-litigate indefinitely; let resistance bleed into execution |
| Timid person needs to contribute | Pre-seed the question; direct attribution in meetings; 1:1 rehearsal | Put them on the spot without warning; say "just speak up" |
| Rogue employee — first offense | Diagnose whether the process is broken; have the direct conversation | Immediately escalate to formal process; let it slide |
| Rogue employee — repeat offense | Name the pattern explicitly; offer the autonomy-for-transparency deal | Tolerate it because their output is strong |
| Disengaged employee | CAMP diagnosis first; give them hard problems; ask directly | Let it slide; hope they recover on their own |
| Disengaged — re-engagement failed | Name it, offer exit with dignity | Keep managing them as if the problem is solvable |
| Feedback not landing | Check specificity; deliver stakes; escalate ladder | Repeat the same feedback in the same way and expect different results |
| Promotion decision — ready | Promote and state the reason publicly | Delay because of uncertainty that's not really about performance |
| Promotion decision — not ready | Direct conversation with specific gaps and timeline | Vague feedback; let them find out in the calibration outcome |
| Key person quits | 48-hour protocol; learn, triage, communicate | Counteroffer after 5 days; let the rumor spread before you speak |

## People Scenarios — Scripts for the Hard Moments

### Scenario 1: The star engineer who is technically right but organizationally toxic

> "I want to have a direct conversation. Your technical judgment is sound — I'm not here about that. I'm here about the pattern of how you show up in rooms with others. [Specific incident]. The impact was [specific]. Here's what I need you to understand: technical correctness does not grant permission to dismiss people in the way I've seen you do. Not because of feelings — because it actively degrades the quality of the analysis we get from the room. I need you to tell me specifically what you're going to do differently. Not a general intention — what specifically."

### Scenario 2: Someone who got passed over for promotion and is now disengaged

> "I want to actually talk about what happened in the promo cycle. I don't think we've really talked about it — we just moved past it. What's your read on what happened and where you stand now?"

Listen. Then:

> "Here's my honest read. The gap I saw was [specific]. Here's what ready looks like from my perspective: [specific]. I want to make a commitment to you: if you close this gap, I will go into the next calibration meeting with a strong case. I'd rather you hear this directly from me now than stay in ambiguity for six months. What do you need from me?"

### Scenario 3: The employee who is brilliant but has no situational awareness

> "I want to tell you something that I think will matter for your career. You are technically one of the strongest people on this team. That's not in question. What I'm seeing is a gap between the quality of your thinking and your ability to bring people with you. [Specific recent example]. The impact was [specific]. At the level you want to get to, technical depth gets you in the room. The ability to bring people with you determines what happens in the room. I want to work with you on that. Can we make that part of what we focus on in the next quarter?"

### Scenario 4: Addressing a team member who spoke to you disrespectfully in a meeting

Within 24 hours, privately:

> "I want to name something from the meeting today. When [specific thing happened], the way you responded was [specific behavior]. I'm not going to manage your frustration — frustration is legitimate. What I can't have is [specific behavior] in a team setting. Here's why: [specific impact on the room]. I want to understand what was going on for you. But I also need to be clear that going forward, that's not how disagreement gets expressed on this team."

Do not skip this conversation because the person is senior or because you don't want conflict. Silence on that moment signals to the whole team what is acceptable.

### Scenario 5: Finding out your report has been job searching

> "I want to have an honest conversation. I've gotten information that you may be exploring other opportunities. I'm not angry about it — that's your right and you don't owe me disclosure. But I'd rather we talk directly than work around it. Is there something going on that I should know about? And is there anything I can do or change that would make you want to stay?"

Then listen without defending the org. If their answer reveals a fixable gap, fix it. If the answer reveals they're ready to move on, help them do it well:

> "If you decide to go, I want to make sure you leave well. How can I support that?"

## How to Talk About This in Interviews

### What Interviewers Are Actually Asking

When they ask "Tell me about a difficult person you've managed," they are testing:
- Do you diagnose root cause or just react to behavior?
- Do you have a model, or are you winging it?
- Do you take responsibility for your role in the dynamic?
- Can you give a specific outcome, not just a story of effort?

### Exact Phrases That Work

"My first step was to diagnose whether this was a skill problem, a will problem, or an environment problem — because those require completely different interventions."

"I've learned that most rigidity in capable people is either legitimate expertise being expressed badly, or a trust problem in the relationship. I needed to figure out which one before I did anything."

"I gave feedback three times informally before naming it as a pattern. When I named it as a pattern, I also told them what was at stake. That's the conversation that changed things."

"The goal wasn't to make them comply — it was to get them to the point where they understood the impact well enough to choose to change."

### What to Avoid

Do not say: "We just had a conversation and they got it." No interviewer believes one conversation resolved a people challenge.

Do not say: "I had to let them go." without being able to describe the full escalation ladder — what you tried, when you escalated, what the documented history looked like.

Do not say: "They were just not a fit." That is the answer of a manager who never diagnosed the problem.

Do not say: "I give a lot of autonomy to my team." in a way that implies you were hands-off with a problem. Autonomy is not the same as avoidance.

### STAR Framing for People Questions

**Situation**: "I had a principal engineer who was objecting to every architectural decision in design reviews — loudly, persistently, and in ways that were shutting down other voices in the room."

**Task**: "My job was to figure out whether the objections were substantive, and whether the behavior was a communications problem or a trust problem."

**Action**: "I ran a diagnostic: I listed his objections for the last 60 days and categorized them — how many were technically correct, how many were preference, how many were ego. Then I had a direct conversation where I asked him to name the specific failure mode he was protecting against, not just the objection."

**Result**: "He had one legitimate concern I hadn't acted on. I acted on it publicly and credited him. The volume of objections dropped by 80% over the next 30 days. The ones that remained were substantive. He became one of my strongest design review contributors."

## Drills — Practice Exercises With Claude

**Drill 1 — The Diagnostic Drill:**

Prompt to use: "Give me a description of a people problem on a team — brief, ambiguous. I'll diagnose root cause using the CRED model and choose an intervention level before asking any clarifying questions. Then you tell me what the real root cause was and whether my intervention would have worked."

This trains you to resist jumping to the intervention before the diagnosis.

**Drill 2 — The Script Drill:**

Prompt to use: "Give me a situation type — rigid, timid, rogue, disengaged, or won't-change. I'll give you my opening sentence for the direct conversation. You score it on specificity, non-judgment, and whether it opens or closes the conversation, then give me a better version to compare against."

This trains the muscle of leading with a sentence that invites rather than defends.

**Drill 3 — The STAR Interview Drill:**

Prompt to use: "Ask me a behavioral interview question about managing a difficult person. I'll answer it in STAR format. You score it on: did I name a root cause, did I describe a real intervention with a script, did I give a specific outcome, and did I take responsibility for my role in the dynamic? Then give me the version a Director-level candidate would give."

This trains the gap between what you did and how you articulate it in a 3-minute interview answer.

---

The fundamental shift from Manager to Director in people leadership is this: managers manage people, Directors manage the conditions that make people effective. Every intervention in this module starts by asking whether the environment is the problem before concluding the person is the problem. That question — asked first, every time — is what separates Directors who build great teams from managers who blame their team for not being great.
