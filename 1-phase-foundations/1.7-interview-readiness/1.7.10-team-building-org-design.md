# 09 — Team Building & Org Design

## The Director's Org Design Mandate

Org design is one of the highest-leverage things a Director does — and one of the most neglected. Most Directors inherit an org structure and manage within it reactively. Effective Directors actively *design* the structure that enables their strategy, then adapt it as the strategy evolves.

---

## Concept 1: Org Design Principles

### Conway's Law

> "Organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of those organizations."

**Translated for SRE Directors**: Your team structure will produce software and platform architectures that mirror the team structure. If you have siloed teams, you'll have siloed services with poor interfaces. If you want loosely-coupled, well-interfaced systems — design the team that way first.

**The Inverse Conway Maneuver**: Deliberately design your team structure to produce the architecture you want. Want a self-service platform? Create a platform team that communicates via API — not via meetings.

### Team Cognitive Load

Team Topologies introduces a crucial concept: **cognitive load**. Every team has a finite capacity for complexity. When cognitive load exceeds team capacity, delivery slows and reliability degrades.

Three types of cognitive load:

| Type | Description | SRE Example |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Intrinsic** | Core domain complexity — necessary | Understanding Kubernetes internals to design a reliable platform |
| **Extraneous** | Incidental complexity — unnecessary overhead | Writing JIRA tickets to request observability access, manual cert renewals |
| **Germane** | Learning new things that matter — investment | Implementing OpenTelemetry, designing error budget policies |

**Director's job**: Reduce extraneous load (automate, simplify, Platform-as-a-Service). Protect germane load time (learning, innovation). Don't let intrinsic load overwhelm teams.

## Concept 2: The Team Charter

Before creating or redesigning a team, write a charter. This is the team's identity document.

### Team Charter Template

```
Team Name: [Name]
Team Type: Stream-Aligned / Platform / Enabling / Complicated Subsystem

PURPOSE
What value does this team create? For whom?
(One paragraph — write it so any engineer in the company understands why this team exists)

SCOPE
What is in scope for this team?
What is explicitly out of scope? (This is as important as scope)

SUCCESS METRICS
How will we know if this team is doing its job? (3-5 measurable outcomes)
- Metric 1: [SLO achievement, DORA metric, etc.]
- Metric 2:
- Metric 3:

INTERACTION MODEL
How does this team interact with others?
- Collaboration (short-term, joint work) with: [team names, purpose, expected duration]
- X-as-a-Service (we provide): [what we provide, how to access it]
- X-as-a-Service (we consume): [what we consume, from whom]
- Facilitating (we enable): [which teams, for what period]

SKILLS REQUIRED
- Core capabilities needed on this team
- Skills the team needs to develop in the next 12 months

CONSTRAINTS AND DEPENDENCIES
- External dependencies
- Regulatory/compliance constraints
- Resource constraints

LAUNCH DATE / REVIEW DATE
```

## Concept 3: Hiring for Director-Level Impact

### The Hiring Funnel You Own (Not Just Approve)

Directors don't just sign off on hires — they *design* the hiring system that consistently brings in the right people.

**The four levers**:
1. **Job description quality**: Does the JD accurately describe the work, not just list buzzwords? A bad JD attracts the wrong candidates — filtering them costs 10× as much as writing the JD well.
2. **Sourcing channels**: Where are the right candidates? Referrals, specific communities, conferences, LinkedIn search operators, alumni networks.
3. **Interview design**: Are you testing what the job actually requires? Technical interviews that test whiteboard algorithms for an SRE role are measuring the wrong thing.
4. **Decision process**: Do you have a clear rubric? Are decisions made fast enough to beat competing offers?

### The Structured Interview Framework

For Director-level hires and above, use structured interviews:

| Round | Focus | Who | Duration |
|-------|-------|-----|---------|
| Screen | Motivation, background, culture fit | Recruiter or Manager | 30 min |
| Technical depth | Domain knowledge (specific to the role) | 2 engineers | 60 min |
| Leadership/management | Past behavior (STAR format) | Director or peer Director | 60 min |
| Cross-functional | How they work with others | Peer from another team | 45 min |
| Executive | Vision alignment, strategic thinking | VP | 30 min |

**The rubric**: Write 4-5 competencies you're evaluating. Score each candidate 1-4 per competency. Compare against the rubric — not against each other. Debias by collecting scores before the group debrief.

### Manager Hiring: The 5 Questions That Actually Matter

When hiring a manager (your direct report), focus on:

1. "Tell me about a time you gave feedback to someone on your team that was hard to deliver. What happened?" _(Radical Candor)_
2. "Tell me about a team that wasn't performing. What did you diagnose, and what did you change?" _(Problem-solving + management judgment)_
3. "How do you prioritize your team's work when there's more demand than capacity?" _(Strategic clarity, managing up)_
4. "Tell me about a disagreement with your manager or a peer Director. How did you handle it?" _(Influence, professional maturity)_
5. "Where do you see the gap in your current management capability and what are you doing about it?" _(Self-awareness + growth orientation)_

## Concept 4: The Succession Plan

Directors are responsible for ensuring their org can survive their own departure — and their key managers' departures.

### The One-Page Succession Map

| Role | Current Holder | Internal Successor (Ready Now) | Internal Successor (12-18 months) | Risk if Vacant |
|------|--------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|---------------|
| Director of Platform SRE | You | — | Manager A (with development) | High |
| Platform Manager | Manager A | IC B (with 6mo stretch) | New hire (recruiting) | High |
| Observability Lead | Engineer C | Engineer D (with cross-training) | — | Medium |

**Director's commitment**: For every "High" risk role — there must be a development plan in motion. A risk with no mitigation is a RAID issue.

### The Developmental Move Playbook

To develop your managers into Director-ready leaders, give them:

| Type of Move | What It Develops | Example |
|-------------|----------------|---------|
| **Stretch assignment** | New domain, same org | Platform Manager leads the DR exercise program |
| **Acting Director** | Full Director experience, bounded | Cover your vacation — make real decisions, attend VP meetings |
| **Cross-functional rotation** | Org breadth, relationship building | 6-month embed in Product to understand business context |
| **Strategic project ownership** | Upward visibility, executive exposure | Own the OKR planning process for the org |
| **Sponsorship** | Advocacy + exposure | Include them in your VP presentations. Let them present a section. |

## Concept 5: Culture Architecture

### The Culture Stack

Culture at a team level is shaped by:

1. **What you measure** (What gets tracked gets done)
2. **What you reward** (What gets celebrated gets repeated)
3. **What you tolerate** (What you accept defines your floor)
4. **What you model** (Your behavior is the organization's permission structure)

**Director's most powerful cultural lever**: What you *tolerate*. You can have great values on your wall. If you tolerate a senior engineer who bullies teammates, or a manager who never gives feedback, or an on-call culture where heroes are celebrated instead of runbooks — you've defined your culture through tolerance, not aspiration.

### Building Psychological Safety

Google's Project Aristotle identified psychological safety as the #1 predictor of team performance — above all other factors.

**Psychological safety**: Team members believe they can speak up, share concerns, admit mistakes, and take interpersonal risks without fear of negative consequences.

**Director behaviors that build it**:
- Model vulnerability: Share your mistakes in staff meetings. "I made the wrong call on X. Here's what I learned."
- Respond well to bad news: When a manager brings you a problem, your first response should never make them regret bringing it. "Thank you for surfacing this early" reinforces the behavior.
- Protect dissenters: When someone says something unpopular in a meeting, make sure they don't pay a social price. "That's a fair challenge. Let's think it through."
- Separate performance from worth: People can fail at a task without being a failure. Language matters.

**Psychological safety measurement**: Two survey questions tell you a lot:
- "In the last month, have you withheld information or concerns from your manager because you were worried about the consequences?" (Should be "No" for > 90%)
- "If you made a mistake on this team, would it be held against you?" (Should be "No" for > 90%)

## Concept 6: Attrition Risk Management

### The Attrition Cost

Cost of losing one engineer (fully loaded):
- Recruiting: $30-60K (agency fee or internal cost)
- Ramp time: 3-6 months at ~50% productivity = $60-90K in lost productivity
- Knowledge loss: Unquantifiable but significant for domain experts
- Team disruption: Remaining team picks up slack, morale impact, potential cascade attrition

**Total per departure for a senior SRE: $100-200K.** For a manager: $150-300K.

### The Attrition Risk Scorecard

Rate each person on your team quarterly on 3 factors:

| Factor | Low Risk | Medium Risk | High Risk |
|--------|---------|------------|----------|
| **Flight risk** | "I'm staying" | "I'm comfortable but open to outside" | Actively interviewing |
| **Business impact** | Easily backfilled | Specialized but documented | Single expert, undocumented |
| **Sentiment** | Engaged, growing | Neutral, not growing | Disengaged, frustrated |

**High risk on 2+ factors = immediate retention intervention:**
- 1:1 conversation: "I want to understand your career trajectory and make sure we're aligned."
- Development offer: What would make their current role more compelling?
- Compensation check: Are they at market? (Know the answer before the conversation.)
- Escalation if needed: Some situations require your VP's air cover (offer match, special comp, promotion path).

### The Stay Interview

Run these annually with every key person, quarterly with any high-risk person. Not to be confused with exit interviews (too late).

**Stay interview questions**:
- "What do you look forward to when you come to work?" (Understand what's working)
- "When did you last think about leaving? What prompted it?" (Surface the real concerns)
- "What would make your role more fulfilling?" (Career aspirations, not just salary)
- "What's one thing I could do as your manager to better support you?" (Give them agency to shape the relationship)
- "If you were to leave, what would it most likely be for?" (Pre-mortem the departure)
