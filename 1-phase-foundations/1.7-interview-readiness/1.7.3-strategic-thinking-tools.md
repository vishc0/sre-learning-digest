# 02 — Strategic Thinking Tools

## Why Directors Need Strategy Tools

Senior Managers execute against a strategy handed to them. Directors *define* the strategy for their domain — and defend it to both the executives above them and the teams below them. Without tools, strategy becomes gut feel that can't survive a whiteboard challenge or a budget review.

This module gives you 5 tools Directors use to think, communicate, and decide strategically.

---

## Tool 1: OKRs — Objectives and Key Results

### What It Is

OKRs are a goal-setting framework where:
- **Objective (O)**: A qualitative, inspiring statement of what you want to achieve. It answers: *Where are we going?*
- **Key Results (KRs)**: 2-5 measurable outcomes that prove you achieved the objective. They answer: *How will we know we got there?*

The critical distinction: **KRs are outcomes, not outputs.**

| Output (bad KR) | Outcome (good KR) |
|----------------|------------------|
| "Deploy monitoring stack" | "P95 alert latency < 90s for 95% of production alerts" |
| "Run 3 DR exercises" | "RTO achieved in < 4 hours in all 3 exercises" |
| "Hire 2 SREs" | "Team deploys are fully self-sufficient with no SM involvement by Q3" |
| "Document runbooks" | "Mean time to diagnosis drops from 45 min to < 15 min" |

### Director-Level OKR Cadence

Directors own **team-level OKRs** (quarterly) that nest under **org-level OKRs** (annual) and are decomposed into **manager-level OKRs** (also quarterly).

```
Company OKR: Achieve 99.95% platform availability
  └── Director OKR: Zero unplanned customer-impacting outages in H2
        └── Manager OKR (Platform): Automate top 5 manual toil tasks by Oct
        └── Manager OKR (Incident): Reduce MTTR below 15 min for P1 events
        └── Manager OKR (DevSecOps): 100% of deploys gated by automated security scan
```

### The Confidence Score

Each KR gets a confidence score (0.0–1.0) updated weekly:
- 0.7 = "on track" — not too comfortable
- < 0.5 = needs intervention
- > 0.9 = too easy — you sandbagged

OKRs should feel like a stretch. If you're hitting 100% of KRs every quarter, your targets are too conservative.

### Writing a Director OKR in 10 Minutes

1. Start with: "What does success look like for my org in 90 days?"
2. Write 3-4 aspirational sentences — those are your Objectives
3. For each, ask: "How would my VP know we achieved this?" — those are your KRs
4. Gut check: Is each KR measurable without subjective judgment?
5. Gut check: Is each KR an outcome a customer or executive would care about?

## Tool 2: Wardley Mapping — Strategic Platform Positioning

### What It Is

A Wardley Map visualizes your components (services, capabilities, teams) on two axes:
- **X-axis (Evolution)**: Genesis → Custom → Product → Commodity
- **Y-axis (Visibility)**: User-facing (top) vs. infrastructure (bottom)

This answers: *What should we build vs. buy vs. commoditize?*

### Reading the Map

```
High Visibility (User/Business Facing)
│
│  [Customer Portal]─────────────────────────────────
│         |
│  [Notification Service] ──────────[SLO Dashboard]
│         |                              |
│  [Message Broker] ──────────── [Observability Stack]
│         |                              |
│  [K8s Platform]──────────────── [Cloud Infra]
│
Low Visibility (Infrastructure)
      Genesis    Custom    Product    Commodity
      (novel)             (managed)  (utility)
```

**Genesis**: New, experimental. High uncertainty. Small investment, high exploration.
**Custom**: Built to spec. Competitive differentiator. Worth internal investment.
**Product**: Available off-shelf. Buy or use managed service.
**Commodity**: Utility (AWS, Azure, Splunk Cloud). Minimize cost, maximize reliability.

### Director-Level Decision Rules from Wardley

| Quadrant | Decision |
|---------|---------|
| Genesis | Experiment with small team. Don't standardize yet. |
| Custom → Product | Watch for open-source / vendor options. Plan migration. |
| Product | Use managed service. Stop building. |
| Commodity | Automate fully. Negotiate vendor contracts. No humans in the loop. |

**T-Mobile SRE translation**: Your RabbitMQ cluster is in "Custom" territory. Kubernetes is moving from Custom to Product (EKS managed). Observability is Product (Splunk Cloud, Datadog). The question a Director asks: *Should we be building custom RabbitMQ automation or migrating toward a managed event streaming product?* Wardley gives you the framework to have that conversation with a VP.

### How to Build One

1. Start with your user (customer, internal engineer)
2. List what they need (direct dependencies)
3. List what *those* things need (transitive dependencies)
4. For each, ask: "Is this Genesis, Custom, Product, or Commodity?"
5. Draw the map. Look for mismatches: are you investing in commodities? Are you ignoring genesis-stage strategic bets?

## Tool 3: Horizon Planning (McKinsey Three Horizons)

### What It Is

McKinsey's Three Horizons model splits your portfolio of work into:

| Horizon | Timeframe | Focus | SRE Director Example |
|---------|-----------|-------|---------------------|
| H1 — Core | 0-12 months | Defend and extend existing business | Maintain 99.95% SLA, reduce MTTR |
| H2 — Growth | 12-36 months | Emerging opportunities gaining traction | AIOps implementation, self-healing platform |
| H3 — Options | 3-5 years | Seed future options | eBPF-based observability, autonomous remediation agents |

### Director Application

**Budget allocation target** (adjust for your organization):
- H1: 70% of investment
- H2: 20%
- H3: 10%

**The trap**: H1 expands to consume everything during crises. H2 and H3 investments get deferred. Then in 3 years, the platform is stale and competitors have moved ahead.

**The Director's discipline**: Protect H2 and H3 time even when H1 is on fire. These are not optional — they are the difference between a platform leader and a platform maintainer.

**Quarterly question to ask yourself**: "What did I do for H2 and H3 this quarter?" If the answer is "nothing" — you are being operationally reactive, not strategically leading.

## Tool 4: The Balanced Scorecard (adapted for SRE/Platform Directors)

### What It Is

The Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton) measures organizational performance across four lenses — preventing the trap of optimizing only one dimension (usually cost).

### SRE Director Scorecard

| Perspective | Core Question | Metrics |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| **Customer** | Are customers experiencing reliable services? | SLO achievement %, CSAT/NPS on platform, P1 frequency |
| **Internal Process** | Are our engineering processes healthy? | DORA metrics (deploy freq, MTTR, change fail rate), toil %, on-call burden |
| **Learning & Growth** | Is the team building the right capabilities? | Certifications, knowledge transfers, attrition risk score, training completion |
| **Financial** | Are we managing cost efficiently? | Cloud cost per request, cost/availability ratio, HC efficiency |

### How to Use It

Review all four quadrants monthly with your managers. The moment one is red, look for causes in the other three:
- Customer SLO degrading? → Check internal process (toil overload, change failure rate)
- High team attrition? → Check financial (underpaid?) and process (on-call burden)
- Cost spiraling? → Check internal process (efficiency of toil automation)

**The power**: Prevents the "we look great on reliability but the team is burning out and no one is growing" failure mode.

## Tool 5: SWOT + TOWS for Team Strategy

### SWOT — The Classic

| | Helpful | Harmful |
|-|---------|---------|
| **Internal** | Strengths | Weaknesses |
| **External** | Opportunities | Threats |

Run this for your org at the start of each half-year. Involve your managers.

**SRE Director Example**:
- **Strength**: Deep RabbitMQ + Kubernetes expertise. Strong incident command culture.
- **Weakness**: No IaC/GitOps capability in the team. Single-expert risk in observability.
- **Opportunity**: Company AI initiative creates demand for MLOps platform reliability.
- **Threat**: EKS managed service makes platform team redundant if not repositioned.

### TOWS — Turning SWOT Into Strategy (the part most people skip)

TOWS generates strategic options from SWOT:

| | Opportunities | Threats |
|-|--------------|---------|
| **Strengths** | **SO** — Use strengths to capture opportunities | **ST** — Use strengths to neutralize threats |
| **Weaknesses** | **WO** — Fix weaknesses to capture opportunities | **WT** — Defensive: minimize weaknesses, avoid threats |

**Example**:
- **SO**: Use RabbitMQ expertise to lead AI workload reliability (new revenue-generating platform)
- **ST**: Package incident command culture as "platform reliability program" to justify team existence even as K8s commoditizes
- **WO**: Hire a GitOps engineer to capture the CI/CD automation opportunity
- **WT**: Document single-expert knowledge now before attrition creates a threat

## Putting It Together: A Strategic Review Ritual

Run this quarterly (2-hour session, you + your managers):

1. **SWOT update** (30 min) — what changed in the external environment? What changed internally?
2. **OKR retrospective** (30 min) — what did we achieve? What did we miss? Why?
3. **Horizon check** (15 min) — what H2/H3 work happened? What needs protection next quarter?
4. **Wardley scan** (15 min) — did any component move on the evolution axis? What does that mean for build/buy decisions?
5. **Next quarter OKRs** (30 min) — draft, based on the above
