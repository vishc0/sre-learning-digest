# 03 — People Leadership Frameworks

## Why This Module Matters at Director Level

At SM level, people leadership is about developing individuals. At Director level, it's about developing your managers as leaders — and building the *culture* in which great leadership happens at scale.

This module gives you the frameworks and tools for: coaching managers, giving candid feedback, managing talent, and building psychological safety.

---

## Framework 1: Situational Leadership II (Hersey & Blanchard)

### The Core Idea

There is no single "best" leadership style. The right style depends on the **development level** of the person you're leading on a *specific task*. People are not globally "junior" or "senior" — they have different development levels for different domains.

### The Four Development Levels

| Level | Competence | Commitment | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **D1** | Low | High | New to task. Enthusiastic but uncertain. "Tell me what to do." |
| **D2** | Some | Low | Has some skills but lost confidence or motivation. "Is this even worth it?" |
| **D3** | High | Variable | Very capable but inconsistent confidence. "I can do it — but do you trust me?" |
| **D4** | High | High | Expert and self-directed. "Give me the goal and get out of my way." |

### The Four Leadership Styles

| Style | Directive Behavior | Supportive Behavior | Match |
|-------|-------------------|--------------------|----|
| **S1 — Directing** | High | Low | D1 — Tell what, how, when. Close supervision. |
| **S2 — Coaching** | High | High | D2 — Still directive but explain WHY. Lots of dialogue. |
| **S3 — Supporting** | Low | High | D3 — Facilitate, encourage. Ask for their input. |
| **S4 — Delegating** | Low | Low | D4 — Set goal. Step back. Trust completely. |

### Director Application: Diagnosing Your Managers

This is where it gets powerful. You don't use SL2 on ICs — your managers do. You use it to **calibrate how you lead your managers**.

A manager who is D4 on incident management (been doing it for 5 years) might be D1 on budget planning (first time owning HC). Your style shifts per domain:

- **Budget planning**: S2 (coaching) — frequent check-ins, explain the "why" behind every number, work through the model together
- **Incident command**: S4 (delegating) — set the outcome standard, trust them to execute, debrief after

**The most common Director error**: Applying S4 to a D2 manager. You stop checking in because they "should be able to handle it." They flounder silently. Surprise failure.

**Test for yourself**: For each of your managers, for each of their key responsibilities, what's their D-level? Are you leading them with the matching S-style?

## Framework 2: Radical Candor (Kim Scott)

### The Two Axes

**Care Personally** (y-axis): Do you genuinely care about this person's success and wellbeing?
**Challenge Directly** (x-axis): Do you tell them the hard truth in the moment?

```
         Care Personally
              HIGH
               |
  Ruinous  | Radical
  Empathy  | Candor
(too nice, |
 no truth) |
___________|___________  Challenge
           |           Directly
  Neglect  | Obnoxious
(checked   | Aggression
  out)     | (jerk)
              LOW
```

### The Four Quadrants in Practice

**Radical Candor** (High Care, High Challenge): "I'm telling you this because I care about your career and I trust you can handle the truth." This is the goal. It feels uncomfortable at first — that's how you know it's working.

**Ruinous Empathy** (High Care, Low Challenge): You see the problem. You don't say it because you don't want to hurt them. They never improve. They get blindsided at performance review. *This is the most common failure mode for managers who are "nice."*

**Obnoxious Aggression** (Low Care, High Challenge): You say the hard thing but without warmth. "This code is garbage." The person hears criticism without context that it comes from investment in them. Destroys trust.

**Manipulative Insincerity** (Low Care, Low Challenge): Political. Praise in public, complain behind their back. "I told them it was great even though it wasn't — I didn't want the conflict." Toxic.

### Radical Candor in 1:1s — The Structure

**For positive feedback (praise)**:
- Be specific: not "great job" → "the way you structured that blameless postmortem stopped the blame spiral in the room — I saw three engineers visibly relax"
- Timely: within 24-48 hours, not at performance review
- Public when appropriate: praise publicly, criticize privately (SL axiom)

**For constructive feedback (criticism)**:
1. Ask if you can share an observation (creates consent)
2. Describe the specific behavior or output, not the person
3. Describe the impact — on the team, the customer, the project
4. Listen. They may have context you don't.
5. Collaborate on what success looks like next time

**The "It's Not About You" rule**: Frame all feedback around the work and its impact, never the person's character. "That decision missed the risk" not "you're not thinking strategically."

## Framework 3: The GROW Coaching Model

### What It Is

GROW is the most widely used coaching framework in executive and management development. It works because it structures a conversation to move from problem to action in ~30 minutes.

**G** — Goal  
**R** — Reality  
**O** — Options  
**W** — Will / Way Forward  

### The Questions That Drive Each Phase

**Goal** — What do you want?
- "What would you like to get out of this conversation?"
- "What does success look like in 3 months for this situation?"
- "If we solve this today, what changes?"
- "How will you know when you've achieved it?"

**Reality** — What's happening now?
- "What's actually going on? Tell me what you're observing."
- "What have you already tried?"
- "What's making this hard?"
- "What are you most worried about?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, where are you right now relative to your goal?"

**Options** — What could you do?
- "What options do you see?"
- "What else could you do?" (ask this 3 times — the best answers come after the obvious ones)
- "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"
- "What would you advise someone else in this situation?"
- "What are the pros and cons of each option?"

**Will / Way Forward** — What will you do?
- "Which option will you pursue?"
- "What specifically will you do, and by when?"
- "What might get in the way? How will you handle it?"
- "What support do you need from me?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how committed are you to this plan?"

### Director Application

Use GROW when a manager comes to you with a problem. **Resist the urge to tell them the answer.** A manager who leaves your 1:1 with your answer has learned nothing. A manager who leaves with *their* answer — which you helped them discover — grows as a leader.

GROW is not for:
- Crisis (use direct instruction)
- When they genuinely lack information you have (share the info, then GROW)
- When you have a firm directive (say so, then use GROW to implement it)

**How to signal you're using GROW**: "I'm going to coach you through this rather than give you my answer. That okay?" Most managers will say yes and be grateful.

## Framework 4: The 9-Box Talent Grid

### What It Is

A 3x3 grid plotting your team members on:
- **X-axis**: Performance (Low → High)
- **Y-axis**: Potential (Low → High)

Used for: succession planning, development investment, HC decisions, performance actions.

### The 9 Boxes

```
         POTENTIAL
     Low   | Medium  | High
     ───────────────────────
H    Solid | High    | Star /
i    Perf  | Impact  | Future
g    Low   | Player  | Leader
h    Pot'l |         |
     ───────────────────────
M    Core  | Key     | Rising
e    Player| Employee| Star
d    (maint)|        |
     ───────────────────────
L    Risk  | Dilemma | Rough
o    Employee|       | Diamond
w    (exit)|        | (invest)
```

### Director Actions by Box

| Box | Label | Director Action |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| High Perf, High Pot | Star | Accelerate development. Stretch assignments. Succession path. |
| High Perf, Low Pot | Solid Performer | Retain. Recognize. Don't over-invest for growth they don't want. |
| Low Perf, High Pot | Rough Diamond | Investigate WHY. Wrong role? Wrong manager? Invest if fixable. |
| Low Perf, Low Pot | At Risk | Performance plan. Honest conversation. Exit if no change. |
| Medium/Medium | Core | Steady state. Appreciate. Ensure they don't drift. |

### The Calibration Session

Directors run 9-box calibration with their managers (not with ICs). Goal: ensure consistent standards across your org. A "High Performer" in one team should be comparable to a "High Performer" in another.

**The two calibration questions**:
1. "What does this person do that makes them 'high'? Give me a specific example."
2. "Could this person do your job? (for high potential)" — no? Medium potential. Yes? High.

**Bias to watch**: Recency bias (last 2 weeks override the whole year), halo effect (one great project biases all ratings), familiarity bias (people you like get higher ratings).

## Framework 5: Lencioni's 5 Dysfunctions of a Team

### The Pyramid

```
        5. Inattention to Results
           (team goals vs. personal goals)
        4. Avoidance of Accountability
           (no one confronts peers)
        3. Lack of Commitment
           (ambiguous decisions, everyone hedges)
        2. Fear of Conflict
           (artificial harmony — no real debate)
        1. Absence of Trust
           (people don't show vulnerability)
```

This is a *bottom-up* problem. You cannot fix layer 3 without fixing layer 2. You cannot fix layer 2 without fixing layer 1.

### Diagnosing Your Team

**Absence of Trust signs**: Managers don't admit mistakes in your staff meetings. No one says "I don't know." Blame happens across team boundaries.

**Fear of Conflict signs**: Meetings are polite. Disagreements happen in the hallway after the meeting, not in the room. Decisions made in the meeting get relitigated in Slack.

**Lack of Commitment signs**: "We agreed to X" but two weeks later the team is not executing on X. "I thought we decided Y?" Ambiguous follow-ups.

**Avoidance of Accountability signs**: Managers don't give each other hard feedback. Problems fester. "That's their problem, not mine" mentality.

**Inattention to Results signs**: Individual team achievements are celebrated while org-wide goals are missed. People optimize for their own team's metrics.

### Director-Level Interventions

**For Absence of Trust**: Model vulnerability. Share your mistakes in staff meetings. Ask for help publicly. Use team-building that creates shared risk/challenge (not just pizza parties).

**For Fear of Conflict**: Mine for conflict. When a meeting is too smooth: "Who has a concern about this that hasn't been said?" Normalize productive disagreement. Protect the person who dissents.

**For Lack of Commitment**: End every meeting with explicit decisions: "What are we committing to? Who owns it? By when?" Make it visual (action items, owners, dates — visible to all).

**For Avoidance of Accountability**: Create peer accountability mechanisms. Shared OKRs. Public tracking boards. "What does the team think about X's progress?" directed at the *team*, not just the person.

**For Inattention to Results**: Lead with org-level results in every staff meeting before team-level results. Celebrate collective wins. Connect individual work to organizational outcomes explicitly.

## The Manager 1:1 Template

A healthy 1:1 with your direct-report managers (weekly, 45 min) is the highest-leverage tool you have. Structure:

**First 10 minutes**: Their agenda — what do they need from you?
- What's blocking their team this week?
- What decision do they need from you?
- What are they worried about?

**Next 15 minutes**: Your agenda — what do they need to know?
- Context from above (VP strategy, org changes, business signals)
- Feedback on something specific from the past week
- One coaching question (GROW)

**Next 15 minutes**: Development
- How are they growing as a manager? (not "are you okay" but "what did you learn?")
- One specific opportunity or challenge for the next month

**Last 5 minutes**: Close
- What's the one thing they're taking away?
- What do you need from them before next week?

**Anti-pattern**: 1:1s that become status updates. Status should be async (in writing). 1:1s are for relationship, coaching, and context. If your manager's first thing in a 1:1 is "everything's fine, no blockers" — they don't trust you enough to be honest yet.
