# 06 — Change Management

## Why Directors Drive Change (and Why It Fails)

McKinsey's research: 70% of large-scale organizational changes fail. They fail not because the technical change was wrong — but because the human change was unmanaged. Directors who can architect the human side of change are rare. This module gives you two complete frameworks and the practical toolkit.

---

## Framework 1: ADKAR — The Individual Change Model

### What It Is

ADKAR (Prosci) focuses on the individual human journey through change. Organizations don't change — people do. And every person in your org goes through five stages before they've successfully changed.

**A** — Awareness of the need for change  
**D** — Desire to participate and support the change  
**K** — Knowledge of how to change  
**A** — Ability to demonstrate the skills and behaviors  
**R** — Reinforcement to sustain the change  

### Why It Matters: Change Stalls at the Weakest Stage

ADKAR is sequential. If someone is stuck at Desire, giving them more Knowledge training won't help. Identify *where* they're stuck, then intervene at that stage.

| Stage | Where People Get Stuck | Intervention |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Awareness** | "Why are we doing this? I haven't heard anything." | Town halls, manager comms, business case made visible |
| **Desire** | "I understand but I don't agree / don't care / will lose something" | One-on-one conversation. Surface WIIFM (What's In It For Me). Address fears. |
| **Knowledge** | "I want to but I don't know how" | Training, documentation, job aids, shadowing |
| **Ability** | "I've been trained but I can't do it consistently yet" | Practice, coaching, safe-to-fail environment, feedback loops |
| **Reinforcement** | "I changed but drifted back" | Recognition, accountability, process embed (make the new way the easy way) |

### Running an ADKAR Diagnostic

At a key milestone in a change program, survey your team (anonymously) with 5 questions rated 1-5:

1. I understand *why* this change is happening and why it matters. _(Awareness)_
2. I personally want to support this change. _(Desire)_
3. I know what I need to do differently as a result of this change. _(Knowledge)_
4. I feel confident and capable of doing it. _(Ability)_
5. I believe this change will stick in our organization. _(Reinforcement)_

**Read the results**: The lowest-scoring dimension is your intervention point — not the highest-scoring one.

### ADKAR in an SRE Context: Example

**Change**: Moving from reactive on-call to proactive SLO-driven reliability ownership.

| Stage | What this looks like | Your action |
|-------|---------------------|-------------|
| Awareness | Engineers don't know why SLOs matter | Business-connected all-hands: "X incidents this quarter cost us Y. SLOs prevent Z." |
| Desire | "We already do reliability. This is just more process." | Surface WIIFM: SLOs reduce on-call load, protect error budget, create career progression |
| Knowledge | Engineers don't know how to write SLOs | Workshop: SLI selection, SLO calculation, error budget math |
| Ability | First SLOs are too tight or too loose | Coaching from SRE Chapter Lead. Safe-to-iterate first cycle. |
| Reinforcement | SLOs are written but never reviewed | Weekly SLO review in team rituals. Errors budget visible on dashboard. Incident reviews reference SLO. |

## Framework 2: Kotter's 8-Step Change Model

### What It Is

Kotter's model (John Kotter, Harvard) is for *organizational* change — teams, programs, culture shifts. Where ADKAR is individual-level, Kotter is organizational-level.

### The 8 Steps

**Phase 1 — Creating a Climate for Change**

**Step 1: Create a Sense of Urgency**
- Show the burning platform. Not manufactured panic — real consequence.
- "If we don't automate our deployment pipeline in 6 months, our change failure rate will continue degrading and we'll lose SLA credits from Product."
- The goal: 75%+ of your stakeholders genuinely believe change is necessary.
- Failure mode: Leadership says "we should change" but nobody believes it's urgent. Change stalls.

**Step 2: Build a Guiding Coalition**
- Identify 3-5 influential people (formal and informal) who will champion the change.
- Include skeptics who come around — they're more credible than enthusiasts.
- Include people across organizational lines — not just your own team.
- Failure mode: Only your direct reports are in the coalition. The rest of the org waits to be told.

**Step 3: Form a Strategic Vision and Initiatives**
- "By the end of 2026, every deployment is automated, tested, and reversible. Our on-call rotation has error-budget policies and no one is paged for something a runbook can handle."
- Concrete, vivid, 12-18 months out. Not aspirational fluff.
- Failure mode: Vision is too abstract ("we will be excellent at reliability"). No one knows what to do differently tomorrow.

**Phase 2 — Engaging and Enabling the Whole Organization**

**Step 4: Communicate the Vision**
- Repeat it relentlessly. 7× 7 ways. In team meetings, 1:1s, all-hands, Slack, code reviews.
- Simplify it. Use analogies.
- Let your managers tell the story in their own words — not just echo yours.
- Failure mode: Vision is communicated once in a slide deck. Engineers hear it, nod, forget it.

**Step 5: Enable Action by Removing Barriers**
- Actively remove obstacles: outdated process, misaligned incentives, tools that don't support the new way.
- "If we want SLOs to be team-owned, we need teams to have access to the SLO tooling without filing a ticket. Let's fix the access model."
- Failure mode: Declaring the vision but leaving in place all the structures that made the old way necessary.

**Step 6: Generate Short-Term Wins**
- Find wins that are achievable in 60-90 days and make them visible.
- "Team A automated certificate renewal. Saved 2h/week. Here's the before/after."
- Celebrate these visibly, even if small.
- Failure mode: All the payoff is 18 months out. People lose faith and revert.

**Phase 3 — Sustaining Change**

**Step 7: Sustain Acceleration**
- After early wins, don't declare victory.
- Use the credibility from Step 6 to tackle harder problems.
- Failure mode: The 90-day win leads to celebration, then the team returns to old habits.

**Step 8: Institute the Change**
- Make the new way the *only* way. Embed it in process, tooling, hiring criteria, performance expectations.
- "SLO ownership is now part of every engineer's performance expectations and team charter."
- Failure mode: Change depends on individual champions. Champion leaves. Change disappears.

### Running a Change Program with Both Frameworks

ADKAR + Kotter work together:

```
Kotter Step 1-3 → Creates Awareness (ADKAR A)
Kotter Step 2 (coalition) + 4 (comms) → Builds Desire (ADKAR D)
Kotter Step 5 (remove barriers) + training → Creates Knowledge + Ability (ADKAR K + A)
Kotter Step 7-8 (sustain) → Reinforcement (ADKAR R)
```

## Managing Resistance: The Director's Toolkit

### Why People Resist

Resistance is not irrational. People resist because:
1. **Loss**: They perceive they will lose something (status, autonomy, expertise relevance)
2. **Uncertainty**: They don't know what the change means for *them*
3. **Distrust**: They've seen "changes" fail before
4. **Disagreement**: They genuinely believe the change is wrong

Each cause requires a different response:

| Resistance Cause | Wrong Response | Right Response |
|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| Fear of loss | "Change is good for you" | Acknowledge the loss. Make it visible. Discuss transition support. |
| Uncertainty | "Details TBD" | Provide as much certainty as possible. Be honest about what's still TBD. |
| Distrust | "This time is different" | Show track record. Short wins early. Follow through on every commitment. |
| Genuine disagreement | "You're wrong" | Hear them out. Incorporate valid concerns. Explain why you're still proceeding. |

### The Resistance Conversation Framework

When a manager or influential engineer is resisting a change:

1. **Listen first**: "Tell me about your concern. I want to understand it fully before I respond."
2. **Acknowledge**: "That's a legitimate concern. I hear it." (Even if you disagree with the solution, validate the concern.)
3. **Clarify**: "Is your concern about [X] or [Y]?" Resistors often anchor on surface symptoms — the real concern is often deeper.
4. **Engage**: "Given that concern, what would need to be true for you to support this?" — This makes them a problem-solver, not a blocker.
5. **Be honest about limits**: "I can address X. I can't change Y — here's why." False promises destroy trust.
6. **Close**: "Are you in? Even if not fully convinced, can you give it a fair run?" Conditional commitment is OK. Silent opposition is not.

### The Influence Without Authority Framework

Directors often need to drive change across teams they don't own. This requires influence, not authority.

**The 6 Principles of Influence (Cialdini)**:

| Principle | What It Is | Director Application |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Reciprocity** | People return favors | Help other teams before you need their help. Borrow credibility. |
| **Commitment** | People honor public commitments | Get small yeses in writing. Build toward the big yes. |
| **Social Proof** | People follow peers | Find early adopters. Make their success visible. Others follow. |
| **Authority** | People respect expertise | Your operational depth is authority. Use it. Reference data. |
| **Liking** | People say yes to people they like | Invest in relationships before you need them. |
| **Scarcity** | Rare = valuable | "This headcount/tooling/platform capability is available now — may not be later." |

## The Change Communication Plan

Every significant change (org reorg, tool migration, process shift) needs a communication plan:

| Audience | Message | Channel | Frequency | Owner |
|---------|---------|---------|-----------|-------|
| Your managers | Full context, early | 1:1 + staff meeting | Before announcement | You |
| Your engineers | What changes for them, WIIFM | Town hall + manager cascade | At announcement | Managers |
| Peer Directors | Cross-team impact, dependencies | Direct conversation | Before announcement | You |
| Your VP | Status, risks, escalation needs | Weekly update | Ongoing | You |
| External stakeholders | Impact on SLAs, timelines | Formal communication | As needed | You |

**The cascade rule**: Information should travel top-down but reach each layer before it reaches the layer above them going *sideways*. Your engineers should not learn about a reorg from a peer in another team before their manager has told them.
