# 04 — Operational Excellence Tools for SRE Directors

## Your Home Territory

This is the module where your existing SRE expertise becomes a *management superpower* — if you translate it into the right frameworks and language. Most Director-level candidates from engineering struggle with strategic language. You have the opposite advantage: deep operational intuition that needs to be packaged in Director vocabulary.

## Tool 1: Team Topologies — Organizational Design for Platform Teams

### What It Is

Team Topologies (Skelton & Pais, 2019) is the current standard framework for designing engineering organizations. It defines four team types and three interaction modes.

### The Four Team Types

**1. Stream-Aligned Team**
- Owns end-to-end delivery of a value stream (feature team, product team)
- Has everything needed to deliver without external dependency
- SRE equivalent: an embedded SRE team within a product engineering organization
- Director question: *Are my SREs stream-aligned to the products they support, or are they centralized with coordination overhead?*

**2. Platform Team**
- Provides self-service capabilities for stream-aligned teams
- Goal: reduce cognitive load on stream-aligned teams
- SRE equivalent: your central SRE platform team providing K8s, observability, CI/CD
- Director question: *Does my platform team enable self-service, or do stream-aligned teams have to file tickets and wait?*

**3. Enabling Team**
- Temporary. Goes into stream-aligned teams to build capabilities, then leaves.
- Goal: upskill other teams so they no longer need the enabling team
- SRE equivalent: a DevSecOps enablement team that embeds in product teams to implement SLOs, then exits
- Director question: *Do I have any enabling team capacity, or are my SREs trapped in permanent support mode?*

**4. Complicated Subsystem Team**
- Owns a complex component requiring deep specialist knowledge
- E.g., RabbitMQ cluster management, ML platform, specialized security tooling
- Director question: *Is this a genuine complicated subsystem, or am I calling it that to avoid sharing knowledge?*

### The Three Interaction Modes

| Mode | When | Cognitive Load |
|------|------|---------------|
| **Collaboration** | Short-term, joint problem-solving | High — use sparingly |
| **X-as-a-Service** | Platform provides a capability; consumer self-serves | Low — ideal at scale |
| **Facilitating** | Enabling team upskills stream-aligned team | Medium — temporary |

**The Director Decision Rule**: Move interactions toward X-as-a-Service as capabilities mature. Collaboration mode at scale = team can't grow.

### Applying Team Topologies to T-Mobile SRE

```
Current State:
[SRE Central] ←→ [DND Platform] ←→ [RabbitMQ Subsystem]
     |
     ↓ (all teams file tickets to SRE for help)
[Product Teams A, B, C, D]

Problem: SRE is bottlenecked. Every team waits on central SRE.

Future State with Team Topologies:
[Platform SRE] — provides K8s, observability, CI/CD as X-as-a-Service
[Enabling SRE] — embeds in product teams Q by Q to build SRE capability
[RabbitMQ Subsystem] — self-contained, provides API/dashboard to consumers
[Stream-aligned SREs] — embedded in Product Teams, own their own SLOs

Result: Platform team frees itself from ticket queue. Product teams own reliability.
```

This is a Director-level org design conversation — not an SM-level technical conversation.

## Tool 2: DORA Metrics as a Management Framework

### What DORA Is

DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) identified four metrics that predict both software delivery performance and organizational performance:

| Metric | What It Measures | Elite Target |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Deployment Frequency** | How often you ship | Multiple times/day |
| **Lead Time for Changes** | Code commit → production | < 1 hour |
| **Change Failure Rate** | % of changes causing incidents | 0-15% |
| **MTTR (Time to Restore)** | How fast you recover | < 1 hour |

### Director-Level Use of DORA

**As a governance tool**: Monthly DORA review with your managers. Not to punish — to identify systemic blockers. A high Change Failure Rate means your testing or deployment process is broken. MTTR above threshold means your on-call runbooks or alert routing are broken.

**As a communication tool to VPs**: "Our deployment frequency doubled this quarter, meaning teams are shipping business value twice as fast. Change Failure Rate held at 8%, below industry average. This demonstrates the team is moving faster *and* staying stable."

**As a benchmark**: DORA buckets teams into Elite, High, Medium, Low performers. This gives you language for a VP conversation: "Our platform team moved from Medium to High DORA performer this year. The gap between Medium and Elite is primarily in MTTR — here's our plan."

**DORA + Team Topologies**: Stream-aligned teams with X-as-a-Service platform have better DORA scores than teams in collaboration mode. This is the business case for org redesign.

### The DORA Dashboard

Build a Director-level view (weekly):

```
Team           | Deploy Freq  | Lead Time | CFR  | MTTR   | Tier
---------------|-------------|-----------|------|--------|------
Platform SRE   | 8x/week     | 2h        | 6%   | 45min  | High
Embedded SRE A | 3x/week     | 4h        | 12%  | 90min  | Medium
Embedded SRE B | 1x/2wks     | 3d        | 18%  | 4h     | Low ⚠️
```

The Low-tier team is a Director intervention point — not a coaching point for the manager, a *root cause analysis* point for you.

## Tool 3: The Operational Excellence Maturity Model

### Five Levels

Use this to assess and roadmap your teams' operational capability:

| Level | Description | Characteristics |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **1 — Reactive** | Fighting fires | No runbooks. High toil. Hero culture. P1s drive the calendar. |
| **2 — Defined** | Consistent process | Runbooks exist. Incident process documented. Basic alerting. |
| **3 — Measured** | Metrics-driven | SLOs defined. MTTR tracked. Postmortems run consistently. |
| **4 — Managed** | Error-budget discipline | Error budgets enforced. Toil <50%. Proactive capacity planning. |
| **5 — Optimizing** | Self-healing, predictive | AIOps in use. Auto-remediation. Chaos engineering. Minimal human-in-loop. |

**Director application**: Use this as a quarterly assessment. Each team is at a different level. Your roadmap is about moving each team up one level per half. Don't try to jump from Level 1 to Level 4 — it fails.

## Tool 4: The SRE Toil Ledger (Operational Debt Tracking)

### Why Directors Need a Toil Ledger

Toil (manual, repetitive operational work) is the enemy of reliability improvement. Google's SRE book recommends keeping toil below 50% of SRE time. But SMs track toil by feel. Directors need a *ledger*.

### The Toil Ledger Template

| Toil Item | Hours/Week | Automatable? | Priority | Owner |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------|-------|
| Manual RabbitMQ queue draining | 4h | Yes | P1 | Team A |
| Manual certificate renewal | 2h | Yes | P1 | Team B |
| Alert noise (false positives) | 6h | Partial | P2 | Platform |
| Manual deployment approvals | 3h | Partial | P2 | DevSecOps |
| Status page updates during incidents | 1h | Yes | P3 | On-call |

**Total tracked toil**: 16h/week  
**Target**: < 8h/week (50% rule)  
**Gap**: 8h/week → this is your automation backlog justification

**Director use**: This ledger is your **headcount conversation anchor**. "We have 16h/week of toil. Automating it requires 8 engineer-weeks of development. The payback period is 16 weeks. This is equivalent to adding 0.4 FTE of productive capacity. Here's the ROI."

## Tool 5: Incident Command System (ICS) Adapted for SRE Directors

### Why Directors Need to Know ICS

The Incident Command System was developed by the US Forest Service for wildfire management and adopted by FEMA. Google's SRE practice adopted it for production incidents. At Director level, you're not in the incident — but your team's ICS design determines how fast you recover.

### The ICS Roles at SRE Scale

| ICS Role | SRE Equivalent | Director Concern |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Incident Commander (IC)** | On-call lead or dedicated IC | Who is trained? Is rotation healthy? |
| **Operations Section** | Engineers diagnosing/fixing | Do they have runbooks? Tools? Access? |
| **Communications Section** | Status page + stakeholder updates | Is this automated? Who owns it? |
| **Planning Section** | Situation awareness + timeline | Is this role filled, or ad hoc? |
| **Finance/Admin** | Postmortem + cost tracking | Blameless? Action items tracked? |

**Director review questions after a P1**:
1. Did we have a clear IC? Did everyone know who it was?
2. Were communications consistent and timely (≤15 min updates)?
3. Did the postmortem happen within 48h? Was it blameless?
4. Were action items assigned, dated, and tracked?

### The Incident Review Framework (Director-Led)

Monthly incident review (not individual postmortem — aggregate pattern review):

```
1. Volume: How many P1/P2/P3 incidents this month vs. last month? Trend?
2. MTTR: By tier. By team. Improving or degrading?
3. Repeat Incidents: Same component fails twice = systemic issue. Track these.
4. Escaped Defects: Incidents caused by changes that passed all gates. Root cause?
5. Customer Impact: Hours × users affected × revenue sensitivity
6. Postmortem Quality: Were action items completed? Are they tracked?
```

**The Director's golden rule**: If the same root cause appears in two postmortems in the same quarter — that is a *process failure*, not an *engineering failure*. Hold the process accountable.

## Tool 6: Change Advisory Board (CAB) — Director-Level Design

### What It Is

A CAB is a governance body that reviews changes before production deployment. Done badly, it's a bureaucratic gate that slows everything down. Done well, it's a risk management mechanism.

### The Two-Speed CAB Model

**Standard Changes** (pre-approved):
- Documented, tested, reversible changes
- No CAB review needed
- Examples: routine deploys, cert renewals, patch updates
- Director role: Approve the category, not each instance

**Emergency Changes** (expedited):
- P1 fix needed in production NOW
- Streamlined: IC + 2 approvers, documented after the fact
- Director role: Define the threshold for "emergency" and the accountability process

**Major Changes** (full CAB):
- Irreversible, cross-system, or first-time changes
- Full review: risk assessment, rollback plan, comms plan
- Director role: Chair or delegate chair. Ensure risk is actually assessed, not theater.

**The Director anti-pattern**: A CAB that reviews everything equally. This creates the illusion of control while slowing the teams that are most mature and need the least oversight.
