# 07 — Risk & Governance Frameworks

## The Director's Governance Mandate

Directors own governance for their domain. Not compliance theater — *actual* risk management. The goal is: know what can go wrong, how bad it could be, and have a plan. At Director level, you're also the person who represents your domain's risk posture to VPs and audit committees.

---

## Tool 1: The RAID Log

RAID stands for: **Risks, Assumptions, Issues, Dependencies**

This is the operational backbone of any program or initiative. Directors own the RAID log for their domain — managers own RAID logs for their individual workstreams.

### The Four Elements

**Risk**: Something that *might* happen and would cause a problem if it does. Not yet an issue.
- Characterized by: Probability + Impact
- Managed by: Mitigation (reduce likelihood) or Contingency (reduce impact if it happens)

**Assumption**: Something you believe to be true that you haven't verified. If the assumption is wrong, the plan is wrong.
- Managed by: Convert to a confirmed fact (verify it) or convert to a risk (if it's wrong, what happens?)

**Issue**: Something that *has* happened and is actively causing a problem. Needs owner + action plan + deadline.
- Managed by: Assign owner. Define resolution path. Track to close.

**Dependency**: Something your work depends on from an external source (another team, a vendor, a decision). If the dependency doesn't deliver, your work is blocked.
- Managed by: Identify early. Communicate to the dependency owner. Escalate if at risk.

### The RAID Log Template

| ID | Type | Description | Probability | Impact | Severity | Owner | Mitigation/Action | Status | Due |
|----|------|-------------|------------|--------|---------|-------|------------------|--------|-----|
| R01 | Risk | Single engineer owns RabbitMQ expertise — departure creates critical gap | M | H | **High** | VP Eng | Knowledge transfer docs + 2nd-expert development in progress | Open | Q3 |
| A01 | Assumption | AWS EKS upgrade path is backward-compatible with our ingress configs | — | H | **High** | Platform Mgr | Verify in non-prod by end of sprint 3 | In Progress | Jun 30 |
| I01 | Issue | Splunk license cap hit — alert suppression in effect until renewal | — | H | **High** | Ops Mgr | Emergency renewal in procurement | Open | Jun 28 |
| D01 | Dependency | DevSecOps pipeline readiness — Platform needs security gate config from DevSecOps team by Jul 15 | — | M | **Med** | Platform Mgr | Escalation to DevSecOps Director | At Risk | Jul 15 |

### Director-Level RAID Ritual

- Review at **weekly staff meeting**: new RAID items, status changes, escalations
- Escalate to VP: any High severity item with no mitigation plan
- Close RAID items formally — don't let them linger past resolution

## Tool 2: The Risk Matrix

### The Framework

Score risks on two dimensions: **Likelihood** and **Impact**

```
         IMPACT
         Low   | Medium | High
         ─────────────────────
H   |  Medium | High   | Critical
i   |─────────────────────────
g   |  Low    | Medium | High
h   |─────────────────────────
L   |  Low    | Low    | Medium
    L         M         H
         LIKELIHOOD
```

### Scoring Guidance

**Likelihood**:
- Low: < 20% probability in the current planning horizon
- Medium: 20-60% probability
- High: > 60% probability

**Impact**:
- Low: Recoverable in < 1 hour. Limited customer impact. No SLA breach.
- Medium: 1-4 hour recovery. Degraded customer experience. Possible SLA breach.
- High: > 4 hours or irreversible. Full SLA breach. Revenue impact. Regulatory concern.

### The Four Risk Responses

| Response | When | What You Do |
|---------|------|------------|
| **Mitigate** | High likelihood, any impact | Reduce the probability. Add controls, redundancy, automation. |
| **Transfer** | High impact, medium likelihood | Insurance, vendor SLA, contractual protection, shared ownership |
| **Accept** | Low likelihood AND low impact | Document it. Don't invest. Review periodically. |
| **Avoid** | Critical (high/high) | Change the plan to eliminate the risk entirely. Don't live with critical risks. |

## Tool 3: Risk Appetite vs. Risk Tolerance

### The Distinction (Board/Executive Language)

**Risk Appetite**: How much risk you *want* to take in pursuit of your goals. This is a strategic stance.
- "We are appetite-high on innovation in observability tooling — we will try new tools aggressively."
- "We are appetite-low on production changes during peak holiday traffic — zero non-emergency changes Nov 20 - Jan 5."

**Risk Tolerance**: The maximum risk you *can* absorb before the consequences become unacceptable.
- "We can tolerate up to 2 P1 incidents per month before SLA credits trigger."
- "We cannot tolerate more than 4 hours of platform downtime per quarter."

### Why Directors Need This Language

VPs and boards set risk appetite at the organizational level. Directors operationalize it within their domain. When a manager brings you a risk, your response as Director should reference appetite and tolerance explicitly:

> "This change has a 30% chance of causing a deployment failure. Our risk appetite for pre-holiday releases is low. We will defer to January — the tolerance window isn't there right now."

This is Director language. "It's risky" is SM language.

## Tool 4: Governance Without Bureaucracy

### The Governor's Dilemma

Every governance mechanism adds friction. Some friction prevents disasters. Too much friction prevents progress. Directors must calibrate.

**The question for each governance gate**: "What specific risk does this gate mitigate? What is the cost of the gate in engineer-hours per quarter? Is the risk mitigation worth the cost?"

### The Three-Layer Governance Model

**Layer 1: Policy (Director-owned)**
- Written principles for what is and is not acceptable
- Example: "No production change during peak traffic without a pre-approved rollback plan documented in Confluence"
- Review annually. Don't let policies drift from reality.

**Layer 2: Standards (Manager-owned)**
- The "how" that implements the policy
- Example: Rollback plan template. Approval workflow. Change freeze dates.
- Review semi-annually. Should evolve as teams mature.

**Layer 3: Process (Team-owned)**
- The day-to-day execution
- Example: PR checklist, deployment runbook, CAB ticket format
- Continuous improvement. Teams own this.

### The Governance Health Check

Quarter review for each governance mechanism:

1. **Is it preventing real incidents?** If no incident has been prevented by this gate in 12 months, question whether it's needed.
2. **Is it enforced consistently?** If some teams follow it and others don't, the inconsistency creates new risk.
3. **Is it automatable?** Manual governance gates are prone to human error. Prefer policy-as-code (OPA, Conftest).
4. **Is the cost proportional?** If a gate takes 4 engineer-hours to pass for a 30-minute low-risk change, something is wrong.

## Tool 6: Audit Readiness

### What Auditors Look For (Director Domain)

When internal audit, SOC 2, or ISO 27001 assessments touch your domain, auditors typically examine:

| Area | What They Check | What You Need |
|------|---------------|--------------|
| **Change Management** | Every production change is authorized and documented | CAB records. Change tickets. Approval trails. |
| **Access Control** | Least privilege. Access reviews. | IAM policy docs. Quarterly access reviews completed. Off-boarding records. |
| **Incident Response** | Defined process. Evidence it's followed. | IRP documented. Postmortems in Confluence. SLA evidence. |
| **Business Continuity** | DR plan exists and was tested | DR runbooks. Test exercise results. RTO/RPO achieved. |
| **Monitoring & Alerting** | Alerts exist for critical systems | Alert coverage docs. On-call rosters. Escalation paths. |
| **Vendor Management** | Third-party risk assessed | Vendor risk register. Contract SLAs documented. |

### The Director's Audit Readiness Posture

**Continuous readiness** beats **pre-audit scramble**:
- Postmortems are in Confluence, blameless, and have closed action items
- Access reviews happen on schedule (quarterly) — not just before audits
- Change management records are in a system, not in someone's head
- DR exercises are run, results documented, gaps tracked

**When auditors ask you a question you don't know**: Don't guess. "Let me get you the accurate record rather than speak from memory" is an acceptable answer. Guess wrong in an audit → much worse outcome.

## Tool 7: The RACI Matrix

### What It Is

RACI clarifies who does what in a process or decision.

**R** — Responsible: Does the work  
**A** — Accountable: Owns the outcome. One person only. Decision authority.  
**C** — Consulted: Input before the decision. Two-way communication.  
**I** — Informed: Notified after the decision. One-way communication.  

### The Rules

1. **One A per row** — if two people are both Accountable, no one is. The hardest discipline in any RACI.
2. **Rs can be multiple** — more than one person can do the work
3. **C ≠ veto** — Consulted means your input is heard, not that you approve
4. **Don't over-RACI** — if every cell has 3 letters, the RACI is useless. Keep it tight.

### Director-Level RACI Example: Major Incident Response

| Activity | Director | Platform Manager | On-Call IC | Comms Manager | VP |
|---------|---------|-----------------|-----------|--------------|-----|
| Declare P1 | I | I | **A/R** | I | I |
| Customer communication | C | I | I | **A/R** | I |
| Technical resolution | I | C | **A** | I | I |
| Stakeholder escalation | **R** | I | C | C | **A** |
| Postmortem initiation | C | **A/R** | R | I | I |
| RCA presentation to VP | **A/R** | C | C | I | I |

### When Not to Use RACI

RACI works for: processes, recurring decisions, cross-team dependencies, governance.

RACI doesn't work for: novel situations, highly collaborative teams, exploratory work where roles shift. Don't force RACI onto Agile sprints — it kills collaboration.
