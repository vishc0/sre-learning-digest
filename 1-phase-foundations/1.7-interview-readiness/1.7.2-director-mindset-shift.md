# 01 — The Director Mindset Shift

## The Core Transition Nobody Explains

The move from Senior Manager to Director is not a promotion in degree — it is a change in *kind*. The skills that made you excellent as a Senior Manager will actively work against you as a Director if you don't consciously upgrade them.

---

## The Three Identity Shifts

### Shift 1: From Expert to Architect

**Senior Manager (SM)**: Your value is knowing the domain deeply. When an incident happens, you know what to ask. When a system fails, you can diagnose it. Your team trusts you because you've been in the trenches.

**Director**: Your value is designing systems so incidents are handled without you. You are the **architect of capability**, not the capability itself. If your teams cannot function when you're not in the room, you have failed as a Director — even if every incident is resolved perfectly.

**The test**: Can your managers run their teams for 30 days without asking you a single operational question? If not, you are still operating as a Senior Manager with a Director title.

**Action tool — The "Founder's Trap" Audit**

Once a month, log every decision you made in the past 2 weeks. Classify each:
- **A**: Only I could make this (strategy, budget above threshold, people above a level)
- **B**: My manager *could* make this but came to me anyway
- **C**: This should have never reached me

Target: A decisions = 30%, B decisions = moving to your managers, C decisions = eliminate. A Director spending > 50% of time on C decisions is misfiring.

---

### Shift 2: From Managing People to Managing Managers

**SM**: You set technical direction, run 1:1s, give feedback, grow individual contributors.

**Director**: You set the *environment* in which your managers do that. You coach the coaches. You are one level removed from execution.

**The leverage equation:**

```
SM output = your effort × your team size
Director output = your effort × number of managers × their team size
```

A Director who manages 3 managers who each manage 8 people has leverage over 24 people. But only if the 3 managers are operating at full capacity. Growing your managers *is* your primary delivery mechanism.

**The 4 things Directors do for managers**:
1. Contextualize — give them the "why" behind strategy so they can translate it down
2. Shield — absorb organizational noise so their teams can execute
3. Develop — invest in their growth as leaders, not just operators
4. Calibrate — ensure their standards align with the organization's expectations

**Tool: Manager Health Check (monthly)**

For each of your direct-report managers, answer:
- Do they know their team's OKRs *and* how those connect to mine?
- Are they running consistent 1:1s and giving candid feedback?
- Do they know what "great" looks like for each person on their team?
- Can they represent their team's work to skip-level stakeholders?

Red flag: You are doing any of these things *for* your managers.

---

### Shift 3: From Tactical to Strategic Thinking

**SM** operates in a 0-90 day horizon. Quarterly planning is the outer edge of your world.

**Director** operates across three horizons simultaneously:

| Horizon | Timeframe | Focus |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| H1 — Run | Now → 3 months | Operational health, current quarter delivery |
| H2 — Grow | 3–12 months | New capabilities, team development, process change |
| H3 — Transform | 12–36 months | Platform evolution, org design, strategic bets |

**The Director failure mode**: Getting sucked entirely into H1. H1 always screams loudest. H3 whispers. But H3 work in Q1 determines whether you have the *platform* to handle H1 incidents in Q5.

**Tool: Weekly Time Allocation Audit**

Track 2 weeks of calendar blocks. Classify each meeting/activity by horizon:
- Target: H1 = 50%, H2 = 35%, H3 = 15%
- Warning: H1 > 70% means you're reacting, not leading
- Warning: H3 = 0% means you have no strategic agenda

## The Language Shift

Directors speak differently. Not because language is cosmetic — because language signals thinking level and shapes how executives respond to you.

| SM Language | Director Language |
|------------|------------------|
| "We fixed the RabbitMQ issue" | "We reduced consumer lag incidents by 40% and the error budget impact is now below our policy threshold" |
| "I need 2 more engineers" | "Our reliability backlog exceeds team capacity by ~30%. The cost of unmitigated tech debt is X outage-hours/quarter. Adding HC reduces that by Y and has a payback period of Z months" |
| "The team is overwhelmed" | "We have a demand/capacity imbalance. Here's the prioritization framework I'm applying to stay within sustainable load" |
| "I disagree with the approach" | "My concern is about risk exposure. If we go this route, the P(failure) is medium with High impact. Here's an alternative with similar speed but lower blast radius" |
| "That's not my team's problem" | "That crosses into shared accountability. Let me propose a RACI so we have clear ownership before this escalates" |

## Director-Level Decision Framework

When a decision lands on your desk, run it through this:

```
1. Is this REVERSIBLE or IRREVERSIBLE?
   - Reversible: move faster, delegate further, accept more risk
   - Irreversible: slow down, involve more people, document the reasoning

2. Is this MINE to decide or should it be pushed DOWN or UP?
   - Down: if a manager can make this with context I can provide in 2 minutes
   - Up: if the blast radius exceeds my organizational authority
   - Mine: if it requires cross-team coordination or has strategic implications

3. What is the DECISION-MAKING QUALITY standard needed?
   - 70% confident → decide and move. Waiting for 95% confidence is procrastination.
   - < 50% confident → what information would move this to 70%? Go get it. But set a deadline.
```

**Amazon's "one-way door / two-way door" rule**: Type 1 decisions (one-way doors) need careful deliberation. Type 2 (two-way doors) should be made fast and delegated low. Directors who treat all decisions as Type 1 create bottlenecks. Directors who treat all decisions as Type 2 create chaos.

## The Organizational Trust Currency

Directors operate on *trust capital*. You deposit it with every delivery you make, every commitment you keep, every conversation where you represent your team accurately. You withdraw it when you overpromise, when your teams fail silently, or when you surprise your peers and executives.

**Trust deposits:**
- Delivering on commitments without being chased
- Proactively surfacing bad news early with a plan attached
- Accurately representing your team's capacity and limits
- Advocating for your team's resources with clear business logic

**Trust withdrawals:**
- Silent failures (bad news discovered by someone else)
- Sandbagging (undercommitting to look good)
- Shooting the messenger culture (your managers stop bringing problems)
- Scope creep you enabled without renegotiating commitments

**The rule**: Directors never let their VP be surprised by something the Director knew about — even 12 hours earlier. Surprises are trust destroys. Early warning with a plan is trust currency.
