## How to Read This RACI

**RACI notation**:

| Code | Meaning | Rule |
|------|---------|------|
| **R** | Responsible — does the work | Multiple Rs allowed per activity |
| **A** | Accountable — owns the outcome; escalation stops here | Exactly ONE per activity — never shared |
| **C** | Consulted — must be asked before action is taken; two-way dialog | Input required, not optional |
| **I** | Informed — notified after the action; one-way communication | No input required |
| **—** | Not involved | No role in this activity |

**The most important rule**: Every row has exactly one **A**. If two teams both think they are Accountable for the same activity, you have a gap that will surface as a blame match during the next major incident.

## Accountability Gap Analysis — Where Organizations Most Often Get This Wrong

The cells below mark activities where Accountability is most frequently contested, unclear, or incorrectly assigned in real organizations. These are the fault lines that produce blame matches during major incidents.

| Activity | Common Wrong Assignment | Correct Accountable | Why it matters |
|----------|------------------------|--------------------|----|
| Rollback decision during a P1 | MIM Team or no one | **SRE** | MIM coordinates; SRE owns the technical call. If MIM owns rollback, they'll avoid it to keep the peace. |
| Postmortem completion | MIM Team or Manager | **SRE** | SRE led the technical response; they own the technical narrative. MIM owns coordination, not the learning. |
| CMDB accuracy | SNOW Team | **Every team that deploys** (SNOW enforces) | SNOW doesn't know what's running — the deploying team does. SNOW owns the system; teams own the data. |
| SLO target setting | SRE alone | **SRE (A) + Service team (R)** | SLOs without product team buy-in get ignored when they conflict with feature deadlines. |
| DLP-induced outage response | Network Engineering | **DSEC** | Looks like a network problem; the policy is the cause. If DSEC doesn't own the fix, it never gets fixed. |
| Illumio-blocked traffic resolution | SRE or Network | **ILL Team** | Same pattern — looks like connectivity, is policy. Illumio team must be the Accountable party. |
| Security patch execution | CYB pushes, BE deploys, nobody accountable | **SRE (A) + CYB (C)** | Patching is a change to production. SRE owns production changes even when Cybersecurity initiates them. |
| War room tooling (MS Teams) during P1 | Whoever set it up | **MSFT Team (A) / MIM (R)** | If Teams is down during a P1 and nobody owns the backup, the war room collapses. |
| GitLab outage during active incident | Engineering teams scramble | **GIT Team (A)** | GitLab going down during a P1 prevents hotfix deployment. GIT team owns this platform, including during incidents. |
| Perf test go/no-go verdict | PERF produces data, no one owns the verdict | **SRE (A)** | PERF runs the test; SRE interprets results against SLO thresholds and makes the go/no-go call. |

## Key Principles This RACI Enforces

**1. SRE is Accountable for reliability outcomes — not for all reliability work.**
Every R in these tables that belongs to BE, FE, MW, TKE, DB, FW, ILL represents work that team does. SRE's A means they set the standard, measure compliance, and own the outcome if it is not met.

**2. Security and reliability are not competing concerns — they share change governance.**
The security teams (CYB, DSEC, FW, ILL) appear heavily in Table 2 and 4 because their changes carry reliability risk. The RACI makes explicit that security-mandated changes go through the same change governance process as any other change — they are not exempt.

**3. The tooling teams (GIT, SNOW, JIRA, MSFT, TKE) have their own SLOs.**
Table 5 establishes that internal platforms are first-class production services. Their reliability obligations are as real as the customer-facing services they support.

**4. The business teams are the consumer — not a passive observer.**
PO and BIZ appear in Tables 3 and 6 because they co-own SLO negotiation and resource prioritization. Reliability is a negotiated contract between SRE and the product organization — not a technical constraint imposed on the business.

**5. One Accountable per activity eliminates blame matching in incidents.**
The most important use of this RACI is not daily operations — it is the conversation after a major incident. "Who was accountable for this?" has a documented answer. The postmortem is about why the process failed, not about assigning a team to blame.
