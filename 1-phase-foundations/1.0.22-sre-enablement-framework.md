## What This Framework Is

PREF is a system-agnostic workbook that takes any platform, service, or system from zero SRE maturity to fully autonomous, data-driven reliability operations. It is built for engineering leaders who are enabling SRE across an organization where the operational foundation (ITSM, Change, Release, Environment, Problem Management) is already mature and functioning.

PREF defines 10 SRE capability dimensions, 6 maturity levels per dimension, and the implementation playbook to advance through each level. It specifies who does what, what tooling is required, what evidence proves a level is achieved, and what assumptions must hold for the framework to succeed.

**The Level 5 end state** is not a dashboard. It is an operational console — a Reliability Management Console (RMC) — where a single screen shows you system health, identifies the decision to be made, suggests the action, and executes it on your instruction. No investigation. No extrapolation. No digging through logs.

> **Concrete example**: You open the RMQ console and see queue depth climbing on `dnd-priority-high` while p99 latency on the notification dispatcher is crossing its SLO threshold. The system has already identified that this flow feeds three dependent flows — opt-out confirmation, delivery receipt, and fallback SMS — which will hit the same pressure in 4 minutes at current trajectory. One screen shows all of this. You review the suggested action: scale notification-dispatcher to 18 replicas, scale opt-out-service to 6 replicas, open circuit breaker on fallback-SMS to protect the downstream. You approve. It executes in 90 seconds. The queues stabilize. The SLO holds. No Slack thread. No war room. No manual kubectl commands.

That is the target. Everything in this framework builds toward it.

## Section 1 — Assumptions Register

These assumptions must hold for PREF to succeed. If any are violated, address them before investing in SRE capability — a broken foundation makes SRE work impossible or invisible.

### Category A: ITSM and Process Maturity

| # | Assumption | Why it matters if violated | Signal that proves it is true |
|---|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| A1 | Every production change has a SNOW ticket with rollback plan | Without change records, incident-to-cause correlation fails. AI change correlation has no data. MTTR extends. | Change record completeness rate > 95% |
| A2 | Incident Management is structured: IC assigned, war room opened, timeline documented | SRE cannot function as the technical lead if there is no one managing the room | IC assignment time < 5 min for P1, postmortem completion rate > 90% |
| A3 | Problem Management is active: postmortems completed, action items tracked | SRE improvements stall if root causes are not documented and acted on | Action item closure rate > 80% within 30 days |
| A4 | Change freeze windows are published at least 2 weeks in advance | Reliability planning and on-call staffing depend on knowing when high-risk periods occur | Freeze calendar published and accessible to SRE |
| A5 | CMDB is maintained: services, their dependencies, and their owners are current | Blast radius assessment is wrong without accurate CMDB. Topoology-aware alerting fails. | CMDB staleness rate < 10% of services |
| A6 | Release Management produces a change event feed accessible to observability tools | AI-assisted change correlation (incident to deployment correlation) requires the feed | Change events visible in observability platform within 5 min of deployment |

### Category B: Tooling and Platform Maturity

| # | Assumption | Why it matters if violated | Signal that proves it is true |
|---|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| B1 | Kubernetes platform (TKe) is operational and stable | SRE capability built on an unstable cluster is built on sand | TKe control plane SLO > 99.9% |
| B2 | GitLab CI/CD pipelines are reliable and API-accessible | Automated rollback and deployment require programmatic pipeline access | GitLab availability SLO > 99.5% |
| B3 | Vault (secrets) is HA-configured and accessible | Services that cannot retrieve secrets at startup cannot be restarted safely | Vault availability SLO > 99.99% |
| B4 | SNOW is API-accessible for automated incident creation | Manual ticket creation during incidents adds cognitive load and delays documentation | SNOW API available, automated incident creation tested |
| B5 | RabbitMQ / Kafka clusters are production-grade with monitoring exporters enabled | Messaging observability requires Prometheus exporter enabled on brokers | `rabbitmq_overview` metrics visible in Prometheus |
| B6 | An observability backend exists (Splunk, Grafana + Prometheus, or Datadog) | SRE cannot build SLOs, alerts, or dashboards without a working telemetry backend | Observability platform SLO > 99.5% |
| B7 | OTel Collector or equivalent agent is deployed as DaemonSet | Automatic metric enrichment with K8s metadata requires a collector in the cluster | OTel Collector DaemonSet running on all nodes |
| B8 | Services emit structured JSON logs with at minimum: timestamp, level, service, trace_id | Correlation across logs, metrics, traces requires this baseline | Log schema compliance > 90% of services |

### Category C: People and Culture Maturity

| # | Assumption | Why it matters if violated | Signal that proves it is true |
|---|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| C1 | Teams accept on-call responsibility for services they own | SRE cannot be the only on-call for all services. Embedded ownership is required. | On-call rotation covers all production services with at least one owning team |
| C2 | Engineering managers treat reliability as a delivery requirement, not SRE's problem | Error budget policy has no teeth if managers protect feature velocity over reliability | Reliability work appears in sprint backlogs proportional to error budget consumption |
| C3 | Postmortems are blameless | Blame-based postmortems produce defensive incomplete RCAs. Learning stops. | Postmortem language reviewed for blame; action items are system/process changes, not people changes |
| C4 | Leadership has funded SRE tooling and headcount | Framework implementation requires engineering capacity and tool licensing | SRE team is funded and tools are licensed |
| C5 | Teams will participate in chaos/game day experiments | Resilience validation requires willing participation from owning teams | Game day calendar has at least quarterly cadence with team sign-ups |
| C6 | Bar Raiser / design review enforces reliability requirements before production | Retrofitting reliability is 10× more expensive than building it in | Reliability checklist is a hard gate in the Bar Raiser / design review process |

### Category D: Security and Compliance Maturity

| # | Assumption | Why it matters if violated | Signal that proves it is true |
|---|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| D1 | Security changes go through the same change management process as all other changes | Security-change-induced outages are among the most common. Exemptions are a reliability risk. | Zero security changes with no SNOW change record in last 90 days |
| D2 | DLP policies are in test mode before enforcement | Enforcement mode changes silently break service connectivity | DLP change log shows test period before each enforcement promotion |
| D3 | Certificate expiry monitoring exists for all production TLS certificates | Cert expiry is one of the most preventable and most common causes of P1 incidents | Cert expiry monitoring coverage 100% of production certs, alerts 30 days in advance |
| D4 | Access to production is logged and reviewed quarterly | SRE needs audit evidence; unauthorized access during incidents creates legal complications | Access review records current within 90 days |
| D5 | PII fields are documented per service | SRE cannot design safe logging and telemetry without knowing which fields are restricted | Data classification inventory exists and is current |

### Category E: Data and Telemetry Baseline

| # | Assumption | Why it matters if violated | Signal that proves it is true |
|---|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| E1 | Services emit metrics at minimum: request rate, error rate, latency (histogram) | RED framework requires these three. Without them, SLO measurement is impossible. | RED metrics present for > 80% of production services |
| E2 | A service dependency topology map exists (which service calls which) | Blast radius assessment and AI-driven incident correlation require the graph | Service dependency map current in CMDB or service mesh telemetry |
| E3 | Deployment timestamps are available in the observability platform | Change correlation (incident started N minutes after deployment X) requires this | Deployment events visible as annotations in Grafana / Splunk |
| E4 | Baseline seasonal patterns are documented (business hours, peak days, events) | Anomaly detection without baselines fires on normal variation | Seasonal baselines defined for all critical service metrics |
| E5 | Log volume and metric cardinality are within platform capacity | Overloaded observability platforms drop data under load — exactly when it is needed most | Observability platform at < 70% ingest capacity at peak |

### Category F: Leadership and Investment

| # | Assumption | Why it matters if violated | Signal that proves it is true |
|---|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| F1 | SLO targets have been negotiated between SRE and product teams | SLOs without product buy-in get ignored when they conflict with feature deadlines | SLOs documented, signed off by product and SRE leadership |
| F2 | Error budget policy is documented and has consequences | Error budgets with no enforcement are decorative | Error budget policy document approved by VP Engineering |
| F3 | Reliability investment is funded in proportion to error budget consumption | Toil and technical debt accumulate without dedicated engineering capacity | Reliability backlog items are Sprint-planned (not perpetually deferred) |
| F4 | Leadership receives reliability reports and acts on them | Reports produced but never consumed do not drive improvement | Monthly reliability scorecard reviewed in leadership meeting |

### Dimension 1 — Observability Foundation

**What it governs**: The complete telemetry pipeline — what signals are collected, how they are structured, how they flow from service to storage, and how they are made queryable for decision-making. Without this dimension, every other dimension is blind.

**Reference**: See `1.0.4-observability-telemetry.md` — log design principles, metric instrumentation, MELT framework, 4-impact model.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists | Key gaps |
|-------|------------|----------|
| L0 | No structured telemetry. Logs exist in files, not indexed. No metrics exposed. | Everything |
| L1 | Application logs written to stdout. Basic alerting on process up/down. No trace IDs. | No structure, no correlation, no SLI data |
| L2 | Structured JSON logs. Basic RED metrics (rate, error, duration) per service. Trace IDs present in some services. | Incomplete coverage, no semantic conventions, high cardinality issues |
| L3 | Full MELT instrumentation per service. OTel SDK integrated. W3C trace context propagated. Mandatory context fields enforced (service, env, region, trace_id, correlation_id). Retention tiers defined. | No anomaly detection, no topology correlation |
| L4 | All L3 plus: service dependency topology in telemetry. Change events correlated. Seasonal baselines computed. AIOps anomaly detection active. Cardinality guardrails enforced. Observability-as-code (dashboards, alerts in Git). | Human still interprets AI output; no action automation |
| L5 | All L4 plus: real-time topology-aware signal routing. AI continuously enriches telemetry with context. Feedback loop from incident outcomes trains anomaly models. Telemetry directly feeds RMC action surfaces. | — |

#### Implementation Steps

**L0 → L1**
- [ ] Define logging standard: all services must log to stdout in JSON format
- [ ] Deploy log aggregation (Fluent Bit DaemonSet → Splunk / Loki)
- [ ] Instrument basic health check endpoint per service (`/health`, `/readiness`)
- [ ] Deploy Prometheus + Node Exporter for infrastructure metrics
- [ ] **Team**: Backend Teams instrument services; TKe deploys DaemonSet; GIT Team adds logging standard to CI lint gate
- [ ] **Gate**: 100% of services logging to aggregator, logs queryable

**L1 → L2**
- [ ] Enforce Semantic Conventions (mandatory fields: service, env, region, trace_id, span_id, version, correlation_id)
- [ ] Instrument RED metrics on all services: `http_requests_total{status}`, `http_request_duration_seconds` (histogram), error rate
- [ ] Add OTel SDK to all services (or ADOT for AWS-native)
- [ ] Enforce cardinality contract: no user_id, request_id, or timestamp in metric labels
- [ ] Deploy OTel Collector as DaemonSet with K8s metadata enrichment
- [ ] **Team**: Backend/FE Teams add OTel SDK; SRE publishes telemetry schema contract; Observability Platform Team configures Collector
- [ ] **Gate**: RED metrics queryable for > 90% of services; trace IDs present in > 80% of logs

**L2 → L3**
- [ ] Complete trace propagation across all service boundaries (W3C traceparent header)
- [ ] Integrate USE metrics for all infrastructure (CPU, memory, disk, network per service)
- [ ] Define RabbitMQ/Kafka consumer metrics: queue depth, consumer lag, DLQ depth, publish rate vs consume rate
- [ ] Define retention tiers: 15-day hot, 90-day warm, 1-year cold
- [ ] Create golden dashboards per critical service (RED + USE + SLI in one view)
- [ ] Publish observability-as-code: all dashboards and alert rules in Git
- [ ] **Team**: MW Team adds broker exporters; DB Team adds database exporters; SRE COE defines golden dashboard templates; Observability Platform Team deploys Thanos for long-term retention
- [ ] **Gate**: MELT complete for all Tier 1 services; dashboards deployed from Git; retention policy active

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Map service dependency topology and inject into telemetry (service mesh or OTel resource attributes)
- [ ] Ingest deployment events as change annotations in Grafana / Splunk
- [ ] Compute seasonal baselines per service per metric (time-of-day, day-of-week profiles)
- [ ] Deploy AIOps anomaly detection (Splunk MLTK, Dynatrace Davis, or Datadog Watchdog)
- [ ] Implement alert correlation rules (one correlated incident alert vs. 40 symptom alerts)
- [ ] Enforce observability cost governance: cardinality budget per service, ingest budget per team
- [ ] **Team**: Observability Platform Team deploys AIOps engine; SRE COE tunes baselines; TKe Team enables service mesh for topology telemetry
- [ ] **Gate**: AI anomaly detection active, false positive rate < 20%; change correlation working for > 90% of deployments

**L4 → L5**
- [ ] Build real-time topology impact graph (when service X degrades, which downstream services are affected?)
- [ ] Implement AI feedback loop: every alert disposition (actionable / noise / auto-resolved) feeds model retraining
- [ ] Connect telemetry to RMC action surface (alert → correlated context → suggested action → approval → execution)
- [ ] Enforce telemetry quality scoring: services with incomplete telemetry are flagged in reliability scorecard
- [ ] **Team**: SRE COE builds RMC integration; Observability Platform Team provides streaming API for telemetry; BE/FE Teams maintain telemetry quality scores
- [ ] **Gate**: RMC shows correlated incident view with suggested action; AI false positive rate < 5%; feedback loop active

#### Team Coordination per Level

| Level | SRE | BE/FE Teams | MW Team | TKe Team | DB Team | GIT Team | Obs. Platform |
|-------|-----|-------------|---------|----------|---------|----------|---------------|
| L0→L1 | Defines standard | Instruments | Instruments | Deploys DaemonSet | Instruments | Adds CI lint | Configures aggregator |
| L1→L2 | Publishes schema contract | Adds OTel SDK | Adds broker metrics | Enriches with K8s metadata | Adds DB exporter | Enforces gate | Configures Collector |
| L2→L3 | Defines golden dashboards | Propagates traces | Adds consumer metrics | Enables service mesh | Adds slow query metrics | Deploys dashboards-as-code | Deploys Thanos |
| L3→L4 | Tunes AIOps | Maintains telemetry quality | Adds topology labels | Provides topology map | — | Provides deploy events | Deploys AIOps engine |
| L4→L5 | Builds RMC integration | Maintains telemetry SLA | — | Provides streaming topology | — | — | Provides streaming API |

### Dimension 2 — SLO/SLI Architecture

**What it governs**: Defining what "reliable" means for each service in measurable terms (SLIs), setting targets (SLOs), tracking the remaining reliability budget (error budget), and enforcing consequences when that budget is consumed.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists | Key gaps |
|-------|------------|----------|
| L0 | No SLOs. Reliability is "it works or it doesn't." | No measurement, no baseline, no target |
| L1 | Uptime % tracked manually (ping monitoring). No SLI definition. | Not user-centric; cannot distinguish degradation from outage |
| L2 | SLOs defined for Tier 1 services. SLI measurement inconsistent. No error budget tracking. No enforcement. | Defined but not operational |
| L3 | SLOs defined for all services in tiers 1–2. SLIs measured from user journey (not just infra). Error budgets calculated. Error budget policy documented and signed off. Burn rate alerts active. | No automated enforcement; human decision on budget exhaustion |
| L4 | All L3 plus: multi-window burn-rate alerting (5-min fast burn + 1-hour slow burn). SLO-based canary gates (deployment auto-rolls back on SLO breach). AI-projected breach window (at current burn rate, budget exhausts in N hours). | No auto-policy enforcement |
| L5 | All L4 plus: error budget exhaustion triggers automated policy (release freeze notification, on-call escalation, reliability sprint initiation). SLO targets auto-adjusted based on historical patterns. RMC shows SLO health + budget + burn rate per service with one-click investigation. | — |

#### Implementation Steps

**L0 → L2** (combined — L1 adds no meaningful SLO capability)
- [ ] Define SLI types per service: availability (success rate), latency (p99 histogram), throughput, freshness (for async), correctness (for data pipelines)
- [ ] Negotiate SLO targets with product teams — document and sign off
- [ ] Implement SLI measurement in Prometheus recording rules or Splunk saved searches
- [ ] Calculate error budget: `(1 - SLO_target) × window_seconds`
- [ ] Build SLO compliance dashboard per service
- [ ] **Team**: SRE COE defines SLI framework; BE/FE/MW/DB Teams instrument their SLIs; PO Team negotiates targets; Observability Platform Team implements recording rules
- [ ] **Gate**: SLOs defined and measured for all Tier 1 services; error budgets calculated; dashboard live

**L2 → L3**
- [ ] Implement multi-window error budget burn rate calculation
- [ ] Define and deploy burn-rate alert rules: fast burn (5× rate, 1hr window) and slow burn (2× rate, 6hr window) — from Google SRE Workbook
- [ ] Formalize error budget policy: define what happens at 50%, 75%, 90%, 100% consumption
- [ ] Link SLO dashboards to runbook for each SLO (alert payload includes runbook URL)
- [ ] Extend SLOs to Tier 2 services
- [ ] **Team**: SRE COE writes burn-rate alert rules; RMC/CAB Team incorporates error budget status into release go/no-go; PO Team acknowledges error budget policy consequences
- [ ] **Gate**: Burn-rate alerts firing correctly; error budget policy document approved; Tier 2 SLOs instrumented

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Implement SLO-gated canary: Argo Rollouts configured to auto-rollback on SLO breach during canary phase
- [ ] Implement AI-projected SLO breach window: "at current burn rate, budget exhausts in X hours"
- [ ] Multi-service SLO dependency view: if service A's SLO depends on service B's health, model that dependency
- [ ] SLO compliance trend analysis: is this service getting more or less reliable over time?
- [ ] **Team**: GIT Team integrates Argo Rollouts; SRE COE builds AI projection model; Observability Platform Team adds trend analytics

**L4 → L5**
- [ ] Automate error budget policy execution: release freeze notification to RMC/CAB when budget < 10% remaining
- [ ] Auto-generate reliability sprint work items in JIRA when budget < 25% remaining
- [ ] SLO target adjustment recommendation: AI suggests SLO revision when target consistently too tight or too loose
- [ ] RMC integration: SLO health → error budget status → burn rate → projected breach → one-click investigation
- [ ] **Gate**: Error budget policy executes without human trigger; JIRA auto-populated; RMC SLO view live

### Dimension 3 — Incident Intelligence

**What it governs**: The entire incident lifecycle from detection through resolution — but engineered, not just processed. Incident Intelligence means alerts are smart, triage is automated, context is pre-assembled, and the time from signal to action is measured in seconds, not minutes.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists | Key gaps |
|-------|------------|----------|
| L0 | Users call the service desk. No alerting. | Detection is customer-reported |
| L1 | Alert storms. Static threshold on CPU/memory. No correlation. On-call gets paged for noise. | Alert fatigue; no triage; MTTD measured in hours |
| L2 | SLO-unaware alerts replaced with some meaningful signals. L1/L2 triage process exists. SNOW tickets created manually. | No correlation; alerts require human interpretation; runbooks reference wrong procedures |
| L3 | SLO burn-rate alerts. Runbooks linked in alert payload. L1/L2 execute runbooks for known conditions. IC assigned within 5 min for P1. Postmortems completed within 5 days. MTTD < 5 min for instrumented failures. | No AI correlation; human still assembles context |
| L4 | AI correlates alerts to single incident. Change correlation active (incident linked to deployment). Blast radius map shown automatically. Pre-assembled incident context (metric + log sample + trace + likely cause + runbook). MTTD < 2 min. | Human still executes remediation |
| L5 | Self-healing for classified failure types. AI root cause with confidence score. Incident context delivered to on-call before pager fires. RMC shows: what is failing, who is impacted, what changed, suggested fix, one-click execution. MTTD < 30 sec for instrumented failures. | — |

#### Implementation Steps

**L0 → L2**
- [ ] Replace static threshold alerts with SLO burn-rate alerts
- [ ] Define alert severity taxonomy: P0 (immediate customer impact + revenue), P1 (significant customer impact), P2 (degraded, not failed), P3 (warning, no customer impact)
- [ ] Deploy PagerDuty / OpsGenie with escalation policies per severity
- [ ] Build L1/L2 triage runbooks for 10 most common alert types
- [ ] Integrate SNOW auto-creation from PagerDuty for P1/P2
- [ ] **Gate**: Alert-to-SNOW-ticket automation working; L1/L2 resolving > 30% of alerts without SRE escalation

**L2 → L3**
- [ ] Add runbook URL to every alert payload (no alert fires without a runbook)
- [ ] Implement alert routing: severity × service → correct team paged
- [ ] Define and rehearse P1 war room process: IC assigned, Teams channel opened, scribe active
- [ ] Build postmortem template and SLA (5 business days)
- [ ] Instrument MTTD, MTTA, MTTR as SRE operational KPIs
- [ ] **Gate**: 100% of P1/P2 alerts have runbook; IC assignment < 5 min; MTTD < 5 min for all Tier 1 failures

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Deploy AI alert correlation (BigPanda, Moogsoft, or OpsGenie AI)
- [ ] Build change correlation: deployment event + anomaly start time → link in incident ticket
- [ ] Build blast radius map from service topology: automatic dependent service list shown in incident view
- [ ] Pre-assemble incident context: one alert → one incident record with metric graph, log sample, recent deployment, and runbook pre-loaded
- [ ] Integrate Illumio and firewall topology into blast radius assessment
- [ ] **Gate**: AI correlation reducing alert volume by > 50%; change correlation working; blast radius shown in < 60 sec of alert

**L4 → L5**
- [ ] Implement auto-remediation for classified failure types (consumer lag → KEDA scale-out; error rate → circuit breaker; canary regression → auto-rollback)
- [ ] Build AI root cause engine with confidence scoring
- [ ] Build pre-incident notification: AI predicts breach 10 min before SLO fires, notifies on-call with context
- [ ] Connect to RMC: incident card shows state → impact → cause → action → approval button
- [ ] Implement feedback loop: every auto-remediation outcome (success/failure) retrains the model
- [ ] **Gate**: > 60% of classified incidents self-heal without human action; MTTD < 30 sec; RMC incident card live

### Dimension 4 — Toil Elimination and Automation

**What it governs**: Identifying, measuring, and systematically eliminating repetitive manual work (toil) that scales linearly with service growth. Automation transforms toil into code; self-healing transforms code into autonomous action.

**Toil definition** (Google SRE): work that is manual, repetitive, automatable, tactical, devoid of enduring value, and scales linearly with service growth.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists |
|-------|------------|
| L0 | All operational work is manual. No tracking of how much time is spent on operations. |
| L1 | Some scripts exist but are tribal knowledge, undocumented, not in source control. |
| L2 | Toil is measured (% of engineer time). Runbooks are written. Scripts are in source control. |
| L3 | Runbooks-as-code (Ansible, Python, shell) executed on-demand. Toil ratio < 50%. Common operations automated. Toil tracked per team per quarter. |
| L4 | Auto-remediation for top 5 toil categories. Toil ratio < 30%. New toil items trigger automation sprint within 1 quarter. |
| L5 | Toil ratio < 10%. All classified toil is automated. Humans govern automation policy; machines execute. New toil is automatically detected from on-call log patterns. |

#### Implementation Steps

**L0 → L2**
- [ ] Define toil taxonomy: oncall tickets that require manual action, repeated > 3×/month
- [ ] Measure current toil ratio: time on operational work / total engineering time (target: < 50%)
- [ ] Write runbooks for top 10 operational tasks (in Confluence/SharePoint, linked from SNOW)
- [ ] Move operational scripts to Git repository with README
- [ ] Assign toil reduction as a standing quarterly objective for SRE team
- [ ] **Gate**: Toil ratio measured; top 10 runbooks written; scripts in Git

**L2 → L3**
- [ ] Convert runbooks to executable code (Ansible playbooks, Python scripts, kubectl scripts)
- [ ] Build self-service operations portal: common tasks executable by L1/L2 without SRE involvement
- [ ] Implement event-driven automation: KEDA triggers consumer scaling on queue depth; Argo Rollouts handles canary rollback
- [ ] Add DLQ replay automation: triggered by SNOW ticket, no manual kubectl required
- [ ] **Gate**: Toil ratio < 50%; top 5 toil items automated; L1/L2 can execute without SRE for classified tasks

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Implement auto-remediation pipeline: alert → classification → safe/unsafe check → auto-execute or page
- [ ] Build remediation safety guardrails: auto-actions bounded by blast radius and reversibility
- [ ] Automate certificate rotation: Cert-Manager + Vault → auto-renew 30 days before expiry
- [ ] Automate DLQ replay for classified message types with idempotency validation
- [ ] Automate runbook versioning: every auto-remediation run logs the playbook version executed
- [ ] **Gate**: Toil ratio < 30%; > 5 classified toil items fully automated; auto-remediation safety bounds documented and tested

**L4 → L5**

- [ ] Build toil elimination backlog automation: detected toil → auto-created JIRA ticket → assigned to owning team
- [ ] Implement full self-healing for stable failure classes: no human required for classified incidents

- [ ] **Gate**: Toil ratio < 10%; self-healing active for > 10 classified failure types; toil detection running

### Dimension 5 — Capacity and Performance Management

**What it governs**: Knowing how much headroom each system has, predicting when it will run out, and scaling proactively before user impact — not reactively after an incident.

**The RMQ example (user's own scenario)**: You see queue depth climbing and latency rising on the notification dispatcher. The system tells you: at current trajectory, you will breach your SLO in 4 minutes. It identifies dependent flows (opt-out confirmation, delivery receipt, fallback SMS) that will be impacted. It suggests scaling actions. You approve. Done.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists |
|-------|------------|
| L0 | No capacity awareness. Scaling happens after customer impact. |
| L1 | Manual monitoring of CPU/memory. Scaling is reactive. |
| L2 | HPA configured for CPU. Load tests run before major launches. Capacity reviews quarterly. |
| L3 | KEDA configured for business metrics (queue depth, request rate). Load tests for all Tier 1 services pre-launch. Saturation points documented. Capacity SLOs defined. |
| L4 | Predictive scaling: ML model forecasts demand and pre-scales. Demand graph per service per time window. Dependent flow impact modeled automatically. |
| L5 | RMC capacity dashboard: current headroom, trajectory, dependent flow impact map, suggested scale action, one-click execution. Auto-scaling on prediction without human approval for low-risk actions. |

#### Implementation Steps

**L0 → L2**
- [ ] Deploy HPA for all stateless services (CPU + memory triggers)
- [ ] Run baseline load test for Tier 1 services: find saturation point
- [ ] Document saturation points in service runbook
- [ ] Define capacity SLI: `current_load / max_capacity < 0.8` (headroom SLI)
- [ ] **Gate**: HPA active on all stateless services; saturation points documented

**L2 → L3**
- [ ] Deploy KEDA for RabbitMQ-backed consumers: trigger on `rabbitmq_queue_messages` metric
- [ ] Configure KEDA for Kafka consumers: trigger on consumer group lag
- [ ] Pre-launch load tests with SLO pass/fail verdict (PERF Team delivers; SRE gate)
- [ ] Model dependent flow impact: if notification dispatcher scales, which downstream services see increased load?
- [ ] Capacity review cadence: quarterly for all services, monthly for Tier 1
- [ ] **Gate**: KEDA active for all async consumers; dependent flow map exists; load test pass/fail verdict used in release gate

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Build time-series demand forecasting per service (Prophet, ARIMA, or SageMaker)
- [ ] Feed forecast into pre-scaling: scale out 15 min before predicted peak
- [ ] Build business event calendar integration: planned promotions, launches, and system maintenance drive pre-scaling decisions
- [ ] Implement capacity headroom SLO: alert when headroom < 20% for sustained period
- [ ] **Gate**: Predictive scaling reducing reactive scaling events by > 50%; business event calendar integrated

**L4 → L5**
- [ ] Build RMC capacity view: per service → current load, headroom %, trajectory, projected breach time, dependent flows, suggested action
- [ ] Implement push-button scaling from RMC: review → approve → KEDA/HPA/manual scale executes
- [ ] Implement one-click dependent flow scaling: system identifies and proposes correlated scale actions
- [ ] Auto-approve low-risk scaling (within pre-approved bounds: +2 replicas, known safe service)
- [ ] **Gate**: RMC capacity view live; push-button scaling working end-to-end; dependent flow suggestions accurate

### Dimension 6 — Change Safety and Deployment Reliability

**What it governs**: Ensuring every change to production is safe, detectable, and reversible. The deployment pipeline itself is a reliability system — it should prevent bad changes from reaching users and restore the system automatically when they slip through.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists |
|-------|------------|
| L0 | Direct to production. No test gates. No canary. Rollback is "redeploy the old version" |
| L1 | Basic CI gates (unit tests). Manual deployment. Manual rollback. |
| L2 | BVT post-deployment. CAB-approved changes. Manual canary (percentage routing set manually). Change records exist. |
| L3 | SLO-gated canary via Argo Rollouts. Auto-rollback on SLO breach. Change event feed to observability. Deployment frequency and change failure rate tracked (DORA). |
| L4 | AI-assisted canary analysis: "this canary shows the same pattern as deployment X that caused an incident — recommend pause." Change risk scoring: predicts likelihood of deployment causing an incident. |
| L5 | Zero-touch deployment for low-risk changes: CI/CD pipeline carries the change to production, validates SLO, promotes or rolls back — no human in the loop. High-risk changes require human approval. RMC shows all in-flight deployments with SLO health. |

#### Implementation Steps

**L0 → L2**
- [ ] Add BVT suite covering all SLI-critical flows, executed within 5 minutes post-deployment
- [ ] Configure Argo Rollouts with manual canary (SRE approves promotion)
- [ ] Require change record and rollback plan for every production change
- [ ] Integrate change events into Grafana/Splunk as annotations
- [ ] **Gate**: BVT running post-deployment; change records 100%; canary in place for Tier 1

**L2 → L3**
- [ ] Configure SLO-gated canary: Argo Rollouts auto-pauses at SLO breach, auto-rolls back at configurable threshold
- [ ] Implement DORA metric tracking: deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR
- [ ] Wire deployment events to observability change feed (timestamp + version + service)
- [ ] Define risk tiers for changes: low/medium/high/critical → different CAB approval requirements
- [ ] **Gate**: Auto-rollback tested and working; DORA metrics tracked; change feed live

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Build change risk scoring: ML model trained on past deployments (features: service criticality, change size, time of day, test coverage score, author history)
- [ ] Implement AI canary pattern matching: detect when current canary matches historical bad-deployment signatures
- [ ] Build deployment safety dashboard: all in-flight deployments + canary health + risk score + rollback readiness
- [ ] **Gate**: Change risk scoring predicting high-risk deployments with > 70% precision; AI canary analysis active

**L4 → L5**
- [ ] Implement fully automated canary for low-risk changes (risk score < threshold, all tests passing, SLO healthy)
- [ ] Build human approval workflow for high-risk changes: RMC shows risk assessment, change details, approve/reject button
- [ ] Implement deployment freeze automation: error budget < 10% → automated CAB freeze notification
- [ ] **Gate**: > 40% of low-risk changes deploy with zero human approval; high-risk approval flow working in RMC

### Dimension 7 — Resilience Engineering

**What it governs**: Proactively validating that systems withstand failure — not by hoping they do, but by deliberately injecting failure and measuring the response. Resilience Engineering transforms reliability from an assumption into a verified property.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists |
|-------|------------|
| L0 | Resilience never tested. Failure modes unknown. |
| L1 | DR documented but untested. No failure injection. |
| L2 | Annual DR test. Manual game day run once. Circuit breakers exist but not validated. |
| L3 | Quarterly game days. Chaos experiments for top 5 failure classes. Results tracked. Circuit breakers validated. Retry and timeout policies tested. |
| L4 | Continuous chaos: experiments run on every new service deployment in staging. Blast radius scoring per experiment. Resilience scorecard per service. |
| L5 | Self-validating resilience: chaos experiments run automatically, results feed AI model, services that fail chaos experiments are blocked from production until remediated. |

#### Implementation Steps

**L0 → L2**
- [ ] Document all known failure scenarios for Tier 1 services (what happens if DB goes down? if RabbitMQ loses a node? if a network partition occurs?)
- [ ] Implement circuit breakers on all external service calls
- [ ] Validate circuit breakers work: inject dependency failure, verify circuit opens within expected time
- [ ] Schedule first game day — test one known failure scenario with all team leads present
- [ ] **Gate**: Circuit breakers deployed and tested; first game day completed; DR test passed

**L2 → L3**
- [ ] Deploy Gremlin, LitmusChaos, or AWS FIS for structured chaos injection
- [ ] Define chaos experiment library: pod kill, network latency injection, resource exhaustion, dependency timeout
- [ ] Run quarterly game days with hypothesis-driven experiments (hypothesis: "if we kill 50% of notification-dispatcher pods, queue depth stays < 10,000 within 60 seconds")
- [ ] Define resilience SLOs: system must recover from defined failure classes within specified time
- [ ] **Gate**: Chaos tool deployed; 5+ experiments defined; quarterly cadence established

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Run chaos experiments automatically in staging on every new service deployment
- [ ] Build blast radius scoring: for each experiment, what was the measured impact scope?
- [ ] Build resilience scorecard per service: which experiments passed, which failed, which are pending?
- [ ] Integrate resilience score into Bar Raiser review (new services must pass baseline chaos before production)
- [ ] **Gate**: Chaos in staging CI pipeline; resilience scorecard live; Bar Raiser gate enforced

**L4 → L5**
- [ ] Implement production chaos with automatic safety bounds (only during business hours, only for services with auto-rollback, only if error budget > 50%)
- [ ] Implement resilience regression detection: if a service that previously passed chaos now fails, block its deployment
- [ ] Feed chaos results into RMC: view per service — resilience score, last experiment date, next scheduled experiment
- [ ] **Gate**: Production chaos running safely; resilience regression detection blocking bad deployments

### Dimension 9 — Platform Reliability

**What it governs**: The reliability of the substrate itself — Kubernetes (TKe), RabbitMQ/Kafka (MW), databases (DB), network and segmentation (FW, Illumio), GitLab (GIT), and Vault. These platforms are dependencies for everything else. If they have no SLOs, the services built on them cannot have meaningful SLOs either.

**Key principle**: A production service's SLO cannot exceed the SLO of its most critical underpinning platform. If TKe has 99.9% availability, no service on TKe can credibly claim 99.99%.

#### Maturity Level Descriptors

| Level | What exists |
|-------|------------|
| L0 | Platform health assumed. No SLOs on platforms. No platform dashboards. |
| L1 | Basic uptime monitoring on platforms. No SLIs defined. |
| L2 | Platform dashboards exist. SLOs defined for Tier 1 platforms (TKe, RabbitMQ, Vault). |
| L3 | Platform SLOs enforced. Platform on-call defined. Platform incidents tracked separately from service incidents. Dependency SLO ceiling enforced (service SLO capped at platform SLO). |
| L4 | Platform predictive maintenance: TKe node anomaly detection, RabbitMQ broker health forecasting. Platform capacity separately modeled. |
| L5 | Platforms self-heal from defined failure classes. Platform SLO health in RMC alongside service SLOs. Platform incidents visible with dependent service blast radius. |

#### Implementation Steps and SLO Targets per Platform

| Platform | L2 SLO | L3 SLO | L5 SLO | Key SLIs |
|---------|--------|--------|--------|---------|
| TKe (Kubernetes) | 99.9% | 99.95% | 99.99% | API server latency < 1s p99; node ready rate; pod scheduling latency |
| RabbitMQ | 99.9% | 99.95% | 99.99% | Broker availability; publish success rate; consumer lag < threshold |
| Vault | 99.99% | 99.995% | 99.999% | Secret retrieval latency < 100ms; seal rate = 0 |
| GitLab CI/CD | 99.5% | 99.9% | 99.95% | Pipeline success rate; pipeline execution latency |
| DB Platform | 99.95% | 99.99% | 99.999% | Query latency p99; replication lag < 1s; connection pool < 80% |
| Network/FW | 99.99% | 99.995% | 99.999% | Packet loss rate < 0.01%; firewall change-induced incident rate = 0 |

**L0 → L3**
- [ ] Define SLIs and SLOs for each platform in the table above
- [ ] Deploy platform-specific dashboards (kube-state-metrics, RabbitMQ exporter, Vault telemetry, etc.)
- [ ] Assign on-call rotation to each platform team (TKe, MW, DB, GIT, FW)
- [ ] Enforce dependency SLO ceiling: document and enforce in the SLO architecture
- [ ] **Gate**: All Tier 1 platforms have SLOs measured and dashboards live

**L3 → L5**
- [ ] Deploy TKe predictive maintenance: node resource pressure anomaly detection with pre-drain before failure
- [ ] Deploy RabbitMQ predictive capacity: broker memory and file descriptor pressure forecasting
- [ ] Implement platform-level self-healing: TKe node auto-drain on pressure; RabbitMQ queue auto-shard on depth; Vault auto-unseal on seal
- [ ] Build platform dependency map: show in RMC which services are impacted when a platform degrades
- [ ] **Gate**: Platform self-healing tested; platform SLO trend improving; platform RMC view live

### Dimension 10 — Decision Intelligence (The RMC)

**What it governs**: The integration of all nine other dimensions into a single operational surface — the Reliability Management Console (RMC) — where every decision is data-driven, context is pre-assembled, actions are suggested, and execution is push-button.

This is the Level 5 end state. Every other dimension feeds into it.

#### What the RMC Is

The RMC is not a dashboard. It is an operational control surface. The difference:
- A dashboard **shows** you information and expects you to figure out what to do
- The RMC **shows** you information, **tells** you what decision is pending, **suggests** the action, and **executes** it when you approve

The RMC has five views:

**View 1 — System Health Overview** (Audience: All tiers)
- Service health map (dependency topology, color-coded by SLO status)
- Platform health strip (TKe, RabbitMQ, DB, Vault, GitLab — all with SLO indicator)
- Error budget summary per service (% remaining + burn rate + projected exhaustion time)
- Active incidents with AI-assigned root cause confidence score
- In-flight deployments with canary SLO health

**View 2 — Capacity and Flow Management** (Audience: On-call SRE, engineering manager)
- Per-service: current load, headroom %, trajectory (growing/stable/declining)
- Per RabbitMQ queue: depth, consumer rate, publish rate, time-to-DLQ-breach
- Dependent flow graph: if you scale service A, which other services will see load change?
- Suggested scaling actions with one-click approval
- Business event calendar overlay (planned promotions, launches)

**View 3 — Incident Intelligence** (Audience: On-call SRE)
- Active incidents with correlated context: metric graph + log sample + trace + deployment change + suggested cause
- Blast radius map: which services and user journeys are currently impacted?
- Available auto-remediation actions with safety bounds shown
- Runbook link embedded in incident card
- One-click actions: scale out, rollback deployment, open circuit breaker, page SME

**View 4 — Change Safety** (Audience: SRE, Release Management)
- All in-flight deployments with canary SLO status
- Change risk scores for pending changes
- Error budget status (is this a good time to deploy high-risk changes?)
- Deployment freeze status (auto-activated if budget < 10%)
- One-click: promote canary, pause canary, rollback canary

**View 5 — Reliability Scorecard** (Audience: Director/VP, Leadership)
- SLO compliance by service (traffic-light, 30-day rolling)
- DORA metrics with trend vs. prior month
- Toil ratio per team
- Incident trend (P1/P2 count, MTTR trend)
- Error budget health heatmap
- Top 3 reliability risks with AI-generated business impact estimate

#### The RMQ Console Scenario — End-to-End

> You open View 2 (Capacity and Flow Management). You see:
>
> `dnd-priority-high` queue depth: 14,200 messages (threshold 10,000 — breached)
> `notification-dispatcher` p99 latency: 892ms (SLO: 500ms — BREACHING)
> Consumer rate: 3,100 msg/min → Publish rate: 5,400 msg/min → Gap: 2,300 msg/min
> Projected DLQ breach: 11 minutes at current rate
>
> System analysis (pre-computed, shown automatically):
> - Root pattern: Consumer throughput degraded — CPU throttled on 12/15 dispatcher pods
> - Dependent flows identified: opt-out-confirmation (will see +35% load in 3 min), delivery-receipt (will see +18% load in 5 min), fallback-sms (will see +8% load in 8 min)
>
> Suggested actions (shown with safety bounds):
> ✓ Scale notification-dispatcher: 15 → 22 replicas (within Karpenter node capacity)
> ✓ Scale opt-out-confirmation: 4 → 8 replicas (within quota)
> ✓ Scale delivery-receipt: 6 → 9 replicas (within quota)
> ✓ Pre-open circuit breaker on fallback-sms (protect downstream from cascade)
>
> You review the suggestion. The reasoning is sound. You click "Approve All."
> Within 90 seconds: dispatcher at 22 replicas, queue depth stabilizing, latency dropping, opt-out scaling in progress.
> The SLO holds. No SNOW ticket. No Slack thread. No war room.

This is not science fiction. It requires:
- KEDA configured on all consumers (Dimension 5)
- RabbitMQ metrics flowing into Prometheus (Dimension 1)
- Service dependency map in telemetry (Dimension 1)
- SLO breach detection active (Dimension 2)
- AI capacity trajectory model (Dimension 5 L4)
- Circuit breaker automation (Dimension 4)
- RMC integration layer connecting all of the above (Dimension 10)

#### Maturity Level Descriptors for Decision Intelligence

| Level | What exists |
|-------|------------|
| L0 | No integrated view. Information in separate tools, no correlation. |
| L1 | Grafana dashboards exist. Multiple screens required. Manual interpretation. |
| L2 | Team-level dashboards consolidated. Correlated metric/log view. |
| L3 | SLO dashboards per service with burn rate. Incident view with linked context. |
| L4 | AI-generated incident summaries. Suggested actions with supporting data. Multi-service correlated view. |
| L5 | RMC: all five views live. Push-button execution. Human governs; machine executes. |

#### Implementation Steps for Decision Intelligence

**L0 → L3**: Delivered by Dimensions 1–3 (observability, SLOs, incident intelligence). Decision Intelligence is the integration layer — it cannot be built before the underlying dimensions reach L3.

**L3 → L4**
- [ ] Build unified incident view: correlated MELT + AI cause + blast radius + runbook in one card
- [ ] Build capacity view: per-service load + trajectory + dependent flow impact
- [ ] Add AI action suggestion engine: for each incident or capacity event, compute suggested action + safety bounds
- [ ] Integrate with all execution systems: Kubernetes API (for scaling), Argo Rollouts (for canary), PagerDuty (for escalation), SNOW (for ticket)
- [ ] **Gate**: Unified incident view live; capacity view with trajectory live; action suggestions populated with safety bounds

**L4 → L5**
- [ ] Build RMC with all five views as described above
- [ ] Implement push-button execution: approve button triggers Kubernetes API, Argo Rollouts, or runbook-as-code
- [ ] Implement human governance layer: high-risk actions require dual approval; low-risk actions require single approval; auto-approved actions logged for review
- [ ] Build reliability scorecard view for leadership (View 5)
- [ ] Implement feedback loop: every RMC action (approved, rejected, overridden) feeds AI model
- [ ] **Gate**: All five views live; push-button execution tested; feedback loop active; leadership using View 5 monthly

These are the gaps that prevent organizations from progressing through the maturity levels. Each one is real, common, and addressable.

### Gap 1 — Telemetry Exists But Nobody Acts On It

**What it looks like**: Splunk has 2TB/day. Engineers open it only during incidents. 95% of dashboards are never viewed. SLOs are "defined" in a spreadsheet.

**Why it happens**: Telemetry was built by the observability team to prove the platform works, not to drive decisions. Nobody defined what decision each metric enables.

**How to break through**: For every metric, write the decision it enables. If no decision maps to it, delete the metric. For every dashboard, define its audience and cadence (who opens this, when, and what decision does it inform). If no answer exists, retire the dashboard.

**Framework fix**: Dimension 10 (Decision Intelligence) is the forcing function. Build the RMC and work backwards to identify which telemetry is required to support each RMC view. Everything else is noise.

---

### Gap 2 — SLOs Defined But Never Enforced

**What it looks like**: SLOs exist in a document. Error budgets are calculated. The budget is at 2% remaining. A new feature release is scheduled. It ships anyway.

**Why it happens**: Error budget policy has no organizational authority. SRE defines the budget; product management controls the release decision. Without an escalation path, the budget is decorative.

**How to break through**: Error budget policy must be signed by VP Engineering, not just SRE COE. The policy must define automatic consequences (automated release freeze notification, required SRE sign-off on next release) that activate without a human deciding to enforce them. This is an organizational governance decision, not a technical one.

**Framework fix**: Dimension 2 gate criteria requires the error budget policy to be signed by VP Engineering before the level is considered achieved.

---

### Gap 3 — Automation Exists But Is Not Trusted

**What it looks like**: KEDA is configured. Auto-rollback is wired. When it fires, engineers override it manually within 60 seconds because they "don't trust it."

**Why it happens**: The automation was deployed without being tested visibly. Engineers have no evidence that it worked correctly in the past. One false-positive rollback in a high-pressure incident destroyed trust permanently.

**How to break through**: Every auto-action must produce a visible, human-readable audit log: "At 14:23:01, auto-rollback triggered because p99 latency crossed 892ms threshold. Deployed version v3.4.1 → v3.4.0. Post-rollback SLO status: healthy. Confidence: HIGH." Engineers must see the automation working correctly dozens of times before they trust it during an incident.

**Framework fix**: Dimension 4 L3 gate criteria requires automation to be tested in staging with the audit log visible to engineers before it is activated in production.

---

### Gap 4 — AI Anomaly Detection Fires Too Many False Positives

**What it looks like**: Dynatrace/Splunk MLTK is deployed. It generates 200 anomaly alerts per day. Engineers stop looking at them within a week.

**Why it happens**: AI models were trained without seasonal baselines. Business-hours traffic variation looks like an anomaly to a model that doesn't understand business hours. No feedback loop exists — when engineers dismiss an alert as noise, the model doesn't learn.

**How to break through**: (1) Train models on at least 90 days of data including full seasonal patterns before activating alerts. (2) Implement mandatory feedback loop: every alert dismissal requires a one-click label (noise / actionable / duplicate). (3) Track model accuracy weekly. Alert on model degradation. Retrain on a schedule.

**Framework fix**: Dimension 1 L4 gate criteria requires false positive rate < 20% before AIOps is declared active. Feedback loop is a required implementation step for L4 → L5.

---

### Gap 5 — Security Changes Bypass Reliability Controls

**What it looks like**: A critical CVE is announced at 3pm Friday. Cybersecurity pushes an emergency patch to all services by 8pm. Three services fail their health checks on restart. P1 at 9pm.

**Why it happens**: Security teams operate under their own urgency model. In their view, the security risk of waiting outweighs the reliability risk of a fast patch. They are not wrong — but the framing is binary. A patch can be both urgent AND safe.

**How to break through**: Emergency patch pipeline (Dimension 8 L4). The pipeline is pre-built and rehearsed before a critical CVE hits. When the CVE arrives, the security team triggers the pipeline, which automatically rebuilds images, runs BVT in staging, validates SLO, and deploys with auto-rollback. The patch ships in 4 hours instead of 5 hours — and safely.

**Framework fix**: The emergency patch pipeline is a Phase 3 deliverable. It must be rehearsed quarterly (drill: simulate CVE, trigger pipeline, measure end-to-end time and rollback success rate).

---

### Gap 6 — Cross-Team Coordination Collapses Under Incident Pressure

**What it looks like**: The RACI exists. Every team knows their role in theory. During a P1 at 2am, the DB team can't be reached, the Illumio team doesn't know they're needed, and SRE engineers spend 45 minutes investigating a problem that the Firewall team could identify in 5 minutes.

**Why it happens**: The RACI was documented but the human relationships and protocols were never rehearsed. Nobody knows who to call, how to escalate, or what information to bring.

**How to break through**: (1) On-call contact for every team posted in a shared channel and updated monthly. (2) Quarterly incident simulation that exercises the cross-team escalation paths. (3) Every runbook identifies which team to escalate to for each symptom class. (4) MIM Team maintains a live roster of on-call contacts, updated at every shift change.

**Framework fix**: Dimension 3 L3 gate criteria includes cross-team escalation paths tested in a quarterly simulation.

---

### Gap 7 — Platform Reliability Is Assumed, Not Measured

**What it looks like**: TKe has no SLO. RabbitMQ has no SLO. When TKe's etcd latency climbs to 800ms, pods take 40 seconds to schedule, and every service's latency SLO appears to be breaching — but the root cause is the platform, not the services. SRE investigates application code for 2 hours before someone checks etcd.

**Why it happens**: The platform teams never defined SLOs because nobody asked them to. SRE treats the platform as infrastructure, not as a service.

**How to break through**: Dimension 9 explicitly requires platform SLOs as a gate. The dependency SLO ceiling principle makes this a logical necessity: you cannot define a 99.99% SLO on a service that runs on a platform with 99.9% SLO.

**Framework fix**: Phase 1 includes platform SLO definition as a required gate item. Platform on-call is defined as part of Phase 1.

---

### Gap 8 — The RMC Degrades Without Data Feedback Loops

**What it looks like**: The RMC is built. AI actions are suggested. Engineers override them manually 40% of the time. The AI model doesn't see these overrides. The model keeps suggesting the same wrong action. Engineers stop using the RMC for decisions. It becomes an expensive dashboard.

**Why it happens**: Level 5 is not a deployment state — it is a continuously maintained operational state. AI models degrade without new training data. If engineer behavior (overrides, approvals, rejections) doesn't feed the model, the model calcifies.

**How to break through**: Feedback loops are not optional. Every action taken or not taken through the RMC is a training signal. Build the feedback infrastructure before the RMC goes live — it is much harder to retrofit. Define model accuracy SLOs and alert when they degrade.

**Framework fix**: Dimension 10 L5 gate criteria explicitly requires the feedback loop to be active and producing retraining data before Level 5 is declared achieved.

## Quick Reference — PREF Framework Summary

```
PLATFORM RELIABILITY ENABLEMENT FRAMEWORK (PREF)

10 Dimensions × 6 Levels = Your reliability maturity map

Dimensions:
  1. Observability Foundation    — Can you see it?
  2. SLO/SLI Architecture        — Can you measure it?
  3. Incident Intelligence        — Can you detect and respond to it?
  4. Toil Elimination            — Can you stop doing it manually?
  5. Capacity Management         — Can you scale ahead of it?
  6. Change Safety               — Can you change it without breaking it?
  7. Resilience Engineering      — Can you prove it survives failure?
  8. Security Reliability        — Can you secure it without breaking it?
  9. Platform Reliability        — Is the substrate you run on reliable?
 10. Decision Intelligence       — Can you act on it from one screen?

Levels:
  L0 — Blind       L1 — Reactive     L2 — Aware
  L3 — Proactive   L4 — Predictive   L5 — Autonomous

Phases:
  Phase 1 (L0→L2): Foundation    — Get visible
  Phase 2 (L2→L3): Proactive     — Get ahead
  Phase 3 (L3→L4): Predictive    — AI assists
  Phase 4 (L4→L5): Autonomous    — Machine executes, human governs

The end state:
  You look at one screen.
  It shows what is happening, what will happen, and what to do about it.
  You approve.
  It executes.
  Reliability is no longer a fire you fight.
  It is a system you govern.
```
