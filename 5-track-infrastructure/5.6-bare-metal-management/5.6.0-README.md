# Bare Metal Management — Director/VP Interview Prep Framework

**Target**: SRE Manager / Principal SRE preparing for Director or VP Platform Engineering interviews  
**Context**: T-Mobile enterprise telecom — hybrid physical datacenter + cloud, EKS, Splunk, incident command  
**Goal**: Command the bare metal conversation at Director level — not as a hands-on operator, but as the person who decides when bare metal is the right call, how to procure it, how to operate it reliably, and when to get off it.

---

## Why Bare Metal Still Matters at Director Level

Most Director/VP interviews in platform engineering will surface bare metal in one of three ways:

1. **Strategy question**: "We have a large physical footprint. What's your philosophy on rationalization vs. retention?"
2. **Technical depth question**: "Walk me through how you'd manage a fleet of 500 servers." 
3. **Cost question**: "When does owning hardware make more sense than cloud?"

You do not need to know how to rack a server. You need to know how to decide, fund, and govern bare metal — and be able to speak to the operational depth that makes those decisions credible.

## How to Use This for Interview Prep

### For a phone screen (30–45 min)
Read Chapter 1 and Chapter 8. Know the TCO crossover argument and be able to name three workload types that justify bare metal. Know the decision tree in Chapter 8 cold.

### For a technical deep-dive (60–90 min)
Add Chapters 3, 5, and 7. Be ready to walk through how you'd monitor a fleet, how IPMI works at a conceptual level, and how bare metal K8s differs from EKS operationally.

### For a panel or exec presentation
Chapter 8 plus the procurement RFI template. If asked "how would you approach a server refresh program," the RFI template is your answer structure.

### For a case study question
The Hardware Failure Response Playbook is a complete incident response story. RAID Levels Reference backs up storage design reasoning.

## Key Director-Level Themes

**1. Bare metal is a workload routing decision, not a legacy burden.**  
The question is never "should we have bare metal" — it's "which workloads belong there, and what is the total cost of that routing decision over a 3-year horizon."

**2. Out-of-band management is the difference between 10 engineers managing 100 servers and 10 engineers managing 10,000.**  
IPMI/iDRAC/iLO is the control plane for physical infrastructure. If you can't address a server without walking the datacenter floor, you don't have a platform — you have an appliance collection.

**3. Kubernetes on bare metal is a capability investment, not a cost-cutting measure.**  
It removes the hypervisor tax on GPU/HPC workloads. It introduces control plane ownership overhead. Know when EKS wins and when it doesn't.

**4. Hardware SLOs are the gap most SRE teams have.**  
You are monitoring the OS. You are probably not monitoring the hardware underneath it. That gap is where hardware surprises come from.

**5. Provisioning automation is the multiplier.**  
An engineer who provisions manually tops out at 50–100 servers. An engineer with a proper provisioning pipeline manages 1,000+.

## Prerequisites

- Comfortable with Linux system administration concepts
- Basic networking (VLANs, DHCP, PXE boot flow)
- Kubernetes fundamentals (pods, nodes, deployments, CSI, CNI)
- Incident command and on-call operational experience
- Capital planning and vendor management familiarity
