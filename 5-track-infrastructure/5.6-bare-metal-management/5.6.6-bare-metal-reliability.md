# Chapter 6 — Bare Metal Reliability

## Hardware Failure is Probabilistic, Not Random

Hardware does not fail randomly. It fails according to well-characterized statistical distributions, and those distributions are documented in vendor datasheets, JEDEC standards, and field failure data that cloud providers and large enterprises publish. Understanding the failure rates and failure modes of each hardware component lets you design a sparing strategy that converts unplanned failures into planned maintenance events.

The fundamental shift from reactive to predictive hardware operations is the reliability engineering story for bare metal.

---

## Hardware Failure Modes by Component

### CPU

CPU failures are rare — MTTFs of >1 million hours are typical. When they do occur, they manifest as:
- **Machine check exceptions (MCE)**: CPU detects an internal error; kernel panics. The MCE logs in `/var/log/mcelog` or via `mcelog --client`.
- **Thermal failure**: prolonged operation above design temperature accelerates electromigration in CPU interconnects. Manifests as intermittent MCEs progressing to hard failures.
- **Voltage regulator failure**: the VRMs (Voltage Regulator Modules) near the CPU are more likely to fail than the CPU itself. VRM failure = no CPU power = no POST.

**Detection**: MCE events in kernel logs, `mcelog`, or IPMI SEL. Temperature sensor alerts. **Replacement**: CPU failures require board-level or full server swap — there is no field CPU replacement in most enterprise server configurations; the socket is permanently populated.

### Memory

Memory is the highest-failure-rate major component in servers (excluding disks). The failure progression is predictable:

1. **Corrected single-bit errors (CSBE)**: ECC corrects transparently. First sign of DIMM degradation. Monitor IPMI SEL for increasing frequency.
2. **Multi-bit uncorrectable errors (MBE)**: ECC cannot correct. Kernel panic. Server crash.
3. **Hard failure**: DIMM slot not recognized at POST. Server cannot boot, or boots with reduced capacity.

**DIMM failure rates**: industry field data suggests ~0.5–1.5% annual failure rate per DIMM under normal conditions. For a server with 32 DIMMs (a fully populated high-memory server), that implies one DIMM replacement every 2–6 years per server. At fleet scale (100 servers × 32 DIMMs = 3,200 DIMMs), expect 16–48 DIMM replacements per year.

**ECC correction rate trending**: a DIMM correcting 1 error per month is different from a DIMM correcting 100 errors per day. The correction rate trend is the failure prediction signal. Set an alert on correction rate acceleration, not just on any correction event.

### Disk (Rotating HDD)

The most common hardware failure mode. Disk failures follow a bathtub curve:
- **Infant mortality** (0–3 months): early failures from manufacturing defects. Run-in ("burn-in") testing during provisioning catches these before production.
- **Useful life** (3 months – 3 years): failures are random; characterized by AFR (Annualized Failure Rate).
- **Wear-out** (>3–5 years): failure rate increases as mechanical components wear. AFR can double or triple.

**AFR by drive type** (industry field data from Backblaze, Google, cloud providers):
| Drive Type | Annual Failure Rate |
|------------|-------------------|
| Consumer HDD | 1.5–3% |
| Enterprise SAS | 0.5–1% |
| Enterprise NVMe SSD | 0.1–0.5% |
| Consumer SSD | 0.1–0.3% |

For a 100-server deployment with 12 drives per server (1,200 drives), enterprise SAS at 1% AFR = 12 drive replacements per year. Plan spare drive inventory accordingly.

### NIC

NIC failures present in two distinct modes:

- **Link flap** (intermittent link up/down): usually physical — cable damage, SFP transceiver degradation, switch port issue. Does not cause data corruption; bonding handles failover. Investigate and resolve the physical cause.
- **Hard failure** (link stays down or NIC unresponsive): NIC hardware failure. For bonded NICs, bonding failover to the surviving NIC. Server remains operational. Schedule NIC replacement at next maintenance window.
- **Silent packet drop** (link up, no errors, but traffic drops): the insidious case. NIC firmware bug or hardware degradation where the NIC reports healthy but silently drops packets. Detected only through end-to-end latency/throughput monitoring or packet capture.

### PSU

Servers with redundant PSUs (N+1 PSU configuration) tolerate single PSU failure without impact. The alert is operational: repair the redundancy before a second PSU failure.

PSU failure modes:
- **Complete failure**: PSU shuts down; monitoring detects PSU status change via IPMI. Replace within 48 hours.
- **Degraded output**: PSU voltage slightly out of tolerance. IPMI voltage sensors detect this. Can damage other components if not addressed.
- **Fan failure within PSU**: PSU fan fails, PSU overheats, PSU shuts down to protect itself. Often the prelude to complete PSU failure.

## RAID Levels for Reliability

Choosing a RAID level is a reliability decision with performance implications. For bare metal, this is your design choice — there is no "default" like cloud storage abstraction.

| RAID Level | Min Disks | Fault Tolerance | Usable Capacity | Read Perf | Write Perf | Rebuild Impact | Write Hole Risk | Best Use Case |
|------------|-----------|----------------|----------------|-----------|------------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| RAID 0 | 2 | 0 disks | N × disk | High | High | N/A | No | Scratch/temp only. No fault tolerance. |
| RAID 1 | 2 | 1 disk | 50% | High | Same as single | Fast (copy) | No | Boot drives, small critical data |
| RAID 5 | 3 | 1 disk | (N-1) × disk | High | ~25% penalty | Heavy (reconstruct) | **YES** | General-purpose storage |
| RAID 6 | 4 | 2 disks | (N-2) × disk | High | ~40% penalty | Heaviest | **YES** | Large arrays, critical data |
| RAID 10 | 4 | 1 per mirror pair | 50% | High | High | Fast (copy) | No | Databases, high I/O |
| RAID 50 | 6 | 1 per RAID 5 group | Variable | Very high | High | Heavy | YES | Large arrays needing performance |
| RAID 60 | 8 | 2 per RAID 6 group | Variable | Very high | High | Heaviest | YES | Maximum protection + performance |
| JBOD | 1 | 0 | 100% | Single disk | Single disk | N/A | No | Software-defined storage (Ceph, ZFS) |

**RAID 5 write hole**: a RAID 5 write operation is not atomic. If a server crashes mid-write across a stripe, the parity can become inconsistent with the data. On RAID 5, if you then also lose a disk, you may have silent data corruption after rebuild. Mitigated by: NVRAM-backed RAID controller cache (flushes write atomically), or ZFS RAID-Z (no write hole).

**Rebuild duration rule of thumb**: 1 TB of data takes approximately 4–8 hours to rebuild under load (I/O competition from application and rebuild). For 8 TB disks: 32–64 hours of rebuild window during which RAID 5 is in "one disk away from data loss" state. This is why RAID 6 (2-disk fault tolerance) is preferred for large arrays.

**Director choice**: for most server workloads, RAID 10 or RAID 6 for data volumes. RAID 1 for boot drives. JBOD for Ceph or ZFS nodes (software manages the redundancy).

## Memory ECC: Operational Reality

ECC in enterprise servers is:
- **Single-bit correction**: the server silently fixes the error and logs it to IPMI SEL. Normal operations continue. No user impact. This is expected and managed.
- **Multi-bit error (UEFI/BIOS Machine Check)**: the server cannot correct the error. It asserts a machine check exception. Behavior depends on OS configuration:
  - Linux default: kernel panic, server crashes
  - Linux with `mce=tolerant` (not recommended for production): attempt to continue; data corruption risk
  - Hardware (BIOS): may also force restart depending on firmware settings

**DIMM identification from SEL**: when an ECC error is logged, the IPMI SEL entry identifies the DIMM slot (e.g., "DIMM A1"). This maps to the physical DIMM label inside the server. Replacement requires identifying the correct slot — the server service manual maps slot labels to physical locations.

```
# Example SEL entry for ECC error:
# 0x012 | 04/15/2025 14:23:11 | Memory #0x21 | Correctable ECC error | Asserted
# Look for: Memory sensor, DIMM slot identifier, error type
```

## NIC Bonding Failure Modes in Detail

When one NIC in an LACP bond fails:
- The remaining NIC takes all traffic
- LACP partner port detects the link down; removes failed port from aggregation
- All active connections survive if they were on the surviving NIC; connections on the failed NIC may time out depending on LACP failover timing

**The critical period**: in LACP fast mode, failover takes ~1 second. In slow mode (default), ~30 seconds. During this window, half of your traffic (whatever was routed to the failed NIC) is dropped. For stateless applications, this is a brief disruption. For stateful connections (database sessions, WebSocket connections), 30 seconds of connection failure is a visible outage.

**LACP vs Active-Backup failover timing:**
| Mode | Typical Failover | Detection Mechanism |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| LACP fast mode | ~1 second | LACP PDU timeout |
| LACP slow mode | ~30 seconds | LACP PDU timeout |
| Active-Backup | ~100–200ms | Link down detection |

For applications with tight availability SLOs, use LACP fast mode or Active-Backup, and test failover timing in a lab before relying on it in production.

## Disk Failure Response: Operational Sequence

1. Alert fires: RAID degraded, SMART threshold, predictive failure flag
2. Identify failed drive: RAID management tool identifies slot; BMC can blink the drive LED (`storcli64 /c0/e252/s3 set locate`)
3. Check for hot spare: if configured, rebuild begins automatically
4. If no hot spare: order replacement drive immediately; rebuild begins on drive arrival
5. Physical replacement: if hot-swappable bay, drive swap does not require server downtime; if not hot-swappable, schedule downtime
6. Post-replacement validation: confirm rebuild completes successfully; verify RAID status returns to Optimal; check for new SMART errors on replacement drive (infant mortality check)
7. Log the event: asset record update, incident ticket, trending data

## Director Frame: Sparing Strategy as Reliability Engineering

The conversation about hardware reliability at Director level is ultimately about sparing strategy: how many spare drives, DIMMs, NICs, and PSUs do you stock, and how do you decide?

The answer is probabilistic:

> "We calculate expected failure rates per component type from vendor AFR data and field experience, then size spare inventory to cover 95th percentile demand over a 30-day replenishment cycle. For disks, that means stocking approximately 2% of fleet drive count as hot spares plus depot spares. For DIMMs, we stock by server class — high-memory DB servers have dedicated DIMM spares, compute tier servers share a pool. Our sparing strategy converts unplanned hardware failures into planned maintenance events. The only hardware failure that should cause an unplanned incident is an unanticipated simultaneous multi-component failure — and we design RAID levels and server redundancy to tolerate those within defined parameters."
