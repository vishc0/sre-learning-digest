# Chapter 8 — Director Decision Framework

## Making Hardware Strategy a Capital Efficiency Story

Hardware strategy at Director and VP level is not a technical conversation. It is a capital efficiency conversation with technical constraints embedded in it. Finance needs to understand what they are buying, why, and what the return looks like. Engineering needs to understand the operational commitments implied by the hardware choices. This chapter provides the frameworks to navigate both conversations.

---

## The Workload Placement Decision Tree

Before any hardware procurement discussion, the workload must be classified. This decision tree applies to any new workload or workload migration:

```
Is this workload steady-state or bursty?
├── BURSTY (>50% utilization swing, unpredictable) → Cloud-native (EKS/EC2)
│   Exception: network functions requiring dedicated hardware → bare metal cloud (Equinix Metal)
│
└── STEADY-STATE (>60% sustained utilization, predictable)
    │
    ├── Does it require kernel-bypass networking, GPU at scale, or direct hardware access?
    │   ├── YES → Bare metal (owned or bare metal cloud)
    │   └── NO → Continue below
    │
    ├── What is the compliance posture?
    │   ├── Requires dedicated physical hardware, no multi-tenancy → Bare metal
    │   └── Cloud compliance acceptable → Continue below
    │
    ├── TCO horizon?
    │   ├── >24 months steady state → Run TCO model (bare metal likely wins)
    │   └── <18 months or uncertain → Cloud (avoid CapEx for uncertain workloads)
    │
    └── Default → Cloud-native, revisit at 12-month TCO checkpoint
```

**The key discipline**: run the TCO model before the purchase order, not after. A workload committed to cloud for 3 years that was actually a bare metal candidate is money left on the table. A workload committed to bare metal that is actually bursty is capital tied up in idle hardware.

## Hardware Procurement Strategy: RFI/RFQ Process

### Phase 1: Define Requirements Before Talking to Vendors

The failure mode in hardware procurement is starting with vendor conversations before requirements are locked. Vendors will propose what they sell, not what you need. Requirements first:

- **Workload profile**: what is the compute, memory, storage, and network requirement per server? (Use performance benchmark targets, not vague descriptors)
- **Volume and timeline**: how many servers, phased delivery schedule, deployment target date?
- **Lifecycle horizon**: 3-year, 4-year, or 5-year refresh cycle? This affects depreciation, warranty terms, and support contract length.
- **Operational requirements**: what management tooling must integrate with? (Foreman, MAAS, Ansible, Terraform). What OS is certified?

### Phase 2: Benchmark Requirements

Do not accept vendor performance claims at face value. Specify your benchmark requirements in the RFI/RFQ:

| Benchmark | What It Measures | Use Case |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| **SPECint_rate2017** | Integer compute throughput (multi-core) | General application servers |
| **STREAM** | Memory bandwidth | Memory-intensive workloads (databases, HPC) |
| **fio** (custom profile) | Storage I/O (IOPS, throughput, latency) | Storage-intensive workloads |
| **iperf3** | Network throughput | Network-function validation |
| **HPL (Linpack)** | Floating point compute (used for HPC rankings) | HPC, GPU validation |
| **MLPerf** | AI/ML inference throughput | GPU inference workloads |

Require vendors to provide benchmark results on the proposed configuration, run against your specified workload profile. Benchmark results are part of the contractual specification — if delivered hardware does not meet benchmark, it is a contractual nonconformance.

### Phase 3: Reference Architecture Validation

Before ordering at scale, run a pilot: order 4–8 servers representing the proposed configuration. Run the workload in a staging environment for 4–6 weeks. Validate:
- Performance meets benchmark
- OS provisioning pipeline works
- Management tooling integration works
- Firmware update process validated
- Any hardware issues discovered and resolved

Never skip the pilot. The cost of discovering a configuration problem after a 500-server order is orders of magnitude higher than the cost of a 4-server pilot.

## Vendor Relationships: Dell, HPE, Supermicro, Lenovo

At Director level, vendor relationship is a strategic asset. Your vendor rep is the escalation path when a 4-hour parts replacement commitment is not being met at 3 AM. The relationship quality matters for incident response.

| Vendor | Enterprise Position | Best For | Avoid When | Pricing Tier |
|--------|--------------------|---------|-----------|----|
| **Dell EMC (PowerEdge)** | Largest market share; strongest enterprise sales and support infrastructure | General-purpose enterprise, large fleet, strong iDRAC ecosystem | Budget-constrained; premium pricing | $$$ |
| **HPE (ProLiant)** | Strong in telco, HPC, dense compute; ProLiant DL series proven | Telco NFV, HPC, HPE OneView shops | Need cost optimization; HPE Pointnext support can be expensive | $$$ |
| **Supermicro** | Cost-competitive; highly configurable; strong in GPU-dense configurations | GPU clusters, cost-sensitive deployments, custom configurations | Tier 1 enterprise support requirements; long-term support contracts | $$ |
| **Lenovo (ThinkSystem)** | Strong in some verticals (financial services, manufacturing); XCC is solid | Shops with existing Lenovo relationships; XClarity integration | Broad enterprise support coverage; parts depot network smaller than Dell/HPE | $$ |

**Typical enterprise discount off list price**: Dell 25–40%, HPE 25–40%, Supermicro 10–20% (already lower list), Lenovo 20–35%. These discounts are negotiated; the starting point depends on volume and relationship.

## Support Tier Analysis: When Each Is Justified

Support tier choice is a risk management decision. The question is: what is the cost of hardware downtime vs. the cost of the premium support tier?

| Tier | Response | What You Get | When Justified |
|------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Basic (NBD)** | Next business day parts | Parts shipped next business day; remote diagnosis | Dev/test servers, low-criticality workloads |
| **4-Hour** | Technician on-site within 4 hours with parts | Engineer arrives, replaces parts | Production servers; any server in a cluster where single-server failure degrades service |
| **24x7x4** | Same as 4-hour, any time including weekends/holidays | Critical for 24/7 operations | Database primary nodes, control plane nodes, any server where failure = service impact |
| **Mission Critical** | 2-hour response, dedicated account team | Highest tier; includes proactive monitoring | Network functions, transaction processing, tier-0 systems |

**The math for T-Mobile production**: a single bare metal K8s node failure that takes the cluster from N nodes to N-1 nodes. If N-1 is still sufficient, NBD may be acceptable. If N-1 triggers SLO breach (pod scheduling fails, workloads degrade), you need 4-hour or 24x7x4. The support tier must match the availability SLO of the workload.

**Cost delta**: 4-hour support typically adds 15–25% to the annual support cost versus NBD. 24x7x4 adds another 20–30%. Over a 4-year lifecycle, support costs can equal the hardware purchase price. Include support in the TCO model.

## Refresh Strategy: 3-Year vs 4-Year vs 5-Year Cycles

| Cycle | CapEx Impact | Operational Impact | Reliability Risk | Best For |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|----------|
| **3-year** | Highest (most frequent replacement) | Lower operational complexity | Low (servers rarely leave useful life) | Performance-sensitive workloads where new-gen improvements matter |
| **4-year** | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate (some servers enter wear-out phase) | Standard enterprise; balances cost and risk |
| **5-year** | Lowest (amortized longest) | Higher (aging hardware needs more maintenance) | Higher (disk AFR increases significantly after year 4) | Cost-optimized environments with robust spare management |

**Dell and HPE ProSupport contracts** typically align with 3-year or 5-year windows, with 5-year being the maximum standard support term. Hardware older than the support contract end date becomes a liability — vendor may decline to support or quote at punitive rates.

**The depreciation angle**: most finance teams depreciate hardware over 3 years (straight-line). A 5-year lifecycle means years 4–5 are fully depreciated — the operational cost (support, power, space) is pure OpEx with no new CapEx. This is the CFO's perspective on the refresh trade-off.

**Recommendation for T-Mobile**: 4-year lifecycle for standard compute, 3-year for GPU nodes (GPU performance-per-dollar improves ~40% per generation; a 3-year-old GPU node has meaningful disadvantage vs current-gen for training workloads). 5-year for storage-dense servers where the storage capacity is the asset (drives are replaced on failure; the chassis amortizes longer).

## Making the Case to Finance

Capital requests for server hardware fail when they are presented as "we need more servers." They succeed when they are presented as a capital efficiency story.

**Frame 1 — TCO vs Cloud**:
> "Running these 50 GPU nodes on AWS P4 instances for the model inference workload costs $X/month = $Y over 3 years. Owning equivalent bare metal GPU nodes is $Z in CapEx + $W in support = ($Z+$W) over 3 years. We save $(Y-Z-W) over the period, with better performance and no cloud-provider pricing risk."

**Frame 2 — Refresh as Risk Reduction**:
> "Servers in their 5th year have a statistically higher failure rate. Proactive refresh reduces unplanned downtime risk by an estimated X hours/year, which at our revenue rate represents $Y in avoided revenue impact. The refresh CapEx is $Z. Payback period: Z/Y years."

**Frame 3 — Capacity Ahead of Growth**:
> "Our 5G traffic growth trajectory requires X% additional compute capacity by Q3. Bare metal procurement has a 12-week lead time. We need to commit by [date] or we will miss the capacity window and defer to cloud at 3x the ongoing cost."

Each frame connects hardware investment to a business outcome that finance can evaluate: savings, risk reduction, or revenue protection. Never present hardware requests as infrastructure needs in isolation.

## Director Frame: The Interview Answer on Hardware Strategy

The consolidation question — "how do you think about bare metal vs cloud?" — with the complete Director-level answer:

> "Hardware strategy is a capital allocation decision, not an infrastructure religion. My default is cloud-native for agility and operational simplicity. I route workloads to bare metal when three conditions align: the workload has specific hardware requirements that VM or cloud cannot meet at the required performance level, the utilization is predictable and sustained enough for CapEx to pencil out better than OpEx over a 3-year horizon, and the team has the operational capability to manage the additional complexity. For T-Mobile specifically — telco network functions, GPU inference at scale, and high-throughput database tiers are the categories where bare metal consistently wins. For everything else, EKS. We review workload placement quarterly against the TCO model and migrate as economics shift."
