# Datacenter Physical Security Incident Response Playbook

**Scope**: Any event involving suspected or confirmed unauthorized physical access to secured datacenter zones, including badge anomalies, tailgating alerts, and camera-detected access violations.
**Severity**: Treat as P1 until scope is determined. An unresolved physical access event is a security incident, not a facilities event.
**Primary ownership**: Physical Security team (or Facility Security Manager at colo). SRE/IT Director notified immediately. Legal and Compliance notified if insider threat indicators are present.

---

## Detection Triggers

Physical access incidents are detected through several systems — each detection method carries different initial information:

**Badge access anomaly**:
- Access denied event followed by successful access event within 5 minutes at the same door (potential credential sharing or tailgate enabling)
- Terminated employee badge used for access attempt (immediate: this badge should have been deactivated)
- Access at hours inconsistent with role or schedule (1am–5am access by someone with no documented change ticket)
- Access to zones outside the individual's authorized zone list
- Multiple failed access attempts at a door (attempted physical brute-force or confused visitor)

**Tailgating alert**:
- Mantrap / airlock interlock alarm (second door opened while first still open)
- People-counting camera alert (more people exit mantrap than authenticated)
- Tailgate detection sensor (weight, IR, or vision-based) alert at monitored doors

**Camera alert**:
- Motion detected in a zone outside business hours
- Unescorted individual detected in a zone where all access should be escorted
- Person detected in a restricted zone without a corresponding badge access event

**Escalation from facility staff**:
- Security guard or remote hands staff observing suspicious behavior
- Report from an employee who witnessed a tailgating or access concern

## Immediate Response: First 15 Minutes

**Step 1: Do not broadcast (2 minutes)**
Do NOT announce the suspected access incident in general Slack channels, open incident bridges, or to uninvolved parties. If this is an insider threat event, the subject may be monitoring communications. Initial response is need-to-know only.

**Step 2: Determine who to call first (3 minutes)**
```
Was the access to a zone containing sensitive equipment, regulated data, or
network infrastructure?
    YES → Physical Security Lead AND IT Director simultaneously
    NO  → Physical Security Lead first, IT Director within 10 minutes

Is there any indication of an active intruder still on-premises?
    YES → Facility security (armed if present) or local law enforcement (911)
          DO NOT physically confront an unknown individual
    NO  → Physical Security Lead to lead the investigation
```

**Step 3: Secure the evidence immediately (5 minutes)**
This is the most time-critical step from a compliance and legal perspective:

1. **Do NOT wipe, reset, or overwrite any logs** — badge access logs, camera footage, alarm logs, mantrap event logs are evidence
2. Notify the badge/physical access system administrator: preserve logs for the relevant time window plus 4 hours on either side
3. Notify the CCTV system operator: do NOT allow scheduled recording overwrites of the relevant footage; if the system has insufficient retention, export immediately
4. Create a dated log entry recording when you first learned of the incident and what you did

---

## Badge Log Review Procedure

**Step 4: Pull and review badge logs (15 minutes)**

**Time window to pull**: Start 24 hours before the detected event; end at present. This catches any reconnaissance access earlier in the day or day prior.

**What to extract from the badge system**:
- All access events (granted AND denied) at the affected door(s) during the window
- All access events by the individual(s) of interest during the window (full facility-wide, not just the affected door)
- All access events by personnel who accessed the affected zone in the window (to identify who may have been present)

**What to look for**:
- Sequences: denied → granted at same door within short window (credential sharing or tailgate setup)
- Timing: access events at unusual hours without a corresponding change management ticket
- Location: a user accessing a zone they have no authorized access to (this shouldn't generate a "granted" event, but it may indicate an access control misconfiguration)
- Frequency: unusually high number of access events in a short period (could indicate someone propping a door and re-entering repeatedly)
- Gaps: an individual should have an entry event AND an exit event for each zone visit; a person with entries and no corresponding exits may still be in the facility

**Correlation**: Cross-reference badge log times with camera footage at the same doors. Discrepancies between "badge shows access" and "camera shows who was there" are the key finding.

## CCTV Review Procedure

**Step 5: Pull and review camera footage (20 minutes)**

**Who has access**: Only designated security personnel and IT management with documented authorization. Do not give raw access to general IT staff.

**Chain of custody begins NOW**: Document the following before reviewing footage:
- Date/time you accessed the CCTV system
- Your name and role
- What footage you are reviewing (camera IDs, time window)
- The reason for access (incident reference number)

**Review procedure**:
1. Identify all cameras with coverage of the affected zone and access points
2. Export footage from 30 minutes before and after the detected event time
3. Review: confirm who was physically present vs. who badge logs show
4. Identify any individuals who entered the camera's view without a corresponding badge event
5. Note any individuals whose face is not clearly visible (deliberate avoidance of camera)
6. Export still images of any individuals of interest for identification

**Retention**: Do NOT delete the exported footage. Store with the incident record. Format for export: original format with timestamp overlay (do not convert or compress — this may be needed as legal evidence).

**Blind spots**: Every physical space has camera blind spots. Note which areas of the affected zone are NOT covered — this determines what the footage can and cannot rule out.

## Security Team Engagement

**Internal Physical Security**:
- Engage from the start for all access incidents
- Provide them: incident summary, badge log extract, CCTV footage access
- They lead the investigation on-site; SRE/IT lead supports from a systems and impact perspective
- Do NOT perform your own physical investigation of the space (you may contaminate evidence)

**External law enforcement**:
Involve law enforcement when:
- Evidence indicates a crime was committed (theft of equipment, data theft, vandalism)
- An intruder is confirmed to have been on-site without authorization
- There is a credible threat to personnel safety

Do NOT involve law enforcement prematurely for internal policy violations or ambiguous events — that's a personnel/HR matter. Involve them when a crime is suspected.

**Escalation within law enforcement**:
- Local police department: physical trespassing, theft
- FBI: if the incident involves network infrastructure that may have been tampered with (critical infrastructure implications)
- FCC/CPUC: if network infrastructure that is subject to regulatory requirements was potentially compromised

## Evidence Preservation Protocol

**The cardinal rule**: Do nothing that modifies, overwrites, or moves evidence without first documenting its current state.

**Digital evidence (badge logs, CCTV, alarm logs)**:
1. Export to a named, timestamped archive immediately
2. Compute and record file hash (SHA-256) of all exported evidence files — this proves the evidence wasn't modified after export
3. Store in a location with controlled access and audit logging
4. Do not share over general email — use encrypted file transfer or a secured evidence management system

**Physical evidence** (if equipment was touched, tampered with, or left behind):
1. Do not touch anything in the scene
2. Photograph in place before any physical handling
3. Contact Physical Security to manage physical evidence collection
4. Document rack IDs, equipment labels, and specific items of interest

**Log retention**: Notify your log retention systems (Splunk, SIEM) to preserve logs for the relevant time period beyond their normal retention schedule. Physical access systems, network authentication logs (for any systems in the affected zone), and environmental monitoring logs should all be preserved.

## Notification Chain

**Notify immediately** (within 30 minutes of incident classification):
- Physical Security Lead
- IT Director / SRE Director
- Facility Manager (for colo: Colo NOC and Facility Security Manager)

**Notify within 2 hours** (if incident appears to involve unauthorized access confirmed):
- Legal (in-house counsel or legal on-call)
- Compliance (if any regulated data or network infrastructure is in the affected zone)
- HR (if a current or former employee is a person of interest)

**Notify within 24 hours** (if incident is confirmed and involves regulated infrastructure):
- CISO
- Privacy Officer (if personal data was potentially accessible)
- Relevant regulatory contacts (FCC, CPUC) if network infrastructure involved

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not notify the person of interest that they are under investigation
- Do not post updates to general IT channels
- Do not share investigation details with anyone outside the need-to-know list

## Incident Documentation for Compliance Audit

Every physical security incident must be documented regardless of whether it turns out to be significant. An undocumented incident that later surfaces in an audit is far more damaging than a documented minor incident.

**Required documentation fields**:
- Incident ID (use your ITSM system)
- Date/time of detection and date/time of notification
- Detection method (badge anomaly, camera alert, staff report)
- Affected zones (specific cage/aisle/room)
- Personnel of interest (do NOT include full names in the ticket if it's an active investigation — use "Person of Interest A" and maintain a separate secured record)
- Evidence collected and preservation method
- Actions taken and timeline
- Investigation findings
- Resolution and remediation
- Compliance notifications (who was notified, when)

**Audit trail**: Every action taken during the investigation should be logged with timestamp and actor. The audit trail proves that the controls worked (or that a failure was identified and remediated) — this is the SOC 2 evidence that the physical access control control is effective.

**Post-incident access review**: Following any confirmed unauthorized access event, perform an immediate access review of the affected zone — who has access, is access appropriate, are there any other anomalies. Document this review as part of the incident record.
