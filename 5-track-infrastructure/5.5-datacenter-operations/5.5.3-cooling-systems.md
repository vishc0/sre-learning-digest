# Chapter 3: Cooling Systems — The 40% OpEx Problem

## Cooling Is Where the Money Goes

Cooling accounts for approximately 40% of datacenter operating expenditure in a typical enterprise facility. In legacy DCs with poor containment and oversized cooling plants, it's higher. Understanding cooling architecture isn't optional for a Director — it's where your single largest OpEx lever lives.

---

## CRAC vs CRAH: The Operational Difference

Both Computer Room Air Conditioning (CRAC) and Computer Room Air Handler (CRAH) units move air through a datacenter. The critical difference is how they reject heat.

**CRAC units** contain a self-contained refrigerant cycle — compressor, condenser coil, expansion valve, evaporator — within the unit itself. They operate independently of external water or chiller systems. The upside: simpler installation, no dependency on a chiller plant. The downside: compressor-based cooling is less efficient than chilled water at scale, and each unit has its own refrigerant charge and compressor to maintain.

**CRAH units** use chilled water supplied by a central chiller plant. The unit itself is essentially a fan coil — it blows room air across a chilled water coil and returns the water to the chiller for re-cooling. No refrigerant in the unit itself. The upside: central chiller plants are more efficient at scale and allow economizer (free cooling) integration. The downside: dependency on the chiller plant, chilled water piping throughout the facility, and a more complex infrastructure footprint.

**Operational implication**: A CRAC unit failure is a localized event — you lose one unit's cooling capacity and activate the N+1 spare. A chiller plant failure (in a CRAH-based system without redundancy) can take down all cooling simultaneously. This is why CRAH deployments require N+1 or 2N chiller redundancy to maintain the same effective fault tolerance as CRAC deployments.

**T-Mobile context**: Older T-Mobile-adjacent facilities may have legacy CRAC deployments. Newer or refreshed facilities trend toward CRAH with economizers as part of PUE improvement programs.

## Hot Aisle / Cold Aisle Containment

This is the single highest-leverage operational change you can make in most existing datacenters without major capital investment. Without containment, hot exhaust air from server rear panels mixes with cold supply air before servers intake it — you're re-cooling partially heated air, driving CRAC/CRAH units to work harder than necessary.

**Standard layout**: Servers are racked face-to-face (cold aisle) and back-to-back (hot aisle). Cold supply air from the raised floor or in-row units enters server front intakes. Hot exhaust exits server rears into the hot aisle. Without containment, the hot air mixes across the top of the racks and recirculates back to the cold aisle.

**Cold aisle containment (CAC)**: Encloses the cold aisle with doors at each end and a ceiling or roof above. Cold supply air is trapped in the aisle, forcing it through servers rather than mixing with hot exhaust. This is the more common implementation — easier to retrofit than HAC.

**Hot aisle containment (HAC)**: Encloses the hot aisle and routes exhaust air directly back to cooling units (via ceiling plenum or dedicated return path). Slightly more effective thermally but more complex to retrofit — requires a return air path from the hot aisle to the cooling units.

**What happens without containment**: Temperature stratification fails. Hot spots develop in specific rack positions, especially the top of racks. Servers throttle CPU frequency due to thermal limits. CRAC units run at maximum output because they're fighting recirculating hot air. PUE climbs. You add more cooling units instead of fixing the airflow problem.

**Blanking panels**: The simplest, cheapest element of containment. Empty rack units must be filled with blanking panels — without them, hot air from the rear hot aisle short-circuits through empty U-spaces to the front cold aisle. A rack with 10U of empty space and no blanking panels is constantly pulling hot air forward. This is a $5 fix per U-space that has measurable thermal impact.

## Liquid Cooling: When Air Stops Being Enough

Traditional air cooling reaches its practical limit at roughly 20–25 kW per rack. Above that, the physics of moving enough air to reject that much heat becomes impractical — the air velocity required creates noise, airflow management complexity, and still can't keep up with the thermal density.

**Direct Liquid Cooling (DLC)**: Coolant loops run to heat exchangers or cold plates mounted directly on CPUs, GPUs, and memory. The fluid absorbs heat directly from the component and carries it to a heat exchanger or cooling distribution unit (CDU). Highly efficient — liquid's heat capacity is ~3,500x that of air per unit volume. Required for AI/ML GPU clusters at 50–100+ kW/rack.

**Rear-Door Heat Exchangers (RDHx)**: A chilled water heat exchanger mounted on the rear door of a standard rack. Hot exhaust air passes through the door, is cooled, and exits at near-ambient temperature. Lower integration complexity than DLC (no changes to servers or components). Effective up to ~30–40 kW/rack. Can be retrofitted onto existing racks.

**Immersion cooling**: Servers (or just boards) submerged in dielectric fluid. Single-phase (fluid stays liquid) or two-phase (fluid boils and condenses). Extremely effective — eliminates all fans, very high density possible. Very high implementation cost and operational complexity; most commonly seen in HPC and Bitcoin mining at present. Beginning to appear in AI inference deployments.

**When liquid cooling is justified**: If your rack density exceeds 20 kW/rack, you need at least a rear-door HX solution. If you're deploying GPU clusters for AI/ML workloads, DLC is rapidly becoming the standard. The question isn't whether liquid cooling is right — it's whether your facility power and fluid infrastructure can support it.

## Cooling Failure as an Incident Type

Cooling failures are not discrete events — they cascade. Understanding the cascade is how you make the right decisions under pressure.

**Temperature thresholds** (ASHRAE A1/A2 class, most enterprise equipment):
- Inlet temperature operating range: 59–80.6°F (15–27°C) — optimal zone
- Warning threshold: 80°F / 27°C — begin investigating, prepare mitigation
- Critical threshold: 95°F / 35°C — equipment begins throttling, risk of thermal shutdown
- Emergency: 104°F+ / 40°C+ — thermal shutdowns likely, risk of component damage

**Cascade failure sequence**: Single CRAC unit fails → remaining units compensate → hot spots develop at high-density racks → servers begin thermal throttling → CPU performance degrades → workloads slow → additional servers consume more power trying to keep up → thermal load increases faster than remaining cooling can handle → temperature rises accelerate → within 15–30 minutes in a high-density environment, you're making emergency decisions.

**Cooling failures are time-critical differently than power failures**: A power failure is binary — the servers are up or they're down. A cooling failure is a slow-motion disaster that gives you a window to act but punishes delay.

## Free Cooling (Economizer Mode)

Free cooling uses outside air or water-side economization to reject datacenter heat without running mechanical refrigeration. When outside conditions allow, it's essentially free — you're paying only for fan/pump energy, not compressor energy.

**Airside economization**: Outside air is filtered and used directly or mixed with return air. Works when outside air temperature and humidity are within acceptable ranges. Climate-dependent — highly effective in the Pacific Northwest, less so in Texas August.

**Waterside economization**: Chilled water is pre-cooled using a cooling tower or dry cooler before entering the chiller. When the wet-bulb temperature is low enough, the chiller plant can be partially or fully bypassed. Broader applicability than airside — works in more climates.

**Economizer hours**: A key metric in facility design is how many hours per year the facility can run in economizer mode. A datacenter in Seattle might operate in free cooling 70% of the year; a facility in Phoenix might achieve 20%. This directly affects the achievable PUE and the OpEx case for facility selection.

## Director Frame: Cooling as a Cost Lever

The cooling conversation in an interview demonstrates operational depth. The key points to land:

Cooling is 40% of OpEx, and the levers are containment (low capital, high impact), cooling system efficiency (COP/EER of cooling units), and economizer utilization (climate-driven, contract-driven in colo scenarios). When you walk into a DC you don't know, the first 10 minutes of assessment are: what's the inlet temperature, is containment in place, are blanking panels installed, and is economizer enabled. Those four questions tell you whether you're looking at a well-run facility or a cost problem waiting to be discovered.

For new facilities or major refreshes, the cooling architecture choice (CRAC vs CRAH, economizer availability, liquid cooling readiness) should be driven by projected rack density, not by what was installed last time.
