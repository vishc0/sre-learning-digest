# Chapter 4: Colocation vs Owned Datacenter — The Build/Buy/Cloud Decision

## The Decision Framework

Choosing between colocation, owned datacenter, and cloud is fundamentally a capital allocation and strategic control decision — not a technical one. The wrong answer in an interview is treating it as purely economic. The right answer shows you understand the axes: control, compliance, economics, agility, and time horizon.

The decision has three vectors:

**Build (own the facility)**: Maximum control, highest CapEx, lowest per-unit cost at large scale, slowest to deploy, hardest to exit.

**Colo (rent space in someone else's facility)**: Reduced CapEx, shared infrastructure cost, faster deployment, contractual control constraints, monthly exit optionality.

**Cloud (IaaS/PaaS)**: Near-zero CapEx, highest per-unit cost at scale, maximum agility, no physical control, shared compliance model.

Most mature enterprise portfolios operate across all three simultaneously. The Director answer is not "pick one" — it's "here's how I partition the workload portfolio across all three based on criticality, compliance requirements, and unit economics."

---

## When to Colocate

**CapEx avoidance**: Colo converts a multi-million-dollar facility capital spend into a monthly operating expense. For organizations managing CapEx budgets tightly (or avoiding balance sheet infrastructure in a cloud-first mandate), colo is the path of least resistance.

**Speed**: A colo deployment can go from contract to powered racks in 30–60 days. Building a greenfield datacenter takes 18–36 months.

**Geographic expansion**: Entering a new market where you need compute presence but don't have the volume to justify an owned facility. Colo lets you plant a flag without a 10-year real estate commitment.

**Edge of technology curve**: If your organization's density needs are uncertain (e.g., AI workloads scaling rapidly), colo gives you the ability to expand in someone else's facility that already has the power and cooling infrastructure for high-density deployments.

**When colo is wrong**: When you have regulatory requirements that prohibit shared facility environments (uncommon but exists in some defense/intelligence contexts). When your volume is large enough that the colo margin (what the provider charges above their cost) exceeds the cost of owned infrastructure. The break-even is typically 5–7 years at sustained scale.

---

## When to Own

**Long-term economics at scale**: Owning a datacenter amortized over 20 years with a known, stable workload is cheaper per unit than colo. The colo provider's margin (typically 30–50% on power alone) disappears when you own the facility.

**Control and compliance**: In regulated industries, physical control of the facility — not just cage access, but facility-level control — sometimes matters. For T-Mobile, there are network elements (particularly in law enforcement assistance contexts) where facility control is not optional.

**Competitive differentiation**: For telecom, the physical DC is also network infrastructure. T-Mobile's owned facilities often co-locate network equipment and IT compute in ways that are impossible or economically irrational in a commercial colo. The integration of the network fabric with the compute fabric is a technical and strategic advantage.

**When ownership is wrong**: When the workload is volatile, uncertain, or declining. Owning a DC and then underutilizing it is a pure sunk cost problem. The flexibility premium of colo or cloud is worth paying when demand is unpredictable.

---

## When to Exit to Cloud

Cloud is the right answer when: agility matters more than unit cost, the workload is variable enough that reserved capacity would be chronically under- or over-provisioned, the team doesn't have the skills or desire to operate physical infrastructure, or the time horizon is short enough that the CapEx payback doesn't close.

The common mistake is treating "cloud is cheaper" as a premise rather than a conclusion. Cloud is frequently more expensive per unit at scale. The value proposition is agility, managed services, and the elimination of operational overhead — not raw compute cost.

**T-Mobile hybrid reality**: T-Mobile operates a hybrid model. RAN (Radio Access Network) equipment stays physical. Core network elements (5G SA, IMS) may run on cloud-native platforms but the infrastructure is often T-Mobile-managed (private cloud, dedicated hardware). Enterprise IT and internal tooling increasingly run on AWS/Azure. Developer tooling and test/dev workloads are cloud-native. The Director job is knowing which bucket each workload belongs in and why.

## Colocation Contract Terms — What Actually Matters

Understanding colo contracts is a Director competency. These are the terms that determine your actual operational capability and cost:

**Power SLA**: The guaranteed watts (or kW) your cage/cabinet/suite is allocated. Usually stated as a committed kW draw with a ceiling on burst. The SLA covers what happens when the provider fails to deliver: typically service credits, not full compensation. Critical question: what is the credit structure if the provider drops below their power SLA? Credits of 10–50% of monthly fee are common; they don't compensate for an outage that costs you $500K/hour.

**Remote Hands SLA**: The colo provider's staff who will perform physical tasks in the DC on your behalf. Standard SLA tiers: Smart Hands (simple tasks like cable reconnects, power cycling) vs. Advanced Hands (rack and stack, hardware replacement). Response time SLA varies: 15–30 minutes for emergency, 4–8 hours for standard. Scope exclusions matter: most providers exclude firmware updates, complex troubleshooting, and work requiring specialized vendor knowledge. Know what's in and out of scope before you need it at 2am.

**Cross-connects**: Physical cables connecting your cage to a network provider or another tenant's cage. Cross-connect costs are a significant and often underestimated line item in colo total cost. Pricing: $200–$600/month per cross-connect for a standard 10GbE fiber cross-connect; more for higher bandwidth or copper. Provisioning time: 3–5 business days standard, 1–2 days expedited (at premium). Own your cross-connect list — it drives your recurring cost and your carrier optionality.

**Cage vs cabinet vs suite**: Cage is a floor area enclosed by chain-link or mesh panels (your space, provider's building). Cabinet is a single rack within a shared cage or row. Suite is an enclosed room — most control, most cost. At T-Mobile scale, dedicated cages or suites are the norm; shared cabinet environments aren't appropriate for sensitive network equipment.

**MRC vs NRC**: Monthly Recurring Charge (your ongoing fee) vs Non-Recurring Charge (one-time setup fees for power circuits, cross-connects, cage build-out). NRCs are negotiable — particularly on large deployments. Providers may waive or credit NRCs for multi-year commitments.

## Building the TCO Model

The question interviewers ask after "tell me about your colo strategy" is "how did you know it was the right economic decision?" The TCO model is the answer.

**Owned facility costs**: Land and construction, mechanical/electrical infrastructure, power at utility rate, cooling OpEx, staff (facility management, security, maintenance), depreciation.

**Colo costs**: MRC (space + power + cooling), NRC (one-time setup), cross-connects, remote hands, bandwidth from the facility.

**Cloud costs**: Compute on-demand or reserved, egress, managed services premium.

**Break-even analysis**: Colo vs owned typically breaks even at 5–7 years at a scale of 1MW+ of committed power. Below 1MW or below 5-year commitment, colo wins on economics. Above 10MW with a 10+ year horizon, owned wins. The band in between is where the model actually matters.

**What the model must include that people forget**: Exit costs. Exiting a colo contract before term triggers penalty fees (typically 50–100% of remaining MRC). Exiting an owned facility involves decommissioning costs, equipment relocation, and potentially a long tail of property management costs. Exits are expensive — build them into the model on day one.
