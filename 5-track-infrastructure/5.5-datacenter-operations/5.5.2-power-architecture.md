# Chapter 2: Power Architecture — UPS, Generators, PDUs, and PUE

## Why Power Architecture Is a Director Conversation

Power is the single largest capital and operational cost variable in a datacenter. A Director who can read a power audit, understand where the inefficiencies are, and translate PUE into a dollar figure for the CFO is demonstrably more valuable than one who treats power as a facilities problem. This chapter gives you the vocabulary and the numbers.

---

## UPS Design: Flywheel vs Battery

The Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) sits between the utility feed and your IT load. Its job: clean, condition, and bridge power during the gap between utility loss and generator pickup.

**Battery UPS (VRLA/Li-Ion)**: Most common in enterprise DCs. Valve-regulated lead-acid (VRLA) batteries have a useful life of 3–5 years before capacity degrades significantly. Lithium-ion batteries are now entering the enterprise market — higher upfront cost ($2–3x), but 10+ year life and one-third the footprint. The operational discipline challenge with battery UPS is the testing cadence: batteries that haven't been load-tested recently may not deliver rated runtime when you need them.

**Flywheel UPS (Rotary UPS)**: Stores energy as kinetic energy in a spinning mass rather than electrochemistry. Provides a very short bridge (10–30 seconds) — enough for the generator to start and the ATS to transfer. No battery degradation curve, no hazardous materials disposal, lower maintenance overhead. The limitation is runtime: flywheels don't give you 10–15 minutes of battery backup. They're designed to bridge cleanly to generator power and nothing more.

**Runtime expectations**: Battery UPS at rated load typically provides 10–15 minutes. This is enough time to start a generator, execute an orderly transfer, and confirm the load is stable. It is not enough time to run a full graceful shutdown of a production EKS cluster if the generator fails. That's why generator reliability — not UPS runtime — is the critical path.

## Generator Sizing and Operation

Generators are sized in kW (kilowatts) of output. Sizing for a datacenter generator is not the same as sizing for a building generator — IT load is non-linear, and generator startup adds its own transient loads.

**Sizing rule of thumb**: Generator capacity should be 125–150% of the IT load it will carry. A 1MW IT load needs a 1.25–1.5MW generator to handle startup surge and provide headroom for load growth.

**Fuel consumption**: Diesel generators consume approximately 0.5–0.7 gallons of diesel per kW of output per hour. A 2MW generator at 75% load (1.5MW) burns roughly 750–1,050 gallons/hour. At 72 hours of runtime (the typical emergency fuel reserve target), that's 54,000–75,600 gallons on-site. Know what your fuel contract covers — replenishment SLA during an extended grid outage is a negotiated term with your fuel supplier.

**Transfer time**: The time from utility loss detection to generator carrying full load is typically 10–30 seconds. This is why UPS runtime matters — the UPS must bridge this window. ATS (Automatic Transfer Switch) performs the switching. The ATS is a mechanical device with a measurable MTBF; it should appear on your PM schedule.

**Transfer sequence**: Utility fails → UPS picks up load → UPS triggers generator start signal → Generator starts and reaches stable voltage/frequency → ATS closes generator side, opens utility side → UPS output synchronized → Load transfers to generator through UPS. The UPS stays in-line throughout.

## PDU Redundancy: A and B Feeds

A Power Distribution Unit (PDU) takes high-voltage generator/UPS output and distributes it to individual circuit breakers feeding rack-level connections. PDU design determines how much redundancy reaches the IT equipment.

**Single-corded servers on A/B feeds**: Each rack has two PDUs — an A-feed PDU and a B-feed PDU sourced from two independent UPS/generator paths. Servers with dual power supplies draw from both A and B simultaneously (or A primary, B standby). When one path fails, the server continues operating on the other path — no impact.

**What happens when one PDU fails**: Single-corded equipment (some storage, older network gear, some PDUs downstream) on the failed path goes down. Dual-corded equipment continues on the surviving path. This is why a rack audit for single-corded vs dual-corded equipment is a critical operational document. If you don't know which equipment is single-corded, you don't know your actual blast radius when an A-feed trips.

**Circuit breaker discipline**: Every circuit breaker in a PDU should be labeled with: the rack(s) it feeds, the kW allocation, and the equipment connected. Unlabeled breakers are a change management failure. In a power event, unlabeled breakers mean someone is guessing when deciding what to shed.

## PUE: The Director's Efficiency Metric

Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) is the ratio of total facility power to IT equipment power:

```
PUE = Total Facility Power / IT Equipment Power
```

A PUE of 1.0 is theoretically perfect — every watt drawn from the utility goes to running compute. Real facilities use power for cooling, lighting, power conditioning losses, and overhead. The gap between 1.0 and your actual PUE is OpEx you're paying for infrastructure, not compute.

**Industry benchmarks**:
- 2.0+: Legacy, inefficient — common in 1990s-era DCs still in operation
- 1.7–2.0: Below average — typically older facilities with poor cooling design
- 1.4–1.6: Average enterprise DC
- 1.2–1.4: Efficient modern DC, good containment and cooling design
- 1.1–1.2: Hyperscaler best-in-class (Google, Microsoft, AWS purpose-built)
- 1.03–1.1: Edge of what's physically achievable

**PUE as a cost lever — the real math**: A 10MW IT load facility running at PUE 1.8 draws 18MW from the utility. The same facility at PUE 1.2 draws 12MW. At $0.07/kWh (commercial rate), that's a difference of 6MW × 8,760 hours × $0.07 = **$3.67M per year**. This is the number you give the CFO, not the PUE delta.

**What moves PUE**: Cooling efficiency is the primary lever. Hot aisle/cold aisle containment alone can move PUE from 1.8 to 1.5 in an existing facility. Airside economization (free cooling) can push it to 1.3–1.4 in climates where outside air is usable for significant portions of the year. Rack density management (reducing stranded cool air) matters too.

**How to read a power audit**: A power audit captures total facility power at the utility meter, IT load at the PDU output, and overhead loads (CRAC units, lighting, UPS losses, chillers). The gap is where the OpEx opportunity lives. Walk through it asking: where is cooling drawn from? What's the CRAC unit efficiency rating (kW of cooling per kW of power consumed — the EER/COP)? Is there economizer mode available but not enabled?

## Director Frame: The Capital Efficiency Conversation

PUE is a bridge from the engineering floor to the boardroom. The way to use it in a Director interview:

When evaluating a DC investment decision — whether to refresh aging cooling in an owned facility, whether to renegotiate a colo contract, whether to exit to cloud — PUE delta should be part of the NPV model. A facility upgrade from PUE 1.8 to 1.4 on a 10MW load represents $2.4M/year in saved power costs. That funds the refresh payback in 3–5 years.

The question a Director should always ask about any DC facility: "What's the PUE, what's the trajectory, and what would it take to get 0.2 better?" If the answer is "we don't track PUE," that's the first finding in your assessment.
