# Chapter 8: Director Decision Framework — DC Strategy as a Competitive Question

## This Chapter IS the Interview Answer

When a VP-level interviewer asks "how do you think about datacenter strategy?" they're not looking for a definition of Tier III or an explanation of PUE. They're evaluating whether you think about physical infrastructure as a strategic asset or as an inherited cost center. This chapter builds the strategic frame that separates Director-level answers from SRE manager-level answers.

---

## Datacenter Exit Strategy

Every owned or colocated datacenter should have a documented exit strategy — not because you plan to exit, but because an organization that has thought through the exit has thought through what the facility is actually doing for them strategically.

**Triggers for evaluating a DC exit**:
- Lease/contract expiration approaching (18–24 months out is the window to decide)
- Facility reaching end of capital refresh cycle (major infrastructure spend required to continue)
- Workload profile shift (cloud migration has reduced physical footprint below economic threshold)
- Regulatory or compliance posture change (requirement for new certifications the current facility can't meet)
- Facility performance issues (PUE trajectory, reliability incidents, physical limitations)
- Consolidation opportunity (merging multiple smaller facilities into a fewer, more efficient facilities)

**What you can't move easily**:
Network elements are the hardest physical infrastructure to exit. Co-located network equipment — particularly DWDM optical gear, physical cross-connects, and anything with regulatory holdplace requirements (CALEA/lawful intercept infrastructure) — has long migration lead times and sometimes regulatory approval requirements. Any DC exit strategy that doesn't explicitly account for network element migration timelines will miss the critical path.

Legacy applications with physical hardware dependencies (mainframe, Oracle Exadata, specialized DSPs) may require significant re-architecture before they can move. These are not "lift and shift" targets — they're "strangler pattern or stay" decisions.

**Sequencing a DC exit**:
1. Audit the full workload inventory — categorize by cloud-ready, re-architect needed, hardware-dependent, network-element
2. Network elements last — sequence everything else out while network stays; negotiate a colocation runway or network element relocation separately
3. Hardware-dependent workloads second-to-last — if they can't move to cloud, they move to a consolidated facility or are replaced
4. Cloud-ready workloads first — these drive the fast early wins and free up space/power faster
5. Plan the physical decommission (deinstall, data destruction, equipment disposal) as a project with resource and cost estimates

## Cloud Migration Economics from the DC Perspective

The cloud migration conversation that's wrong: "We're moving everything to cloud to reduce costs."
The cloud migration conversation that's right: "We're selectively migrating workloads to cloud where the agility and unit economics favor it, and retaining physical infrastructure where control, compliance, or unit economics favor it."

**What to move to cloud (good economics)**:
- Variable workloads with high peak-to-average ratio (cloud's elasticity is the differentiator)
- Development and test environments (don't need physical DC reliability; don't need to run 24/7)
- SaaS-replaceable workloads (ITSM, email, collaboration — if someone else runs it better, let them)
- Workloads where managed services eliminate significant operational overhead (ML/AI training, data lakes)

**What to retain physical for (bad cloud economics at scale)**:
- Consistent, predictable, 24/7 workloads at high utilization — reserved cloud capacity is cheaper than on-demand but still more expensive than owned compute at the same utilization
- Workloads with extreme data gravity (large datasets with high I/O — egress costs make cloud economics unworkable)
- Network infrastructure — latency, throughput, and regulatory requirements make cloud non-viable for radio access network and core packet processing

**The honest migration cost**: Cloud migration costs are chronically underestimated. The actual cost includes: migration tooling and labor, re-architecture for cloud-native patterns (refactoring that wasn't budgeted), parallel running costs during migration (pay for cloud AND existing physical until you can cut over), training for the ops team on cloud-native operations. A realistic migration estimate includes 12–18 months of parallel cost and 30–50% over the raw infrastructure cost in project labor.

## Colocation Negotiation — What to Push On

Colo providers negotiate. Knowing what they'll give vs. what they'll never give is leverage.

**What colo providers will negotiate**:
- Price (MRC per kW) — especially on multi-year commitments, large power volumes, and when market occupancy is high (they need your business)
- NRC waivers — setup fees on power circuits, cage build-out, cross-connect installation; providers routinely waive these for 2+ year commitments
- Cross-connect MRC — particularly if you're bringing a lot of tenants (T-Mobile's scale gives you pricing leverage)
- Remote hands pricing — volume discounts if you have predictable monthly remote hands consumption
- Escalation caps on MRC — lock in maximum annual increase rate (2–3% vs open-ended is worth fighting for)
- Volume discounts tied to total kW commitment across multiple facilities from same provider

**What colo providers will NOT give**:
- Power SLA structure — the credit model (service credits for power SLA breach, not cash compensation, and credits are capped) is standard across the industry; trying to negotiate cash compensation for power outages will not succeed
- Unlimited liability for data loss — facility providers are infrastructure providers, not data custodians; liability caps in the contract protect them from data breach claims
- Audit rights without appointment — you can usually get a "right to audit" clause but it will require advance notice and scope agreement
- Real-time facility metrics access — some providers are getting better at this, but live PUE, power, and cooling data is still a premium offering at most providers

**Your leverage points**:
- Commitment volume and duration: more kW, longer term = better pricing
- Competitive bids: negotiate with at least two providers simultaneously; the knowledge that you're in active discussions with a competitor shifts leverage
- Existing relationship: if you're already a customer, renewal negotiation is easier if started 12–18 months before contract end (vs. 3 months, where they know you're not going anywhere)
- Future growth: selling your 3-year growth story is real leverage if credible; providers price for long-term occupancy

## Tier III vs Tier IV: When the Premium Is Worth It

Tier IV costs 2–3x more than Tier III to build and operate. The question is whether the delta is justified.

**When Tier IV is worth it**:
- The workload's hourly cost of downtime exceeds the annualized premium of Tier IV within 2–3 years
- Regulatory requirement for continuous availability (E911, financial settlement, emergency services)
- Customer SLA commitments that carry financial or contract penalties for any downtime
- Workload has no DR alternative (active-passive in another facility doesn't work because of latency, data gravity, or regulatory reasons)

**When Tier IV is NOT worth it**:
- When an active-active DR configuration across two Tier III sites achieves the same availability at lower cost (two Tier III sites, properly architected, can deliver better than Tier IV availability at comparable cost)
- When the workload has a workable degraded mode during downtime
- When cloud burst or failover is a viable alternative
- When the application's MTTR (Mean Time to Recover) is measured in hours anyway — Tier IV facility reliability doesn't help if the application stack takes 4 hours to come back online

**The two-Tier-III alternative**: Two geographically distributed Tier III sites in active-active configuration achieve fault tolerance (any single facility failure doesn't impact service) at roughly the same or lower cost as a single Tier IV site. This is often the better engineering answer. The cases where Tier IV wins over two Tier IIIs are latency-sensitive workloads that cannot tolerate network distance between active nodes, and regulatory requirements that mandate a single specific facility.

## DC as Liability vs Competitive Moat — The Telecom Argument

For most enterprise companies, the datacenter is infrastructure cost — a necessary expense to run the business. The strategic question is how to minimize it.

For telecom, the argument is more nuanced and this is where T-Mobile context gives you a differentiated interview answer.

**The liability case (standard enterprise view)**:
- Physical infrastructure is capital-intensive, depreciates, requires specialized staff
- Cloud providers operate at 10x scale and can deliver infrastructure more cost-effectively
- Physical presence creates compliance complexity without proportional benefit
- Competitive advantage in telecom comes from spectrum, network design, customer experience — not who runs the better datacenter

**The moat case (telecom-specific)**:
- The radio access network is physical by definition — the DC that aggregates RAN traffic must be physically close and physically controlled
- Network interconnects (peering, transit, lawful intercept) require physical presence in specific facilities (carrier hotels like Equinix NY5, One Wilshire, etc.) — you cannot fully virtualize your way out of these requirements
- Low-latency applications (VoLTE, 5G network slicing, MEC) require compute to be physically close to the RAN — edge datacenters are not a cloud-native concept, they're a physical presence requirement
- Physical control of infrastructure is a differentiator in government and enterprise sales where data sovereignty and physical security are procurement requirements

**The Director answer**: T-Mobile's physical DC strategy is not "eliminate physical infrastructure" — it's "own the physical that is structurally advantaged, and consume cloud for everything else." The network elements, the edge compute nodes, and the compliance-sensitive workloads stay physical. Everything else is evaluated on unit economics and agility. This is a more sophisticated answer than either "we're going all-cloud" or "we believe in owning our infrastructure."

## Putting It Together: The Strategy Statement

If you're asked in a VP interview "what's your philosophy on datacenter strategy?" the answer has three parts:

1. **Workload segmentation**: Classify every workload by cloud-fit, compliance requirement, and network adjacency requirement. These three filters tell you where each workload should live.

2. **Tier and redundancy right-sizing**: Apply Tier selection based on the workload's actual cost of downtime and availability requirement — not a blanket policy. Not everything needs Tier IV. Most things are fine at Tier III with good operational discipline.

3. **Total cost of ownership over time horizon**: Evaluate owned, colo, and cloud on a 3–5 year TCO model that includes exit costs, refresh cycles, and realistic migration costs. The model that ignores exit costs and refresh cycles will always overstate the economics of the current choice.

This is the Director answer. Every piece of it is something you can support with specific examples from your operational background.
