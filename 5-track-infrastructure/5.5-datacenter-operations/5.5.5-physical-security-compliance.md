# Chapter 5: Physical Security and Compliance — What Auditors Actually Check

## Why Physical Compliance Is a Director Problem

Physical security compliance fails in one of two ways: either an organization treats it as a facilities team problem and the Director gets blindsided at audit time, or it's treated as a checkbox exercise and the auditor finds the gaps anyway. Neither is acceptable when the outcome is a failed SOC 2 report that blocks enterprise sales or a regulatory finding that triggers remediation orders.

For T-Mobile — operating in a federally regulated telecom environment, subject to CPNI rules, CALEA compliance, and enterprise customer security requirements — physical compliance is not optional and not delegable. The Director owns it.

## SSAE 18 SOC 2 Type II Physical Controls

SSAE 18 (Statement on Standards for Attestation Engagements) replaced SSAE 16. A SOC 2 Type II report covers the Trust Services Criteria (TSC) over a period of time (typically 12 months), not just a point-in-time snapshot. Type I is point-in-time; Type II demonstrates that controls were operating effectively over the audit period.

**What physical controls are in scope for SOC 2 Type II**:

Physical access controls (CC6.4): The organization restricts physical access to facilities and protected information assets. This means documented access procedures, badge access systems with logs, visitor escort procedures, and evidence that access is reviewed and revoked on a defined schedule.

Environmental protections (A1.1): Temperature, humidity, and power monitoring — with documented response procedures for threshold breaches.

Data handling and disposal (CC6.5): Physical media destruction and disposal procedures. Auditors want certificates of destruction from approved vendors, not just a policy document.

**What auditors actually look at** (not what the policy says, but the evidence):

- Badge access logs with timestamps — exported and reviewed, not just stored
- Access list reviews — documented quarterly or semi-annual reviews, evidence of terminated employees having access revoked within defined SLA
- Visitor logs — paper or digital, with escort confirmation
- Physical entry records correlated to badge logs — are there entries that don't correspond to badge events?
- Camera footage retention — auditors want to see that the retention policy is configured, not just stated
- Maintenance personnel access records — third-party technicians visiting for HW maintenance; are they escorted or unescorted?

## Badge Access Systems

Badge access is the primary physical control and the primary source of audit evidence. The system must do more than restrict access — it must generate logs that demonstrate the controls worked.

**Log requirements**: Every badge event (access granted, access denied, door held open, door forced) should be logged with: cardholder ID, door/zone ID, timestamp, access result. Logs must be retained for the audit period plus any regulatory retention requirement — typically 12 months minimum, often 24–36 months for compliance-heavy environments.

**What constitutes an anomaly**:
- Access granted at unusual hours (midnight–5am) by someone with no business justification
- Multiple failed access attempts followed by a successful entry (potential credential sharing or tailgating)
- Access events in zones the cardholder doesn't normally enter (lateral movement indicator)
- After-hours access without a corresponding change management ticket
- Terminated employee badge active post-termination (this is a findings automatic — zero tolerance for auditors)

**Access review cadence**: SOC 2 Type II requires evidence of periodic access reviews — not just a policy that says reviews happen quarterly, but a documented record of the review, who reviewed, what was found, and what was remediated. If access reviews are manual (spreadsheet exported from badge system, emailed to managers), the process is fragile. Automated provisioning tied to HRIS is the operationally mature approach.

## Camera Systems

CCTV coverage requirements for a SOC 2-compliant datacenter:

**Coverage requirements**: All entry/exit points, aisles within the secured perimeter, server room floors, shipping/receiving areas. Blind spots are findings — auditors will ask for a camera coverage map and compare it to the floor plan.

**Retention period**: Minimum 30 days; 60–90 days is common for enterprise compliance. Some financial or healthcare-adjacent environments require 180 days. The retention period must be configured in the recording system, not just stated in policy — auditors will verify by checking the actual recording history.

**Chain of custody for evidence**: If footage is requested for a security investigation or audit, the process for extracting, preserving, and documenting that footage must be defined. Chain of custody means: who accessed the footage, what was exported, how was it stored, who reviewed it, what was the finding. An undocumented extraction process invalidates the footage as audit evidence in some legal contexts.

**Who has access**: Access to CCTV footage should be role-restricted. Security team and designated management, not general IT staff. Access to the CCTV system itself (to review live or recorded footage) should be logged separately.

## Mantrap / Airlock Design

A mantrap (or airlock) is a physical access control mechanism consisting of two interlocked doors — the second door cannot open until the first has closed and the person inside has authenticated. It prevents tailgating (following someone through a door without authentication).

**Operational requirements**: The interlock must be functional — both doors should not be able to be simultaneously open (even briefly) without triggering an alert. The interlock controller itself needs backup power (typically UPS-backed). Tailgating detection (via weight sensor, IR sensor, or camera with analytics) should generate real-time alerts.

**Tailgating as an audit finding**: Auditors in a physical walkthrough will observe whether tailgating is possible. If the mantrap doors are wedged open, propped with equipment, or the interlock is bypassed "temporarily," that's a findings candidate. It's also a sign of an operational culture that hasn't internalized why the control exists.

**Visitor escort procedures**: Every visitor who enters a secured zone must be escorted by an authorized employee at all times. The escort is logged (visitor log entry includes escort name, time in, time out). The escort is responsible for the visitor's behavior in the facility. If the visitor needs to perform work (e.g., hardware maintenance), the escort remains present and observes the work performed.

## Compliance Audit Evidence Package

When a SOC 2 Type II audit is approaching, the evidence package is assembled from the prior 12 months of operational records. What to prepare:

**Access control evidence**:
- Badge access system configuration (access zones defined, approval workflow documented)
- Access list as of audit start date and end date (showing changes over the period)
- Access review records (quarterly reviews, manager sign-offs, revocations completed)
- Terminated employee access revocation records (showing timestamp of termination vs. timestamp of badge deactivation)

**Environmental monitoring evidence**:
- Temperature/humidity sensor logs (threshold alerts generated, responses documented)
- UPS test records (annual or semi-annual load tests, results)
- Generator test records (monthly no-load, quarterly load test results)

**Incident records**:
- Any physical security incidents (anomalous access, suspected tailgating, equipment tampering) — the record itself, plus the response and remediation documented

**Third-party access records**:
- Vendor/contractor access logs, escort records, work orders authorizing the access

## Director Frame: Physical Compliance as a Revenue Enabler

The framing that lands in Director interviews: physical compliance is a sales blocker when you miss it, and a competitive differentiator when you can demonstrate it. Enterprise customers — particularly in financial services, healthcare, and government — will conduct their own physical security reviews (often as part of vendor due diligence) before signing large contracts.

A SOC 2 Type II report with a clean physical controls section is the artifact that lets the sales team say "yes" when a prospect asks "can we audit your facility?" The Director who understands this treats physical compliance as a revenue protection function, not a checkbox exercise.

When the audit finding is a physical control failure — an unreviewed access list, a camera blind spot, an improperly configured retention period — the remediation is straightforward but the finding appears in the SOC 2 report for the entire audit period. Customers read those findings. Fix the root cause, not just the finding.
