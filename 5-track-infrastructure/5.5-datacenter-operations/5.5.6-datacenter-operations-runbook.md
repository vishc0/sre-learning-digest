# Chapter 6: Datacenter Operations Runbook — SOPs That Signal Operational Maturity

## What a Runbook Reveals About an Organization

A datacenter without documented SOPs is one where institutional knowledge lives in people's heads. When those people leave, get promoted, or get paged at 3am by someone who doesn't know the procedure, the absence of runbooks becomes an incident. The Director who inherits an undocumented DC environment should treat documentation as a technical debt paydown — measurable, prioritized, and resourced.

This chapter covers the core operational SOPs that should exist in any mature physical infrastructure environment.

---

## SOP 1: Server Racking and Installation

**Before racking begins**:
1. Confirm the target rack location against the capacity tracker (rack ID, U-position, kW allocation headroom)
2. Verify the rack has A-feed and B-feed PDU connections and that both circuits are active and within load tolerance
3. Check that blanking panels are in place for all unused U-spaces in the rack and adjacent racks
4. Confirm the rack weight capacity can accommodate the new equipment (full server racks weigh 1,500–3,000 lbs; floor load limits matter)
5. Obtain the authorized change ticket number — no racking without a change ticket

**Racking procedure**:
1. Install rail kit per vendor specifications — incorrect rail installation is a safety hazard and a damage risk
2. Label the server with: asset tag, hostname, date installed, rack ID, U-position (front and rear label)
3. Connect power: A-feed to PSU 0 (left power supply), B-feed to PSU 1 (right power supply) — confirm with the cabling standard for the site
4. Cable management: use hook-and-loop ties, route to patch panel, leave 12 inches of service loop for future moves; no zip ties on production cable runs (they're destructive to remove)
5. Connect to ToR switch: confirm port and record in the port inventory
6. Power on and verify both PSUs active (amber fault light off on both)
7. Update capacity tracker: record kW actual (measured at PDU), U-spaces consumed

**Post-installation verification**:
- Confirm server is discoverable on the network (ping, SSH)
- Confirm environmental sensors are reading (IPMI/iDRAC/iLO accessible)
- Update CMDB with physical location (rack ID, U-position, A/B feed circuit IDs)
- Close change ticket with actual vs. planned comparison

## SOP 2: Cable Management Standards

Poor cable management is a physical DC equivalent of technical debt — invisible until it causes a problem, and then it causes a big one. The wrong cable pulled, a rat's nest that prevents air from flowing, an unlabeled patch that takes 20 minutes to trace during an incident.

**Structured cable management principles**:
- Every cable has two labels: one at each end, within 4 inches of the termination point
- Label format: [source port ID] ↔ [destination port ID] (e.g., "SW01-E01 ↔ SRV042-NIC0")
- Color coding by purpose: blue = production data, green = management/IPMI, red = SAN/storage, yellow = cross-connects/uplinks (use the site standard, but use one consistently)
- Horizontal cable managers on every other U-space in the patch zone
- Vertical cable managers on both sides of every rack
- 1U patch panels for short-run structured cabling; avoid direct rack-to-switch runs across the data center floor

**Patch panel strategy**:
- Top-of-rack (ToR) switches connect to a patch panel in the same rack, not directly to servers
- Servers connect to the patch panel via short patch cables (1–3 feet)
- Longer runs go from patch panel to switch or to another patch panel
- This means moves, adds, and changes happen at the patch panel, not by reaching behind servers
- Document every patch panel port in the port inventory before it's used

**What unstructured cable management costs**: During an incident, a cabling rat's nest means every cable trace takes 3–5 minutes instead of 30 seconds. A 45-minute Mean Time to Diagnose (MTTD) for a physical connectivity issue is often a cable management problem. Track this — if post-incident reviews repeatedly cite "time to identify physical connectivity" as a delay, the cabling standard is the root cause.

## SOP 3: Power Circuit Management

**Circuit breaker labeling standard**:
Every breaker in every PDU must be labeled with: rack ID, circuit function (A-feed or B-feed), maximum amperage, current load (updated quarterly from PDU management software). Unlabeled breakers are a safety hazard and a compliance finding.

**Load balancing across PDUs**:
Target 60–70% utilization per PDU at peak load. Above 80% is a headroom warning; above 85% is a risk threshold (NEC code requires circuits to be protected at 80% of rated ampacity for continuous loads). PDU management systems (like Raritan, Vertiv, Eaton) provide circuit-level monitoring — this should be in your monitoring stack.

**Before adding load to a circuit**:
1. Check current PDU utilization in the circuit management system
2. Calculate the new equipment's expected draw (from specs or measured)
3. Confirm the new total stays within 80% of circuit capacity
4. Confirm A and B feeds to the target rack are balanced (A-feed and B-feed should be within 20% of each other under normal operation)
5. Document the planned change in the capacity tracker before executing

**Circuit breaker trip response**: If a PDU circuit trips unexpectedly, do NOT immediately reset it. First: identify what lost power (which equipment on that circuit), determine if there's an active incident in progress that explains the trip, check if the trip was at maximum rated load. Reset only after investigation — a breaker that trips again immediately indicates a fault condition (short circuit, failing component drawing excessive current). Escalate to facility engineering before the second reset.

## SOP 4: Remote Hands Engagement

Remote hands is a colo provider service where facility staff perform physical tasks on your behalf. Knowing how to use it effectively — and what not to ask for — is an operational competency.

**How to write an effective remote hands ticket**:
Every remote hands request must include:
- Physical location: cage ID, rack ID, U-position (don't assume they'll find it)
- Equipment identification: hostname, asset tag, make/model
- Task description: step-by-step, written as if for someone who has never seen the equipment
- Expected outcome: what "success" looks like ("the server should POST and display boot screen on the KVM")
- Decision points: "If you see [X], do [Y]; if you see [Z], stop and call [contact] before proceeding"
- Contact info: your on-call mobile number; the ticket is not a substitute for live escalation capability
- Time constraint: if this is urgent, state it explicitly with the reason

**What remote hands can do**: Power cycle equipment, swap cables, seat or unseat hardware, read console output, install vendor-shipped hardware (if simple), run labeled cables to patch panels.

**What NOT to ask remote hands to do**: Firmware updates, BIOS configuration, complex troubleshooting, interpretation of error states, anything requiring vendor-specific tooling or knowledge. These tasks exceed standard remote hands scope and will either be refused or performed incorrectly. For these, engage vendor TAC with remote hands as a physical assist.

**SLA expectations**: Standard response is typically 15–30 minutes during business hours, 30–60 minutes after hours. Emergency response SLA (usually available for premium) is 15 minutes. Always confirm the SLA when opening the ticket.

## SOP 5: Change Management for Physical Changes

Physical changes are harder to reverse than logical changes. A misconfigured firewall rule can be reverted in seconds. A cable that was removed and re-run incorrectly might take hours to diagnose. Physical change management must be tighter than logical change management, not looser.

**Change ticket requirements for physical changes**:
- Pre-change state documentation: current rack diagram, current cable trace, current PDU circuit load
- Change description: exactly what will be physically moved, removed, or installed
- Impact assessment: which services are affected, what is the blast radius if something goes wrong
- Rollback procedure: how do you undo the change (not "reverse the steps" — write the actual reverse procedure)
- Validation steps: how do you confirm the change succeeded
- Approval: who approved the change (especially for production-impacting changes)

**Maintenance window for physical changes**: Unlike software changes that can sometimes be done live, physical changes to power or cooling paths should be scheduled during maintenance windows with application teams aware and standing by. The exception is replacing a component that has already failed — that's an incident, not a change.

**Post-change**: Update physical documentation (rack diagram, cable inventory, capacity tracker, CMDB) before closing the ticket. Post-change documentation that doesn't happen in the ticket is documentation that doesn't happen at all.

## Director Frame: Operational Maturity Shows in the Runbook

In a VP interview, you may be asked "how do you assess the operational maturity of a team you inherit?" The runbook is your answer. Ask to see the SOPs for three specific scenarios: racking new hardware, responding to a power event, and adding a cross-connect. If the SOPs exist and are current, the team has process discipline. If they don't exist, you're looking at the first three things your new team builds.

The Director role is to ensure these procedures exist, are reviewed annually (or after every incident that exposes a gap), and are actually used — not just stored in a Confluence page that was last updated two years ago.
