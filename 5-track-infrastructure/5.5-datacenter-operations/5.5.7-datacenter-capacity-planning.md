# Chapter 7: Datacenter Capacity Planning — The Capital Conversation

## Why Capacity Planning Is a Director's Job

A datacenter capacity failure — running out of power, cooling, or space — is not a surprise event. It's a planning failure. By the time the facility team is telling you there's no room in the rack or the PDU is at 95% utilization, you're managing a crisis that should have been a budget line item 18 months earlier. The Director who owns capacity planning owns the capital conversation before it becomes an emergency.

---

## Rack Density Planning

Rack density (measured in kW/rack) is the most important physical capacity variable to track and project. Everything else — cooling, power, space — derives from it.

**Historical density trends**:
- Pre-2010: 2–4 kW/rack — 1U servers with single processors
- 2010–2015: 4–8 kW/rack — multi-core, blade chassis, increasing virtualization density
- 2015–2020: 8–15 kW/rack — higher core count CPUs, memory-dense servers
- 2020–2024: 15–25 kW/rack — high-density compute, NVMe storage
- 2024+: 30–100+ kW/rack — GPU clusters, AI inference, HPC workloads

**Why density matters for planning**: A facility designed and cooled for 6 kW/rack average cannot simply accept 20 kW/rack workloads by redistributing equipment. The cooling infrastructure — CRAC/CRAH capacity, airflow design, containment — was designed around the lower density. High-density deployments in a low-density facility create hot spots, trigger thermal shutdowns, and force expensive cooling retrofits.

**Planning approach**: Track kW/rack average and kW/rack maximum (the highest-density rack in the facility). If maximum density is approaching the facility cooling ceiling (typically 15–20 kW/rack for air-cooled containment), any new high-density workload requires either cooling infrastructure investment or a different facility.

## Power Capacity Planning

**The gap between installed and usable capacity**:
Installed capacity is what's on the utility feed. Usable capacity is what you can actually load. The difference:
- Utility feed → Transformer losses → UPS losses → PDU losses → IT equipment. Each step has efficiency losses.
- Safety headroom: NEC code requires 20% headroom on continuous loads. A 1MW PDU can only be continuously loaded to 800kW.
- Operational headroom: prudent capacity management targets 70–75% utilization, leaving headroom for growth spikes and N+1 redundancy scenarios.
- A "1MW facility" realistically delivers 600–700kW of committed IT load capacity.

**How to calculate usable capacity**:
```
Installed utility capacity: 2MW
x Derating factor (UPS + PDU efficiency): 0.90
= Available power to IT loads: 1.8MW
x NEC 80% continuous load rule: 0.80
= Code-compliant maximum: 1.44MW
x Operational headroom (75%): 0.75
= Committed capacity target: 1.08MW
```

Know this calculation. When a stakeholder says "we have 2MW of capacity, why can't we add more servers?" this is the answer.

**Stranded capacity**: Stranded capacity is power that is allocated on paper (reserved by change tickets and rack assignments) but not actually consumed by running equipment. It's common in environments where servers are racked but equipment is decommissioned without releasing the power reservation. Track allocated vs. measured kW per rack. A 30% gap between allocated and measured is a sign of significant stranded capacity.

## Network Port Capacity Planning

Network capacity planning in a datacenter has two layers: the access layer (ToR switches connecting servers) and the fabric layer (spine-leaf interconnects).

**Top-of-Rack (ToR) switch planning**:
Standard ToR switch: 48 × 25GbE downlinks (to servers) + 6–8 × 100GbE uplinks (to spine). A rack with 40 servers has 40 ports consumed. At 48-port ToR, that leaves 8 ports of headroom. Track port utilization by rack and by ToR switch chassis.

**Port exhaustion warning signs**: New server requests stacking up because there are no available ToR ports. Temporary cables "borrowing" ports from adjacent racks. Switch replacement (to a higher port count model) being done reactively rather than on a planned refresh cycle.

**Spine-leaf capacity**: Oversubscription ratio (how many server-facing ports per uplink port) is a design decision. A 48-server rack with 48 × 25GbE downlinks and 4 × 100GbE uplinks has a 3:1 oversubscription ratio. This is acceptable for general compute. For storage-heavy or latency-sensitive workloads, 2:1 or 1:1 may be required. Know your ratios and know which workloads are sensitive to them.

## Growth Forecasting

Capacity planning horizons for physical infrastructure:

**12-month horizon**: Driven by approved project pipeline and procurement lead times. What's in flight today that will consume capacity in the next 12 months? Server refresh projects, new application deployments, and planned EKS node pool expansions should all be captured. This is operational planning — specific, committed.

**24-month horizon**: Mix of committed projects and demand forecast from engineering roadmaps. Less specific but more strategic. Are there workloads expected to migrate from cloud back to owned infrastructure (cloud cost repatriation)? Are AI/ML workloads being planned that will drive density increases?

**36-month horizon**: Strategic planning territory. Align with the enterprise technology roadmap. A T-Mobile 36-month DC capacity plan should account for: 5G core capacity growth, network densification (more edge compute), and the direction of the enterprise private cloud vs. public cloud balance.

**The capacity review cadence**: Monthly for 0–12 months (operational), quarterly for 12–24 months (tactical), annually for 24–36 months (strategic). Each review should compare actual consumption against the forecast — drift between forecast and actuals is the leading indicator that the model's assumptions are wrong.

## Refresh Cycles

Physical infrastructure has refresh lifecycles that are longer and more capital-intensive than software. Failing to plan these is a common cause of both performance degradation and unplanned capital requests.

| Asset Class | Typical Refresh Cycle | Planning Lead Time | Notes |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|-------|
| Servers | 3–5 years | 18 months (procurement + staging) | Refresh before warranty expiry drives cost |
| Network equipment (access layer) | 5–7 years | 12 months | Driver is usually EOL support from vendor |
| Network equipment (core/spine) | 7–10 years | 18–24 months | Major architectural impact; plan carefully |
| Storage | 3–5 years | 12–18 months | Data migration complexity adds lead time |
| UPS systems | 10–15 years (system); batteries 3–5 years | 6 months (batteries), 24 months (system) | Battery replacement is the high-frequency cost |
| CRAC/CRAH units | 10–15 years | 12 months | Partial replacement possible; full replacement requires cooling downtime planning |
| Facility structure | 15–25 years | 5+ years | Structural upgrades (power capacity expansion, chiller replacement) require long lead capital planning |

**Refresh calendar management**: Every asset should have an install date and a planned refresh date in the CMDB. The capacity manager should generate a 36-month refresh forecast showing capital requirements by year. Surprises in this forecast are a process failure, not a planning difficulty.

## Director Frame: The CFO Conversation

The datacenter capacity plan that lands with a CFO has three elements:

1. **Current state**: Utilization at the rack, power, and network layer. Headroom remaining. Months until critical thresholds are reached at current growth rates.

2. **Investment required**: Capital and timeline required to avoid capacity constraint. Not "we need more capacity" but "we need $4.2M for a PDU upgrade and ToR switch expansion by Q3 to support the planned network function virtualization deployment."

3. **Consequence of not investing**: At current growth rate, we hit 85% power utilization by [month]. At that point, new rack deployments stop until new capacity is online. The business impact is [X days of delayed project delivery, Y workloads unable to scale].

The Director who presents this conversation clearly, with data, owns the room. The one who shows up with "we're running out of capacity and we need money" does not.
