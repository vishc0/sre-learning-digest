## Redundancy Model Detail

| Model | Definition | Example (Cooling) | Failure Behavior |
|-------|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| N | Exactly enough capacity, no spare | 4 CRAC units, all 4 required | Any single failure causes impact |
| N+1 | One spare unit above requirement | 4 required, 5 installed | One failure: at N (no more spare headroom) |
| 2N | Two complete independent systems | 4 required, 8 installed in two separate loops | Full loop failure: other loop carries all load |
| 2N+1 | Two complete systems plus one spare per system | 4 required, 10 installed (5+5) | Full loop failure plus one additional: still operational |

## Concurrent Maintainability vs Fault Tolerance

These are frequently confused. They are different things.

| Capability | Tier III | Tier IV | What It Means |
|-----------|---------|---------|--------------|
| Concurrent Maintainability | YES | YES | You can perform planned maintenance on any component without shutting down IT load |
| Fault Tolerance | NO | YES | An unplanned failure of any component cannot impact IT load |

**Tier III is NOT fault tolerant**: A single unplanned failure on the active power or cooling path can cause an outage. Tier III protects against *planned* maintenance, not unplanned failures.

**Practical implication**: A Tier III facility with excellent change management and preventive maintenance discipline can achieve better-than-expected uptime, but it will never be fault tolerant to an unplanned event — that requires Tier IV architecture.

## Cost Justification Framework

| Question | Tier III Answer | Tier IV Answer |
|----------|----------------|---------------|
| What does 1.6 hours of downtime per year cost? | [Calculate: hourly cost of downtime × 1.6] | Baseline comparison |
| What is the annual Tier IV premium? | Baseline | [$X/MW × MW of load = annual OpEx delta] |
| Break-even period | N/A | Annual downtime cost savings / annual premium |
| When Tier IV wins | Never (use 2x Tier III active-active instead) | When DR is impossible, latency prohibitive, or regulatory mandate exists |

**The alternative math**: Two geographically distributed Tier III sites in active-active configuration cost approximately the same as one Tier IV site and deliver better effective availability (because a regional disaster can affect even a Tier IV facility).

## T-Mobile Infrastructure Mapping

| Infrastructure Type | Likely Tier | Rationale |
|--------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Core 5G packet gateway | Tier III or IV | Carrier-grade uptime requirement; customer SLA impact |
| IMS / VoLTE core | Tier III or IV | Active voice calls; any downtime is immediately customer-visible |
| E911 / PSAPs | Tier IV or equivalent | Federal mandate for continuous availability |
| RAN aggregation (edge DC) | Tier III | High availability required; not always 2N architecture |
| Enterprise IT systems | Tier II–III | Internal tolerance for occasional planned downtime |
| Dev / test / CI-CD | Tier I–II | Unplanned downtime acceptable; planned maintenance windows workable |
| Lawful intercept (CALEA) | Tier III–IV | Regulatory and legal obligation for availability |

## Certification Notes

- Uptime Institute Tier Certification is a paid, formal process — not all facilities claiming "Tier III design" have actual Tier Certification
- "Tier III compliant" or "Tier III designed" is not the same as "Tier III Certified"
- Ask your colo provider for their Tier Certification certificate — including the date (certifications are not indefinitely valid after major facility changes)
- SOC 2 Type II is not a Tier certification — it's a controls audit — they test different things
