# Chapter 3: DNS as Reliability Infrastructure

## The Core Idea

DNS is the phone book of the internet — but unlike a physical phone book, it has layers of caching that can work for you or against you depending on when a failure happens. If the phone book is accurate and cached everywhere, your service is reachable even if the publisher of the phone book goes offline temporarily. But if you need to update a number urgently, every copy of the cached phone book is working against you until the cache expires.

Understanding DNS as reliability infrastructure means understanding that TTL is not a performance setting — it is a reliability trade-off decision that belongs on your architecture review agenda alongside uptime SLAs and failover runbooks.

---

## Authoritative vs. Recursive DNS: Who Owns What

**Authoritative DNS** is the source of truth for a zone. When you register `example.com` and set your nameservers to `ns1.example.com`, those nameservers are authoritative — they hold the actual records (A, CNAME, MX, TXT, etc.) and answer queries with definitive answers, not cached guesses. You control authoritative DNS through your DNS provider (Route53, Cloudflare, NS1) or self-hosted nameservers.

**Recursive DNS** (also called a resolver) is the intermediary that does the lookup work on behalf of clients. When your laptop queries `api.example.com`, it asks your configured resolver (typically your ISP's DNS, your corporate DNS server, or a public resolver like 1.1.1.1). The resolver doesn't know the answer — it queries the root nameservers, then the `.com` TLD servers, then `example.com`'s authoritative nameservers, gets the answer, caches it for the TTL duration, and returns it to your laptop.

The reliability boundary: **authoritative DNS going down is a real outage; recursive DNS going down affects a subset of clients but your authoritative servers are still healthy.** Your job as a platform engineer is to ensure authoritative DNS is highly available and geographically redundant, because if it goes down and TTLs expire, your service vanishes from the internet even if every application server is healthy and ready.

The relationship looks like this:

```
Client
  → Recursive Resolver (ISP / 8.8.8.8 / 1.1.1.1)
      → Root Nameservers (13 root clusters, anycast)
      → TLD Nameservers (.com, .net, etc.)
      → Authoritative Nameservers (YOUR nameservers — the source of truth)
          → Returns: A record, TTL
  ← Resolver caches and returns to client
```

## TTL Strategy: The Core Reliability Trade-Off

TTL (Time to Live) is how long a resolver caches a DNS record before re-querying the authoritative nameserver. It is set by you on your authoritative records. It is the single most important DNS reliability lever you control.

**Low TTL (60–300 seconds)**
- Resolvers discard cached answers quickly and re-query
- Failover is fast — change the A record, and within TTL seconds, clients see the new destination
- Increased query volume against your authoritative DNS — more load, more cost, more attack surface
- If your authoritative DNS is unreachable, clients start failing within 60–300 seconds as caches expire

**High TTL (3600 seconds / 1 hour or more)**
- Resolvers hold cached answers for a long time
- If your authoritative DNS goes offline, clients continue resolving successfully for up to the TTL period — the cache acts as a buffer
- Failover is slow — a TTL of 3600 means it takes up to an hour for clients to see a changed record after you update it
- Good for stable records (MX, NS, TXT verification records) that rarely change

**The operational standard: pre-incident TTL lowering.** Before any planned maintenance that requires a DNS change, lower the TTL hours in advance. If you normally run TTL=3600 and need to failover at 14:00, lower TTL to 60 at 10:00. By the time you execute the change, all resolver caches have already expired and been re-seeded with the 60-second TTL. Your actual failover propagation is now 60 seconds, not one hour.

Most incidents where DNS failover was painfully slow can be traced to someone forgetting to pre-lower the TTL. Make it part of your change runbook, not an afterthought.

## DNS-Based Load Balancing

DNS can distribute traffic without any application-layer load balancer. The mechanisms available in Route53 (and most enterprise-grade DNS providers):

**Weighted routing**: two A records for the same hostname, each with a weight. Route53 returns one or the other proportionally. Useful for canary deployments and gradual traffic shifts. Not true load balancing — a client that receives the "wrong" record sticks to it until TTL expires.

**Geolocation routing**: return different records based on where the query originates. US clients get US data center IPs; EU clients get EU data center IPs. Useful for data residency compliance and latency optimization. Edge case: clients using a resolver outside their actual geography (VPN users, corporate proxies) may route to the wrong region.

**Latency-based routing**: Route53 measures latency from the client's resolver to each AWS region and returns the record for the lowest-latency region. More accurate than geolocation for actual network performance, but it uses resolver location as a proxy for client location — same edge case applies.

**Failover routing**: primary record is returned unless a health check fails, then the secondary record is returned. This is the DNS-native failover mechanism. Works well for active-passive architectures where the secondary is a known standby endpoint.

## DNS Failure Modes

**Authoritative DNS server down — the most dangerous failure.**
New lookups fail immediately for queries that bypass cached results (low TTL, first-time resolution). Clients with cached entries continue working until TTL expires, creating a deceptive partial-outage window. If TTL is high, you may not notice for an hour. If TTL is low, you will notice in minutes. The failure is total once caches expire — no DNS resolution means no connectivity to your service, regardless of application health.

**TTL expiry during outage — the cache cliff.**
Your authoritative DNS is down. Clients that resolved your hostname before the outage are still working — they have a cached record. As TTLs expire client by client, those clients start failing. This creates a rolling degradation over the TTL window, not a clean cutover. Users who accessed the service recently (fresh cache) keep working; users who haven't accessed it in a while start failing first. Your error rate climbs gradually, which can delay incident declaration.

**NXDOMAIN propagation — removing a record takes time.**
You delete an A record. From your authoritative DNS perspective, the record is gone immediately. But resolvers that have already cached that record continue serving it until their cached TTL expires. This is usually desirable (continuity) but becomes a problem if you are trying to forcibly redirect traffic away from a decommissioned endpoint — clients keep going there until their cache expires.

**Resolver misconfiguration.**
Corporate environments often run internal resolvers that have authoritative zones for internal hostnames. When these resolvers go down, internal service discovery fails. Kubernetes DNS (CoreDNS) is a resolver — when CoreDNS pods are unhealthy, service-to-service communication inside the cluster fails even though the pods themselves are running. From an incident perspective, this looks like application failures; the actual failure is Layer 3/5 DNS resolution.

## Case Study: The 2021 Facebook/Meta Outage

On October 4, 2021, Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp went offline globally for approximately six hours. The root cause was a BGP configuration error during a routine backbone maintenance operation.

**What happened:** A command sent to assess global backbone capacity inadvertently withdrew the BGP routes that connected Facebook's data centers to the internet. Facebook's edge routers lost their routes and withdrew their BGP advertisements globally. Facebook disappeared from the global routing table.

**Why DNS made it catastrophic:** Facebook operates their own authoritative DNS servers — `a.ns.facebook.com`, `b.ns.facebook.com`, etc. Those nameservers are physically hosted inside Facebook's network. When BGP withdrew Facebook's routes, those nameservers became unreachable from the outside internet. DNS resolvers worldwide tried to reach Facebook's nameservers to resolve `facebook.com` and got no response.

The failure compounded itself: even if a resolver had a cached answer for `facebook.com`, the IP addresses in that answer were Facebook's load balancers — also inside the unreachable network. Resolution worked (for cached entries) but connectivity failed.

**The dependency most people missed:** DNS depends on BGP. Your authoritative nameservers are hosted somewhere. That somewhere has an IP address. That IP address is reachable via BGP. If the BGP route to your authoritative nameservers disappears, DNS fails — even if the nameserver software itself is perfectly healthy. Facebook's nameservers were only reachable via Facebook's own AS. There was no secondary path.

**The Director-level lesson:**
Your authoritative DNS infrastructure must be reachable via paths independent of your primary network. This is why enterprises use DNS providers like Route53, Cloudflare, or NS1 rather than self-hosting authoritative DNS — those providers have their own independent BGP presence, anycast infrastructure, and network paths. Your authoritative DNS being offline requires your DNS provider's entire global network to fail, not just your network.

If you self-host authoritative DNS: it must be hosted in a different AS from your application infrastructure, or it must be reachable via multiple independent BGP paths. Single-AS dependency for DNS is an architectural risk that should not survive a Director-level design review.

## DNS as a Failover Tool: Health-Check-Based DNS

Route53 health checks, NS1 Pulsar, and Cloudflare Load Balancing all offer the same capability: continuously probe an endpoint, and if it fails health checks, automatically update DNS to remove it from rotation or switch to a failover record.

This is powerful and has important caveats:

**The TTL constraint still applies.** Health-check-based failover updates the DNS record. That update propagates only as resolvers' cached TTLs expire. A health-check that detects failure in 30 seconds means nothing if your TTL is 3600 — clients continue routing to the failed endpoint for up to an hour.

**The resolver behavior caveat.** Resolvers are not required to honor TTLs strictly. Some aggressive resolvers cache longer than the TTL specifies. Some clients hard-code DNS answers. Negative TTL (NXDOMAIN caching) is controlled by the SOA record's minimum TTL field and is often set separately from positive record TTLs.

**The operational model:** for DNS-based failover to be meaningful, TTL must be low (60–300 seconds) and health checks must be configured with aggressive thresholds. This creates the low-TTL operational overhead discussed earlier — more queries, more cost, more dependency on authoritative DNS availability.

## Director Decision Frame

TTL is a business decision. The question is not "what TTL should I use?" — the question is "what is the recovery time objective for a DNS-dependent failure, and is the operational cost of the low TTL required to meet that RTO acceptable?"

| TTL | Failover Speed | Auth DNS Dependency | Query Volume | Right For |
|-----|---------------|-------------------|--------------|-----------|
| 60s | ~1 min | High — cache expires fast; auth DNS must be always available | High | Active failover, canary, rapid iteration |
| 300s | ~5 min | Medium | Medium | Standard services with moderate SLA |
| 3600s | ~1 hour | Low — cache buffers auth DNS outages | Low | Stable records, high-volume globally distributed services |

**Questions you ask in design reviews:**
- What is the TTL on our primary service records, and does it match our failover RTO?
- Are our authoritative nameservers reachable via paths independent from our primary network?
- Do we pre-lower TTLs before planned maintenance that requires DNS changes?
- If health-check-based failover triggers, how long does it actually take for 90% of clients to see the new record? (Answer: TTL + resolver caching variance — test this, don't assume)

**What you watch in incidents:**
- Is DNS resolution succeeding? Check from multiple locations and multiple resolver types (public, corporate, mobile).
- What is the TTL remaining on cached records? This tells you how much time you have before the cache cliff arrives.
- Is the authoritative DNS reachable, or is it the resolver tier that is affected?

---

## Key Takeaways

- Authoritative DNS is the source of truth you control; recursive resolvers cache and serve your records. Authoritative DNS failure is a platform-level outage once caches expire.
- TTL is a reliability trade-off, not a performance setting — low TTL enables fast failover but increases query load and dependency on authoritative availability; high TTL provides cache resilience but slows change propagation.
- Pre-incident TTL lowering is the standard operational practice before any planned maintenance requiring DNS changes — it belongs in every change runbook.
- The 2021 Facebook outage is the canonical example of DNS-BGP dependency — authoritative nameservers hosted inside the same AS as your application infrastructure means a BGP failure takes DNS offline simultaneously, eliminating the cache buffer.
- Health-check-based DNS failover is only as fast as your TTL allows — a 30-second health check interval with a 3600-second TTL produces a 1-hour failover window.
- At the Director level, TTL decisions must be linked to RTO commitments — if your SLA requires 5-minute failover, your TTL must be 300 seconds or less, and your authoritative DNS availability must be architected to match.
