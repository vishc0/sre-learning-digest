# Chapter 2: BGP and Routing

## The Core Idea

BGP — Border Gateway Protocol — is the postal routing system of the internet. Each country (Autonomous System) decides its own internal routing rules and publishes a list of destinations it can deliver to. Neighboring countries agree to forward mail to those destinations. No central authority controls the whole system — it is a web of bilateral agreements, which makes it extraordinarily resilient and extraordinarily fragile at the same time.

You have seen BGP from the inside at T-Mobile — from the carrier side, where T-Mobile itself is an Autonomous System, peering with other carriers and upstream providers to route traffic for millions of subscribers. This chapter reframes that knowledge for the enterprise and platform engineering context: what BGP looks like when you are the customer, not the carrier.

---

## Autonomous Systems: The Unit of Internet Routing

An **Autonomous System (AS)** is a network under a single administrative control that has a unified routing policy. T-Mobile is an AS. Google is an AS. Your company's data center is potentially an AS.

An **ASN (Autonomous System Number)** is the globally unique identifier assigned to each AS by IANA (via regional registries like ARIN). ASNs were originally 16-bit (1–65535); the internet ran out and expanded to 32-bit. Private ASNs (64512–65534 in 16-bit space) exist for internal use, the same way RFC1918 addresses are private IPs.

**Why does an enterprise get its own ASN?**
- You have your own IP address block (Provider Independent, or PI space) that you want to advertise from multiple ISPs simultaneously
- You want to control your own routing policy — which ISP gets inbound traffic, how traffic is weighted, when to fail over
- You are operating at a scale where relying entirely on your ISP's routing decisions is operationally unacceptable

Most enterprises using a single ISP don't need their own ASN. The moment you want multi-ISP redundancy with real control, you need one.

---

## BGP Session Types: iBGP vs eBGP

**eBGP (External BGP)** — sessions between different autonomous systems. This is how you peer with your ISPs. eBGP neighbors are typically directly connected (adjacent routers), and routes received via eBGP are trusted as external paths to the internet.

**iBGP (Internal BGP)** — sessions within the same AS. If you have two routers in your AS, they use iBGP to share the external routes they've learned. iBGP has a critical constraint: routes learned via iBGP are not re-advertised to other iBGP peers (to prevent loops). This is why large networks use **Route Reflectors** — dedicated routers that redistribute iBGP routes to all peers, centralizing the route distribution within the AS.

The analogy from your middleware experience: iBGP is like an internal message bus (RabbitMQ) that distributes routing information between your own systems; eBGP is the connection to an external broker (your ISP) where you exchange routes with the outside world.

## How Route Advertisement Works

When you peer with an ISP via eBGP, you advertise your IP prefixes — the CIDR blocks you own. Your ISP accepts those advertisements and propagates them to their upstream peers, and eventually to the global routing table. This is how the rest of the internet learns that traffic for your address block should be sent toward your ISP, and then to you.

In return, your ISP advertises a default route (0.0.0.0/0) to you — meaning "send everything else to us and we'll handle it." Or, if you want full routing tables (expensive in memory, powerful in control), they advertise the entire internet BGP table (~950,000+ prefixes as of 2026).

**BGP attributes control routing decisions.** The most operationally relevant:
- **AS_PATH** — the list of ASNs a route has traversed; longer path = less preferred (loop prevention + path selection)
- **LOCAL_PREF** — used within your AS to prefer one ISP exit over another; higher = more preferred
- **MED (Multi-Exit Discriminator)** — a hint to your ISP about which of your entry points you prefer for inbound traffic; ISPs may ignore it
- **Communities** — tags you attach to routes to influence how your ISP handles them (e.g., blackhole communities for DDoS mitigation)

## BGP as a Reliability Tool: Multi-ISP Architecture

This is the Director-level use case. With a single ISP, you are trusting that ISP's reliability completely. When their upstream has an issue, you have no recourse. Multi-ISP BGP gives you active-active or active-passive redundancy at the internet edge.

**Active-active**: advertise your prefixes to both ISPs simultaneously. Traffic comes in via both; outbound traffic goes via whichever path BGP selects (controlled via LOCAL_PREF). If one ISP fails, BGP withdraws routes via that path and traffic shifts automatically.

**Active-passive**: advertise your prefixes to ISP-A with a longer AS_PATH (making it less preferred), so traffic normally uses ISP-B. ISP-A is the standby. Failover is automatic but slower.

**The convergence question**: BGP failover is not instantaneous. Depending on timer configurations (hold timers, keepalive intervals), BGP failure detection can take 90–180 seconds by default. BFD (Bidirectional Forwarding Detection) is used to reduce this to sub-second. In a Director design review, asking "what are your BGP hold timers and do you have BFD enabled?" is the question that separates platform engineers from configuration managers.

## Anycast: Same IP, Many Locations

Anycast is a routing technique where the same IP address is advertised from multiple physical locations. BGP routes traffic to the topologically nearest advertisement. No client-side configuration required — the routing system handles it transparently.

**How Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1 works**: Cloudflare advertises the 1.1.1.1 prefix from hundreds of Points of Presence worldwide. When your device queries 1.1.1.1, BGP routes that query to the nearest Cloudflare POP. If that POP goes offline, BGP withdraws its advertisement and traffic shifts to the next nearest. From the client's perspective, 1.1.1.1 always works and always feels fast.

**T-Mobile carrier context**: Anycast is used for carrier services where geographic proximity matters — voice media gateways, SMS delivery systems, DNS resolvers for subscriber devices. You route to the nearest instance, not a specific one. This is operationally identical to how platform engineering uses anycast for global load distribution.

**Enterprise use cases**:
- Global DNS resolver infrastructure (your authoritative DNS servers)
- DDoS scrubbing — anycast pulls attack traffic to distributed scrubbing centers
- Content delivery — CDN edge nodes all announce the same prefix; clients route to the nearest edge

## BGP Failure Modes

**Route withdrawal**: your routes disappear from the global table. Your ISP stops advertising your prefixes. New connections to your services cannot be established — the internet doesn't know how to reach you. Existing TCP connections may continue briefly until they time out. This is what happened during major outages at Fastly, Cloudflare, and others — a configuration change caused route withdrawal.

**Route leak**: a BGP peer accidentally re-advertises routes they should not. In 2019, a small ISP in Pennsylvania leaked routes it had learned from Verizon to Cloudflare, causing Cloudflare's traffic to be redirected through a network that couldn't handle it. The fix required BGP communities and prefix filtering — controls your network team should have in place before you need them.

**BGP hijack**: a malicious or misconfigured AS announces your IP prefix, attracting traffic meant for you. More specific prefixes win in BGP (a /25 beats a /24), so an attacker announcing a more specific prefix can intercept traffic. RPKI (Resource Public Key Infrastructure) is the mitigation — cryptographic validation that only the legitimate owner can announce a prefix.

## BGP in Multi-Cloud

AWS, Azure, and GCP each have their own ASNs. When you establish AWS Direct Connect or Azure ExpressRoute, you are running eBGP between your on-premises routers and the cloud provider's edge routers. You advertise your on-premises prefixes into the cloud; the cloud advertises its VPC/VNet prefixes back to you.

This means the reliability analysis for Direct Connect is a BGP reliability analysis. If the BGP session drops — due to a physical fiber cut, a misconfigured route filter, or a timer expiry — traffic falls back to internet-based connectivity (if you have it configured) or fails. In a Director-level architecture review, "what happens to Direct Connect BGP if the physical circuit drops and how fast does the failover to internet routing occur?" is a non-negotiable question.

## Director Decision Frame

You do not configure BGP. Your network engineers do. Your job is to make the architectural decisions that determine whether BGP is a reliability asset or a single point of failure.

**Decisions you own:**
- Single ISP vs. multi-ISP: a cost/reliability trade-off. Multi-ISP BGP adds cost (second circuit, router hardware, ASN registration, operational complexity). What is the business case for the additional ISP?
- Active-active vs. active-passive: active-active maximizes utilization but requires more careful traffic engineering. Active-passive is simpler to operate. Which fits your team's maturity?
- Private ASN vs. public ASN: if you're running BGP only internally or with a single provider, private ASN is sufficient. Public ASN is needed for multi-provider or multi-cloud BGP.

**Questions you ask in design reviews:**
- Are our BGP prefixes protected by RPKI? If not, we are vulnerable to hijack.
- What is the BGP convergence time on our ISP failover path? Have we tested it?
- Do we have prefix filters on eBGP sessions? An unfiltered session is a route leak waiting to happen.
- When Direct Connect BGP fails, what is the automatic failover path? Has that path been tested under production load?

**When BGP fails in an incident:**
- Check BGP session state first — is the session established? If the session is down, routing is down regardless of what the application layer thinks.
- Check prefix advertisements — are your prefixes still in the global routing table? Tools: `bgp.he.net`, `bgpview.io`, `RIPE RIS`.
- Engage your ISP's NOC with your ASN and prefix — they have visibility you don't.

---

## Key Takeaways

- BGP is the routing layer of the internet; enterprises use it to control how traffic enters and exits their network across multiple ISPs or cloud providers.
- An ASN is required for meaningful multi-ISP redundancy — without it, you are dependent on your ISP's routing decisions during a failure.
- BGP failover is not instantaneous; default timers allow 90–180 seconds of failure before rerouting. BFD reduces this to sub-second and should be standard in any SLA-sensitive architecture.
- Anycast uses BGP to route traffic to the nearest instance of a service — the same IP, many locations, transparent to clients.
- The three BGP failure modes (withdrawal, leak, hijack) have different blast radii and different mitigations; RPKI addresses hijack, prefix filters address leaks, multi-ISP addresses withdrawal.
- Your T-Mobile background gives you carrier-side BGP intuition — translate that into the enterprise context by asking convergence time, prefix filtering, and RPKI questions that most platform engineers skip.
