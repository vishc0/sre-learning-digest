# Chapter 8: Director Decision Framework — Network Capacity and Resilience

## The Core Idea

Network engineering is a team you manage, not a skill you operate. Your job at Director level is to set the architecture direction, ask the right questions during reviews, and make the build/buy/partner decisions that individual contributors and even senior managers avoid because the tradeoffs are cross-functional, expensive, and politically charged.

This chapter is the capstone. Everything in ch01–ch07 — routing, DNS, load balancing, CDN, security — has been about understanding the domain deeply enough to govern it. This chapter is about the five decisions that actually separate a Director from a Senior Manager: peering vs. transit economics, multi-ISP architecture for carrier-grade reliability, ISP contract terms that protect your organization during outages, the build/buy/hybrid choice for network capacity, and the resilience architecture questions you ask in every design review.

An interviewer probing at Director/VP level is not asking if you know what BGP is. They are asking: have you made a network capacity decision that involved capital allocation, vendor negotiation, and organizational change? What did you decide, and why?

## Section 1: Peering vs. Transit — The Economics of Internet Connectivity

### Transit: What Everyone Starts With

Transit is the straightforward model. You pay an ISP — AT&T, Lumen, Zayo — a monthly fee, and they carry your traffic to and from the rest of the internet. Your 1Gbps of traffic leaves your data center, crosses your ISP's backbone, and reaches whatever destination it needs to reach. Simple, scalable, fully managed. You need no BGP expertise, no autonomous system number, no presence at an exchange point.

The cost model: you pay per Mbps of committed bandwidth, plus burst fees above that commit. At small scale, transit is the right answer. At large scale — sustained traffic above 1Gbps, especially to a small number of large destinations — you start paying a significant premium for the ISP to carry traffic that could flow more directly.

### Peering: What Scale Forces You to Consider

Peering is a bilateral agreement between two networks to exchange traffic directly, typically at no cost (settlement-free peering). Instead of your traffic going: your network → your ISP → Google's ISP → Google, it goes: your network → Internet Exchange Point → Google's network. Fewer hops, lower latency, and no per-byte cost to the transit provider.

To peer, you need: an autonomous system number (ASN) from ARIN, a router capable of running BGP, and physical presence at an Internet Exchange Point (IXP) where peering happens. Major IXPs: Equinix in the US, DE-CIX in Frankfurt, AMS-IX in Amsterdam, LINX in London. At these facilities, you plug a port into the IXP fabric and establish BGP sessions with other members.

### Why T-Mobile Peers

T-Mobile operates at a scale where transit economics become untenable. The notification platform alone handles 25M messages per day — but carrier-wide, T-Mobile's traffic volume with Google (Android device traffic), Apple (iMessage, push notifications), Meta (WhatsApp, Instagram), Netflix, and Akamai is in the hundreds of gigabits sustained. Paying transit rates on that volume is tens of millions of dollars annually. Peering with those networks at major IXPs eliminates that cost entirely for traffic to peers.

T-Mobile also benefits from latency reduction. Push notifications from your platform to Android devices traverse fewer hops when T-Mobile peers directly with Google rather than backhauling through a transit provider. At 25M messages per day, a 10ms improvement per message compounds into measurable end-user experience improvement.

### When Enterprises Should Evaluate Peering

The director-level decision framework:

| Criteria | Consider Peering | Stay with Transit |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Sustained outbound traffic | > 1 Gbps | < 500 Mbps |
| Major metro presence | Yes — colocation in Equinix/Digital Realty | No DC presence |
| Engineering team | BGP-capable network engineers on staff | Network team at junior/mid level |
| Traffic destination | Concentrated: top 10 destinations = 80%+ of traffic | Distributed: thousands of small destinations |
| Payback horizon | 12–18 months to break even on IXP fees | > 24 months — stay on transit |

Peering is an infrastructure investment: ASN fees, IXP port fees ($500–$5,000/month per port), router hardware or colocation costs, and BGP engineering time. The decision requires a capital allocation, which means a Director or VP sponsor.

**Director interview frame**: "I evaluated peering when we crossed 2Gbps sustained to a handful of large CDN and cloud destinations. I built the business case showing transit cost at that volume versus IXP port cost plus engineering overhead. The 14-month payback got approved. We brought up peering sessions with Cloudflare, Google, and Akamai and immediately reduced transit cost by 35%."

## Section 2: Multi-ISP Architecture for Telecom-Grade Reliability

### Why Single ISP Is Not a Reliability Strategy

A single ISP is a single point of failure. It doesn't matter how reliable your application is — if the ISP's fiber is cut between your data center and their PoP, you're dark. Every 99.99% SLA you've committed to your customers is contingent on your upstream connectivity, and your ISP's SLA to you is typically 99.9% — that's 8.7 hours of permitted downtime per year. You cannot build a 99.99% platform on a 99.9% network layer.

The mitigation is architectural: two ISPs, with real independence, carrying your traffic simultaneously.

### Active-Active vs. Active-Passive

**Active-active**: both ISPs carry live traffic all the time. Traffic is distributed between them using BGP traffic engineering — you advertise your prefixes to both ISPs with identical (or tuned) BGP attributes and let routing distribute the load. When one ISP fails, BGP reconverges and all traffic shifts to the surviving ISP within 30–90 seconds. No wasted capacity.

Complexity: BGP tuning is required to ensure traffic distributes the way you intend. Without tuning, asymmetric routing can result in one ISP carrying 90% of traffic while the other is idle. You need a network engineer who understands BGP local preference, MED, and AS-path prepending.

**Active-passive**: one ISP carries all traffic; the second ISP is configured but idle, waiting for failover. Simpler BGP configuration, but you're paying for capacity you're not using. Failover requires BGP reconvergence — typically slower than active-active because the passive path's routes may be deprioritized.

**Recommendation**: active-active for anything with a 99.99% SLA. The BGP complexity is manageable and the capacity utilization argument justifies the operational overhead.

### Physical Diversity: The Detail That Actually Matters

The most common mistake in multi-ISP architecture is treating two ISPs as diverse when they share physical fiber. ISP A and ISP B may both have a PoP in your building — but if they both use the same fiber conduit from the street to your building, one backhoe in the parking lot takes both ISPs out simultaneously. This happens regularly.

What true diversity requires:
1. **Different ISP providers** — not two subsidiaries of the same parent company
2. **Different physical fiber paths** — confirmed in writing, not verbally
3. **Different entry points into the building** — different conduit, different MPOE (Minimum Point of Entry)
4. **Different last-mile providers** — where ISPs lease last-mile from a local exchange carrier, verify they're on different duct systems

Demand a LOA (Letter of Authorization) and physical path documentation from each ISP. A diagram showing the fiber route from the street to your cage is not excessive — it is due diligence. Carriers often share infrastructure without disclosing it proactively.

**Director decision**: write physical path diversity into every ISP procurement RFP as a contractual requirement, not a preference.

## Section 3: ISP Contract Negotiation — What a Director Must Own

### SLA Terms That Actually Matter

Most ISP SLAs promise availability, latency, and packet loss. Read them carefully — the definitions determine whether a breach is measurable.

**Availability**: typically defined as 99.9% monthly. That is 8.7 hours of downtime per month, measured as "the circuit is completely unavailable." Partial degradation — 50% packet loss, elevated latency — often does not count as a service outage under the contract. Negotiate: degraded performance (packet loss > X%, latency > Y ms for > Z minutes) should count toward SLA calculation.

**Latency**: commonly stated as "less than 50ms RTT within region." Verify what "within region" means — an ISP's definition of region may be larger than yours. Negotiate specific city-pair measurements.

**Packet loss**: typically < 0.1%. Anything above this causes TCP to retransmit, which compounds under load. A 0.5% packet loss rate is barely visible in monitoring but will degrade p99 latency significantly on high-throughput connections.

### Credit Provisions: What They Actually Mean

SLA breach credits sound like protection. Read the fine print. Credits are almost always: a percentage of monthly service fee, applied as a credit against future invoices, capped at 30 days of service fee. For a $10,000/month circuit, a 4-hour outage credit might be $1,000 — far less than the business impact of the outage.

Negotiate:
- **Credit = hours down × hourly rate**, not a fixed percentage of monthly fee
- **No cap** or a higher cap (e.g., 60 days of service credit for a major outage)
- **Credit issued proactively** upon confirmed breach, not after you open a dispute ticket
- **Business impact clause** — rare, but possible with large spend commitments: credits proportional to documented business impact

Set expectations internally: SLA credits are not compensation for outages. They are a signal that the ISP takes the commitment seriously and an incentive for them to meet it. Budget accordingly.

### Escalation Paths and MTTR Commitments

ISPs rarely commit contractually to MTTR (Mean Time to Repair). They will commit to response time — "a technician will begin troubleshooting within 4 hours." These are different things. A technician who begins troubleshooting at hour 4 and resolves at hour 12 has met their response SLA but been down for 12 hours.

Negotiate MTTR explicitly: 4 hours for a major circuit failure is achievable for a premium service tier; 2 hours is aggressive but achievable with a dedicated enterprise account team.

More importantly: get named NOC contacts and an enterprise escalation path. The difference between a 4-hour resolution and an 8-hour resolution during a major outage is often whether you can call a named person who owns your account versus calling a general 800 number and waiting in queue with thousands of other customers.

**Director action**: after signing any ISP contract, conduct a tabletop exercise — "we just lost the primary circuit at 2 AM." Walk through the escalation path to validate it actually works before you need it in production.

## Section 4: Build vs. Buy Network Capacity

### The Three Models

**Build (own)**: colocation space + your own routers, switches, and fiber. Maximum control over hardware, configuration, and failure domains. Maximum cost: CapEx for hardware, OpEx for colocation and power, and network engineering headcount to operate it. Right choice when: compliance requires it (government, defense), latency requirements cannot be met by cloud networking, or traffic volume makes cloud networking cost-prohibitive.

**Buy (cloud provider networking)**: VPC, CloudFront, AWS Shield, Route53, Direct Connect. Fully managed, elastically scalable, no hardware. You lose control over failure domain isolation — an AWS networking event can affect your entire presence in a region. Right choice when: engineering team is small, CapEx budget is unavailable, or flexibility and time-to-market outweigh control.

**Hybrid (the T-Mobile model)**: carrier-grade physical network for the core (owned infrastructure, MPLS backbone, physical data centers), cloud for edge and CDN (AWS CloudFront, Route53, Shield), Direct Connect for inter-connect between physical and cloud. The physical network provides the reliability and control the carrier SLA demands. The cloud provides elastic scale and geographic distribution at the edge.

### Decision Matrix

| Factor | Build (Own) | Buy (Cloud) | Hybrid |
|--------|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Traffic volume | > 100 Gbps sustained | < 10 Gbps | 10–100 Gbps |
| Compliance requirement | Strict (FISMA, FedRAMP) | Flexible | Mixed |
| Engineering team | 10+ network engineers | < 3 network engineers | 4–10 |
| Capital availability | CapEx available | OpEx preference | Balanced |
| Time to capacity | Months (hardware lead time) | Hours | Weeks for physical, hours for cloud |
| Failure domain control | Maximum | Minimum | Partial |

The Director frames this as: **volume, control, compliance, team, and capital**. Any organization that says "we'll just use AWS networking for everything" without running this matrix has optimized for convenience, not for their actual requirements.

## Section 5: Resilience Architecture — The Questions a Director Asks

In every network architecture review, you ask five questions. Not because you want to show technical knowledge — because the answers reveal whether the design will hold under failure.

**1. What is the blast radius if this network component fails?**
"If the core router in this data center fails, what services are down, for how long, and how does traffic reconverge?" A good answer names specific services, gives an estimated impact duration, and references the failover mechanism. A bad answer is "we have redundancy" without specifics.

**2. What fails when the WAN link goes down?**
In a hybrid cloud environment, a WAN link failure (Direct Connect, MPLS, site-to-site VPN) can silently break dependencies that were designed assuming connectivity. Services that authenticate against Active Directory, fetch configuration from an on-prem config server, or write to an on-prem database all fail in non-obvious ways. The answer to this question should be a documented dependency map, not a guess.

**3. How long before DNS cache expires?**
DNS TTL is a resilience decision masquerading as a configuration parameter. Low TTL (60 seconds) means fast failover — DNS change propagates quickly. High TTL (3600 seconds) means slow failover but lower DNS query load and better performance for end users. Setting TTL correctly requires knowing your failover time target and your DNS query volume. "We haven't thought about it" is a significant gap.

**4. Is the fiber path physically diverse?**
As described in Section 2, two circuits from two ISPs are not diverse if they share a conduit. Ask for physical path documentation in every network design review that involves multi-site connectivity.

**5. What does the health check failure behavior look like?**
Load balancer health checks are the mechanism that routes around failed backends. But misconfigured health checks cause their own failures: thresholds too aggressive (a slow health check response causes unnecessary failover), thresholds too lenient (a genuinely unhealthy backend stays in rotation). The question forces the design to document the health check configuration and its failure behavior explicitly.

## Director Interview Talking Points

Three network decisions separate a Director from a Senior Manager:

**Decision 1: Multi-ISP architecture** — not "do we have two ISPs" but "how did we ensure physical path diversity, what is our BGP failover target, and have we tested it?" Senior managers know you should have two ISPs. Directors have designed the architecture, written the procurement requirements, and run failover tests.

**Decision 2: Private vs. public connectivity strategy** — the choice between Direct Connect, internet-over-IPsec, and public internet for inter-service communication is a cost, security, and reliability decision that requires cross-functional ownership. Directors own the policy; engineers implement it.

**Decision 3: DNS TTL as an organizational risk tolerance decision** — TTL is not a technical parameter, it is a business decision. A 60-second TTL means your SRE team can redirect traffic in 60 seconds. A 3600-second TTL means you are willing to accept up to an hour of impact during a DNS-based failover. That trade-off is a Director conversation, not an engineer configuration choice.

Frame it this way in interviews: "The thing I learned running network at [T-Mobile scale] is that the technical decisions are the easy part. The hard part is getting the organization to treat network architecture decisions with the same rigor as application architecture decisions — documented, reviewed, tested, and owned."
