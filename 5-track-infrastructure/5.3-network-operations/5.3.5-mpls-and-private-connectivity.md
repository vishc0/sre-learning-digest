# Chapter 5: MPLS and Private Connectivity

## The Core Idea

MPLS is like a dedicated lane on the highway — you pay a premium, but you get guaranteed throughput and predictable latency regardless of how congested the other lanes get. The public internet is shared lanes: your packets compete with everyone else's, latency spikes during peak hours, and you have no contractual guarantee about what happens in between. For most web traffic, shared lanes are fine. For carrier-grade voice, financial transactions, or latency-sensitive database replication, you need the dedicated lane.

That lane-versus-shared-highway choice underpins every private connectivity decision at enterprise scale.

## What MPLS Actually Does

**Multi-Protocol Label Switching** replaces the normal IP routing decision at every network hop. In a standard IP network, each router reads the full destination IP address and consults its routing table to decide where to forward the packet — this happens at every hop across the path. Under MPLS, the first router in the network assigns a short label to the packet. Every subsequent router in the MPLS domain reads only that label (a fast table lookup) and forwards accordingly. The label is swapped at each hop until the packet exits the MPLS domain.

The operational consequence: MPLS routers make forwarding decisions faster and more predictably than IP routers, and the path is pre-computed rather than dynamically discovered at each hop. This is why MPLS networks can offer **CIR (Committed Information Rate)** — a contractually guaranteed minimum bandwidth. If you pay for 1 Gbps CIR, you get 1 Gbps even at 3 AM on New Year's Eve.

T-Mobile and other carriers use MPLS as the substrate for carrier-grade services: voice interconnects, roaming partner traffic, and backhaul between cell sites and the core network. If you've worked with carrier infrastructure, you've worked adjacent to MPLS whether you realized it or not.

## MPLS Use Cases in Practice

- **Enterprise WAN** — connecting offices, data centers, and remote sites over a private carrier network. Branch office in Chicago connects to HQ in Seattle over MPLS with predictable latency and no internet congestion.
- **Carrier voice and video** — VoIP requires low jitter (< 30ms) and low packet loss (< 1%). MPLS QoS policies prioritize voice packets with guaranteed treatment.
- **Financial services** — trading systems where 10ms of latency variance can mean the difference between a filled and missed order.
- **Healthcare and compliance** — data that must travel over private paths to satisfy HIPAA or PCI requirements.

## SD-WAN: The Enterprise Shift Away from MPLS

SD-WAN — Software-Defined Wide Area Network — is the modern answer to "MPLS is expensive and we have good broadband everywhere now." The architecture: instead of one expensive MPLS circuit, deploy multiple cheaper circuits (broadband, LTE, sometimes MPLS as one of several paths) and use software-defined policy to route traffic across them intelligently.

A voice call goes over the lowest-jitter path in real time. A bulk file transfer goes over the cheapest path. If one circuit degrades or fails, traffic shifts to another automatically — no manual failover.

| Dimension | MPLS | SD-WAN |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Cost | High (carrier-grade pricing) | Low-to-medium (commodity internet + software) |
| Latency predictability | High (CIR, pre-computed paths) | Medium (path quality varies by underlay) |
| Failover | Manual or pre-provisioned backup | Automatic, policy-driven |
| Management complexity | Low (carrier manages) | Higher (you manage the overlay) |
| Vendor lock-in | High (carrier-specific) | Medium (SD-WAN vendor, but underlay is commodity) |
| Time to provision new site | Weeks to months | Days |

**The director-level read on SD-WAN:** most enterprises are migrating from MPLS to SD-WAN for cost reasons, retaining MPLS only for the most latency-sensitive or compliance-critical paths. The operational shift is that you absorb management complexity in exchange for cost savings — which requires a capable network engineering team. If your team doesn't have that depth, managed SD-WAN (where a vendor operates the overlay for you) is the right transition path.

## Private Cloud Connectivity

The same dedicated-lane logic that applies to enterprise WAN applies to connecting your on-premises infrastructure to cloud providers.

**AWS Direct Connect** — a dedicated private circuit between your facility (or a colocation provider) and AWS. Traffic never traverses the public internet. AWS advertises your Direct Connect endpoint via BGP, and you route cloud-bound traffic over the private path. Latency is predictable (because the path is fixed), and throughput is guaranteed (you select a port speed: 1 Gbps or 10 Gbps). Cost: port-hour fee plus data transfer fees, which at high volumes are significantly cheaper than internet egress from AWS.

**Azure ExpressRoute** and **Google Cloud Interconnect** implement the same concept for their respective clouds.

**When to choose private connectivity over public internet:**

| Condition | Decision |
|-----------|----------|
| Latency SLA < 10ms to cloud | Private connectivity required |
| Data transfer > 10 TB/month | Private connectivity likely cheaper than internet egress |
| Regulatory requirement (HIPAA, PCI, FedRAMP) that data not traverse public internet | Private connectivity required |
| Disaster recovery or database replication to cloud | Private connectivity strongly preferred |
| Standard HTTPS API traffic, latency-tolerant | Public internet + TLS is sufficient |
| Development or test workloads | Public internet |

## The Procurement Reality

This is where the director-level perspective diverges sharply from the engineering-level perspective.

An AWS Direct Connect circuit is not an infrastructure ticket you open when you need it. The procurement and provisioning process runs 2-6 months: carrier selection, circuit order, cross-connect provisioning at a colocation facility, BGP configuration, testing, and cutover. If a compliance audit reveals in February that your architecture requires private cloud connectivity, you are solving a June problem at best.

The implication for platform engineering leadership: private connectivity decisions must be made in the architecture phase of a project, not during implementation. When you're in a design review for a new workload that will handle PII, financial data, or latency-sensitive operations between on-premises and cloud, the private connectivity question belongs on the agenda — not as an afterthought.

Budget cycles compound this. Direct Connect has a recurring monthly cost (port-hour fee) plus variable data transfer cost. This goes into the infrastructure budget, not the project budget. A director who understands this avoids the scenario where a team delivers a new capability that then can't go to production because the connectivity isn't funded or provisioned.

## Director Decision Frame

Private connectivity decisions are capital-allocation and procurement decisions dressed in technical clothing. The questions to drive in a design review:

- What is the latency SLA for this workload, and is public internet variance acceptable for that SLA?
- What is our monthly data transfer volume to cloud? At what volume does Direct Connect become cheaper than internet egress?
- Does this workload touch data that has a regulatory requirement for private transit?
- If we need Direct Connect, when does the procurement process need to start to meet the delivery date?
- Do we have existing Direct Connect capacity that this workload can share, or is this a new circuit?

MPLS for carrier services is operational knowledge — you need to understand it to reason about T-Mobile's carrier infrastructure. Direct Connect and ExpressRoute are investment decisions you'll make or influence at director level. The framing in both cases is identical: what are you buying (predictability and privacy), what does it cost (premium over commodity), and when do you need to start the process (earlier than the engineering team thinks).

## Key Takeaways

- MPLS provides predictable latency and guaranteed bandwidth (CIR) by pre-computing paths and using label-based forwarding instead of per-hop IP routing decisions.
- SD-WAN replaces expensive MPLS with policy-driven routing across multiple commodity circuits. Cost savings come with added management complexity — a managed SD-WAN offering is the right call for teams without deep network engineering depth.
- AWS Direct Connect, Azure ExpressRoute, and GCP Interconnect provide dedicated private circuits to cloud. Use them when latency SLAs are tight, data volumes are high, or compliance requires private transit.
- The 2-6 month provisioning timeline for Direct Connect is the most important operational fact for a director. Private connectivity is an architectural decision, not an implementation detail.
- At T-Mobile scale, understanding MPLS as carrier infrastructure substrate is table stakes for credible carrier platform discussions. The BGP relationship between carrier MPLS domains and upstream providers is the same BGP that governs Direct Connect routing.
