# Chapter 7: Database Chaos Engineering — Knowing Your Failover Actually Works

> **Director framing**: "An untested failover is not a failover plan. It is a hope. My job is to replace hope with evidence."

---

## Why Database Chaos Is Different

When you kill a stateless application pod in Kubernetes, the worst outcome is a few seconds of elevated error rates while the scheduler brings up a replacement. The blast radius is bounded and reversible. Kubernetes designed stateless recovery to be automatic and safe.

Databases are not stateless. When you kill a database primary incorrectly — without understanding quorum, without confirming replica state, without verifying application retry behavior — you can lose writes, corrupt replication streams, or partition your cluster into two islands that both think they are writable. The blast radius is asymmetric: the damage can exceed the recovery time by orders of magnitude.

This asymmetry is exactly why most teams avoid testing their database failover. The fear is rational. But the consequence of not testing is worse: you discover your failover is broken during a production incident at 2 AM, under pressure, with customers affected, not during a controlled experiment on a Tuesday morning with the whole team watching dashboards.

**The discipline of database chaos engineering is about replacing false confidence with measured evidence.**

---

## The Foundational Principle

Every team says they have replicas. Very few have actually done this:

- Promoted a replica to primary under production-level write load
- Measured the application error rate during the election window
- Confirmed that reads did not route to a lagging replica
- Verified that the monitoring fired before the incident did
- Walked the runbook and found the steps that were wrong

Chaos engineering is not about breaking things for sport. It is a structured methodology to surface the gap between what the runbook says will happen and what actually happens. In distributed databases, that gap is almost always larger than the team expects.

## Five Fault Injection Patterns for Databases

### 1. Leader/Primary Kill

**What you inject**: Terminate the primary database pod or process abruptly — SIGKILL, not graceful shutdown.

**What you observe**:
- Does Patroni (Postgres) or Cassandra's coordinator routing detect the failure and reroute correctly?
- What is the application error rate during the election period? How long does it last?
- Are writes acknowledged before the kill actually persisted, or are they silently lost?
- Does the application retry correctly, or does it treat a connection reset as a fatal error?

**What you learn**: Whether your high-availability configuration is theoretical or operational.

**Common finding**: The application does not retry idempotently. The first failover you run in a game day will surface retry logic that was written optimistically and never tested under failure conditions.

---

### 2. Replica Lag Injection

**What you inject**: Throttle network bandwidth from primary to replica using `tc netem` or Chaos Mesh's NetworkChaos. Drop the replication bandwidth from 1 Gbps to 10 Mbps and sustain it for 10 minutes.

**What you observe**:
- Does your alerting fire on replica lag? At what threshold?
- Does your application route reads to the lagging replica, returning stale data?
- Does your read routing logic have a max-acceptable-lag cutoff, or does it route blindly?

**What you learn**: Whether your read routing has a staleness contract or just a best-effort hope.

**DND relevance**: At 25M messages/day, a notification status read from a replica that is 30 seconds behind is not just a technical metric — it means a customer looking at their notification history sees a state that does not match reality. This is a product trust issue, not just a reliability issue.

---

### 3. Disk Fill

**What you inject**: Write synthetic data to the database host's data volume until it reaches 95%+ of capacity. Use a simple write loop or a dedicated chaos tool.

**What you observe**:
- Does the database enter read-only mode gracefully, or does it crash?
- Does alerting fire at 80%, 90%, or not until the database is already degraded?
- Does the PagerDuty alert route to someone who knows what to do?

**What you learn**: Whether your disk monitoring is proactive or reactive.

**Common finding**: Alerting is set at 90% because someone thought that was conservative. The database runs compaction at 85% and consumes the remaining headroom in under 10 minutes. The alert fires at the same time the database goes read-only.

---

### 4. Network Partition

**What you inject**: Split the database cluster into two groups of nodes that cannot communicate with each other. In EKS, this is achievable with NetworkChaos policies targeting specific pod-to-pod communication paths.

**What you observe**:
- Which partition remains writable? Is this deterministic, or does it depend on election timing?
- Does the application have split-brain protection, or will it write to both sides?
- When the partition heals, does the cluster converge correctly, or does it require manual intervention?

**What you learn**: Whether your cluster's quorum configuration actually enforces a single write path.

**Cassandra-specific note**: Cassandra's tunable consistency model means a network partition behaves differently depending on your consistency level. At `QUORUM` writes, you need the majority of replicas. If your partition isolates the majority from the coordinator, writes will fail fast and cleanly. At `ONE`, a partition can create divergent writes that require read repair to reconcile. Know which you're running before the incident tells you.

---

### 5. Connection Pool Exhaustion

**What you inject**: Inject slow queries (via query injection or a `pg_sleep` / Cassandra slow-query proxy) that hold connections open. Or inject a connection leak by opening connections without releasing them.

**What you observe**:
- Does the connection pool fill? How quickly?
- Does the circuit breaker trip and protect upstream services, or do all upstream services block together?
- Do applications degrade gracefully (serve cached data, return 503 early) or fail in unconstrained cascades?

**What you learn**: Whether your connection pool limits are set correctly and whether your circuit breaker configuration is real or aspirational.

## Chaos Mesh for Kubernetes Database Pods

[Chaos Mesh](https://chaos-mesh.org) is the standard chaos engineering platform for Kubernetes workloads. For databases running as pods in EKS, it supports:

- **PodChaos**: Kill, pause, or restart database pods on a schedule or trigger
- **NetworkChaos**: Inject latency, packet loss, bandwidth throttling, or partition between specific pod selectors
- **IOChaos**: Inject disk I/O latency, errors, or bandwidth limits against specific paths (e.g., the Cassandra data directory)
- **TimeChaos**: Skew the system clock on database pods — critical for distributed systems that depend on timestamp ordering

### Example: Cassandra Node Kill Experiment

```yaml
apiVersion: chaos-mesh.org/v1alpha1
kind: PodChaos
metadata:
  name: cassandra-node-kill-experiment
  namespace: chaos-testing
spec:
  action: pod-kill
  mode: one                        # Kill exactly one pod
  selector:
    namespaces:
      - dnd-platform
    labelSelectors:
      app: cassandra
  duration: "5m"                   # Observe for 5 minutes before auto-recovery
  scheduler:
    cron: "@daily"                 # Run during scheduled game day windows
```

**What this experiment verifies for DND**:
- Does coordinator failover happen within your SLO window?
- Does your Splunk dashboard show the node loss within 30 seconds?
- Does the application's retry policy absorb the coordinator timeout without surfacing errors to the API layer?

### Cassandra-Specific Consideration: Coordinator Behavior

In Cassandra, unlike Postgres or MySQL, there is no fixed primary. Any node can be the coordinator for any query. When you kill a node, the client driver transparently routes to another coordinator. The risk is not coordinator election — it is **replica availability for the partition key** of the query being executed.

If you are running at `QUORUM` consistency and you kill one node in a three-node cluster, any query whose partition key is primarily owned by the killed node will require the other two replicas to respond. If one of those replicas is also degraded (compaction, GC pause, network), your write will fail. The chaos experiment surfaces whether your three-node topology actually provides the redundancy you assume.

## Controlled Chaos in Production

### Game Day Structure

A game day is a scheduled, structured chaos experiment — not a surprise drill. Structure:

1. **Pre-brief (30 min)**: Review the experiment, confirm abort criteria, assign roles (experiment runner, dashboard observer, incident commander if needed, communication lead)
2. **Steady state baseline (15 min)**: Capture current error rate, latency P50/P99, throughput. This is your before-state.
3. **Inject (duration per experiment)**: Execute the chaos. Everyone watches dashboards in silence for the first 2 minutes.
4. **Observe and record**: Capture actual metrics, timeline of events, any unexpected behavior
5. **Abort if necessary**: If P99 write latency exceeds 2x SLO baseline, abort immediately. Document what triggered the abort.
6. **Recover and verify**: Confirm cluster returns to steady state. Measure recovery time.
7. **Debrief (30 min)**: What happened vs. what we expected. What three things do we need to fix?

### Blast Radius Controls

Always define before starting:
- **Abort criteria**: specific, measurable threshold that triggers immediate halt
- **Rollback procedure**: how to undo the chaos injection if abort is triggered
- **Customer impact scope**: which user-facing features are at risk and what the fallback is
- **Communication protocol**: who gets notified if the experiment goes wrong

## Director Decision Frame

> "I require one database chaos experiment per quarter for every production database tier. Not because I enjoy breaking things — because I need empirical evidence that my team's runbooks match reality.

> The first time you run a failover drill, you will find three things that do not work. That is not a failure of the team. That is the value of the experiment. Find those three things in a Tuesday morning game day. Do not let a 2 AM incident find them for you.

> I measure the maturity of a database reliability program not by how many replicas it has, but by how recently it tested promoting one."

---

## Interview Application: How to Talk About This

When asked "How do you ensure database reliability at scale?" at a Director or VP level, the answer is not a list of features. It is a program:

- Quarterly chaos experiments against every Tier 0 database
- Game day structure with defined abort criteria
- Runbook validation as the primary output — chaos experiments are how you keep runbooks honest
- Metrics pipeline from Chaos Mesh experiments into your observability platform (Splunk/Datadog) so experiment results are traceable

The Cassandra angle for DND: "At 25M messages per day, an untested failover is not a risk I am willing to carry. We run structured node-kill experiments under simulated peak load and measure delayed vs. lost notifications as our primary chaos metric. This tells me whether my consistency configuration is actually protecting customers."
