# Chapter 06: Database SLOs and Observability — Measuring Health Before Users Feel It

---

## Why Database Observability Is a Different Problem

A service can be returning HTTP 200 while the database behind it is silently burning. Slow queries return data — they just return it late. A replica three minutes behind primary still serves reads — just stale ones. A connection pool at 95% utilization still accepts connections — until it doesn't, and then nothing does.

The application layer masks database degradation until the threshold is crossed. By the time users feel it, you've already lost. This is why database SLOs are harder than service SLOs: the failure mode is gradual, the signals are one layer removed, and the alerting on application metrics tells you what already broke, not what is about to.

The answer is to instrument and alert on **leading indicators** — signals that precede the user-visible failure by minutes or hours.

---

## The Three Leading Indicators of Database Failure

These are not the metrics that tell you the database is down. These are the metrics that tell you it is about to be.

### 1. Connection Pool Utilization Approaching Saturation

A connection pool is a fixed-size resource. When it is 80% utilized, the next traffic spike will not be absorbed — it will queue. When the queue fills, connections are dropped. Connection drops surface as 500 errors in the application tier, and your on-call team spends the first 20 minutes of the incident looking at the wrong layer.

**Analogy**: This is the same signal as a RabbitMQ queue filling up before consumer lag causes message drop. The queue depth is not the failure — it is the pre-failure warning. If you're watching queue depth at 80% on RabbitMQ, you already know this pattern.

**What to watch**: Pool utilization %, queue depth (connections waiting), connection acquisition timeout rate. Alert at 70% pool utilization. Page at 85%.

### 2. Replication Lag Growing

A replica falling behind primary is not just a freshness problem — it is a disaster recovery problem. If your application routes reads to replicas (which it should, for read scale), users are seeing data that is seconds or minutes old. If the primary fails while replica lag is 5 minutes, your RPO is 5 minutes, not the 30 seconds your runbook claims.

For Postgres: replication lag shows up in `pg_replication_slots` and `pg_stat_replication`. A growing slot lag means WAL is accumulating on primary. If a replica drops its replication slot (because it fell too far behind and the slot was dropped to reclaim disk), WAL will fill your primary's disk.

For Cassandra: the equivalent concept is hinted handoff — when a replica is down, the coordinator stores hints and replays them when the replica comes back. If a node is down long enough that hints expire (`max_hint_window`), data divergence occurs and you need a repair cycle.

**What to watch**: Seconds of lag for Postgres replicas, hint queue depth for Cassandra, and for any system — the trend, not just the instantaneous value.

### 3. P99 Query Latency Creep

Individual queries getting slower is the earliest symptom of a structural problem: a missing index, a lock contention pattern, a compaction backlog (Cassandra), or a table bloat condition (Postgres autovacuum falling behind). The P99 moves first; the median stays stable for longer. If you are only watching median latency, you will miss this.

The SLO breach pattern: P99 rises slowly over days, crosses the alert threshold during a traffic spike, and the on-call team treats it as a spike rather than an accumulation. Root cause analysis reveals it has been drifting for a week.

**What to watch**: P99 and P999 read latency, P99 write latency. Alert when P99 crosses 2x your baseline. Investigate when it is 1.5x baseline for more than 30 minutes.

## Database SLI Definitions

Be precise about what you are measuring. "Availability" means different things to different teams.

| SLI | Definition | Why This Matters |
|-----|-----------|-----------------|
| **Query availability** | Successful queries / total queries (not connection success — a timeout is a failure) | Connections succeeding while queries time out is a common masked failure mode |
| **Read latency P50/P99/P999** | End-to-end query time from application perspective | P50 hides tail. P999 captures the worst 1-in-1000 experience |
| **Write latency P50/P99** | End-to-end commit time including replication acknowledgment | For Cassandra QUORUM writes, this includes replica response time |
| **Replication lag** | Seconds behind primary (Postgres) / coordinator timeout rate (Cassandra) | Determines your actual RPO vs. your documented RPO |
| **Connection pool utilization** | % of pool active, plus queue depth | The leading indicator for connection storm failures |
| **Backup freshness** | Time since last verified successful backup restore test | "We have backups" means nothing until you've proven you can restore |

## Cassandra-Specific Metrics in Splunk

These are the metrics that matter for a platform running 25M messages/day. All sourced from Cassandra's JMX metrics, typically ingested via Telegraf or Prometheus and forwarded to Splunk.

**`ClientRequest.Latency.Read` and `ClientRequest.Latency.Write`**
End-to-end latency from the coordinator node's perspective. This is the right number — not the local node latency, but the full round-trip including replica responses. If this is climbing, you are approaching an SLO breach.

**`StorageProxy.ReplicaTimeouts`**
Replicas not responding within the timeout window. The coordinator sent the request; the replica did not respond in time. This is your early warning for node degradation, GC pressure, or compaction interference on individual nodes.

**`Compaction.PendingTasks`**
Compaction backlog depth. Cassandra's LSM-tree model requires periodic compaction — merging SSTables into larger ones and discarding tombstones (deleted data markers). When compaction falls behind, reads must scan more SSTables, latency climbs, and disk usage grows. A persistent non-zero backlog is a problem. A growing backlog is an emergency.

**`CQL.PreparedStatementsExecuted` vs `CQL.RegularStatementsExecuted`**
A high ratio of unprepared (regular) statements indicates the application is not using prepared statements — this forces the coordinator to parse and plan every query rather than using cached plans. This surfaces as increased coordinator CPU and modestly higher latency at scale.

**Sample Splunk SPL for P99 write latency by node:**
```spl
index=cassandra sourcetype=cassandra-metrics metric_name="ClientRequest.Latency.Write.p99"
| timechart span=5m avg(value) as p99_write_ms by host
| where p99_write_ms > 50
```

**Sample SPL for compaction backlog alert:**
```spl
index=cassandra sourcetype=cassandra-metrics metric_name="Compaction.PendingTasks"
| stats latest(value) as pending by host
| where pending > 100
| sort -pending
```

## PostgreSQL-Specific Metrics

**`pg_stat_activity`**
The live view of what connections are doing. The danger signal is not a high connection count — it is connections in `idle in transaction` state. A connection holding an open transaction blocks autovacuum on affected rows and can hold locks that queue other queries behind it. A growing `idle in transaction` count is a connection leak.

**`pg_replication_slots`**
Each replica consumes a replication slot. The slot tells Postgres how far behind the furthest consumer is — Postgres must retain WAL back to that point. If a replica falls behind and disconnects, its slot remains. WAL accumulates. Disk fills. **This is one of the most common causes of Postgres primary disk exhaustion in production.** Monitor `pg_replication_slots.confirmed_flush_lsn` lag and alert when a slot has been inactive for more than your WAL retention policy allows.

**`pg_stat_bgwriter`**
Checkpoint frequency. Postgres flushes dirty pages to disk during checkpoints. If `checkpoints_req` (requested checkpoints) is high relative to `checkpoints_timed` (scheduled checkpoints), your write pressure is forcing checkpoints faster than the configured interval. This is a sign that `checkpoint_completion_target` or `max_wal_size` needs tuning — and that your writes are I/O-intensive enough to warrant attention.

## SLO Targets for a Production Database Platform

These targets are directional — set them based on your service-level agreements with the business, not arbitrary percentages.

| Metric | Target | Alert Threshold | Escalation Trigger |
|--------|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Query availability (reads) | 99.95% | < 99.9% sustained 5 min | < 99.5% at any point |
| Query availability (writes) | 99.9% | < 99.5% sustained 5 min | < 99.0% at any point |
| Read P99 latency | < 20ms | > 50ms sustained 5 min | > 200ms at any point |
| Write P99 latency (Cassandra QUORUM) | < 50ms | > 100ms sustained 5 min | > 500ms at any point |
| Replication lag (Postgres) | < 10s | > 60s sustained | > 300s (5 min) |
| Connection pool utilization | < 70% | > 80% sustained | > 90% at any point |
| Compaction pending tasks (Cassandra) | < 20 | > 50 sustained 15 min | > 200 at any point |
| Backup freshness | < 24h since last verified | > 48h | > 72h |

**Error budget framing**: If your write availability target is 99.9%, your monthly error budget is 43 minutes of downtime. A single bad deployment that takes writes down for 10 minutes consumes 23% of your monthly budget. Budget-based thinking forces prioritization that point-in-time alerting does not.

## Director Decision Frame

> "I don't ask 'is the database up?' during an incident review. I ask three questions: What was the P99 write latency in the 30 minutes before the incident? What was replication lag trending? What percentage of the connection pool was consumed? If your team can't answer those three questions in 30 seconds using a dashboard, your observability is not production-grade. The database health story should be readable in a single pane — not reconstructed from logs after the fact."

**The maturity model for database observability:**

| Level | Capability | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| L1 — Reactive | You know the database is down when users report errors | No database SLOs, alert on application 500 rate only |
| L2 — Active | You alert on database metrics, but only on thresholds | Alert on disk > 85%, connection count > N |
| L3 — Leading | You alert on leading indicators before user impact | Alert on pool utilization, replication lag trend, P99 creep |
| L4 — Predictive | Baselines + anomaly detection; error budget tracking | Automated SLO burn rate alerts, capacity forecast dashboards |

Most teams operate at L2. Director/VP-level platform engineering expectations are L3, trending toward L4.

## Key Takeaways for Interview Contexts

- Database SLOs must be defined at the query level, not the connection level. A connection that succeeds and times out is a failure.
- The three pre-failure signals — connection pool saturation, replication lag growth, P99 latency creep — are detectable 15-60 minutes before users feel them. If you're not alerting on these, you are always responding, never anticipating.
- Cassandra compaction backlog and Postgres replication slot lag are the two most commonly missed signals in Cassandra and Postgres environments respectively. Both have caused preventable production incidents at scale.
- Error budget framing converts abstract availability percentages into operational urgency. "We have 43 minutes of error budget left this month" is a conversation that drives prioritization. "We're at 99.9% this month" does not.
- At Director/VP level, the ask is not "can you build a dashboard?" — it is "can you define what healthy looks like, instrument it, and use it to drive reliability investment decisions?"
