# Chapter 8: The Director Decision Framework — Build, Buy, Consolidate, or Migrate

> **Director framing**: "The most expensive database decision is the one made by an application team in a weekend hackathon that the platform team then has to operate for five years. My job is to make the right choice obvious and the wrong choice hard."

---

## The Core Inheritance Problem

Every Director of Platform Engineering who steps into a new role faces the same situation within the first 90 days: an inherited database estate that was not designed — it accumulated. Application teams chose their data stores for their own reasons, at their own pace, with their own SLO expectations. Nobody optimized the portfolio. Nobody rationalized the operational cost.

Your job is to look at that estate and answer four questions:

1. **What do we build ourselves** — because our access patterns require control that no managed service exposes?
2. **What do we buy** — because the operational overhead of self-hosting exceeds the cost of the managed service?
3. **What do we consolidate** — because we are running five databases where two would do, and we are paying five operational taxes?
4. **What do we migrate** — because what was right three years ago is wrong now, and we are carrying technical debt that compounds every quarter?

These are not purely technical decisions. They are organizational, financial, and strategic decisions that happen to involve technology. The Director-level skill is knowing which lens to apply first.

## Managed vs. Self-Hosted: The Real Trade

The managed vs. self-hosted decision is not about features. It is about **where you want your team to spend its cognitive load**.

### AWS RDS / Aurora (Managed Postgres/MySQL)

AWS handles: OS patching, database minor version upgrades, automated backups with point-in-time recovery, multi-AZ failover, storage auto-scaling.

**What you give up**:
- OS-level metric access (you cannot `iostat` on an RDS instance — you get CloudWatch approximations)
- Control over configuration parameters that AWS does not expose (some Postgres extension tuning, kernel-level settings)
- Custom extensions that require superuser or OS access

**What you gain**:
- The operational surface area shrinks to schema management, query performance, and capacity planning — all of which are differentiated engineering work
- Failover is automated and tested by AWS; you are not managing Patroni configuration or replica promotion scripts
- The RDS SLA is AWS's problem to meet, not yours

**Decision rule**: If your team does not have a dedicated database reliability engineer, and your access patterns are standard relational workloads, RDS/Aurora is almost always right. The skill to self-host Postgres correctly — Patroni, pgBouncer, WAL-E, streaming replication, promotion scripts — takes years to develop and is expensive to maintain.

### Aurora Serverless

Auto-scaling database capacity, billed per ACU-second. Capacity scales up and down without provisioning.

**When it fits**: Unpredictable workloads with high variance (dev/test environments, seasonal applications, internal tools with spiky usage patterns).

**When it breaks**: Cold start latency on the first connection after a scale-to-zero event. For a customer-facing API with sub-100ms SLOs, a 5–30 second cold start is disqualifying. At sustained high throughput, the per-second pricing model can exceed a fixed provisioned instance by 3–5x.

**Director guidance**: Default Aurora Serverless to non-production unless you have modeled the cost curve at your peak load and compared it to provisioned.

### DynamoDB

AWS's managed NoSQL — serverless, globally scalable, zero administration overhead. It is often described as a Cassandra alternative, but this framing is misleading and leads to bad decisions.

| Dimension | DynamoDB | Cassandra |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Data model | Key-value + simple document; secondary indexes are constrained | Wide-column; flexible CQL; composite partition keys |
| Consistency | Eventual or strong (per read); no tunable quorum | Tunable: ONE, QUORUM, ALL per operation |
| Query model | Access by partition key or GSI; full-table scans are expensive | CQL supports range queries, clustering columns, ALLOW FILTERING (with caveats) |
| Operations | Zero; AWS owns everything | Nodetool repair, compaction, JVM tuning, capacity ring management |
| Migration from Cassandra | Significant — data model must be redesigned, not just ported | — |

**Decision rule**: DynamoDB is right when you need managed NoSQL at extreme scale with simple key-value access patterns and you do not have existing Cassandra operations investment. It is wrong when you are already running Cassandra at scale with tuned consistency models, or when your query patterns require anything beyond primary key lookups.

For DND at T-Mobile: migrating 25M messages/day from Cassandra to DynamoDB is not a managed-service win — it is a full data model redesign project with uncertain consistency semantics for notification delivery guarantees.

## Database Consolidation: When Conway's Law Applies

Conway's Law states that systems reflect the communication structures of the organizations that build them. It applies to databases: **your database topology is a mirror of your team topology**.

Teams that own their own microservices tend to own their own databases. This is healthy for autonomy and fast delivery. It becomes expensive when:
- You are operating 15 Postgres clusters for 15 teams where 3 clusters would serve the same purpose
- Each cluster has its own backup schedule, patching window, monitoring configuration, and operational runbook
- The platform team is providing 15x the support surface for 1x the business value

### The Consolidation Checklist

Before consolidating databases, verify:

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| Do the workloads have compatible access patterns? | Mixed OLTP + OLAP on one cluster causes resource contention |
| Are SLOs aligned? | A Tier 0 and a Tier 2 database on the same cluster inherit each other's incidents |
| Is team ownership clear post-consolidation? | Consolidation without clear ownership creates a shared database with no owner — worse than fragmentation |
| Are schemas compatible or cleanly namespaced? | Schema conflicts become organizational conflicts |
| Is the migration reversible? | Consolidation is easier than de-consolidation; plan the rollback before you start |

**Director framing**: Consolidation is not a technical project. It is an organizational negotiation. You are asking teams to give up autonomy (their own database, their own tuning control) in exchange for reduced operational burden. Lead with the operational cost savings and the improved support model, not the architecture diagram.

## Database Reliability Investment Model: Tiering Your Estate

Not every database deserves the same investment. Treating all databases as equally critical is how platform teams exhaust their budget and burn out their engineers on low-value toil.

### Tier 0: Customer-Visible, Revenue-Critical

**Examples**: DND Cassandra (notification delivery), billing database, authentication data store.

**Investment level**: Maximum
- Multi-region or multi-AZ replication with tested failover
- Quarterly chaos experiments (see Chapter 7)
- 15-minute RTO, 1-minute RPO
- 24/7 on-call with runbooks reviewed quarterly
- Dedicated capacity planning review every sprint
- SLO: 99.95%+ availability, P99 write latency < 100ms

### Tier 1: Internal, Business-Critical

**Examples**: Internal reporting database, customer support tooling data store, partner integration stores.

**Investment level**: Standard HA
- Multi-AZ replication, automated failover
- Daily backup with weekly restore testing
- 1-hour RTO, 15-minute RPO
- On-call rotation with shared runbooks
- SLO: 99.9% availability

### Tier 2: Development, Test, Non-Critical

**Examples**: Dev/test environments, internal analytics scratch databases, experiment data stores.

**Investment level**: Best effort
- Single-node or serverless; no HA requirement
- Weekly backups; restore testing quarterly
- Next-business-day RTO
- No on-call; regular business hours support
- SLO: 99% or best-effort

### Defending the Model to Finance

When presenting database investment to finance or executive leadership, translate tiers into business impact:

"Tier 0 databases have direct customer impact. Every minute of downtime on the DND notification store affects customers who are not receiving time-sensitive alerts from T-Mobile services. The investment in chaos testing, multi-AZ replication, and 24/7 on-call for this tier is the cost of the reliability we committed to customers in our SLA. Tier 2 databases affect internal tooling. A 4-hour outage there is an inconvenience, not a customer event. We do not apply Tier 0 investment to Tier 2 problems."

## Build vs. Buy for Distributed Databases

The principle: **most organizations should not build their own distributed database**.

Cassandra, CockroachDB, Vitess, and TiDB exist because building a correct distributed database is an extraordinarily hard problem. It requires deep expertise in consensus protocols (Raft, Paxos), distributed transaction semantics, clock synchronization, compaction and storage engine design, and network partition behavior. The teams that built these systems employed distributed systems researchers with PhDs and took years to get them production-ready.

**The exception** — when a custom solution is justified:
- Your access pattern is so specialized that no existing solution fits (true in fewer than 1% of cases)
- You have the team depth to build it correctly and maintain it indefinitely
- The specialized solution provides a competitive advantage that outweighs the build cost

**Director red flag**: When an application team proposes building a custom data store to avoid operational complexity, the correct response is to examine why the operational complexity of existing solutions seems prohibitive. Usually the answer is that the team has not been given access to the right managed service or golden path.

## The Cassandra STAR Story: Bringing This to an Interview

This framework is abstract without grounding. In a Director or VP interview, you will need to anchor it to specific decisions you made.

### Situation

T-Mobile DND platform. 25 million messages per day processed through a Cassandra-backed notification delivery pipeline. Consumer-facing: T-Mobile customers rely on this for billing alerts, network notifications, and account communications. Every delivery failure is visible to the end customer.

### Task

Ensure database reliability for a distributed system operating at this scale — not just availability in the infrastructure sense, but delivery guarantees that hold under node failure, network degradation, and traffic spikes.

### Action

- Defined consistency level strategy: `QUORUM` writes for notification state mutations (delivery status, deduplication keys), `ONE` reads for non-critical metadata lookups — balancing durability against read latency
- Established `nodetool repair` schedule on a rotating per-node basis to prevent anti-entropy divergence from accumulating over time
- Built a Cassandra metrics pipeline into Splunk: read/write latency by consistency level, pending compaction tasks, dropped messages, hint handoff queue depth — all surfaced on a single dashboard with SLO threshold alerts
- Defined SLOs: 99.95% delivery availability, P99 write latency < 100ms at peak load, zero data loss on coordinator failure
- Ran quarterly chaos experiments: simulated node kills during peak load, measured delayed vs. lost notifications as the primary chaos metric

### Result

Sustained 99.95%+ availability on the notification delivery pipeline over 18 months of production operation. Sub-100ms P99 write latency at peak load (approximately 300 writes/second sustained, with bursts to 2x). Zero data loss events attributable to database failure.

### Transferable

> "The principles I applied at DND — tunable consistency to balance durability and latency, anti-entropy repair to prevent divergence at scale, compaction-aware capacity planning, SLO-driven alerting — apply to any distributed datastore decision a Director faces. When I am evaluating whether to run Cassandra, migrate to DynamoDB, or consolidate on Aurora, I start with the consistency contract the business actually needs and work backward to the technology. The technology is the last decision, not the first."

## Director Decision Frame

> "The most expensive database decision is the one made by an application team in a weekend hackathon that the platform team then has to operate for five years. That team was optimizing for fast delivery in the moment — which is the right instinct. My job is to make sure that instinct leads them to a sustainable choice, not a future incident.

> I do that through three mechanisms: a golden path (here is the approved, supported, cost-optimized database option for your workload class), a service catalog (here are the managed options with their SLO tiers and operational model), and clear SLO expectations (if you choose outside the catalog, you own the operations). When those three things are in place, most teams choose the right option not because they have to, but because it is obviously easier."

## Interview Application: The Decision Framework in 90 Seconds

When a panel asks "How do you approach database strategy as a Director?", here is the structured answer:

"I start with the estate audit: what databases do we have, who owns them, what tier are they, and what is the operational cost of each? From that audit, I make four categories of decisions: what we self-host because we need the control, what we move to managed services to reduce undifferentiated operational burden, what we consolidate to reduce the operational surface area, and what we migrate because the current choice no longer fits the workload.

I anchor every decision to three things: the consistency contract the business needs, the SLO we have committed to, and the team depth we have to operate the solution. Technology comes third, not first.

At T-Mobile DND, that framework led me to Cassandra with a tuned consistency model, a quarterly chaos experiment program, and a Splunk-based observability pipeline. That combination sustained 99.95% delivery availability on 25 million messages per day. The framework transfers directly to any distributed data platform decision."
