# Chapter 08 — The Director's Decision Framework: Buy vs. Build vs. Migrate

## The Director's Lens: Platform Decisions Are Portfolio Decisions

You are not deciding which hypervisor is technically superior. You are managing a technology portfolio — a set of capability investments — with finite budget, finite team capacity, and finite risk tolerance. Every infrastructure platform decision is a capital allocation decision with a 5-year horizon.

Think of your infrastructure stack the way a fund manager thinks about a portfolio: each platform is a position. VMware is a long-duration bond — predictable, expensive, hard to exit. Cloud-native is a growth equity position — high upside, high execution risk, long time-to-value. Nutanix is a value equity play — cost reduction with manageable migration risk. You are not picking a technology. You are rebalancing a portfolio based on new market conditions (Broadcom's pricing), your risk tolerance, and your team's ability to execute.

Frame it that way in every interview. Directors who talk about CPU scheduling lose. Directors who talk about portfolio risk, capital efficiency, and vendor leverage win.

Every platform decision must answer three questions before it reaches an executive:

1. **What does it cost over five years, fully loaded?** (not licensing alone)
2. **What is the execution risk, and who bears it?**
3. **What optionality do we preserve or surrender?**

If you can't answer all three, you're not ready to present.

## TCO: The Only Number That Matters

**Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)** for a virtualization platform has six components. Never present a platform recommendation without all six. Presenting licensing savings without migration cost is like quoting a home purchase price without property tax, insurance, and maintenance — it will be wrong, and you will own the difference.

| Cost Component | Description | Common Undercount |
|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Licensing** | Annual software fees (hypervisor, management, networking, DR) | Forgetting NSX, vSAN, SRM — they add 40–80% to base ESXi cost |
| **Hardware** | Server acquisition, refresh cycle (typically 5-year), warranty | Servers needed for N+1 HA capacity; spare capacity during migration |
| **Power & Cooling** | ~$0.10–0.15/kWh × server TDP × 8,760 hrs × PUE | PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) factor often omitted entirely |
| **Operations** | FTE hours × fully-loaded rate (salary + benefits + overhead) | Undercount by 40% when using headcount cost instead of loaded rate |
| **Migration** | Tooling, professional services, downtime risk buffer, testing cycles | Most teams undercount by 2–3× on first attempt |
| **Opportunity cost** | What your team cannot do while executing the migration | Almost never counted; creates hidden project drag on other roadmap items |

**PUE** (Power Usage Effectiveness) is the ratio of total data center power to IT equipment power. A PUE of 1.4 means for every 1W of server power, you pay for 1.4W total. In co-location or owned data centers, this multiplier matters significantly in long-horizon TCO comparisons.

A migration that looks like it saves $500K/yr on licensing can break even at year 4 once all six components are counted. That does not mean do not migrate — it means know your actual break-even before committing. A Director who shows up with a 3-year TCO model that includes all six components is someone whose recommendation gets approved. A Director who shows up with "Nutanix is 40% cheaper" gets sent back to do more homework.

## The Buy vs. Build vs. Migrate Decision Table

The three realistic paths in the post-Broadcom acquisition era are: stay and negotiate, migrate to alternative on-premises (Nutanix, Proxmox, OpenStack), or migrate to cloud-native (AWS EKS/ECS, Azure AKS, GCP GKE). Each has a legitimate use case. None is universally correct.

| Consideration | Stay on VMware | Migrate to Nutanix/Proxmox | Migrate to Cloud-Native |
|--------------|---------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Sunk VM investment** | Protects existing investment | Replatforms with tool support | Rewrites required for most stateful apps |
| **Time to value** | Immediate (no migration) | 6–18 months | 12–36 months |
| **Team skills match** | VMware admins (existing) | VMware + HCI skills (some retraining) | Cloud/K8s skills (significant retraining or hiring) |
| **Compliance/data residency** | Full on-premises control | Full on-premises control | Depends on provider region and contractual terms |
| **5-year cost trend** | Rising (Broadcom trajectory unclear but upward) | Flat-to-rising | Variable; cloud unit economics improve at scale |
| **Organizational execution risk** | Low (no change) | Medium (hardware + migration) | High (application refactoring + cultural change) |
| **Exit optionality** | Locked into Broadcom roadmap | Locked into Nutanix/Proxmox | Multi-cloud portable with standards adherence |
| **HA and DR complexity** | Mature tooling (vSphere HA, SRM) | Nutanix native HA; DR requires planning | Cloud-native HA; DR is provider-managed |

**When to stay**: Your environment is heavily regulated (HIPAA, PCI-DSS with on-premises audit requirements), your team has deep VMware expertise, and you can negotiate acceptable pricing. Staying is a legitimate business decision — it is not a failure of imagination.

**When to go Nutanix**: You want on-premises control, your team can handle HCI learning curve, and you want to eliminate the Broadcom dependency without a full cloud transformation. Nutanix Move (the migration tool) handles VMware-to-AHV migration with minimal manual effort.

**When to go cloud-native**: Your application teams are already building cloud-native services, your stateless workload percentage is high (>60%), and you are willing to invest in the transformation. Do not start here if your primary motivation is cost — cloud migrations routinely cost more in year 1–2 than staying on-premises.

## Vendor Negotiation: The Leverage Framework

Enterprise software negotiations are asymmetric information games. The vendor knows exactly what you pay, how much switching costs you, and your renewal date. You may not know their quota pressure, their discounting authority limits, or their competitive win rates. Your objective is to level the information asymmetry before the negotiation begins.

Think of this like a RabbitMQ cluster negotiation: Broadcom wants you to stay in their exchange. Your leverage is the ability to route traffic elsewhere. The more credible that alternative routing is, the better your terms.

**Five levers you always have**:

**1. Competitive alternative (BATNA)**
**BATNA** (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement) is the foundational negotiation concept. Get a real quote from Nutanix, Proxmox commercial support, or a cloud migration cost estimate before entering renewal discussions. Not to use it immediately — to have it. A credible alternative changes the negotiation from "what's your budget?" to "match this or we begin transition planning." An alternative you cannot actually execute is not leverage. An alternative backed by a scoped quote and a willing team is.

**2. Renewal timing**
Broadcom's sales teams operate on quarterly quotas. A renewal closing in the last two weeks of a fiscal quarter gets materially better terms than the same renewal closing in week two of a new quarter. Know Broadcom's fiscal calendar. Schedule your negotiation meeting for late in a quarter. Sales reps with quota pressure have more discounting authority than sales reps with full pipelines.

**3. Multi-year commitment**
Broadcom wants ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) certainty. A 3-year commitment provides that certainty — and you should extract value in exchange. Demand as conditions of a multi-year deal: price lock (no per-core price increases during term), named entitlements (no forced bundle migrations mid-term), migration assistance credits, and dedicated TAM access. Never give multi-year without getting something structural in return.

**4. Right-sizing before renewal**
Your current entitlements likely include products you are not deploying. Do not renew what you are not using. Conduct a license audit before renewal (see the cost assessment playbook). Paying for NSX transport nodes you have not activated, or vSAN clusters you never provisioned, is funding Broadcom's margin, not your platform. Right-sizing requires internal courage — procurement may have negotiated a bundle discount — but unused entitlements are pure waste.

**5. Executive escalation**
If your Technical Account Manager says "this is the price," escalate. Broadcom's VP and Director layer has more discounting authority than field TAMs. Frame the escalation as a partnership conversation: "We want to be a long-term VMware customer. We need to find terms that make renewal viable at our scale." Adversarial framing closes doors. Partnership framing opens them while achieving the same outcome.

## How to Build a Business Case That Gets Approved

Not all business cases are the same. The framing that gets approval from a CFO is different from the framing that gets approval from a CTO. Know your audience.

**Cost avoidance** (strongest for defensive spend, CFO audience):
"If we renew at current Broadcom pricing, we will pay $X more than last year. That is budget we do not have committed. Here is the plan to avoid it." CFOs respond to concrete invoice risk. Attach the actual renewal quote — not a projection, the actual document. Cost avoidance cases are easier to approve because the alternative is explicit: overspend or approve the plan.

**Capability investment** (strongest for migration spend, CTO/VP Engineering audience):
"Migrating to cloud-native reduces our infrastructure operations burden by 40%, enables our development teams to deploy three times faster, and aligns with our five-year architecture target state." This framing requires quantified operational metrics — deployment frequency, MTTR, infrastructure incident rate. Pull these from your Splunk dashboards. Capability investment cases are harder because the benefit is probabilistic; cost avoidance cases are easier because the cost is certain.

**The hybrid frame** (most effective for platform migration decisions):
Lead with cost avoidance to get the meeting. Pivot to capability investment to get the budget. The structure: "The Broadcom renewal forces a decision. Here is what staying costs over three years. Here is what modernizing costs over three years. Here is the difference in platform capability we get for the delta." This frame works because it resets the question from "should we spend money on migration?" to "which spending decision are we making?" — and that is a much more tractable executive conversation.

**What gets business cases rejected**:
- Missing the total cost of migration (undercount triggers credibility loss)
- No identified risk owner (executives will not approve plans without accountability)
- Timeline that does not account for team capacity (every migration team is also running production)
- No rollback plan (executives who have survived failed migrations always ask)

## Interview Scenario: The $3M VMware Problem

**Question**: "You inherit a platform where VMware renewal just came in at $3M — 2.5× last year's cost. What do you do in the first 90 days?"

This question is testing whether you can structure ambiguity into a plan. The wrong answer is to immediately name a technology ("we'd migrate to Nutanix"). The right answer demonstrates that you know the sequence: quantify before you evaluate, evaluate before you decide, decide before you execute.

```
Days 1–30: QUANTIFY
  - Full license inventory (what we have vs. what we actually use)
  - Actual renewal quote analysis (per-core breakdown, which bundles, what's new)
  - Workload tiering (what genuinely requires VMware vs. what's there by default)
  - Team skills assessment (who can execute which migration path?)
  - Pull 12 months of infrastructure operational metrics (CPU, memory utilization per host)

Days 31–60: OPTIONS
  - Get competitive quotes: Nutanix, Proxmox commercial support, AWS migration estimate
  - Build 3-year TCO model for each path (stay-and-negotiate / migrate-on-prem / migrate-cloud)
  - Identify 3–5 pilot candidates for migration proof-of-concept
  - Brief CTO and CFO: here is the financial exposure, here are the options, here is the decision needed

Days 61–90: DECISION AND FIRST STEPS
  - Present recommendation with risk matrix (see risk framework above)
  - Begin vendor negotiation armed with competitive alternatives
  - Start pilot migration if migrating — do not wait for full executive decision to run the pilot
  - Establish governance: who owns this decision, who executes, what is the decision deadline?
```

The 90-day frame proves you know how to structure ambiguity without panicking into premature action. Every interviewer who has lived through a failed platform migration will recognize this pattern and respect it. The Director who says "I'd spend 30 days understanding exactly what we have before committing to a direction" is the Director who does not blow up migrations.

**What to say about the negotiation**: "I would not tell the vendor we're migrating until I actually have an alternative ready to execute. Telling Broadcom we're leaving before we can leave just removes our leverage without producing any benefit."

**What to say about risk**: "The biggest risk is not the technology — it's the team's capacity. Every migration wave takes ops attention away from running production. I'd stage waves to never exceed 20% of my team's weekly capacity committed to migration work, so we never sacrifice reliability to hit a migration milestone."
