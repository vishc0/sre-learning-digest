# Chapter 4: Migration Decision Framework — Stay on VMware or Move?

## This Is a Platform Strategy Decision, Not a Technical One

Every organization facing VMware renewal post-Broadcom is running the same analysis. The question isn't "which hypervisor is better?" — it's "what does moving cost vs. what does staying cost, and which risk profile can we absorb?" Directors who frame it technically lose budget battles. Directors who frame it as portfolio risk management win them.

Broadcom's 2023 acquisition of VMware changed the licensing math fundamentally. Perpetual licenses were replaced with subscription bundles. Per-CPU pricing moved to per-core. The result: many organizations saw renewal estimates double or triple. That forcing function is what's driving migration conversations — not technical superiority of alternatives.

---

## The Decision Variables

Before comparing platforms, scope your actual situation across six dimensions:

**1. Sunk investment in VM workloads**: How many VMs do you have, and what percentage are candidates for modernization? If you have 2,000 VMs running legacy Java apps that can't be containerized, migration cost is enormous. If you have 200 VMs and half are stateless services, options open up. The raw VM count is less important than the ratio of modernizable to stranded workloads.

**2. Licensing exposure**: What does your current VMware spend look like, and what's the projected renewal increase? This is your forcing function number. If renewal doubles from $400K to $800K, you have $400K/yr to fund migration and still break even. That number drives your migration budget ceiling — you cannot spend more than the NPV of avoided license costs without justifying it on other grounds.

**3. App modernization readiness**: Migrating hypervisors (VMware → Nutanix) is different from modernizing apps (VMs → containers). Don't conflate them. You can migrate from VMware to Nutanix and still have the same app problem. Ask: does this migration move you toward cloud-native, or just defer the conversation? If you migrate to Nutanix without a modernization plan, you've traded one vendor dependency for another and paid migration cost for no architectural progress.

**4. Team skills**: VMware admins don't automatically become Nutanix admins or Kubernetes engineers. Retraining takes 6–12 months to reach production-confident levels. Underestimating this is the most common migration failure mode. Factor training time, interim contractor support, and the productivity dip during skill ramp as real costs in your business case.

**5. Compliance and data residency requirements**: Some workloads have contractual or regulatory requirements that limit where they can run. PCI-DSS workloads may require specific isolation guarantees. HIPAA workloads may restrict cloud regions. FedRAMP workloads may restrict cloud providers entirely. This can block cloud migration options and narrow your viable platform choices to on-prem alternatives only.

**6. Time horizon**: Is this a 3-year plan or a 12-month mandate? Migration scope must match timeline. Nothing causes more damage than a migration that gets halfway done when budget runs out — you end up paying for two platforms indefinitely while neither is fully operational. Right-size the scope to the funding and timeline you actually have.

## Platform Comparison

| Platform | Best For | Migration Effort | Licensing Model | Key Risk |
|----------|----------|-----------------|-----------------|----------|
| **Stay VMware (VVF/VCF)** | VMware-heavy orgs, locked in, negotiated 3-yr deal | None | Subscription (Broadcom) | Cost escalation at renewal |
| **Nutanix AOS + AHV** | HCI replacement, on-prem preference, VMware-familiar teams | Medium (6–18 months) | Subscription | Vendor dependency shift; Nutanix pricing trends up too |
| **Proxmox VE** | Budget-conscious, open-source acceptable, smaller environments | High | Free (commercial support optional) | Limited enterprise support; operational burden on your team |
| **OpenStack** | Cloud-provider-like control, large ops team, public-cloud feature parity needed | Very High | Free (ops cost dominates) | Extreme operational complexity; most orgs underestimate it |
| **Cloud-native (AWS/Azure/GCP)** | Greenfield workloads, modernization mandate, future-state target | High (12–36 months) | Pay-as-you-go | Skill gap, egress costs, lift-and-shift cost surprises |

**On Nutanix specifically**: the pitch is "VMware-compatible but independent." AHV is mature, the interface is familiar to VMware teams, and the HCI (hyper-converged) model eliminates separate storage arrays. The risk: Nutanix is also a subscription vendor, and their pricing has increased over time. You may be trading one escalating subscription for another with a migration cost on top.

**On Proxmox**: technically solid, genuinely open-source, widely used in Europe and mid-market organizations. The gap is enterprise support maturity — your team carries the operational burden. For a T-Mobile-scale environment, the labor cost to run Proxmox securely and reliably likely exceeds the VMware licensing delta. Best fit: lab environments, dev/test infrastructure, or orgs with strong Linux admin depth.

**On OpenStack**: viable for cloud service providers and large telcos with hundreds of infrastructure engineers. For most enterprise IT shops, it's operational quicksand. The engineering complexity to operate OpenStack at production quality requires a dedicated team. Unless you're building an internal cloud service (T-Mobile has done exactly this in parts of the network), avoid it.

## Workload Tiering: The Migration Sequencer

Don't migrate everything. Tier your workloads before touching any infrastructure:

```
Tier 1 — Stay on VM (permanently or long-term):
  ├── Legacy apps requiring specific kernel versions
  ├── Compliance workloads with VM isolation requirements
  ├── Stateful databases with complex storage dependencies
  └── Apps with no modernization path or owner

Tier 2 — Containerize (migrate to Kubernetes/EKS):
  ├── Stateless services (APIs, workers, schedulers)
  ├── Apps that already run on JVM/Python/Node
  ├── Anything with CI/CD pipeline already in place
  └── Services your teams own and can modify

Tier 3 — SaaS Replace (don't migrate, deprecate):
  ├── Email and collaboration tools
  ├── Monitoring/ITSM platforms with SaaS equivalents
  ├── Dev tooling (Jira, Jenkins → managed CI/CD)
  └── Anything where you're running commodity software
```

The tiering output becomes your migration scope. Tier 1 workloads determine your minimum VMware (or alternative hypervisor) footprint. Tier 2 workloads drive your containerization program. Tier 3 workloads are your cost avoidance wins that don't require any infrastructure migration at all.

This maps directly to Vishweshwar's EKS migration experience — "lift and shift vs. re-architect" is the same decision applied to a different destination. The discipline of workload tiering before any migration is identical whether you're moving from VMware to Nutanix or from VMs to containers on EKS.

## Migration Sequencing Principles

**Stateless first, stateful last, databases last-last.** This is the same principle that applies to Kubernetes migrations. Move stateless services first — if something breaks, rollback is cheap and blast radius is limited. Stateful workloads (message queues, caches, application state) come next — persistent volume mapping and data continuity need to be sorted before cutover. Databases move last because data loss risk is existential and the rollback cost is highest.

**Pilot before wave.** Pick 3–5 non-critical workloads for the first migration wave. Learn what breaks in your specific environment — networking configuration gaps, storage driver incompatibilities, credential management differences. Fix the tooling and runbooks. Then execute subsequent waves at scale with confidence.

**Maintain dual-platform capability during transition.** Never cut over everything at once. Run VMware and the target platform in parallel until the migration is validated at scale. Yes, this costs more short-term — you're licensing both platforms simultaneously. The alternative is a 2am production outage with no rollback path and six months of migration work at risk. The dual-platform cost is insurance.

**Track technical debt created by the migration itself.** Any migration creates temporary workarounds — VM configurations that don't match target-state standards, manual steps that should be automated, documentation gaps. These accumulate into post-migration technical debt that will surface as operational incidents 6–12 months later. Log them as you go and schedule a hardening sprint.

## The Build Business Case Frame

When presenting this to a CFO or CTO, use cost-avoidance framing, not cost-reduction framing.

- **Cost reduction framing**: "We'll save $X if we migrate." This requires proving a positive future state. CFOs are skeptical of migration cost estimates — they've seen migrations go over budget. The savings projection feels speculative.
- **Cost avoidance framing**: "If we don't act, we'll pay $X more per year at renewal. This is the cost of inaction." Cost avoidance is concrete, defensible, and tied to a real invoice they'll receive. It shifts the burden: inaction has a quantified cost.

Combine both in one narrative: "The renewal increase is $800K/yr. Migration to Nutanix costs $600K one-time and saves $500K/yr from year 2 forward. Break-even is 14 months. Here's the 18-month plan with quarterly decision gates so we can stop if the math changes."

The quarterly decision gates are important — they give leadership confidence that you're not committing to an irreversible path. Each gate is a structured go/no-go based on actual migration costs tracked against the model.

## Director Decision Frame

**The question you'll get in interviews**: "If you inherited a VMware environment with a Broadcom renewal coming in 6 months, what's your 90-day plan?"

The correct answer structure:

1. **Days 1–30 — Inventory**: Audit VM count, CPU/memory allocation vs. actual utilization, storage usage, network topology. Without this, every cost model is fiction.
2. **Days 31–60 — Cost model**: Build three scenarios — renew as-is, renew and right-size, migrate to [alternative]. Quantify each with real numbers. Include hidden costs: tooling, training, parallel-platform overlap, professional services.
3. **Days 61–75 — BATNA identification**: What's your negotiating leverage? Have you gotten quotes from Nutanix and AWS? Broadcom will negotiate if you can credibly threaten to leave. A competitive quote is your most powerful tool before signing anything.
4. **Days 76–90 — Stakeholder brief and decision gate**: Present the three scenarios to leadership with a clear recommendation. Include risk table for each option. Get a decision — don't let it drift into another quarter.

Never skip the inventory phase. The number you bring to leadership has to be defensible to the dollar. Estimates built on assumptions without inventory data will be challenged and discredited, and you'll lose credibility for the rest of the project.
