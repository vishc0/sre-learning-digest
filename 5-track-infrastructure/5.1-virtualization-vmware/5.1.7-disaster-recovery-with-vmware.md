# Chapter 7: Disaster Recovery with VMware — SRM, Replication, RTO/RPO

## DR Is Not About Technology — It's About Business Commitment

Before covering SRM mechanics, anchor to the decision that precedes all of it: every DR choice is a business commitment, not an engineering choice. The company decides how much data loss is acceptable (RPO) and how long service can be down (RTO). Engineering's job is to build the cheapest system that meets those commitments — and to be honest when a commitment can't be met at the proposed cost.

This is where a lot of infrastructure teams fail in executive conversations: they lead with products ("we're implementing SRM") instead of commitments ("we're targeting 1-hour RTO and 15-minute RPO for Tier 1 services, and here's what that costs"). The product is the implementation detail. The commitment is what the business cares about.

If you're in a Director interview and someone asks about DR strategy, lead with RPO/RTO. Don't start with products.

## RTO and RPO: Anchored to Incident Command

**RTO (Recovery Time Objective)**: the maximum time the business can tolerate a service being down after a failure. If your notification platform has an RTO of 4 hours, you must be able to restore service within 4 hours of a datacenter failure — measured from time of failure detection, not from when recovery starts.

From Vishweshwar's incident command experience: RTO is the SLA on your incident resolution. It's the threshold where the incident escalates from "P2 we're working it" to "executive bridge, customer impact SLA breach." Breach RTO and you're not just managing an outage — you're managing an executive communication crisis simultaneously.

**RPO (Recovery Point Objective)**: the maximum data loss the business can tolerate, expressed as time. An RPO of 15 minutes means you must have a replica of data that is no older than 15 minutes at the time of failure. All writes made in the last 15 minutes before the failure may be lost.

From incident experience: RPO is the question "what did we lose?" asked after recovery is complete. Lower RPO = more frequent replication = higher network bandwidth consumption = higher cost. Zero RPO = synchronous replication = you pay the latency cost on every write.

**Visualizing the relationship**:

```
     Failure occurs here
            │
────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────▶ Time
            │
     ◄──────┤──────►                        ◄───────────────►
       RPO window                                 RTO window
  (max data loss allowed)               (max downtime allowed)
            │                                       │
    Last acceptable              Service must be
    recovery point               restored by here
```

The combination of RPO and RTO determines your DR architecture. A 24h RPO / 24h RTO means daily backups plus manual restore. A 15-min RPO / 1h RTO means continuous replication plus orchestrated automated failover. A 0 RPO / 5-min RTO means synchronous mirroring with automated failover — stretched cluster territory.

## vSphere Replication: Native VMware DR

**vSphere Replication** is VMware's built-in VM replication product. It continuously tracks changed disk blocks in a source VM and replicates those changes to a recovery site on a configurable schedule.

Kafka analogy: vSphere Replication is like Kafka MirrorMaker — it continuously streams changes from a source site (primary datacenter) to a target site (recovery datacenter), with configurable lag tolerance. The RPO setting is how much replication lag you accept. A 15-minute RPO means the recovery site may be up to 15 minutes behind the primary — like a MirrorMaker consumer group that's allowed to lag by 15 minutes before it becomes a problem.

**Technical characteristics**:
- **RPO range**: 5 minutes to 24 hours (configured per VM individually)
- **Mechanism**: changed block tracking (CBT) — tracks which disk sectors changed since last replication cycle and sends only the delta
- **Network requirement**: WAN connectivity between sites; bandwidth consumption is proportional to VM write rate (a high-write database with 5-min RPO will consume significant WAN bandwidth)
- **Recovery site requirement**: vCenter + ESXi hosts at recovery site; does not require identical hardware
- **Consistency options**:
  - **Crash-consistent**: replicates blocks as-is. On failover, VM recovers as if it had a hard power-off. Application handles inconsistency (most modern apps can).
  - **Application-consistent**: triggers VSS (Windows) or SYNC (Linux) quiesce before snapshot, ensuring in-memory buffers are flushed to disk. Brief I/O pause. Required for databases that cannot tolerate crash-consistent recovery.

**vSphere Replication limitations**:
- Managed through vCenter — if vCenter at the primary site is unavailable, you manage recovery via vCenter at the recovery site
- RPO is the goal, not a guarantee — if network bandwidth is insufficient, actual replication lag may exceed configured RPO
- Does not orchestrate failover sequencing — that's SRM's job

## Site Recovery Manager (SRM): Orchestrated Failover

**SRM** is VMware's DR orchestration product. It does not replicate data — it automates and documents the failover execution process. SRM consumes replication data from vSphere Replication (or array-based replication via SRA — Storage Replication Adapters) and provides the tooling to execute a DR failover reliably and repeatably.

Think of SRM as a pre-scripted, tested incident runbook that executes itself. Instead of your team following a 40-step DR runbook at 3am with adrenaline spiking and steps getting skipped, SRM executes the defined steps in the correct order, handles dependency sequencing, reconfigures networking, and calls your custom validation scripts.

**SRM recovery plan anatomy**:

```
SRM Recovery Plan: Production → DR Site
        │
        ▼
Phase 1: Pre-failover checks
  ├── Verify replication is within RPO window
  ├── Verify DR site capacity is available
  └── Run pre-failover custom scripts (notify monitoring, open change record)

Phase 2: Shutdown / isolate source site (if accessible)
  └── Graceful VM shutdown (skipped if site is truly unavailable)

Phase 3: Promote replicated storage
  └── Promote vSphere Replication snapshots to read-write at DR site

Phase 4: Power on VMs in priority order
  ├── Priority 1 — Infrastructure (DNS, AD, monitoring agents)
  ├── Priority 2 — Database VMs (Cassandra, PostgreSQL)
  ├── Priority 3 — Middleware VMs (RabbitMQ, Redis)
  └── Priority 4 — Application VMs (API servers, workers)

Phase 5: Network reconfiguration
  └── Apply IP customization rules (different subnet/gateway at DR site)

Phase 6: Post-failover validation scripts
  ├── Health check: database connectivity
  ├── Health check: message queue reachability
  ├── Health check: application endpoint responds
  └── Notify monitoring and on-call team
```

**Dependency ordering** is the operational value of SRM. Without it, a DR failover would power on VMs in arbitrary order — app servers come up before databases, connection pools exhaust, services fail to start, cascade begins. SRM enforces the correct sequencing, the same way Kubernetes init containers gate a pod's startup on dependency readiness.

**Non-disruptive DR testing**: SRM can execute a full DR test without impacting production. It creates a **network bubble** — an isolated network environment at the recovery site — and boots the recovered VMs inside it. Production systems continue running. The recovered VMs validate that they can start, that data is present, that applications respond. The test is torn down cleanly. No maintenance window required for DR testing.

This is the difference between organizations that test DR quarterly and those that test annually (or never). With SRM, there's no excuse for untested DR plans.

## Stretched Clusters: Zero RPO Architecture

A **stretched cluster** (vSphere Metro Storage Cluster with vSAN Stretched Cluster) spans two physical datacenters as a single logical vSphere cluster. vSAN storage is synchronously mirrored between sites — every write is acknowledged only after it's committed to both sites.

```
         Site A (Primary)                  Site B (Secondary)
┌────────────────────────┐        ┌────────────────────────┐
│  ESXi Hosts (5 nodes)  │◄──────►│  ESXi Hosts (5 nodes)  │
│  vSAN (witness: Site C)│ sync   │  vSAN mirror           │
│                        │ writes │                        │
└────────────────────────┘        └────────────────────────┘
         │                                    │
         └──────────────────────────────────┘
              Dark fiber, <5ms RTT required
```

A **witness node** (a third site or a small appliance) holds tie-breaking votes to prevent split-brain during a network partition. If both sites lose connectivity to each other, the site that can reach the witness continues operating; the other site shuts down its VMs to prevent data divergence.

**Characteristics**:
- **RPO**: effectively zero — no write is acknowledged until committed to both sites
- **RTO**: near-zero — VM restart via HA on the surviving site, typically 30–120 seconds
- **Latency requirement**: <5ms RTT between sites (synchronous writes must complete within I/O timeout)
- **Bandwidth requirement**: full write throughput must be mirrored — high bandwidth, low latency interconnect required
- **Cost**: very high — dark fiber or dedicated WAN circuit, full hardware duplication at both sites, complexity tax

**When to use stretched clusters**:
- Tier 0 workloads where any data loss is unacceptable and manual failover initiation is too slow
- Financial transactions, real-time communications, payment processing
- Services where the cost of 5 minutes of data loss exceeds the cost of the stretched cluster infrastructure

**When NOT to use stretched clusters**:
- Everything else. Stretched clusters are expensive, operationally complex, and have strict network requirements. The vast majority of workloads don't justify this investment.
- Using stretched clusters as a default instead of tiering workloads is a budget-destroying mistake.

## Tiering Your DR Strategy

Not everything needs SRM with continuous replication. Not everything needs a stretched cluster. Apply the same tiering discipline you'd use for Kubernetes pod priority classes — define tiers, commit to them with business stakeholders, and build to the tier.

| Tier | RTO Target | RPO Target | DR Approach | Cost |
|------|------------|------------|-------------|------|
| **Tier 0** — Mission Critical | <5 min | 0 (zero) | Stretched cluster + synchronous vSAN | Very High |
| **Tier 1** — Business Critical | <1 hr | <15 min | SRM + vSphere Replication (5-min RPO) | High |
| **Tier 2** — Important | <4 hrs | <1 hr | SRM + vSphere Replication (1-hr RPO) | Medium |
| **Tier 3** — Standard | <24 hrs | <24 hrs | SRM + daily snapshot replication | Low |
| **Tier 4** — Dev/Test | Best effort | Best effort | Backup only, no replication | Minimal |

**The tiering conversation** is where you protect your DR budget. If every workload is declared "mission critical" by its owner (and they always will be — no one volunteers their service as non-critical), you'll spend stretched cluster money on dev/test environments and nothing on the actual critical path.

The forcing function is money. Present the cost per tier explicitly:
- "Tier 0 costs $X/month per workload in infrastructure. How many workloads is the business committing to at this tier?"
- "Tier 1 costs $Y/month per workload. We can support Z workloads at this tier in budget."

When business stakeholders see the per-workload cost of each tier, classification decisions become much more honest.

**Document the commitments.** DR tiers are a service-level commitment between IT and the business. Write them down. Get them signed. When a DR event occurs and you meet the committed tier, you've succeeded. When stakeholders complain that recovery took longer than they expected — and they will — you have documented evidence of what was committed and what was funded.

**Director angle**: the interview question is "walk me through your DR strategy." The wrong answer is a product list (SRM, vSphere Replication, stretched clusters). The right answer is: "We started by getting business-committed RTO/RPO for each workload tier. We then sized the DR architecture to meet those commitments at minimum cost. We test DR quarterly using SRM's non-disruptive test capability so we know the plan actually works before we need it. And we review tier classifications annually because business criticality changes — what was Tier 2 two years ago may be Tier 1 today after a product launch." That's the Director-level answer.
