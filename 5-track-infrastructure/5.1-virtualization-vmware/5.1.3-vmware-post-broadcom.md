# Chapter 3 — VMware Post-Broadcom

## The Acquisition That Changed Everything

**Broadcom acquired VMware** for $61 billion — the largest enterprise software deal in history at its time — closing October 2023. Within 90 days of close, Broadcom executed one of the most aggressive enterprise software licensing restructurings in recent memory. The speed and scope of the changes caught even large enterprise customers off guard.

This is not a historical footnote. If your organization runs VMware on-premises, you are either already in a forced renegotiation or your next renewal will be your first at Broadcom's terms. For Director/VP candidates, this is a live case study in vendor strategy, platform risk, and infrastructure cost governance — all three of which are core leadership competencies.

---

## What Broadcom Changed (and When)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Oct 2023 | Broadcom acquisition closes |
| Nov 2023 | Broadcom announces end of perpetual licenses — all future sales are subscription-only |
| Dec 2023 | SKU consolidation announced: 8,000+ VMware product SKUs consolidated into approximately 4 bundles |
| Q1 2024 | First renewal cycles hit at new pricing — customers begin receiving 3–10× price increase notices |
| Q2 2024 | Broadcom discontinues the partner program tier for smaller resellers — reduces competitive distribution and negotiation leverage for mid-market customers |
| Q2–Q3 2024 | Nutanix, Proxmox, and cloud providers report record VMware displacement pipeline |
| Q3 2024 | Broadcom begins enforcement of core-based pricing minimums across all renewal conversations |

The pattern: Broadcom moved fast, before customers had time to develop alternatives. Standard enterprise procurement cycles are 6–18 months for major platform changes. Broadcom's 90-day restructuring window was shorter than most customers' internal approval cycles for a platform migration.

---

## The 4 New Bundles

Broadcom eliminated granular SKU purchasing. The 8,000+ individual product SKUs (each of which could be licensed separately) are gone. You now must buy one of these four bundles:

| Bundle | What It Includes | Best Fit |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| **VMware vSphere Foundation (VVF)** | ESXi + vCenter + vSAN + basic Aria monitoring | Core virtualization without NSX |
| **VMware Cloud Foundation (VCF)** | VVF + NSX + advanced Aria (full SDDC automation) | Full software-defined datacenter |
| **VMware vSphere Essentials Plus** | ESXi + vCenter for ≤3 hosts, ≤96 cores total | Small business only |
| **VMware Tanzu Platform** | Kubernetes platform on top of vSphere infrastructure | Cloud-native on vSphere |

**The critical change**: You can no longer buy just ESXi + vCenter. The previous practice of buying vCenter Standard + ESXi licenses à la carte, then adding vSAN or NSX only where needed, is gone. Customers who only needed ESXi + vCenter are now the primary target of forced upsell to VVF. Customers who had NSX or vSAN are being pushed toward VCF — which bundles everything at VCF pricing whether or not they use all components.

**Pricing basis shift**: Broadcom moved from **per-socket** pricing to **per-core** pricing. Under the old model, a 2-socket server (regardless of core count) was billed at 2 socket licenses. Under the new model, a 2-socket server with 32 cores per socket (64 total cores) is billed at 64 core licenses, with a minimum of 16 cores per CPU enforced.

The per-core shift hits customers with high-core-count modern CPUs hardest. A server with 2× AMD EPYC 9654 (96 cores each, 192 total) now carries nearly 6× the licensing units of the old 2-socket model.

## The Cost Math: A Real Scenario

A mid-size enterprise with 200 ESXi hosts averaging 32 cores per host (6,400 total cores):

| Line Item | Old Model (est.) | New Model (est.) |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| ESXi + vCenter + vSAN (per socket, ~$1,600/socket/yr) | ~$64K/yr (200 × 2 sockets) | — |
| VCF per-core pricing (published range: $80–$150/core/yr) | — | $512K–$960K/yr |
| NSX (previously separate, ~$120K/yr for 200 hosts) | ~$120K/yr | Bundled in VCF |
| Support and subscription | ~$25K/yr | Included |
| **Total annual** | **~$210K/yr** | **~$512K–$960K/yr** |
| **3-year total** | **~$630K** | **~$1.5M–$2.9M** |

The 3–10× pricing increase figure circulating in industry press is directionally accurate. The exact multiple depends on your previous entitlement mix, core count per host, and whether you were previously paying for NSX and vSAN separately. Organizations that only ran ESXi + vCenter with no vSAN or NSX tend to see the largest relative increase because they are now being forced into bundles that include components they do not use.

## The 3 Customer Responses

### Response 1: Stay on VMware — Negotiate Hard

**Who does this**: Enterprises with 5+ years of deep VMware investment — extensive vCenter automation, custom vSphere APIs integrated into ITSM, regulatory requirements around validated and certified platforms, or insufficient platform engineering capacity to execute a migration in parallel with business-as-usual operations.

**How to negotiate**:

Broadcom's sales teams have quota pressure, and customer retention is the primary metric in year 1 post-acquisition. The leverage exists — use it:

- **Demand a 3-year contract with year-over-year price lock**. One-year renewals expose you to annual increases at Broadcom's discretion. A 3-year locked deal limits exposure regardless of what Broadcom does with pricing in years 2 and 3.
- **Use competitive quotes as BATNA** (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement). Get a Nutanix quote, a Proxmox proof-of-concept estimate, and a cloud migration cost model. Bring these into the renewal conversation — not as bluffs, but as evidence that you have a real walk-away option.
- **Right-size the bundle**. If you do not use NSX, push back on VCF and negotiate VVF pricing. If you do not use vSAN, negotiate around it. Broadcom sales will default to the highest bundle; it is your job to anchor the conversation to what you actually consume.
- **Engage Broadcom's executive team**. Your TAM (Technical Account Manager) has limited authority. Executive-to-executive conversations move faster and produce better terms in high-stakes renewal situations.
- **Audit your actual core count** before any conversation. Broadcom will submit a number; you should have your own. Discrepancies are common during the transition from socket-based to core-based auditing.

**Risk of staying**: You are locked into a rising cost trajectory. Even with a 3-year deal, year 4 will be at Broadcom's terms again. You are also accepting ongoing dependency on a vendor whose strategic priorities have demonstrably shifted toward margin extraction over customer partnership.

---

### Response 2: Migrate to Nutanix

**Who does this**: Organizations that want to maintain on-premises hyperconverged infrastructure without Broadcom dependency, have workloads that are not yet cloud-ready, or have already evaluated cloud costs and found on-premises more economical at their scale.

**Why Nutanix is the leading alternative**: Nutanix's **AOS + AHV** stack — their hyperconverged storage platform plus their open-source-based hypervisor — is the most functionally complete VMware replacement available on-premises today. Nutanix has purpose-built migration tooling (**Nutanix Move**) that automates VM export from VMware format (VMDK) to Nutanix format (AHV disk images). Nutanix ran an explicit "Move to Nutanix" marketing campaign targeting VMware customers from November 2023 onward, including commercial terms designed to absorb VMware renewal cost as a switching incentive.

**What the migration involves**:
1. Deploy Nutanix clusters alongside existing VMware environment
2. Run Nutanix Move to replicate and cut over VMs in waves
3. Decomission VMware hosts after validation
4. Retain networking changes (Nutanix Flow = Nutanix's microsegmentation, equivalent to NSX DFW)

**Realistic timeline**: 6–18 months for a meaningful migration wave, depending on workload complexity, team readiness, and how much custom VMware automation needs to be re-implemented in Nutanix Prism APIs.

**Risk of Nutanix**: You are trading one vendor dependency for another. Nutanix is subscription-based with its own pricing trajectory. Nutanix is a significantly smaller company than Broadcom — future acquisition or financial instability is a non-trivial consideration in a 5–10 year platform decision.

---

### Response 3: Accelerate Cloud Migration

**Who does this**: Organizations that already had cloud migration on the 3–5 year roadmap and are using Broadcom pricing shock as a forcing function and executive justification to accelerate the timeline.

**The business argument**: "We were going to spend $1.5M on VMware renewal. We can redirect that budget toward cloud migration and land on a better long-term cost position." This argument works when cloud-native is already the strategic direction — it fails when workloads are not cloud-ready and lift-and-shift cost modeling reveals cloud is more expensive.

**Reality check — three workload tiers**:

| Tier | Workload Type | Cloud Path |
|------|--------------|------------|
| Cloud-ready | Stateless apps, microservices, batch jobs | Lift-and-shift or re-platform → EKS, Lambda, managed services |
| Conditionally ready | Stateful apps with some refactoring | Lift-and-shift with optimization → RDS, ElastiCache, MSK |
| Not cloud-ready | Legacy apps with hardware dependencies, vendor-licensed software with cloud restrictions | Must stay on-premises — reduce VMware footprint but cannot eliminate |

The practical outcome for most enterprises: some VMs move to cloud (typically 30–50% of the estate), some get containerized (another 20–30%), and the remaining on-premises footprint requires either a VMware renewal or a Nutanix/Proxmox migration. The "full cloud migration" story is rarely executable within a VMware renewal cycle.

## Director-Level Angle: The Vendor Negotiation Scenario

You will face a version of this question in Director/VP interviews — and in board-room conversations if you hold the role:

**"How have you handled a major vendor cost increase that threatened your platform budget?"**

The answer framework maps directly to the Broadcom scenario:

1. **Quantify the exposure before escalating** — run the cost model yourself, using your actual core counts and current entitlements, before the renewal call arrives. Never walk into a vendor conversation with an estimate; walk in with a number.
2. **Identify your BATNA with rough cost** — "our alternative is Nutanix at approximately $X, with a 12-month migration effort" is a negotiating position. "We might be able to use something else" is not.
3. **Present options with a recommendation** — leadership expects a decision framework, not a problem statement. "Here are our three paths, here is their cost and risk profile, and here is what I recommend" is the Director-level deliverable.
4. **Negotiate from alternatives** — Broadcom needs your renewal. Use that. Your leverage decays after you sign.
5. **Lock multi-year protection** — if you stay, a price-locked multi-year contract is non-negotiable. Annual renewals at an acquisitive vendor's discretion is not a sustainable position.

The Broadcom/VMware case is a case study in why **platform strategy decisions should never be left to auto-renewal**. Every major platform has a renewal date. The Director who knows those dates 18 months in advance and has alternatives developed before the conversation starts is never caught by surprise pricing.
