# Chapter 8: Director-Level Decision Governance

## Why Governance Matters at Scale

Individual Build vs. Buy decisions compound. A Director who lets each team make independent tool choices in year one will spend years two through five paying the integration tax on those choices. Without a governance process, you end up with 47 monitoring tools — each bought or built by a different team, none integrated, none sharing a common data model, none producing the portfolio-level observability you need to run the platform. You also end up with vendor contracts that have no pricing caps, no exit clauses, and no negotiating leverage because they were signed by individual teams without procurement coordination.

The Director's job is not to approve or deny tool choices made by engineers. The Director's job is to establish a process that produces consistent, evidence-based decisions — and to maintain a portfolio view that sees the aggregate shape of all those decisions together. The individual decision is an engineering activity. The governance process and portfolio view are leadership activities.

Governance is not bureaucracy. Governance is the mechanism by which an organization avoids repeating the same expensive mistake with a different vendor and a different team.

## Decision Authority: Who Decides What

One of the most common governance failures is unclear authority. Engineers propose options without knowing who has final say. Decisions stall in committee. Or worse, decisions get made by whoever had the most energy, regardless of whether they had the accountability.

The Build vs. Buy authority model should be explicit and documented:

**Engineer**: Recommends. Proposes the option, builds the proof of concept, owns the TCO model, documents the alternatives considered. The recommendation must include a cost model, a risk assessment, and a named maintenance owner. Engineers who can do this well are operating at senior/staff level.

**Engineering Manager**: Endorses. Validates that the team has capacity to own the choice — maintenance headcount, skills, willingness to carry this decision across personnel changes. An Engineering Manager who endorses a Backstage adoption without validating the 3–5 engineer requirement from Chapter 6 has failed their governance role.

**Director**: Decides for choices within the budget threshold — typically $250K–$500K per year in total cost of ownership, depending on your organization. Also owns the portfolio view: is this choice consistent with existing standards? Does it replace something we already have? Does it introduce duplicate capability?

**VP/CTO**: Decides above the Director threshold, or for decisions that cross domain boundaries — choices that affect multiple Director-level organizations, or that establish new architectural patterns across the enterprise.

**CFO/Board**: Awareness and approval for multi-million dollar platform decisions, vendor relationships that trigger procurement thresholds, or build investments that require capitalized engineering spend to be reflected on the balance sheet.

**The governance artifact**: a Build vs. Buy Decision Document — a structured template (maintained separately in this framework as `build-vs-buy-decision-template.md`) that every recommendation must complete before reaching the deciding authority. The document answers: what problem is being solved, what options were considered, what is the TCO over 3 years, what is the lock-in assessment, who owns maintenance, and what does the exit strategy look like.

## The Technology Radar as Portfolio Governance Tool

Individual decisions are governed by the Decision Document and the authority model. The aggregate of all decisions is governed by the Technology Radar.

ThoughtWorks popularized the Technology Radar format: a visual model with four rings that express the organization's current stance on every technology in use or under consideration.

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
                    │                ASSESS                    │
                    │   eBPF observability  |  Wasm runtimes   │
                    │  ┌────────────────────────────────────┐  │
                    │  │             TRIAL                  │  │
                    │  │  Istio service mesh | Cilium CNI   │  │
                    │  │  ┌──────────────────────────────┐  │  │
                    │  │  │           ADOPT              │  │  │
                    │  │  │  EKS  |  Terraform  |  OTEL  │  │  │
                    │  │  │  Splunk  |  RabbitMQ  |  Kafka│  │  │
                    │  │  └──────────────────────────────┘  │  │
                    │  │  ┌──────────────────────────────┐  │  │
                    │  │  │         HOLD / RETIRE        │  │  │
                    │  │  │  OpenShift (migrating→EKS)   │  │  │
                    │  │  │  Nagios  |  Legacy AppDynamics│  │  │
                    │  │  └──────────────────────────────┘  │  │
                    │  └────────────────────────────────────┘  │
                    └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Adopt**: Proven, recommended for production use at scale. Teams should standardize on this technology. New projects start here unless there is a documented reason not to. EKS is Adopt. Terraform is Adopt. OpenTelemetry instrumentation is Adopt.

**Trial**: Worth pursuing in projects that can handle the risk of an emerging technology. The goal is to collect learnings and validate the technology in your specific context. Istio as a service mesh implementation is Trial — valuable, but operationally complex enough that not every team should adopt it without guidance.

**Assess**: Worth understanding but not yet ready for broad use. Watch how the ecosystem develops. eBPF-based observability tooling (Pixie, Tetragon) is Assess — technically compelling, but the operational model and integration patterns are still maturing in enterprise environments.

**Hold / Retire**: Proceed with caution. Do not start new projects with this technology. If you are currently using it, plan your migration path. OpenShift moving to Hold at an EKS-standardizing organization is a real governance action — not a judgment that OpenShift is bad, but a signal that the organization has made a strategic choice and will not add new OpenShift workloads.

**The Director's responsibility**: publish the radar, update it on a cadence (quarterly is common), and enforce it through the Decision Document process. A team that proposes a Hold-ring technology in a new Build vs. Buy decision should need to provide compelling justification for why this specific case warrants deviation.

## The Build ROI Model: When Does Building Become Cheaper?

Build decisions require a financial model, not just an engineering preference. The model does not need to be complicated, but it must be honest about all cost categories.

**Build becomes worth analyzing seriously when**:

- The feature set is narrow, stable, and well-understood (wide and evolving feature sets get expensive fast)
- You have existing engineers with the capability, who are allocated and not borrowed from product teams
- The buy option is priced for a usage pattern significantly larger or different from yours
- You will use the capability at a scale where per-unit vendor pricing becomes punitive

**A practical threshold for triggering deeper analysis**: if your projected annual vendor spend exceeds the fully-loaded cost of 1.5 FTEs who could build and maintain the equivalent capability, build is worth a serious TCO comparison. This is not a rule — it is a trigger. The actual analysis must still account for time to value, maintenance complexity, opportunity cost, and what those 1.5 FTEs would otherwise be doing.

**What the build TCO model must include**:

| Cost Category | Build | Buy |
|--------------|-------|-----|
| Year 1 engineering (FTE × burdened cost) | Full | Integration only |
| Year 2–3 maintenance (FTE × burdened cost) | Ongoing | Vendor-covered |
| Vendor licensing / SaaS fees | None | Annual |
| Integration and migration | Moderate | Moderate |
| Training and onboarding | Internal only | Vendor + internal |
| **3-Year Total** | Sum | Sum |

Present both as 3-year totals. Year 1 cost comparisons consistently favor build (no licensing); 3-year totals more often favor buy when maintenance is honestly modeled.

## The Standardize-and-Reduce Principle

Every Director should carry a standing objective to reduce tool count. More tools mean more integrations, more operational knowledge required across the team, more security surface area, and more toil in the form of upgrade cycles, access management, and incident response for tools nobody fully understands.

When evaluating a new buy — or a new build — the evaluation must include the question: **can this replace something we already have?** Not "does it also do X in addition to solving the immediate problem?" but "does it do X well enough that we can retire the incumbent?"

A new observability vendor that solves your synthetic monitoring gap but also has credible APM and log aggregation capabilities should be evaluated as a potential replacement for two or three existing tools, not as an addition to the stack.

Reduction is unglamorous work. It rarely gets announced. But a Director who retires three tools while adopting one has improved the platform more durably than a Director who adopts four tools in a year.

## Presenting a Build vs. Buy Recommendation to a CFO

This is the capability that separates Director candidates from senior engineering managers in interviews. CFOs do not care about technology. They care about risk, cost, and strategic return. A technically correct recommendation presented in engineering language will not land.

**Lead with the outcome**: "We recommend [option] because it reduces our 3-year total cost by $X and eliminates our current dependency on [specific risk]." The first sentence must be the recommendation and its financial or risk basis. Not the background. Not the options analysis. The conclusion.

**Frame costs correctly**: Build costs are capital investment with a payback period. Buy costs are operational expense. CFOs model these differently on the income statement and balance sheet. Know which one you're asking for and use the right language.

**Quantify the cost of inaction**: "If we defer this decision by one year, we will spend an additional $X maintaining the current approach, and we will incur [specific risk] during that period." The cost of inaction is often more compelling to a CFO than the benefit of action.

**Anticipate the questions**:
- What happens if the vendor raises prices? (exit clause, pricing cap in the contract)
- What happens if the vendor is acquired? (portability layer, data export guarantee)
- What is the breakeven point if we build? (show the model)
- Who owns this if the lead engineer leaves? (named team ownership, documented runbooks)

The Build vs. Buy Decision Document answers all of these. Bring it. Reference it. Do not summarize it — leave it as the supporting artifact.

## What Panels Are Testing in Director Interviews

When a Director interview panel asks about Build vs. Buy, they are evaluating five things, not one:

**Structured process**: Can you describe a repeatable decision framework, or do you go with instinct? An instinct-based answer sounds like: "We chose Datadog because it's the best observability platform." A structured answer sounds like: "We evaluated four options against five criteria, built a 3-year TCO model for each, and assessed lock-in risk using our standard scorecard."

**Full TCO understanding**: Do you account for engineering time, maintenance cost, training, and integration — or just licensing? Many candidates can recite licensing costs. Fewer can model maintenance honestly.

**Lock-in literacy**: Can you name the four types of lock-in and explain which mitigation applies to each? Can you describe what exit clauses you negotiate for?

**Escalation judgment**: Do you know what you can decide vs. what requires escalation? Owning decisions within your threshold is competence. Escalating above it is governance discipline.

**Executive communication**: Can you translate a technology recommendation into financial and risk language that a CFO can evaluate? This is the capability most engineering leaders underinvest in.

The answer that wins the panel: "We chose Datadog over building in-house because our TCO model showed it was $400K cheaper over 3 years at our scale. We negotiated a data portability SLA, a 4% annual price cap, and termination for convenience. Our lock-in scorecard rates the relationship at 3 on API lock-in and 4 on data lock-in — which is why the data portability clause was non-negotiable. If Datadog's pricing changed materially, we have a documented 90-day exit plan."

That answer demonstrates every capability the panel is testing.

## Closing: The Framework as a Decision System

The six chapters of this framework are not independent modules. They are a connected decision system:

**Chapter 1** established the framing — Build vs. Buy is a portfolio strategy, not a binary choice, and TCO is the foundational measurement tool.

**Chapter 2** provided the TCO methodology — how to model engineering cost, vendor cost, switching cost, and option value across a 3-year horizon.

**Chapter 3** examined the build decision — when internal capability becomes a genuine competitive advantage vs. when it becomes a maintenance liability.

**Chapter 4** examined the buy decision — vendor evaluation, contract negotiation, and the criteria that separate good vendor relationships from expensive ones.

**Chapter 5** covered migration strategy — TCO for technology transitions, phased migration patterns, and managing the cost of modernization.

**Chapter 6** applied the framework to Internal Developer Platforms — the golden path decision, the Backstage reality check, and platform-as-product discipline.

**Chapter 7** mapped the lock-in taxonomy and provided concrete mitigation strategies and exit clause language.

**Chapter 8** assembled the governance infrastructure — decision authority, the Technology Radar, the ROI model, and executive communication.

Used together, these chapters give a Director the vocabulary, the process, and the artifacts to make Build vs. Buy decisions that are consistent, defensible, financially honest, and strategically coherent. The goal is not perfect decisions — no framework eliminates uncertainty. The goal is decisions you can explain, decisions you can audit, and decisions that produce a technology portfolio you can manage rather than one that manages you.
