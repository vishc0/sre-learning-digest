# Chapter 04: Vendor Evaluation Framework

## Evaluation Is Engineering Work — Treat It That Way

When your team evaluates vendors, engineers are not shipping. A PoC running for four weeks with two engineers is a $30,000–$50,000 investment in evaluation before you have signed anything. Done poorly, vendor evaluation is expensive, inconclusive, and politically corrosive — the team that ran the PoC becomes emotionally invested in the outcome, and objectivity evaporates.

Done well, vendor evaluation is a structured process with defined stages, clear scoring criteria, and a decision that can be defended in a budget review, a board presentation, or an audit. This chapter covers how to run that process.

---

## RFI Before RFP — The Step Most Teams Skip

The two primary instruments of vendor evaluation are the RFI and the RFP. They are not interchangeable, and skipping the first one is a structural mistake.

**RFI — Request for Information** is used when you do not yet know exactly what you need. It is exploratory by design. Send an RFI to 8-12 vendors. The purpose is not to get proposals — it is to learn the market. What capabilities exist? What are vendors calling this problem? What pricing models are common? What integration patterns do mature customers use? The RFI teaches you enough to write a useful RFP.

**RFP — Request for Proposal** is used when you know what you need and want vendors to compete on a defined scope. Send an RFP to 3-5 vendors. The purpose is to get comparable, scoreable proposals. Comparable means apples-to-apples on scope, timeline, and pricing structure.

The most common mistake: teams skip the RFI and go directly to the RFP. The result is that the RFP requirements are written based on conversations with the first vendor the team spoke to — which means you have inadvertently written requirements that favor that vendor's architecture. The evaluation is contaminated before it starts.

Run the RFI. It costs two weeks. It saves three months of bad evaluation.

## Building the Weighted Scoring Scorecard

Every vendor evaluation needs a scorecard built before you start talking to vendors. If you build the scorecard after the vendor presentations, you are reverse-engineering justification for a decision already made.

The criteria and weights below are calibrated for platform infrastructure decisions at enterprise scale. Adjust weights based on your specific constraints — if data portability is a regulatory requirement, exit ease goes up.

| Criterion | Weight | What You Are Actually Measuring |
|---|---|---|
| Functional fit | 25% | Does it solve the problem as defined, not the problem the vendor wants you to have? |
| TCO over 3 years | 20% | Full model per Chapter 03 — not just license cost |
| Vendor stability | 15% | Financial health, market position, acquisition risk, license change history |
| Integration complexity | 15% | How hard to wire into your existing identity, observability, CI/CD, and data stacks |
| Support quality | 10% | SLA commitments, support tier available to you, escalation path to engineering |
| Exit ease | 10% | Data portability standards, contract exit clauses, community/fork availability |
| Reference customer quality | 5% | Customers at your scale, in your industry, with honest assessments |

Score each vendor on each criterion from 1-5. Multiply by weight. Sum the weighted scores. The resulting number is one input into the decision — not the decision itself. A vendor that scores 4.2 versus 3.9 is not automatically the right choice; use the scorecard to surface where the real differences are, then reason about those differences explicitly.

## PoC Design: Test the Riskiest Assumption, Not the Easiest One

A proof of concept proves that the software works in your environment, with your data, on your infrastructure. It does not prove that the software scales to production load, integrates with every downstream system, or is maintainable by your team over a three-year horizon.

The instinct in most PoC designs is to demonstrate the happy path — the feature that will impress the stakeholders in the readout. This is backwards. A PoC should be designed to stress the assumption most likely to cause the decision to fail.

**Before you design a PoC, answer**: What is the single assumption, if wrong, that would make this vendor decision a mistake six months post-implementation? Design the PoC to test that assumption.

For an observability platform: the happy path is ingesting sample logs and showing a dashboard. The risky assumption is whether the platform handles your cardinality — the number of unique label combinations across your Kubernetes clusters, microservices, and multi-region topology. Test cardinality at production scale. Everything else is secondary.

Timebox PoCs to three weeks maximum. After three weeks, you have enough signal to score the vendor on the riskiest dimension. Anything beyond that is diminishing returns at increasing cost.

## Reference Checks: What to Actually Ask

Every vendor will offer you references. Those references will say positive things — they were selected to say positive things. You can still extract useful signal if you ask the right questions.

**What to ask:**

- "What did you wish you had known before you signed the contract?" This bypasses the scripted answer and surfaces real operational experience.
- "What was the actual implementation timeline versus what the vendor promised in the proposal?" Vendors systematically underestimate implementation complexity. Get the real number.
- "Have you tried to exit or renegotiate your contract? What happened?" This question tells you more about the vendor relationship than any other. The vendor's behavior when you have leverage is revealed here.
- "What does support quality look like 18 months after contract signature?" Post-sale support quality degradation is a consistent pattern. You want to know what you are signing up for in year two, not year one.
- "Would you make the same decision again with the information you have now?" A non-answer is itself an answer.

**What to do beyond vendor-supplied references**: Ask the vendor for three references they did not provide — customers who churned, customers who renegotiated, or customers who ran a competitive evaluation and chose a competitor. If the vendor cannot provide a single one, that tells you something. If they provide two, those conversations will be your most valuable reference data.

## The Shortlist-to-Three Principle

Never evaluate more than three vendors in depth. Evaluation is expensive — engineers doing PoCs are not building product. Four vendors in parallel PoC is a two-month engineering distraction that produces diminishing marginal information after vendor three.

Qualify aggressively in the RFI stage. Use the RFP to get to five. Use the scorecard on RFP responses to get to three. Then evaluate those three rigorously with a structured PoC and reference checks. Three vendors in parallel PoC is manageable. Four is where evaluation quality starts to degrade.

---

## Negotiation: Leverage Disappears at Signature

Your maximum negotiating leverage is the moment before you sign. The vendor knows they are competing. Use it.

- Never reveal your preferred vendor during negotiation. Let each vendor believe the competition is real, because it is.
- Use competing proposals explicitly. "Vendor B has offered us equivalent capability at 20% lower total cost. Help me understand what Vendor A offers that justifies the delta."
- Negotiate contract terms beyond price: exit clauses, data portability guarantees, price cap provisions for renewal years, SLA credits, and escalation path commitments. The contract is a risk management document, not just a pricing document.
- The best price is never in the first proposal. A 10-15% reduction is available in most enterprise software negotiations if you are willing to run the process correctly and wait for the final round.

At T-Mobile scale, vendor relationships are multi-year and multi-contract. A decision to adopt a platform vendor for one team frequently becomes the enterprise baseline. A Director-level vendor decision is not just about this contract — it is about the vendor relationship portfolio and the precedents you are setting for the next procurement cycle.

## Sample Vendor Scoring Matrix: API Gateway Platform

| Criterion | Weight | Vendor A | Score A | Vendor B | Score B | Vendor C | Score C |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Functional fit | 25% | Strong feature coverage | 4 | Partial fit, gaps in rate limiting | 3 | Exceeds requirements | 5 |
| TCO (3-year) | 20% | $1.2M | 3 | $900K | 4 | $1.6M | 2 |
| Vendor stability | 15% | Public, profitable | 5 | VC-backed, Series B | 2 | Established, no M&A risk | 4 |
| Integration complexity | 15% | Native Kubernetes operator | 5 | Manual YAML config | 2 | Helm chart, moderate | 3 |
| Support quality | 10% | 24/7 P1 SLA | 4 | Business hours only | 2 | 24/7, dedicated CSM | 5 |
| Exit ease | 10% | Proprietary config format | 2 | OpenAPI-based, portable | 5 | Moderate portability | 3 |
| Reference quality | 5% | Two T-Mobile scale refs | 5 | Startup references only | 2 | Mid-market references | 3 |
| **Weighted Total** | **100%** | | **3.90** | | **3.00** | | **3.55** |

In this example, Vendor A leads on weighted score but has a significant risk on exit ease. Vendor C is closer in score with better exit characteristics. The scorecard surfaces that conversation — the Director now has a structured basis for asking whether the 0.35-point gap justifies Vendor A's exit risk.
