# Vendor Exit Assessment Playbook — Assessing the Cost and Feasibility of Exiting a Current Vendor

**Audience**: Director evaluating whether to exit an existing vendor relationship  
**When to use**: Vendor price increase >20%, vendor acquisition, support degradation, contract renewal upcoming, or when a significantly better alternative has emerged  
**Output**: Go/no-go exit decision with exit cost model, migration plan outline, and approval record  

---

## The Default Position

Switching vendors is expensive in ways that do not appear on the vendor's pricing page. The goal of this assessment is not to find a reason to stay or to leave — it is to make the exit decision with accurate cost information. Teams that exit vendors without running this assessment consistently underestimate the migration cost by 2-4x.

Run this assessment before initiating any replacement RFP. The exit cost often changes which replacement option is viable.

---

## Phase 1 — Trigger Classification

Document why exit is being considered. The trigger determines urgency, leverage, and what you can negotiate.

| Trigger | Urgency | Leverage Notes |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Pricing event (increase >20%) | Medium — contract cycle | Competitors' pricing is your leverage; existing contract terms may limit their ability to raise mid-term |
| Acquisition / ownership change | High — direction unknown | Acquirer's roadmap is not yet set; window to negotiate better terms before the new owner hardens their position |
| Support degradation | Medium | Document specific incidents with timestamps before raising with vendor; SLA credits may be owed |
| Better alternative available | Low — opportunistic | No urgency allows full evaluation cycle; use competing proposal as contract leverage at renewal |
| Strategic direction change | Varies | Internal decision; vendor has no leverage |
| Contract renewal upcoming | High — time-limited | Renewal is the single best leverage point you have; run this assessment 6 months before renewal, not 6 weeks |

Document the trigger in one paragraph. This paragraph goes into the exit assessment report and informs your negotiation posture.

---

## Phase 2 — Current State Documentation

Before modeling exit cost, document what you are actually using from this vendor. This is harder than it sounds. Teams routinely discover at exit time that they use far more of a vendor's platform than they realized during the initial procurement.

Conduct an integration audit:

- **Feature inventory**: which features/modules are actively used vs. licensed but unused?
- **Integration map**: which internal systems call vendor APIs, receive vendor webhooks, or push data to the vendor? Draw the integration map — do not reconstruct it from memory.
- **API surface**: which vendor-specific APIs does your code depend on? Are these proprietary APIs or standard protocols?
- **Data stored**: what data lives in the vendor's systems? Volume, format, age, regulatory requirements (PII, PHI, financial records)?
- **Operational dependencies**: what monitoring, alerting, or operational tooling depends on this vendor?
- **Human dependencies**: which engineers or operators have built workflows that depend on this vendor's UI, CLI, or API?

Produce a written integration map. This document drives the exit cost model in Phase 6.

---

## Phase 3 — Data Export Assessment

Data portability is the most critical risk dimension for any data-holding vendor. Assess it before anything else in the exit process.

Steps:
1. **Review the contract**: what does the data portability clause say? Format, timeline, cost?
2. **Test the export now**: do not wait until you decide to exit. Initiate a test data export immediately. If the export fails, is corrupted, or is in an unusable format, this is a critical risk item.
3. **Validate the output**: exported data that cannot be imported into a replacement system is not data portability — it is data theater. Test that the export format can actually be consumed by a replacement.
4. **Assess volume and timeline**: at your current data volume, how long does a full export take? At what cost (API calls, egress fees)?
5. **Escalation path if export fails**: if the data export fails or the vendor is uncooperative, what is the contractual remedy? Is legal involvement warranted?

If data export testing reveals that you cannot get your data out reliably, escalate immediately — to the vendor account team first, and to legal if the vendor is unresponsive. Data hostage situations (vendor withholding data during billing disputes) are a contract violation in most jurisdictions and most enterprise contracts.

## Phase 4 — Lock-in Inventory

Score the current vendor on all four operational lock-in dimensions. Use the detailed taxonomy in `vendor-lock-in-taxonomy-reference.md`.

**API Lock-in**: How many internal systems call vendor-specific, proprietary APIs that would need to be rewritten for a replacement?
- Count the internal services with direct vendor API dependencies
- Estimate engineering effort to replace each integration
- Score: 1 (standard protocols, easy to swap) → 5 (deep proprietary API dependency across many services)

**Data Lock-in**: Is your data stored in a vendor-proprietary format that requires transformation for use elsewhere?
- Score: 1 (open standard format, export tested and working) → 5 (proprietary schema, export untested or failed)

**Operational Lock-in**: What percentage of your operations team can operate a replacement system?
- If your team knows only this vendor's tooling, replacement system ramp-up adds to migration cost
- Score: 1 (vendor uses standard protocols; team transfers skills easily) → 5 (vendor-specific UI, CLI, and monitoring that has no equivalent elsewhere)

**People Lock-in**: How many engineers have vendor-specific certifications or strong preferences for this platform?
- Certification investment (time + money) creates personal switching cost for engineers
- Score: 1 (no vendor-specific certifications; team is tool-agnostic) → 5 (team has significant vendor certification investment and vocal preference)

Record the composite lock-in score. A score of 3+ on any single dimension is a risk item that drives up exit cost.

---

## Phase 5 — Replacement Identification

Identify 2–3 candidate replacement vendors or OSS solutions. Initiate a short RFI (see vendor evaluation playbook Phase 2) to each.

**Critical constraint**: do not start a full RFP until you understand your exit cost. Exit cost may make switching economically unviable even if the replacement is meaningfully better. Spending 6 weeks on a detailed RFP evaluation before knowing you cannot afford to switch is waste.

At this phase, you need enough information to estimate whether replacement is feasible, not which replacement to choose. The RFI is sufficient for that purpose.

Document for each candidate:
- Does it meet functional requirements from the problem statement?
- Rough pricing at your scale (enough for the exit cost model)
- Integration approach — does it replace like-for-like or require architectural change?

---

## Phase 6 — Exit Cost Modeling

Model the full cost of exiting. Every category below must have an estimate — do not skip a category because it is hard to estimate. A rough estimate with documented assumptions is more useful than an absent estimate.

**Data Migration Cost**
- Engineering time to export data from current vendor (hours × hourly rate)
- Engineering time to transform data to target format (ETL work)
- Engineering time to import and validate data in replacement system
- Infrastructure cost for migration processing (compute, storage, network egress)

**Integration Replacement Cost**
- Per integration: estimated engineering effort to replace the vendor API call/webhook/SDK with the replacement system's equivalent
- Sum across all integrations identified in Phase 2
- Add 30% buffer for undiscovered dependencies (they always exist)

**Retraining Cost**
- Operations team: time to reach equivalent proficiency on replacement system
- Engineering team: if replacement system uses different paradigms or tooling
- Certification costs if applicable

**Transition Period Cost**
- Cost of running both systems in parallel during migration (typically 4–12 weeks)
- This is often the largest single line item — you pay both vendors simultaneously

**Contract Termination Cost**
- Early exit penalty if applicable (check contract)
- Remaining contract commitment if exit is mid-term

**Total Exit Cost**
Sum all categories. Compare to: cost of staying for 1 year, 2 years, 3 years. This comparison drives the go/no-go decision.

---

## Phase 7 — Exit Clause Activation

Review the existing contract for clauses that affect your exit options:

- **Termination for convenience**: can you exit without cause? With how much notice? Any penalty?
- **Data portability SLA**: is there a contractual commitment to provide data export within a specific timeline?
- **Transition assistance**: is the vendor obligated to provide any support during migration?
- **Pricing dispute resolution**: if exit is triggered by a price dispute, does the contract provide a resolution mechanism or freeze period?

If the contract has no exit clause, document this explicitly in the exit assessment as a governance lesson. Add the missing clause to your vendor contract review template checklist (`vendor-contract-review-template.md`) so it is negotiated into future contracts.

If the contract has favorable exit clauses, activate them in writing. Send a formal notice per the contract's required process — verbal discussions do not start the clock.

---

## Phase 8 — Migration Plan Outline

If exit is economically viable (see go/no-go framework below), draft a high-level migration plan for Director/VP approval. The migration plan is a phase outline, not a detailed project plan — the detailed plan comes after approval.

**Phase 1 — Data Export and Validation** (2–4 weeks)
- Execute full data export from current vendor
- Validate export completeness and format
- Store exported data in a vendor-neutral format in your own storage
- Go/no-go gate: is the data out and usable?

**Phase 2 — Replacement Integration and Parallel Run** (4–12 weeks)
- Deploy replacement system
- Rebuild integrations against replacement APIs
- Run replacement in parallel with current vendor (shadow mode or dual-write)
- Validate parity: replacement produces same results as current vendor

**Phase 3 — Cutover and Validation** (1–2 weeks)
- Route production traffic to replacement system
- Monitor closely for 2 weeks with rollback option available
- Confirm all integrations operating correctly

**Phase 4 — Vendor Contract Termination** (per contract notice period)
- Provide formal termination notice per contract requirements
- Complete any contractual transition assistance period
- Confirm data deletion from vendor systems per retention policy

Get Director/VP approval before executing Phase 1. The migration plan is not a commitment to execute — it is a document that demonstrates the exit is feasible and the team has thought through the sequencing.

---

## Go/No-Go Decision Framework

Exit is worth pursuing if ALL of the following are true:

1. **Economic viability**: Total exit cost < (cost differential between current vendor and replacement × chosen time horizon)  
   Example: exit costs $400K; replacement saves $200K/year → break-even at 2 years; viable if you plan to stay on the replacement for 3+ years

2. **Functional viability**: The replacement solution meets the functional requirements documented in your problem statement without significant gaps

3. **Team capacity**: Your team has the engineering capacity to execute the migration without dropping higher-priority work — or you have a credible plan for acquiring that capacity

If any condition is not met, document why and present the no-go recommendation. A documented no-go is a decision, not a failure. It prevents the same analysis from being repeated six months later.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Starting the exit without mapping current integrations**: the integration map always reveals more dependencies than anyone remembers
- **Not testing data export before the exit decision**: discovering data portability problems after you have committed to a replacement is a negotiating disaster
- **Underestimating the parallel-run cost**: running two systems simultaneously is expensive and almost always takes longer than planned
- **Negotiating as if exit is certain before the model is complete**: vendors can smell urgency; run the model before signaling intent to leave
- **Skipping the contract review**: the exit clause you negotiated two years ago may eliminate the early termination penalty entirely — check before assuming
