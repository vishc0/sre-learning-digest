# Build Decision Playbook — Evaluating Whether to Build In-House

**Audience**: Director evaluating a proposal from a team to build a capability internally  
**When to use**: Any time a team proposes building a non-trivial internal tool, platform component, or service that could alternatively be purchased or adopted from OSS  
**Output**: Build vs. Buy recommendation document with TCO model, team capacity evidence, and risk assessment  

---

## The Default Position

Building is not free. The engineering time spent building and maintaining internal tools is time not spent on capabilities that differentiate your product in the market. Before evaluating any build proposal, hold this as the baseline: **the default answer is to not build**. The burden of proof is on the team proposing to build.

This playbook structures that burden of proof into an evaluable process.

---

## Phase 1 — Strategic Capability Test

Before modeling cost or team capacity, answer one question: is this capability a competitive differentiator or a commodity?

Write one sentence:

> "This capability is a **[commodity / differentiator]** because **[reason]**."

The test: would a competitor paying the same price for a vendor solution have the same strategic advantage as you would by building it in-house? If yes, it is a commodity — the market already levels this playing field. If no — if your specific implementation, your proprietary data, or your unique operational context creates an advantage that a vendor solution cannot replicate — it may be a differentiator.

**Examples**:
- Secret rotation tooling → commodity. Every security team needs it; none of them win in the market because of how they rotate secrets.
- A real-time trading signal aggregation engine tuned to your proprietary data feeds → differentiator. The data and the tuning are yours; no vendor replicates that.
- A Kubernetes cost allocation dashboard → commodity. Multiple vendors solve this. Build only if existing solutions fail specific requirements that cannot be addressed through configuration.

Document the result. If the classification is "commodity," the team proposing to build must explain why buying is insufficient before the model proceeds.

---

## Phase 2 — Buy-First Check

Before modeling build cost, document why the buy option is insufficient. This step is required — it cannot be skipped. The proposing team must:

1. Name at least 3 vendors evaluated in the category
2. For each vendor: what specific requirement does it fail to meet?
3. What is the gap that requires a custom build?

If the team cannot name 3 vendors evaluated, they have not done the market scan. Send them back to run Phase 2 of the vendor evaluation playbook before returning with a build proposal.

Common gaps that legitimately justify building:
- No vendor integrates with a proprietary internal system that cannot be exposed
- Compliance requirement prevents sending certain data outside the organization's control plane
- Required latency or throughput performance that no vendor solution meets at your scale
- Vendor pricing at your scale exceeds build TCO by a significant and durable margin

Common gaps that do not justify building:
- "We looked at two vendors and they seemed expensive" (not evaluated; not modeled)
- "We'd have to customize it anyway" (customization cost vs. build cost is not modeled)
- "We can build exactly what we want" (the capability is a commodity; differentiation comes from the product, not the tooling)

---

## Phase 3 — Build TCO Model

Model the full cost of building over 3-year and 5-year horizons. The model must include all cost categories — do not let the proposing team present only the Year 1 engineering cost.

**Year 1 — Build Cost**
- Engineering: [number of FTEs] × [months to deliver] × $20,000/month (fully loaded — salary + benefits + overhead + management overhead)
- Tooling and infrastructure during development
- Security review, penetration test if applicable
- Documentation: user-facing and operational runbooks

**Year 2–3 — Maintenance Cost**
- Engineering maintenance: 20–30% of Year 1 build cost per year (industry standard for non-trivial internal systems)
- This covers: bug fixes, dependency updates, platform version compatibility, feature requests from internal teams
- On-call allocation: if this system is in the critical path, estimate oncall hours and fully-loaded cost
- Dependency management: frameworks, libraries, and runtime versions require ongoing updates

**Ongoing — Opportunity Cost**
- What is the team maintaining this system not building?
- State this explicitly: "The 2 engineers maintaining this system in Year 2 would otherwise be working on [specific product capability]."
- Opportunity cost is real cost. It simply does not appear in a spreadsheet.

**Total Cost Comparison**
Build Year 1 + Year 2 maintenance + Year 3 maintenance vs. vendor license + implementation + Year 2-3 subscription.

If build break-even is beyond 3 years (meaning the cumulative cost of building exceeds the buy alternative for 3 years), the default should be Buy unless there is a strong strategic or compliance reason to override.

---

## Phase 4 — Team Capacity Check

A build plan without named engineers is a wish, not a plan.

Answer these questions with specifics:

1. **Who builds it?** Name the engineers. Not "the platform team" — actual individuals.
2. **What is their current utilization?** If they are already at 80%+ capacity, where does the capacity come from? What gets deprioritized?
3. **What will they stop doing?** This is the hardest question. Force it to be answered explicitly.
4. **Who maintains it in Year 2?** Name the person or team. If the answer is "the same engineers," what is their expected capacity in Year 2?
5. **What is the bus factor?** If the primary owner leaves, who can maintain this system? Is that person available and willing?

If the team cannot answer questions 1, 3, and 4 with specifics, the build plan is not real. Do not approve it.

---

## Phase 5 — Maintenance Ownership

Building a system and maintaining a system are different jobs. Many teams underestimate maintenance burden because they are imagining themselves in build mode, not in production support mode.

Document before approving:

- **Primary owner**: team name + individual DRI (directly responsible individual)
- **Secondary owner**: who covers during PTO, oncall handoff, or if the primary owner leaves?
- **Escalation path**: what is the P1 escalation path for this system at 2am?
- **Bus factor mitigation**: if the primary owner leaves, what is the knowledge transfer and ramp-up plan?
- **Lifecycle mandate**: who has the authority to deprecate this system when its time comes? What triggers deprecation (vendor solution matures, team capacity changes, strategic direction changes)?

Systems without clear ownership degrade slowly until they become technical debt that nobody owns but everyone depends on. Document ownership before the first line of code is written.

---

## Phase 6 — Risk Assessment

Score the build option on four dimensions using Low / Medium / High:

| Risk | Questions to Answer | Scoring |
|------|--------------------|---------| 
| **Timeline risk** | Has this team delivered a comparable scope on time before? What is their track record? | H = team has missed comparable deliveries; L = strong track record at this scope |
| **Maintenance risk** | Is the maintenance plan realistic given other commitments? | H = no named owner in Year 2; L = dedicated team with headcount committed |
| **Quality risk** | Will the output meet production standards (security review, reliability, documentation)? | H = no production standards process; L = existing security review and SRE gatekeeping |
| **Opportunity cost risk** | What product value is foregone by assigning these engineers to this build? | H = engineers would otherwise ship high-revenue or high-strategic-value features; L = engineers are backlog-constrained with lower-priority work |

A build proposal with two or more High-rated risks should be rejected or sent back for plan revision.

---

## Phase 7 — Build vs. Buy Recommendation Document

Complete the Build vs. Buy Decision Document (see `build-vs-buy-decision-template.md`). For a build decision, the document must include:

- Strategic capability classification with rationale
- Evidence of buy alternatives evaluated and why they are insufficient
- TCO model at 3 and 5 years, compared to buy alternative
- Named engineers and capacity evidence
- Named maintenance owner and bus factor mitigation plan
- Risk assessment with scores and mitigations

The document goes to Director-level approval for builds up to $500K. VP/CTO for builds above $500K or with significant ongoing maintenance burden. CFO involvement is warranted when opportunity cost is significant and must be weighed against product roadmap priorities.

---

## Phase 8 — Governance Approval

Route the recommendation document for approval at the level appropriate to the cost and strategic impact. After approval:

1. Archive the decision document as a Technology Radar entry — status: Build (in progress)
2. Set a 12-month review checkpoint: at 12 months, re-evaluate whether the build delivered on its TCO model and whether the buy alternatives have matured
3. Track maintenance cost actuals against the model — if Year 2 maintenance cost exceeds 30% of build cost, escalate for re-evaluation

---

## Red Flag Checklist — Stop and Reconsider

Stop the build evaluation and require plan revision if any of these are true:

- [ ] The team cannot name who will maintain this system in Year 2
- [ ] The TCO model shows build break-even is beyond 5 years against the buy alternative
- [ ] The strategic capability test result is "commodity" and the buy-first check did not find a genuine gap
- [ ] The buy alternative has not been seriously evaluated (fewer than 3 vendors assessed with written gap analysis)
- [ ] The proposing team's engineers are already at >80% utilization with no plan for what gets dropped
- [ ] No named secondary owner or escalation path exists for production incidents
- [ ] The build scope has expanded since the original proposal without a revised TCO model

If three or more red flags are present, the build proposal is not ready. Return it with the specific gaps identified.

---

## Director's Role in Build Proposals

Your job is not to approve or reject build proposals based on intuition. Your job is to apply the framework consistently and require the evidence. Teams that have done the work will have answers. Teams that have not done the work will be frustrated by the questions — and that frustration is diagnostic.

The questions that matter most, in order:
1. Who maintains this in Year 2? Name them.
2. What are we not building as a result? Name it.
3. What vendor did you evaluate most seriously, and what specifically does it not do?
