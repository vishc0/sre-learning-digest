# Vendor Evaluation Playbook — End-to-End Process

**Audience**: Director or Senior Manager running a formal vendor evaluation  
**When to use**: Any new vendor category where spend is expected to exceed $100K/year or integration depth is significant  
**Output**: Vendor selection decision with documented rationale, scorecard, and approval record  

---

## Phase 1 — Define the Problem (Before Contacting Any Vendor)

Before you open a browser or take a vendor call, write a 1-page problem statement. This document anchors the entire evaluation and prevents vendors from defining the problem for you.

The problem statement must cover:

- **Current state**: What is the team doing today? What is the pain? Be specific — "engineers spend 4 hours per week manually rotating secrets across 12 EKS clusters" is useful. "Our secrets management isn't great" is not.
- **Target state**: What does success look like 12 months after a solution is deployed? Define it in measurable terms where possible.
- **Must-have requirements**: The non-negotiables. If a vendor cannot meet these, they are disqualified regardless of other merits. Keep this list short (5-8 items). Example: "Must integrate with existing EKS clusters without sidecar injection" or "Must support SSO via Okta."
- **Nice-to-have requirements**: Features that add value but are not disqualifying if absent. These feed into the scorecard but do not gate inclusion.
- **Budget range**: Define a realistic range before vendor conversations. Vendors negotiate against the number in your head. If you don't have a number, they'll tell you what it should be.

Circulate the problem statement to your engineering leads and product counterparts before issuing any RFI. Disagreements at this stage are cheap. Disagreements after a PoC are expensive.

---

## Phase 2 — Market Scan (RFI Phase)

**Goal**: Cast a wide net, then narrow to a credible shortlist.  
**Timeline**: 2 weeks total (1 week to issue, 1 week response window)

Identify 8–12 vendors in the space using: Gartner/G2 category reports, analyst briefings, peer network recommendations, and CNCF landscape if relevant. Do not rely on inbound vendor outreach — that set is self-selected.

Send a short RFI (5–7 questions maximum) to all vendors. Keep it brief — you are qualifying, not evaluating. Required questions:

1. Provide a 2-paragraph product overview focused on the use case described in the attached problem statement.
2. Name 3 current customers at our scale (describe your environment briefly — number of clusters, approximate transaction volume, etc.).
3. Describe your pricing model: license, consumption-based, seat-based, or hybrid.
4. How does your product integrate with [EKS, Kafka, Splunk, Okta — name your actual stack]?
5. What is your data portability policy? If we exit, how do we get our data out, in what format, and on what timeline?
6. When was the company founded, who are your primary financial backers, and what was your last funding round or revenue milestone?
7. What is the one thing customers tell you is hardest about adopting your product?

Question 7 is the most valuable. Vendors who give a real answer are worth talking to. Vendors who answer with a marketing paragraph are telling you something.

**Output**: Score each vendor's RFI response against your must-have list. Narrow to 3–5 vendors for the RFP phase. Document why each vendor was excluded from further consideration.

---

## Phase 3 — RFP to Shortlist

**Goal**: Get detailed, comparable proposals from serious candidates.  
**Timeline**: 3 weeks (1 week to issue, 3 weeks response window — vendors need time to produce quality responses)

Send a detailed RFP to 3–5 shortlisted vendors. The RFP is not a purchasing document — it is a technical and commercial questionnaire. Include:

- Specific use cases with enough context for vendors to propose an architecture
- Full technical requirements (integration points, performance requirements, security requirements)
- SLA requirements for uptime, support response, and data export
- TCO model inputs: expected usage at Year 1 and Year 3 scale, number of users/clusters/events as applicable
- Request for 2 reference customer contacts at comparable scale — not vendor-curated testimonials, actual contacts you can call

Request back: written proposal, itemized pricing at your stated scale, and 2 reference contacts.

**Output**: A comparable set of written proposals. Run them through your problem statement — does each proposal actually address the problem? Some vendors will answer the RFP they wish you'd asked, not the one you wrote.

---

## Phase 4 — Proof of Concept

**Goal**: Test the riskiest assumption for each finalist vendor in your actual environment.  
**Timeline**: 2–4 weeks maximum. Time-box strictly — PoCs that run long are a form of vendor lock-in.

Select your top 2–3 vendors for a PoC. Before the PoC starts, define in writing:

- **Success criteria**: What specific outcome would cause you to advance this vendor? Be quantitative where possible.
- **Failure criteria**: What outcome disqualifies the vendor? Define this before the PoC so vendors cannot move the goalposts mid-evaluation.
- **Test scope**: Test the riskiest assumption, not the easiest demo. The integration with your hardest system. The performance at your peak load. If a vendor's product fails on the safe tests, you haven't learned anything useful.

Assign one internal engineer to each PoC. That engineer's output is a PoC findings report: what was tested, what passed, what failed, integration complexity observed, and qualitative assessment of vendor responsiveness during the PoC.

Do not let vendors run the PoC for you. A vendor-run demo is a demo. A self-run PoC is evidence.

---

## Phase 5 — Score Vendors

Apply the weighted scorecard (see `vendor-evaluation-scorecard-template.md`). Score each vendor on:

- Functional fit (25%): does it solve the actual problem?
- 3-year TCO (20%): total cost including implementation, not just license
- Vendor stability (15%): financial health, backing, acquisition risk
- Integration complexity (15%): how hard was it actually in the PoC?
- Support quality (10%): based on PoC responsiveness, reference feedback
- Exit ease (10%): data portability, contract terms, lock-in depth
- Reference quality (5%): unprompted reference check findings

Calculate weighted totals. The scorecard is a decision aid, not a decision maker. If qualitative factors are significant, document them alongside the scorecard — do not override the scorecard silently.

---

## Phase 6 — Reference Checks

Call 2 reference customers per finalist vendor. Do not accept vendor-arranged-only references — ask vendors for references, then find additional customers independently via LinkedIn, community forums, or your peer network.

Four questions that matter most:

1. What did not go as planned during implementation?
2. What was the actual implementation timeline versus what the vendor estimated?
3. How is support quality 18 months in, compared to the sales period?
4. Have you evaluated exiting this vendor, and if so, what did you learn?

Reference checks take 30 minutes each. The information from a candid reference is worth more than any RFP response.

---

## Phase 7 — Negotiate

You have competing proposals. Use them. Do not reveal your preference until you have your final contract terms.

Required negotiation targets:
- **Pricing hold**: pricing is locked for 90 days from proposal date to allow for internal approval
- **Data portability SLA**: vendor provides data export in [specified format] within [X] business days of request
- **Annual price increase cap**: maximum [X]% per year, not CPI-linked unless CPI is currently favorable
- **Termination for convenience**: exit with [60–90] days notice, no penalty
- **Transition assistance**: [30–60] days of read-only access post-exit to facilitate migration

Never accept the first proposal. Every vendor builds negotiation margin into initial pricing. Document all concessions in the final contract, not just in email.

---

## Phase 8 — Select and Document

Complete the Build vs. Buy Decision Document (see `build-vs-buy-decision-template.md`). Attach: PoC findings reports, weighted scorecard, reference check notes, final negotiated contract terms. Get required approvals based on cost threshold.

Archive all artifacts as a decision record. This documentation protects you when the vendor raises prices in Year 2 or is acquired in Year 3 — you have evidence of what you evaluated and why you chose what you chose.

## Evaluation Timeline Summary

| Week | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 1–2 | Problem statement finalized + RFI issued to 8–12 vendors |
| 3 | RFI responses reviewed; shortlist of 3–5 selected and documented |
| 4–6 | RFP issued to shortlist; vendor response window |
| 7–10 | PoC with top 2–3 vendors (2–4 weeks time-boxed) |
| 11 | Scoring complete; reference checks conducted |
| 12 | Negotiation with preferred vendor |
| 13 | Selection finalized; decision document complete; approvals obtained |

## Common Failure Modes

- **Skipping the problem statement**: vendors will define the problem for you, and it will happen to be the problem their product solves
- **PoC too broad**: testing general capability instead of the specific hard integration means you learn nothing you couldn't learn from a demo
- **Accepting vendor references only**: vendors give you their happiest customers; find one who's unhappy
- **Evaluating cost without TCO**: license cost is the smallest part of the 3-year total
- **Starting negotiation after revealing your preference**: once the vendor knows you've chosen them, your leverage disappears
