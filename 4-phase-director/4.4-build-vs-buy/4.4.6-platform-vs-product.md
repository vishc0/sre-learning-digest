# Chapter 6: The Internal Developer Platform Decision — Build vs. Buy Your Platform

## What an Internal Developer Platform Actually Is

An Internal Developer Platform (IDP) is a self-service layer that absorbs infrastructure complexity so developers don't have to carry it. Before an IDP exists, deploying an application at a large org means a developer needs to know: which Kubernetes cluster, which namespace naming convention, which Helm chart version, which secrets manager, which CI/CD pipeline template, which observability stack, and how to wire them all together. The cognitive load is enormous and varies team by team.

An IDP changes that. It provides the **golden path** — the blessed, opinionated, pre-wired way to build and deploy at your organization. The developer says "I want to deploy a Python service." The golden path gives them a scaffold, a pipeline, a deployment target, an observability integration, and a service catalog entry — all compliant, all standard, all working. The developer doesn't configure infrastructure. They consume it.

The IDP decision is specific: do you build the golden path in-house (Backstage, custom tooling, homegrown portals) or buy a commercial IDP (Port.io, Cortex, OpsLevel)?

## The Highway On-Ramp Analogy

Think of Kubernetes and your cloud provider as the highway system. It exists. It handles the load. But how do developers get onto the highway?

Building a custom IDP (Backstage) is like designing and constructing your own on-ramp system for your city. You control every detail — the geometry, the merge lanes, the signage, the traffic flow. If your traffic patterns are genuinely unique — specific weight limits (compliance requirements), unusual interchange topology (bespoke deployment models), specialty cargo lanes (regulated data pipelines) — a custom on-ramp might be the only solution that fits.

Buying a commercial IDP is like adopting the standard interchange design the vendor provides. It works for most cities. It was engineered by people who have studied hundreds of traffic patterns. If your traffic is standard, the standard interchange is cheaper, faster, and better than anything you'd build yourself. The mistake most Directors make is assuming their traffic patterns are unique when they're actually standard — and commissioning a custom on-ramp they can't afford to maintain.

## The Backstage Reality Check

Backstage is Spotify's open-source IDP framework, now a CNCF incubating project. It is genuinely powerful. It is also genuinely expensive — not in licensing cost (it's free), but in engineering cost.

**What a production-quality Backstage implementation actually requires:**

- **Year 1**: 3–5 dedicated senior platform engineers. These engineers are not maintaining Backstage — they are *building* it. Writing plugins. Integrating with your CI/CD system. Connecting to your service catalog. Building the golden paths. Wiring up your compliance requirements. The work is real software engineering, not configuration.
- **Year 2 onward**: 2–3 engineers minimum for ongoing maintenance, plugin updates, Backstage version upgrades, and new golden path development.
- **Adoption effort**: someone needs to drive developer adoption. Backstage that nobody uses is a sunk cost, not a platform.

The most common Director-level miscalculation in 2024–2025: approving Backstage adoption as a cost-saving move (it's free!) without budgeting for the team required to build it.

**Before approving Backstage, a Director must be able to answer:**
- Do we have 3–5 senior platform engineers available to own this for 24+ months?
- Are those engineers budgeted and allocated — or are they being borrowed from product teams?
- Is there a named technical owner who will carry this across personnel changes?
- What is our plan if this team loses two members in Year 1?

If any of those answers is uncertain, you should be evaluating commercial IDPs first.

## Build vs. Buy Decision Matrix for IDPs

| Factor | Build (Backstage / Custom) | Buy (Port.io / Cortex / OpsLevel) |
|--------|---------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Developer workflow standard? | No — highly bespoke workflows | Yes — standard CI/CD, standard deploy patterns |
| Compliance/security constraints? | Unique constraints no vendor meets | Standard enterprise compliance (SOC 2, HIPAA, FedRAMP-aligned) |
| Dedicated platform team? | Yes — 3–5 senior engineers available and budgeted | No — platform work is distributed or shared responsibility |
| Time to value priority? | Can wait 12–18 months for maturity | Need impact within 90 days |
| Developer velocity is core differentiator? | Yes — platform is a competitive moat | No — platform is an efficiency tool |

## Who Paves the Golden Path — And Who Maintains It?

A golden path without an owner is a dirt track in 18 months.

Every golden path has a dependency chain: the service template, the CI/CD pipeline, the deployment target (EKS, Fargate), the base container image, the observability integration (OpenTelemetry collector, Splunk HEC), the secrets management pattern. When any link in that chain changes — Kubernetes minor version upgrade, base image CVE patch, Splunk index schema change — the golden path needs updating. If nobody owns that update, developers hit the golden path and find it broken. They work around it. The golden path atrophies. In 18 months, you have 14 different ways to deploy a service and no standard.

**The Director's responsibility**: every golden path that leaves your team's hands must have:
1. A named owner (team, not just a person)
2. A deprecation policy (how long will this path be supported?)
3. A change communication plan (how will developers know when the path updates?)
4. A feedback loop (how do developers report that the path is broken?)

Without these four, you have documentation, not a platform.

## Platform as a Product

The most successful platform teams treat the IDP as a product with internal customers. This means:

- **Product roadmap**: what is the platform delivering next quarter, and why?
- **User research**: talking to developers about their pain points, not assuming
- **Versioned releases**: platform changes are announced, not silently deployed
- **Deprecation policies**: old golden paths are sunset on a timeline, with migration support
- **Adoption metrics**: the KPI is not features shipped, it is percentage of services using the standard path

If your platform team doesn't have a product owner — someone whose job is to understand developer needs, prioritize the roadmap, and measure adoption — you are building a feature, not a platform. Features get used by the people who requested them. Platforms get adopted by everyone, or they fail.

## Commercial IDP Landscape (2025)

The three dominant commercial IDPs address different primary pain points:

- **Port.io**: focuses on the developer portal — software catalog, scorecards, self-service actions. Strong if your primary problem is "developers don't know what exists or who owns it."
- **Cortex**: focuses on service quality scoring and engineering ownership clarity. Strong if your primary problem is "we don't know the health of our services or who is responsible for them."
- **OpsLevel**: focuses on service maturity tracking and standards enforcement. Strong if your primary problem is "teams are at different maturity levels and we can't see or close the gaps."

Evaluate against your primary pain point, not against feature count.

## The Force Multiplier Frame

A 5-person platform team serving 200 developers is a 40x leverage play — theoretically. In practice, the leverage is realized only if the platform works and developers actually use it. A platform with 40% adoption is a 16x leverage play. A platform with 20% adoption is an 8x leverage play that costs the same as the 40x one.

Adoption is the metric. Not features shipped. Not plugins written. Not golden paths documented.

When presenting a platform investment to leadership, the question is not "how many capabilities will we build?" The question is "what percentage of developer workflows will this standardize, and what does that save us in toil, incidents, and onboarding time?"
