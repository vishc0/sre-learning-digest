# Chapter 7: Vendor Lock-In Risk Management

## Lock-In Is Inevitable — That's Not the Problem

Every vendor relationship creates lock-in. You adopt their API, your team learns their tooling, your data lands in their format, your engineers get certified in their ecosystem. The moment you run your first workload on a vendor's platform, you have begun accumulating switching cost.

This is not a failure of strategy. It is the nature of any technology commitment. The goal is not zero lock-in — that is operationally impossible and would produce paralysis. The goal is managed lock-in: accepting switching cost where it is acceptable, preserving exit options where they matter, and never allowing yourself to be so entangled that a vendor can extract arbitrary pricing because the cost of leaving exceeds the cost of paying.

The analogy that works: lock-in is like a long-term apartment lease. The lease itself is not the problem — it gives you stability and often a better rate. The problem arrives when the apartment no longer fits your needs, the landlord raises rent by 40%, and you discover that moving costs so much — in time, money, and disruption — that you have no real choice. The lock-in you managed poorly is the lease that had no termination clause, no rent cap, and required you to leave your furniture behind.

## Lock-In Taxonomy: Four Types, Four Different Mitigations

Not all lock-in is the same. Directors who treat it as a single category make poor mitigation choices. There are four distinct types, each with a different source and a different countermeasure.

### API Lock-In
Your code calls vendor-specific APIs. The calls are not portable. Switching vendors requires rewriting the integration layer.

**Example**: A service built directly against DynamoDB's proprietary SDK — `PutItem`, `GetItem`, `QueryFilter` with DynamoDB expression syntax. Migrating to a different database requires rewriting every data access call. Similarly, SQS-specific SDK patterns for message handling don't translate directly to RabbitMQ or Kafka.

**Mitigation**: Abstraction interfaces. Write your application against an interface you control — `MessageBroker.publish()`, `DataStore.get()` — and implement that interface against the vendor. The interface is yours; the vendor implementation is pluggable. At T-Mobile scale with RabbitMQ and Kafka both in the estate, this pattern is already partially in use — the broker is interchangeable if the abstraction layer is clean.

### Data Lock-In
Your data is stored in a format only the vendor can read, or extraction is expensive, slow, or lossy. This is the most dangerous form of lock-in because it persists after you've decided to leave.

**Example**: Splunk's proprietary index format means you cannot simply copy the index to another system. Your 3-year Splunk index is not portable. Similarly, Snowflake's proprietary table clustering and micro-partition format is not directly transferable. You can export data, but you lose query optimization metadata and historical statistics.

**Mitigation**: Data export SLA in the contract (see exit clause section below). Standard export formats — Parquet, CSV, ORC — contractually guaranteed. Regular export tests before you need them. The test matters: many organizations discover their export process is broken only when they are under crisis conditions trying to leave a vendor.

### Operational Lock-In
Your team knows how to operate the vendor's platform but lacks equivalent proficiency with alternatives. The switching cost is retraining, not technology.

**Example**: An SRE team that has spent three years mastering CloudWatch — its query language, its alarming model, its dashboard patterns — has significant operational lock-in to AWS. They can operate AWS observability expertly. Migrating to a different cloud means retraining an entire operational practice, not just changing a configuration.

**Mitigation**: Vendor-agnostic skills alongside vendor-specific ones. OpenTelemetry instrumentation is portable; CloudWatch Metrics Insights queries are not. Where you have a choice of building skills around standard or proprietary tooling, invest in the standard. Rotation programs that expose engineers to multiple environments also build institutional resilience.

### People Lock-In
Your engineers are certified in, experienced with, and personally prefer a specific vendor ecosystem. They resist switching — not maliciously, but because humans prefer environments where they are competent.

**Example**: A team of Splunk-certified SREs who have invested years in SPL expertise and built dashboards they're proud of. Proposing a migration to OpenSearch is not just a technology change — it is asking them to become beginners again in a domain where they are currently experts. The resistance is real and legitimate.

**Mitigation**: Build vendor-agnostic skills as a parallel investment. Engineers who understand the observability concepts — sampling, cardinality, aggregation, alerting theory — can transfer to new platforms faster. Rotation programs, cross-vendor exposure, and framing new platforms as career growth rather than replacement of expertise all reduce people lock-in over time.

## Portability Layers as Primary Mitigation

Three mechanisms give you structural portability across vendor relationships:

**Abstraction interfaces**: Write code against interfaces you control. The concrete implementation is the vendor. When the vendor changes, you replace the implementation, not the calling code. This is software engineering discipline applied to vendor strategy.

**Standard APIs**: OpenTelemetry over proprietary APM agents. S3-compatible API over AWS-specific SDKs. SQL over proprietary query languages where possible. Every time you choose a standard API over a vendor-specific one, you are buying optionality. Optionality has value even when you never exercise it.

**Data export guarantees**: Contractually require the vendor to provide all your data in a specified open format on request. Specify the format, the timeline (30 days is reasonable), and the cost (should be zero or nominal). Then test it. An untested export process is a theoretical exit option, not an actual one.

## Exit Clause Negotiation — What Every Contract Needs

These clauses belong in every vendor contract above your materiality threshold. Negotiate for them before signing, not after you've decided to leave.

**Termination for convenience**: You can exit the contract at any time with 90-day notice, without requiring vendor fault. Many vendor contracts only allow exit for cause (vendor breach). Termination for convenience preserves your strategic flexibility.

**Data portability SLA**: The vendor must provide all your data in a specified open format within 30 days of an exit request. Named format. Named timeline. Named penalty for failure.

**No data hostage clauses**: The vendor cannot withhold your data pending billing disputes. Your data is yours even during a commercial disagreement. This clause is non-negotiable.

**Pricing cap**: Maximum annual price increase as a percentage, typically 3–5% CPI-linked. Without this, a vendor can triple pricing at renewal when you are too entangled to leave quickly.

**Transition assistance**: The vendor will provide 90 days of read-only access post-exit to enable migration. This prevents the situation where your license expires and your data becomes inaccessible before migration is complete.

## Multi-Vendor vs. Single-Vendor Strategy

These are genuine trade-offs, not a case where one answer is always right.

**Multi-vendor**: Spread workloads across multiple vendors. Reduces lock-in concentration. Preserves negotiating leverage. Increases operational complexity — you need abstraction layers, your team needs breadth of knowledge, and integration between vendor systems requires ongoing engineering effort.

**Single-vendor**: Optimize deeply with one vendor. Reduces operational complexity. Often unlocks better pricing through volume. Vendor gains pricing leverage at renewal. If that vendor is acquired (Broadcom/VMware) or changes pricing model, you have limited options.

**Director's portfolio view**: Not every capability requires the same strategy. Apply it by capability type:

- **Commodity capabilities** (object storage, basic compute): single-vendor acceptable. Switching cost is real but manageable. If the vendor raises prices, migration is painful but survivable.
- **Strategic capabilities** (observability, deployment platform, data platform): multi-vendor or vendor-agnostic by design. These capabilities touch everything. Lock-in here is systemic lock-in.
- **Emerging capabilities** (AI/ML platforms, eBPF tooling, WebAssembly runtimes): avoid deep commitment until the market stabilizes. Prefer open standards and pluggable implementations.

## The Broadcom/VMware Case

In 2024, Broadcom completed its acquisition of VMware and restructured licensing in ways that produced annual cost increases of 3–10x for many enterprise customers. This became the canonical lock-in failure case.

Directors who approved VMware without exit clauses, without data portability SLAs, and without a TCO model for switching now faced a non-choice: pay the dramatically increased licensing cost or undertake a migration that would cost more than paying for 2–3 more years. That is exactly the position Broadcom engineered. The lever was the migration cost, which Broadcom understood better than the customers did.

The lesson for Directors is not "never use VMware" or "never use a major platform vendor." The lesson is: **the exit cost must be understood and documented at the time of adoption, not at the time of exit.** When you sign the contract, you should be able to answer: "If we wanted to leave in year 3, what would that cost us in engineering time, retraining, data migration, and business disruption?" If that number is unknown, the risk is unquantified — which means it is unmanaged.

## The Lock-In Scorecard

For every active vendor contract above your materiality threshold, maintain a lock-in scorecard. Rate each contract on all four lock-in types on a 1–5 scale (1 = minimal lock-in, portable; 5 = deeply entangled, exit extremely costly).

Any contract scoring 4 or 5 on any single dimension warrants a documented exit plan — not because you plan to exit, but because the discipline of writing the exit plan surfaces what you would need to do and forces you to maintain the conditions (export processes, abstraction layers, documented runbooks) that make exit possible.

Lock-in you have documented and consciously accepted is strategic commitment. Lock-in you discovered only when you needed to leave is a trap.
