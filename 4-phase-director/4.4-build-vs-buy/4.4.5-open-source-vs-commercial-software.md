# Chapter 05: Open Source vs. Commercial Software

## OSS Is Not a Category — It Is a Spectrum

The first mistake organizations make with open source software is treating it as a single category. "We prefer open source" is not a strategy. It is a sentence that could mean a hundred different things depending on which project you are talking about.

Linux and Kubernetes are open source. Elasticsearch (pre-2021) was open source. Terraform was open source. MongoDB changed its license. HashiCorp changed Terraform's license. Redis changed its license. All of these are "open source" in the casual sense, but they sit at radically different points on the risk spectrum — and a Director who conflates them will make consistently wrong adoption decisions.

The meaningful distinction is not open vs. closed. It is **foundation-governed OSS** versus **vendor-backed OSS**.

- **Foundation-governed OSS** (Linux, Kubernetes, Kafka): governed by a neutral foundation (Linux Foundation, CNCF, Apache Software Foundation) with a defined governance model, a contributor community spanning multiple companies, and no single entity with the unilateral right to change the license or redirect the project. These projects have structural protections against the single most dangerous OSS risk: the vendor's business interests diverging from yours.

- **Vendor-backed OSS**: open source code maintained primarily by a single commercial entity that also sells a commercial product built on top of it. Elastic, HashiCorp, Confluent, MongoDB, Redis Labs — all fall here. The code is open, but the commercial entity controls the roadmap, the release schedule, and crucially, the license. When their business model requires monetization pressure, the license is the lever they pull.

Conflating these two categories leads to wrong decisions. The framework below is designed to help you distinguish them consistently.

---

## The "Evaluate OSS First" Principle for Infrastructure

For any infrastructure capability — observability, service mesh, API gateway, secret management, container orchestration, message brokering — start with OSS options. This is not ideological; it is strategic.

The infrastructure OSS ecosystem, particularly the CNCF and Apache Software Foundation portfolios, is mature, production-proven, and enterprise-supported. Projects like Prometheus, Grafana, Istio, Envoy, Kafka, and Kubernetes are running at the largest technology companies in the world. The burden of proof for adopting a commercial alternative over a CNCF-graduated project should be high.

Commercial alternatives may win on: managed service convenience, reduced operational burden, integrated support SLA, or specialized capability not yet in the OSS ecosystem. These are real advantages. But they should be the reason you choose commercial — not the absence of a viable OSS option, which is rarely the case in infrastructure.

## Evaluating OSS Health: What to Look At Before You Adopt

Not all OSS projects are equally safe to adopt. A project with three contributors, one corporate backer, and no governance document carries very different risk than a CNCF-graduated project with 500 contributors across 50 companies.

Before adopting any OSS project into your production infrastructure, evaluate:

**Governance model**: Who makes decisions about the project direction and the license? Single company = concentration risk. Foundation governance with a charter = structural protection. The Linux Foundation's OpenTofu project has a Steering Committee with representation rules specifically designed to prevent any single company from controlling the project — this is what foundation governance looks like in practice.

**CNCF graduation status**: The CNCF has three stages — Sandbox, Incubating, and Graduated. Graduated projects (Kubernetes, Prometheus, Argo, Envoy, Jaeger, Flux) have passed a rigorous review of governance, security, production adoption, and community health. Graduated status is the highest signal of OSS maturity available in the infrastructure space. Sandbox projects are early-stage and experimental — appropriate for evaluation, not production standardization.

**Contributor diversity**: What is the bus factor? If the top three contributors work for the same company and that company gets acquired or pivots, what happens to the project? High contributor diversity across multiple employers is a structural protection against project abandonment.

**Commit frequency and issue response time**: A project with 200 open issues and the last commit six months ago is showing early signs of abandonment. A project with active weekly commits, responsive maintainers, and a clear release cadence is healthy.

**License**: Apache 2.0 and MIT are permissive — you can use, modify, and redistribute with minimal restrictions. AGPL (GNU Affero GPL) restricts network use of modified versions. BSL (Business Source License) and SSPL (Server Side Public License) are not open source by OSI definition and carry commercial use restrictions. Know what you are adopting before you adopt it.

## The HashiCorp/Terraform BSL Case: 2023

In August 2023, HashiCorp announced that Terraform — the most widely adopted infrastructure-as-code tool in the industry — would change its license from the Mozilla Public License 2.0 (MPL, an OSI-approved open source license) to the Business Source License (BSL 1.1, not an OSI-approved open source license).

The practical impact: organizations using Terraform in products or services that compete with HashiCorp were now in license violation. Cloud providers, managed service vendors, and IaC platform builders were directly affected. The broader community effect was a crisis of confidence in the entire HashiCorp portfolio — including Vault, Consul, and Nomad, all of which received the same license change.

The community response was swift: the OpenTofu project was forked from the last MPL-licensed version of Terraform and placed under the Linux Foundation. OpenTofu is now the community-governed alternative, developed by a Steering Committee with representation from multiple companies.

For organizations that had standardized on Terraform, this created a three-way choice: (1) remain on BSL Terraform and accept the vendor dependency and license restrictions; (2) migrate to OpenTofu, which is less mature and carries the risks of any fork; or (3) freeze on the last MPL version and accumulate security debt as new patches only apply to BSL Terraform and OpenTofu.

None of these options are free. All three carry cost. The cost was imposed by a license change that the affected organizations had no vote on and no advance notice of.

This is what vendor-backed OSS risk looks like when it materializes.

## Commercial OSS Lock-in Taxonomy

The HashiCorp case is not unique. It is one data point in a consistent pattern:

| Project | Event | Year | Community Response | Director Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Elasticsearch | License change to SSPL (non-OSS) | 2021 | Amazon forked as OpenSearch; Elastic retained name | Orgs on managed ES had to choose Elastic Cloud or OpenSearch |
| HashiCorp / Terraform | License change to BSL | 2023 | OpenTofu fork under Linux Foundation | Three-way choice; no clean option |
| Redis | License change to RSAL + SSPL | 2024 | Valkey fork under Linux Foundation | Managed Redis providers migrated to Valkey |
| CentOS / RHEL | CentOS EOL announced 2020, effective 2024 | 2020-2024 | Rocky Linux, AlmaLinux forks emerged | Orgs on CentOS had to evaluate RHEL, Rocky, or Alma |
| Confluent / Kafka | Kafka core remains Apache; Confluent Platform adds proprietary features | Ongoing | N/A — dual track | Proprietary feature adoption = Confluent lock-in |

The Confluent/Kafka row deserves specific attention because it illustrates a subtler form of lock-in. Apache Kafka itself is Apache-licensed, foundation-governed, and structurally safe. Confluent sells Confluent Platform, which adds proprietary connectors, schema registry features, and management tooling on top of Apache Kafka. Organizations that adopt only Apache Kafka remain portable. Organizations that build integrations against Confluent-specific APIs and proprietary connectors have created lock-in while believing they were running "open source Kafka."

The proprietary extension trap is the commercial OSS vendor's preferred monetization strategy. It is opt-in. And it is easy to opt into incrementally, one convenient feature at a time, until exit is expensive.

## Fork Risk: When the Community Splits, Your Investment Splits

When a widely-adopted OSS project forks — as Terraform did into OpenTofu, as Elasticsearch did into OpenSearch — you face a compulsory decision: which fork do you follow?

Neither fork has the full history of community trust of the original project. The fork is younger, has fewer contributors in its early months, has less tooling ecosystem coverage, and carries uncertainty about long-term sustainability. The original project has the name recognition and the commercial vendor behind it, but now carries license risk.

Your team's existing investment in tooling, training, and automation was built against the pre-fork project. That investment now has to be re-evaluated against whichever fork you choose.

Fork events are disruptive and expensive. The organizations least affected by the Terraform BSL change were the ones that had built against the pure OSS Terraform interface — no HCP Terraform-specific features, no HashiCorp-proprietary APIs. Their migration path to OpenTofu was straightforward because they had not accumulated proprietary extension debt.

## Mitigating Commercial OSS Risk: The Operating Rules

1. **Prefer foundation-governed OSS for anything you intend to standardize on**. CNCF graduated projects are the highest confidence tier. Apache Software Foundation projects are the second tier. Single-vendor OSS with no foundation governance is a higher-risk adoption requiring explicit justification.

2. **Check the license before you adopt, not after**. Apache 2.0 and MIT are permissive and safe for enterprise adoption. AGPL, SSPL, and BSL carry restrictions that matter at scale. Add license verification to your technology radar review process — not as a one-time check, but as an ongoing audit.

3. **Use only the OSS components of vendor-backed OSS projects**. The Confluent rule: run Apache Kafka, not Confluent Platform. Use the community-licensed features. Avoid proprietary extensions, SDKs, and APIs that create integration lock-in while the underlying project remains technically open.

4. **Monitor for license changes**. License changes do not come with advance notice proportional to their impact. Add license monitoring to your quarterly technology radar review. Services like FOSSA and Snyk license scanning can alert you to dependency license changes automatically.

5. **Maintain exit options**. Before standardizing on any OSS project — foundation-governed or otherwise — document what exit looks like. What is the viable fork? What is the commercial alternative? What is the migration path? A Director who cannot answer these questions for their five most critical OSS dependencies has unmapped strategic risk in their portfolio.

## T-Mobile Context: You Are Managing an OSS Portfolio

At T-Mobile's scale, the decision is not "should we use Kafka?" The decision is "what is our OSS portfolio posture, and who owns the license and governance risk for each critical dependency?"

Kafka (Apache, foundation-governed), Terraform (BSL, fork available as OpenTofu), Kubernetes (CNCF graduated, highest governance tier), Prometheus (CNCF graduated), Splunk (commercial, not OSS) — each of these sits at a different point on the risk spectrum. A Director at this scale is managing an OSS portfolio with real financial and operational exposure to license risk, fork events, and vendor pricing decisions.

The discipline is the same as financial portfolio management: know what you own, know the risk of each position, and maintain diversification and exit options against your highest-exposure dependencies.
