# Chapter 02: The Strategic Capability Test

## Not All Capabilities Are Created Equal

The single most important question in any build vs. buy decision is not "how much does it cost?" or "how long will it take?" It is: **does this capability differentiate us, or does it merely enable us?**

Michael Porter's value chain — developed for product companies but directly applicable to technology leadership — separates activities into two buckets. Support activities are infrastructure: HR, legal, finance, procurement. They are necessary but create no direct competitive advantage. Primary activities are the work that actually produces value for customers: product development, customer experience, operational reliability at scale.

The same logic applies to technology capabilities. Some technology is support infrastructure — it must exist for the business to function, but having it doesn't make you better than a competitor who also has it. Some technology is a primary differentiator — it makes you faster, cheaper, more reliable, or more capable than competitors in ways they cannot easily replicate.

A Director who cannot classify a capability into one of these buckets before making a build/buy decision is guessing.

---

## The Canonical Test

Before any build vs. buy discussion, ask this question out loud:

> **"If a competitor paid exactly what we paid for this tool, would they gain the same strategic advantage we would?"**

If the answer is **yes** — it is a commodity. Buy it. Your advantage is not in the capability itself; it is in how you use it, how fast you deploy it, and how much engineering you free up by not building it.

If the answer is **no** — it may be a differentiator. The capability only creates value when combined with something your competitors cannot purchase: your proprietary data, your engineering culture, your specific operational context. This is worth examining as a build candidate.

Note the "may be." Passing this test is necessary but not sufficient to justify building. You still need to answer: can we build it, can we maintain it, and is the build cost lower than the strategic cost of buying it?

## Applied to T-Mobile / Telecom SRE Context

The classification changes based on who you are and what market you compete in. Here is how it maps for a telecom-scale SRE organization.

### Commodity for Everyone — Always Buy

**Ticketing and ITSM**: ServiceNow is the interchange everyone uses. The competitive advantage is not in the ticket system; it is in what you do with the data inside it. Build integrations; do not rebuild the platform.

**HR and identity systems**: SSO, IAM, Active Directory, Workday. No organization differentiates on how employees log in. Buy every component. Customize at the edge only.

**Source control and CI/CD primitives**: GitHub, GitLab CI, GitHub Actions. These are commodity development infrastructure. Building your own SCM or pipeline engine is a capital-I Infrastructure mistake. The engineering hours are worth more elsewhere.

**Incident management**: PagerDuty, OpsGenie. The routing algorithm that wakes someone up at 2 AM is not your competitive advantage. The mean time to detect and the mean time to recover — and the operational culture that drives them — are. Buy the tool; invest in the process.

### Commodity for Most, Differentiator for Some — Know Which You Are

**Observability platforms**: For most companies, Splunk, Datadog, or Dynatrace is the correct answer. The platform is well-engineered, the cost is justified, and building a replacement is a multi-year distraction.

But for a company where SRE operational excellence IS the brand promise — a fintech where 99.999% availability is what customers are paying for, a telecom where network reliability is the marketing message — the observability pipeline may be worth building proprietary components around. Not the entire stack. Specific capabilities: custom signal correlation that vendors cannot model because it depends on your internal topology, anomaly detection trained on your traffic patterns in ways a generic model cannot replicate.

The question is not "is observability important?" The question is "is our observability better than what we could buy, and does that advantage translate to customer outcomes?" If yes, build the parts that create the delta. If no, buy the commodity and invest in the process.

**Deployment platforms and internal developer platforms**: If developer velocity is your stated competitive strategy — if your VP of Engineering has committed to 10x faster deployments as a market differentiator — then an internal developer platform (IDP) may be worth building. If your competitors also use Backstage and your deployment velocity is comparable to theirs, you have not differentiated. You have built a commodity with engineering labor instead of purchasing it.

### True Differentiators — Protect and Potentially Build

The network routing algorithm that determines T-Mobile's latency profile. The churn prediction model trained on 90 million subscribers' behavioral data. The capacity planning model that determines where to pre-position infrastructure before demand spikes. The internal tooling that lets engineers navigate a 50,000-node Kubernetes fleet without getting lost.

These capabilities have two properties: they depend on proprietary data or context that competitors cannot purchase, and they directly affect customer outcomes in measurable ways. These are worth protecting. In some cases, worth building. Always worth keeping out of vendor platforms.

## The Two Traps

### The Build-to-Learn Trap

"We should build this ourselves. The team will learn so much about distributed systems."

This argument appears frequently in engineering organizations. It is not wrong that learning has value. It is wrong as a primary justification for a multi-year capital commitment. Learning is an input; it is not a business outcome.

Build to learn when the learning IS the strategic asset. Security research, novel ML architectures, experimental infrastructure patterns — if the organization intends to become a center of excellence in the capability and that expertise creates competitive advantage, the learning justifies the investment.

Do not build to learn when a vendor has already solved the problem and the capability is not a differentiator. You will pay twice: once in engineering cost, once in the opportunity cost of problems you did not solve while you were learning.

### The Buy-to-Avoid-Complexity Trap

"We should just buy this. Building it is too complex."

Complexity is not a reason to buy. Sometimes complexity is the moat. If a capability is complex to build and competitors cannot easily replicate it, that complexity is protecting your competitive advantage. Outsourcing it to a vendor erases the moat.

Buy to avoid complexity when the complexity creates no competitive advantage — when the complexity is incidental (ITSM workflow management, identity federation, SAML integration) rather than essential (proprietary signal processing, domain-specific ML models, network topology management).

## The Capability Classification Matrix

Before any build/buy decision, place the capability in one of these quadrants. The quadrant determines the direction of analysis — not the final answer, but the right frame for the conversation.

| | **Commodity** | **Differentiator** |
|---|---|---|
| **Buy** | Safe default. Vendor handles maintenance, roadmap, reliability. Invest savings in differentiators. | Trap. You have outsourced your competitive advantage. Vendor owns your moat. |
| **Build** | Trap. You are spending engineering capital on a problem the market has already solved. You own the maintenance forever. | Worth examining. Validate build cost, maintenance capacity, and strategic value before committing. |

The upper-left (Buy a Commodity) is the default for most capabilities. The lower-right (Build a Differentiator) is the category that requires the most rigorous analysis before approving. The other two quadrants are where Director careers get damaged.

---

## How a Director Uses This Test in Practice

Before any build/buy review meeting, require one sentence in the briefing document:

> "This capability is a **[commodity / differentiator]** because **[specific reason]**."

If the team cannot write that sentence, the decision is not ready to review. Send it back. The inability to classify the capability is a signal that the strategic analysis has not been done — and approving a capital commitment without it is how Directors end up defending bad decisions to their VP six months later.

When you can write the sentence, the rest of the decision framework — TCO modeling, vendor evaluation, lock-in risk assessment — flows from it. The classification is the first gate. Everything else is analysis in support of a direction you have already established.
