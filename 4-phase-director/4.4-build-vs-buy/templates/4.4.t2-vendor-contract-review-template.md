# Vendor Contract Review Checklist

> **Instructions for use**: Complete this checklist before signing any vendor contract. Every unchecked item is a risk that must be consciously accepted or negotiated before signature. Items marked CRITICAL must be negotiated — do not sign with these outstanding. Route completed checklist to the Decision Owner and Legal before contract execution.

## Section 1 — SLA Terms

> SLA terms define what the vendor promises to deliver and what you receive if they fail. Vague SLA terms favor the vendor.

- [ ] **Uptime SLA defined**: Vendor commits to [___% uptime]. *Fill in the exact percentage. 99.9% = 8.7 hours downtime/year. 99.5% = 43.8 hours/year. Know the difference before accepting.*
- [ ] **SLA measurement window**: Uptime is measured [monthly / quarterly / annually]. *Monthly measurement is vendor-favorable (resets frequently). Annual is customer-favorable. Monthly is industry standard for SaaS — but know this means each month's SLA is assessed independently.*
- [ ] **SLA credits defined**: Breach results in [___% of monthly fee] credit per [___% of downtime below SLA]. *Credits must be automatic (triggered by breach) or invoicing-required (you must request them — most customers never do). Confirm which.*
- [ ] **Maintenance windows**: Scheduled maintenance [is / is not] excluded from SLA calculation. If excluded: scheduled during [defined hours: ___]. *Vendors who exclude maintenance windows with no time limits can hide downtime as "scheduled maintenance."*
- [ ] **Support response SLAs defined**:
  - P1 (production down / data loss risk): response within [___ hours], resolution target within [___ hours]
  - P2 (significant degradation, workaround available): response within [___ hours]
  - P3 (non-critical, general inquiry): response within [___ business days]
- [ ] **Escalation path defined**: Named vendor contacts for P1 incidents [Yes / No]. *If the contract does not name escalation contacts, you will be routed through general support during a production outage.*
- [ ] **SLA remedies are your sole remedy** clause present: [Yes / No]. *If yes, this clause limits your legal remedy to SLA credits only — you cannot sue for damages beyond the credit value. Flag this for legal review.*

**SLA Notes**: [Document any SLA terms that are non-standard, negotiated, or that require monitoring post-signature]

## Section 2 — Data Portability

> Data portability determines your ability to exit. Assess this section as if you were planning to exit the day after signing — because one day, you will be.

- [ ] **Data export format specified**: Data is available in [CSV / JSON / Parquet / SQL dump / Proprietary format — specify]. *Open standard formats (CSV, JSON, Parquet) are portable. Proprietary formats require transformation effort on exit.*
- [ ] **Data export SLA**: Vendor provides complete data export within [___ business days] of written request. *Standard is 30 days or fewer. More than 60 days is a red flag.*
- [ ] **No data hostage clause** (CRITICAL): Contract explicitly states vendor will not withhold data during billing disputes or contract termination proceedings. [Yes / No — if No, negotiate this clause before signing.] *Without this clause, a vendor can legally withhold your data if you dispute an invoice. This has happened.*
- [ ] **Data retention post-exit**: Vendor retains your data for [___ days] after contract end before deletion. *30–90 days is standard. Too short: you may not complete migration before data is deleted. Too long: raises data governance questions.*
- [ ] **Data export tested pre-signature**: A test export was initiated and validated before contract was signed. [Yes / No]. *For any vendor holding significant data, this is strongly recommended. Discovering that data export is broken after signing eliminates your negotiating leverage.*
- [ ] **Data storage location**: Data is stored in [region/jurisdiction]. *Relevant for compliance — GDPR requires EU data to stay in EU; some regulated industries require US-only storage.*
- [ ] **Subprocessor list**: Vendor has provided list of subprocessors (third parties they share data with). [Yes / No — required for GDPR/CCPA compliance if processing PII]*

## Section 3 — Exit Clauses

> Exit clauses protect you from being trapped. Negotiate these before signing, not when you want to leave.

- [ ] **Termination for convenience**: You can exit the contract with [___ days] written notice, without cause, [with / without] penalty. *60–90 days is standard. More than 180 days is restrictive. "Without cause" is critical — you should not need to prove the vendor failed in order to exit.*
- [ ] **Early termination penalty**: [None / Yes — penalty is [describe the structure: X months of remaining fees, X% of contract value, etc.]] *Document the exact penalty structure. "Early termination fees apply" is not a contract term.*
- [ ] **Transition assistance**: Vendor provides [___ days] of [read-only access / full access / export assistance] after contract termination. *Standard is 30–60 days read-only access for data retrieval. Absence of transition assistance is a flag.*
- [ ] **IP and data ownership**: All data, configurations, and custom outputs generated during the contract remain the property of [your organization]. [Yes / No — if No, negotiate.] *Some vendors claim ownership of aggregate or derived data. Read carefully.*
- [ ] **No anti-competitive exit restrictions**: Contract does not prohibit engaging with named competitors for [___ months] post-exit. [Yes (no restriction) / No (restriction exists — describe)] *Vendor contracts with post-exit competitive restrictions are rare but do exist.*

**Exit Clause Notes**: [Document any exit terms that required negotiation, what was conceded, and what was achieved]

## Section 4 — Pricing Escalation Provisions

> Pricing protection prevents the "we bought it for $X and it's now $3X" scenario that triggers an unplanned exit assessment.

- [ ] **Annual price increase cap**: Maximum annual increase is [___% or CPI, whichever is lower]. *Without a cap, vendors can raise pricing at renewal with no limit. 3–5% cap is achievable for established vendors. CPI-linked cap is appropriate when CPI is low; fix to a percentage when CPI is high.*
- [ ] **Pricing model change protection**: Vendor cannot change the fundamental pricing model (e.g., from seat-based to consumption-based) during the contract term without mutual agreement. [Yes / No] *Pricing model changes are a common way vendors circumvent price caps.*
- [ ] **Volume discount locked**: Agreed volume pricing tiers hold for the full contract term. [Yes / No] *If you negotiated a discount for committing to a volume, confirm it is locked in writing.*
- [ ] **True-up provisions defined**: If usage exceeds contracted volume, overage pricing is [defined rate: $___ per unit]. *"Overage pricing at our standard rates" is not a defined rate. Negotiate a specific overage price before signing.*
- [ ] **Price hold for renewal negotiation**: Vendor will hold current pricing for [___ days] during renewal negotiation period. *Without a hold, the vendor can raise prices the day the contract expires if you have not yet signed a renewal.*

**Pricing Notes**: [Document the negotiated pricing terms, any concessions made, and the annual pricing trajectory expected over the contract term]

## Section 5 — Audit Rights

> Audit rights let you verify vendor claims about security, compliance, and usage. Without them, you are trusting without verifying.

- [ ] **Right to audit vendor security posture**: Contract grants the right to audit vendor's security controls annually, on [___ days] notice. [Yes / No] *For high-security or compliance-relevant vendors, direct audit rights are important. Many vendors substitute SOC 2 reports for direct audits — acceptable if the SOC 2 scope covers your use case.*
- [ ] **SOC 2 Type II report provided annually**: Vendor commits to providing current SOC 2 Type II report within [___ days] of request. [Yes / No — Type II covers a period of operation, unlike Type I which is a point-in-time snapshot.] *Type II is the meaningful certification. Type I is a point-in-time snapshot.*
- [ ] **Penetration test results available**: Vendor shares summary results of annual penetration tests. [Yes / No] *Full report is rarely shared; summary findings with remediation status is a reasonable standard.*
- [ ] **Data Processing Agreement (DPA) in place**: [Yes / No — required for any vendor processing PII, PHI, or financial data under GDPR, CCPA, or HIPAA.] *Absence of a DPA for a data-processing vendor is a legal compliance gap, not just a contractual preference.*
- [ ] **Incident notification SLA**: Vendor notifies you of security incidents affecting your data within [___ hours]. *72 hours is the GDPR standard for notification to regulators; vendor notification to you should be at or before that window.*
- [ ] **Compliance certifications**: Vendor holds [ISO 27001 / SOC 2 Type II / FedRAMP / HIPAA BAA / PCI-DSS — check applicable]. Current certification expiry: [date].

**Audit Rights Notes**: [Document any gaps in audit rights, how they were addressed (or why they were accepted), and the compliance certification status]

## Section 6 — Liability and Indemnification

> Liability terms define who is financially responsible when things go wrong. Read this section carefully — it is frequently the most negotiated section in enterprise contracts.

- [ ] **Vendor liability cap**: Vendor's total liability is capped at [___ × annual contract value]. *1x annual contract value is standard for SaaS. For higher-risk vendors (security, financial data), negotiate 2x. Caps below 1x are unusual and require legal review.*
- [ ] **Consequential damages waiver**: Contract [does / does not] include a mutual waiver of consequential damages (lost profits, business interruption). *Most SaaS contracts waive consequential damages for both parties. This is standard. Review carefully if the waiver is one-sided (vendor waives your consequential damages but retains theirs).*
- [ ] **Data breach indemnification**: Vendor indemnifies your organization for breach notification costs, regulatory fines, and customer claims arising from a breach caused by the vendor's systems. [Yes / No — if No, negotiate for at minimum breach notification cost coverage.] *Without this clause, you absorb the cost of a breach you did not cause.*
- [ ] **IP indemnification**: Vendor indemnifies your organization if their software is found to infringe third-party intellectual property. [Yes / No] *This is standard in enterprise software contracts. Absence is unusual and should be flagged to legal.*
- [ ] **Force majeure scope**: Force majeure clause [is / is not] limited to truly extraordinary events (war, natural disaster). *Broad force majeure clauses that include "internet outages" or "supply chain disruptions" allow vendors to escape SLA obligations under routine circumstances.*

**Liability Notes**: [Document any liability terms that deviate from standard, any indemnification gaps accepted, and any terms requiring ongoing legal monitoring]
