# Build vs. Buy Decision Matrix — Quick Reference

**Purpose**: One-page cheat sheet for rapid orientation on a build vs. buy question. Use this to quickly calibrate the direction of an evaluation, then apply the full playbook.

---

## Primary Decision Matrix

*Rows: Capability Type. Columns: Vendor Market Maturity.*

| | **Mature Market — Proven vendors exist with production references at your scale** | **Emerging Market — No proven vendor; solutions are early-stage or missing** |
|---|---|---|
| **Commodity** — competitors can buy the same solution and get the same strategic position | **Buy** Vendor market has solved this. Build would be reinventing a wheel. Risk: LOW. Note: evaluate OSS as a cost-saving alternative before committing to vendor spend. | **Build or OSS** No mature vendor exists, but this is still a commodity — build the minimum viable version and plan to replace with a vendor solution as the market matures. Risk: MEDIUM. Lock in a technology review at 18 months. |
| **Differentiator** — your specific implementation creates strategic advantage a competitor cannot replicate by paying the same vendor price | **Evaluate carefully** Vendor solutions exist but may commoditize your advantage. Buy only if the vendor solution can be extended or configured to maintain differentiation. If not, Build. Risk: MEDIUM–HIGH depending on vendor configurability. | **Build** No vendor can solve this because the market hasn't matured, and your implementation is a differentiator. This is the strongest justification for a build. Risk: HIGH (delivery and maintenance). Requires named team and realistic TCO model. |

## Complexity Overlay Matrix

*Use when the primary matrix points toward build but the team needs a reality check on whether the build is feasible.*

*Rows: Integration Complexity (how hard is it to connect this to your existing systems?). Columns: Build Complexity (how hard is it to build the core capability?)*

| | **Low Build Complexity — standard problem with well-understood implementation patterns** | **High Build Complexity — novel problem, research required, or performance at scale is unsolved** |
|---|---|---|
| **Low Integration Complexity** — standard protocols; fits cleanly into existing stack | **Build or Buy** — both options are viable. Decision is driven by TCO model and strategic classification. Run the numbers. | **Caution on Build** — the core capability is hard; do not underestimate. Get a realistic scoping estimate from engineers who have done similar work, not the engineers who want to build it. |
| **High Integration Complexity** — proprietary internal systems, unusual protocols, compliance constraints on data movement | **Caution on Buy** — vendor solutions typically assume standard integration patterns. High integration complexity often means significant professional services cost on top of license. Get a realistic integration estimate before accepting vendor TCO. | **Strong caution on Build** — both the capability and the integration are hard. This is where build proposals routinely underestimate by 3–5x. Require a PoC that specifically tests the hard integration before approving the build. |

## Quick Decision Rules

Apply these rules as fast filters before deeper analysis. Any rule that applies is an input to the decision, not a final answer on its own.

- **If a vendor solves it and it's a commodity → Buy**. The market has solved commodity problems. Your build adds cost, not strategic value. The burden of proof is on the build.

- **If no vendor solves it adequately → Build or OSS**. Document what "adequately" means — three vendors evaluated with specific gaps identified. "We prefer to build" is not a gap analysis.

- **If OSS exists with CNCF graduation or Apache Software Foundation governance → Evaluate OSS first**. CNCF-graduated and ASF-governed projects have community governance that reduces single-vendor abandonment risk. OSS is frequently the lowest-TCO option for commodity infrastructure tooling.

- **If build TCO break-even > 3 years → Strong presumption to Buy**. If it takes more than 3 years for the build to become cheaper than the buy alternative, you need a strong strategic or compliance reason to override. "We want control" is not sufficient.

- **If you can't name the engineer who maintains it in Year 2 → Do not Build**. A build plan without a named maintenance owner is a wish. Do not approve a build proposal that cannot answer this question.

- **If vendor has no data portability clause → Negotiate before signing or walk away**. Signing a vendor contract with no data portability clause means you are renting your own data. This is the single most common source of vendor leverage over customers at renewal.

- **If vendor was recently acquired → Re-evaluate TCO and lock-in risk immediately**. Acquisitions change vendor roadmap, pricing strategy, and support investment. Run the exit assessment playbook within 60 days of acquisition announcement, regardless of whether you currently have a concern.

- **If you've already bought it and the vendor raises price >20% → Run exit assessment playbook**. A >20% price increase is a forcing function. Run the exit cost model before accepting or rejecting the increase — the exit cost may make accepting the increase the right decision, or it may not.

## Signal Table

| Signs That Point Toward **Build** | Signs That Point Toward **Buy** |
|----------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| The capability requires your proprietary data or operational context to function — no vendor can replicate it | Multiple vendors have production references at your scale for this exact use case |
| No vendor has a solution that meets your must-have requirements after serious market evaluation | Your team cannot name who will maintain the build in Year 2 |
| Regulatory or compliance requirements prohibit sending data to an external vendor | Build TCO break-even is beyond 3 years against available vendor options |
| The team has shipped comparable scope on time in the past 12 months and has available capacity | The capability is a commodity — a competitor buying the vendor solution gets the same strategic position |
| Build TCO is materially lower than buy alternatives within a 2–3 year horizon | OSS project with CNCF graduation or strong foundation governance exists in this category |
| Vendor market is immature and you need the capability now | The proposing team's engineers are already at >80% utilization |

## Common Mistakes Table

| Mistake | What It Costs | How to Avoid |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Modeling only Year 1 build cost; ignoring maintenance | Maintenance typically runs 20–30% of build cost per year — a 3-year build TCO that omits Years 2–3 maintenance understates cost by 40–60% | Require TCO model that explicitly includes Year 2 and Year 3 maintenance estimates with named owners |
| Signing a vendor contract with no price increase cap | Vendors raise prices 15–30% at renewal once switching cost is established; 3-year TCO model is invalidated | Negotiate annual price increase cap (typically 3–5%) before signing. Walk away if vendor refuses on a multi-year contract. |
| Running a build PoC with the team that proposed the build | The team building the PoC has confirmation bias; they will make it work by any means necessary, which is not what production will look like | Assign a neutral engineer (or external reviewer) to assess the PoC against production-readiness criteria |
| Treating OSS as free | OSS license is free; implementation, integration, maintenance, and security patching are not. Total OSS TCO is often higher than a vendor solution when properly modeled | Model OSS TCO with the same rigor as vendor TCO — implementation + maintenance + oncall + security patching |
| Evaluating vendors on features, not on integration fit | A feature-complete vendor that integrates poorly with your stack has a much higher true cost than a simpler vendor that integrates cleanly | Run PoC against your hardest integration target specifically, not against the vendor's happy path demo scenario |
| Accepting only vendor-provided references | Vendor references are curated success stories; they are useful but incomplete. The customers who had a difficult experience are never on the vendor's reference list | Find at least one independently sourced reference customer for any finalist vendor — LinkedIn, Slack communities, or your peer network |
