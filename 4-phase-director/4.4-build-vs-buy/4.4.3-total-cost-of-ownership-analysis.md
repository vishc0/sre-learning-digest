# Chapter 03: Total Cost of Ownership Analysis

## The Number on the Proposal Is Not the Cost

When a vendor hands you a proposal, the number on the cover page is the cost of *acquiring* the software. It is not the cost of *owning* it. Directors who approve build-or-buy decisions based on acquisition cost alone are making a category error — the same one a first-time homebuyer makes when they look only at the monthly mortgage payment.

The mortgage gets you into the house. Property taxes, maintenance, HOA fees, homeowner's insurance, and the opportunity cost on your down payment are the actual cost of homeownership. A $400K house with a $2,200/month mortgage costs significantly more than $2,200/month. Software works the same way: the license is the mortgage, and everything else is the hidden bill that arrives every quarter for the next five years.

Industry data consistently shows that over a five-year horizon, the total cost of owning software runs 3-5x the initial acquisition or development cost. The initial number is the entry fee. TCO is the rent you pay to stay.

## Build TCO: What You Actually Pay When You Build

Building software feels like control. And it is — but control is not free. The cost components for a build decision are:

**Development cost** is the most visible line item but is routinely under-estimated. A fully loaded engineer (salary, benefits, equity, overhead, tooling, recruiting amortized) runs $15,000–$25,000 per month at most technology companies. A team of four engineers building an internal observability platform for six months is a $360K–$600K investment before anyone deploys a single line of production code.

**Ongoing maintenance** is the line item that kills internal tool budgets. The industry benchmark is 20-30% of initial build cost per year in maintenance: bug fixes, security patch responses, dependency updates as the ecosystem moves, and compatibility work as the rest of your stack evolves. The platform you built for $600K costs $120K–$180K/year to keep running — without adding a single new feature.

**Documentation and onboarding** is almost always skipped in the initial estimate. It always costs you later. When the engineer who built the tool leaves, the undocumented system becomes a liability overnight. The cost of reconstructing intent from code and tribal knowledge often equals 25-50% of the original build cost.

**On-call burden** is real and often invisible in financial models. Every internal tool needs someone paged at 2am. That on-call rotation is engineer time, morale cost, and retention risk. It does not appear in the build estimate, but it appears in attrition.

**Opportunity cost** is the most important and least-discussed component of build TCO. When four engineers spend six months building an internal observability platform, they are not building the next product capability, not reducing technical debt in the revenue-generating system, and not improving developer productivity elsewhere. Opportunity cost is not a soft number — it is a hard strategic cost.

## Buy TCO: What the Proposal Leaves Out

The vendor proposal includes the license fee, sometimes the implementation fee, and occasionally the training cost. It does not include:

**Implementation cost** is consistently underestimated. Systems integrator (SI) fees and internal engineer time to integrate the vendor product into your environment — identity, networking, monitoring, CI/CD, existing APIs — frequently equal or exceed the license cost in the first year.

**Training and certification** is real money. Enterprise software has learning curves. Certifying your team, running vendor training, and productivity loss during the ramp period are measurable costs that belong in the TCO model.

**Ongoing vendor support tier cost** is the maintenance contract. Most enterprise software licenses come with a base support tier. The support tier that actually resolves a P1 incident within four hours is a premium add-on.

**Integration maintenance** is the recurring cost of a buy decision that almost never appears in initial models. Every time the vendor releases a major version, every point integration you built against their API must be tested, potentially adapted, and redeployed. You pay to adapt to their schedule, not yours.

**Exit cost** is the most dangerous omission. What does it cost to leave this vendor in year four if the economics stop making sense? Data migration, replacement evaluation, re-training, contract penalties, and productivity loss during transition are all real costs. Most TCO models exclude them. This is the mistake that bit every VMware customer in 2024.

## The VMware/Broadcom Lesson

A Director who approved VMware vSphere in 2020 ran a TCO model. License costs were known. Support tiers were known. The model looked defensible. What the model did not include: exit cost in a scenario where the vendor's pricing structure changed materially.

In 2024, Broadcom acquired VMware and restructured licensing. Per-CPU perpetual licenses became per-core subscription bundles. Organizations with no exit clause and no exit cost in their TCO model faced licensing increases of 3-5x. The exit cost — migrating workloads off VMware, retraining operations teams, replacing VMware-integrated tooling, and rebuilding monitoring integrations — ran into millions for enterprise-scale deployments.

The 2020 TCO model was not wrong. It was incomplete. It never modeled the cost of being wrong about vendor stability.

## TCO Comparison: Observability Platform (3-Year Model)

The following is a directional model. Precision is not the goal; order-of-magnitude accuracy is. A model accurate to ±30% is sufficient to distinguish a $500K decision from a $5M decision.

| Cost Component | Build (Internal) | Buy (Commercial SaaS) |
|---|---|---|
| **Year 1: Initial cost** | | |
| Development / License | $480,000 (4 eng × 6 mo) | $180,000/year |
| Implementation / Integration | $60,000 | $220,000 (SI + internal) |
| Training / Documentation | $20,000 | $35,000 |
| **Year 1 Total** | **$560,000** | **$435,000** |
| **Year 2** | | |
| Maintenance (build: 25% of build cost) | $120,000 | — |
| License renewal (buy: +8% YoY) | — | $194,400 |
| Support tier | $0 (internal) | $30,000 |
| Integration maintenance | $20,000 | $25,000 |
| **Year 2 Total** | **$140,000** | **$249,400** |
| **Year 3** | | |
| Maintenance | $120,000 | — |
| License renewal | — | $209,952 |
| Support tier | $0 | $30,000 |
| Integration maintenance | $20,000 | $30,000 |
| **Year 3 Total** | **$140,000** | **$269,952** |
| **3-Year TCO** | **$840,000** | **$954,352** |
| **Exit cost (not modeled above)** | Migration to commercial: $50-150K | Vendor switch: $200-500K |

**Crossover point**: In this model, buy wins in year one. Build catches up by mid-year three. At year five, build is likely cheaper on a marginal-cost basis — but only if the team that built it is still present and the system has not accumulated significant maintenance debt.

The crossover point is the key decision variable. If your planning horizon is two years, buy. If it is five-plus years and you have the engineering capacity, build may win on cost alone — before you add strategic value of ownership.

## The Hidden Costs That Never Appear in Proposals

**Vendor lock-in tax**: The cost of staying with a vendor after it stops being optimal. You stay because migration is expensive, not because the vendor is the best choice. That delta is a tax you pay indefinitely.

**Integration debt**: Every point integration between a vendor product and your internal systems is a liability. You now own two sides of that integration — yours and the adaptation layer to theirs.

**Upgrade treadmill**: Enterprise vendors deprecate APIs and force major version upgrades on their schedule. Your team adapts on their timeline. This is a hidden recurring cost that compounds with every integration you have built.

**Support quality degradation**: A well-documented pattern in enterprise software — support quality at contract signing and support quality eighteen months post-signature are measurably different. The team that closed the deal has moved on to the next prospect.

---

## Director Action: Model Before You Approve

Never approve a build or buy decision without a TCO model. The model does not need to be precise. It needs to be directional and complete — covering all cost categories including exit. A one-page spreadsheet with ±30% accuracy is sufficient to distinguish between a $500K decision and a $5M decision. That distinction is the one that matters.

The most important row in any TCO model is the one labeled *exit cost*. If that row is blank, the model is incomplete.
