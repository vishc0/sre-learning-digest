# Chapter 6: Migration Reliability and Risk

## Migration SLOs — Defining Success Before You Start

Reliability targets for normal operations are well-understood: availability, latency, error rate. Migrations need SLOs too, and they are different from steady-state SLOs because the system is in a fundamentally different operational state.

Three migration-specific SLOs must be defined and agreed before the program begins:

**Availability SLO during migration**: the system must maintain X% availability throughout the entire migration window — not just at cutover, but across the full multi-month program. This matters because migrations introduce operational complexity over an extended period. Configuration errors, CDC pipeline lag, dual-write failures, and dependency changes can all affect availability weeks before cutover day. If you have no availability SLO during the migration window, you have no forcing function for catching these early.

**Correctness SLO at cutover**: data must be validated to Y% accuracy before cutover is permitted to proceed. This is not a soft recommendation — it is a binary gate. Define what "Y%" means in terms of the validation framework layers: row count match, checksum match, business rule pass rate, statistical tolerance. If the correctness SLO is not met, the cutover does not happen. This must be agreed in writing before the migration begins, because the pressure to proceed at cutover is always high.

**Recovery time SLO**: if rollback is triggered, full rollback must complete within Z hours. This SLO drives the rollback architecture. If your rollback procedure would take 18 hours but your recovery time SLO is 4 hours, you do not have a valid rollback plan — you have a documented intention to roll back. Test rollback duration explicitly during rehearsals.

These SLOs are not aspirational. They are the definition of what migration success means. Without them, "the migration succeeded" is a judgment call made under pressure. With them, it is a measurement.

## Rollback Planning — Rollback First

The rollback-first principle is simple: if you cannot fully articulate the rollback procedure before the migration begins, the migration is not ready to proceed.

This is directly analogous to change management discipline. Every production change requires a rollback plan documented before the change window opens. Migrations are changes — larger, longer, and higher-stakes than most, which makes the rollback requirement more important, not less.

At cutover, the temptation is to define rollback criteria in real time, on the incident bridge, under pressure, while metrics are moving. That is the moment when rollback decisions are most likely to be wrong — delayed too long because "let's give it another 15 minutes," or triggered prematurely because one engineer saw a spike that others did not.

Define rollback criteria before the migration window opens:

- Error rate exceeds threshold (e.g., greater than 1% increase in 5xx errors sustained for 5 minutes)
- Latency exceeds threshold (e.g., P99 latency doubles relative to pre-cutover baseline)
- Data validation fails at any cutover gate
- Business KPI drops unexpectedly below threshold (payment failures, session creation rate, checkout completions)

The go/no-go decision at cutover is based on pre-defined thresholds, not on real-time judgment. The humans in the bridge are there to read measurements, not to deliberate. Pre-define the thresholds, pre-delegate the rollback authority, and execute.

Director framing: rollback criteria are a governance artifact, not a technical one. They are signed off by the business, the engineering lead, and the migration program owner before the cutover window. If stakeholders will not agree to rollback thresholds in advance, that is a signal that the migration is not sufficiently understood. Surface the disagreement before cutover day, not during it.

## The Canary Migration Pattern

Canary deployment as a reliability pattern is well-understood in application rollout. The same logic applies to migrations.

Migrate 5% of traffic — or 5% of applications in a portfolio migration — first. Observe for a defined stabilization period: this is not a soft "watch it for a while," but a fixed window (24 hours, 72 hours, one week) with explicit pass/fail criteria. If metrics are healthy across the stabilization window, proceed to 25%, then 50%, then 100%.

The 5% canary is not just a risk reduction tactic. It is the learnings wave. Production always holds surprises that staging environments do not reveal: unexpected traffic patterns, dependencies that were not documented, configuration assumptions baked into application code, and operational edge cases that only appear at scale. The canary wave is where you find these at a blast radius of 5%, not 100%.

For portfolio migrations — multiple applications migrating to a new platform — select the canary wave deliberately. Choose applications that:

- Are non-critical (lower blast radius if they fail)
- Are representative of the portfolio's technical patterns (so learnings transfer)
- Have engaged, capable teams who can respond quickly to issues

Do not select the easiest applications for the canary wave. Select the most representative ones. Migrating only simple stateless services first and then hitting the first stateful application at wave 3 is not a canary — it is deferred risk.

## Blast Radius Assessment at Cutover

Before any cutover, the migration team must complete a blast radius assessment. This is not a risk management formality — it is the input that determines the cutover strategy and the level of staffing and preparation required.

The assessment answers four questions:

**User and transaction impact**: how many users, sessions, or transactions per hour flow through this system at the cutover window? This determines the absolute business exposure if the cutover fails and rollback is required.

**Downstream system dependencies**: which systems receive data from, or send data to, this system? Do they have their own cutover dependencies? A downstream system that points to a hardcoded endpoint may not automatically follow the migration, which creates a category of failure that is easy to miss if dependency mapping is incomplete.

**Financial impact of outage duration**: what is the revenue, operational, or compliance cost of a 1-hour, 4-hour, and 24-hour outage? This number directly informs the rollback trigger thresholds and the recovery time SLO.

**Regulatory and compliance scope**: does this system touch payment processing, identity and access management, audit logging, or data residency requirements? Regulatory systems have external cutover constraints — compliance teams, auditors, and sometimes regulators must be notified in advance. Missing this during blast radius assessment creates compliance exposure in addition to operational risk.

Director framing: blast radius assessment is owned by the Director or VP, not delegated to the engineering team. The business impact numbers require business context that engineering teams often do not have. The regulatory scope requires legal and compliance input. This is the document that justifies the level of investment in the cutover window — staffing, rehearsal cycles, rollback infrastructure. Size the investment to the blast radius.

## The No-Rollback Zone

In every migration, there is a point past which rollback becomes impractical or impossible. This is the no-rollback zone, and it must be identified before the migration begins.

Common no-rollback triggers:

- Old system has been decommissioned and licenses or contracts have expired
- Dependent systems have been reconfigured to point to the new endpoint and cannot trivially revert
- Data written after cutover does not exist in the old system — reverting would create data loss in the opposite direction
- Vendor or cloud contract for the old platform has ended

The no-rollback zone changes the risk calculus fundamentally. Before you cross the line, you have options. After you cross the line, your only path is forward. This means that any incident that occurs after the no-rollback zone cannot be resolved by rollback — it must be resolved by remediating forward, which is typically slower and more complex.

Define the no-rollback zone in the migration program plan. Mark it explicitly on the timeline. Brief stakeholders — executives and business owners should understand that once the decommission begins, rollback is off the table. This shapes how aggressively you validate before that point and how conservatively you approach the final decommission steps.

The Director who fails to define and communicate the no-rollback zone creates a stakeholder expectation problem: business leaders who believe rollback is always available will make lower-quality risk decisions throughout the migration.

## Migration Incident Management

Migrations require an enhanced incident posture during cutover windows. Standard on-call rotation is not sufficient.

The structural requirements:

**Pre-staged incident bridge**: the bridge is open before the cutover begins, not opened reactively when something goes wrong. All key parties are already present — they do not need to be paged in.

**Full engineering coverage**: every engineer who might need to take action during the cutover window is available and confirmed available, not just on call. "Available" means responsive within 2 minutes, not within 30.

**Pre-delegated rollback authority**: the person who can authorize rollback is identified in writing before the cutover window opens. During a high-stress bridge call with metrics moving in conflicting directions, escalation chains add delay that the recovery time SLO cannot absorb. Rollback authority must be pre-delegated to someone present on the bridge.

**Pre-defined communication cadence**: stakeholders receive updates at a fixed interval — every 30 minutes during active cutover, hourly during stabilization. Ad hoc communication during a migration creates noise for engineering and creates anxiety in business stakeholders who interpret silence as a problem. Define the cadence and send the updates whether the news is good or not.

## Director's Lens

Migrations are the highest-risk changes in your portfolio. They are long in duration, high in complexity, and involve multiple systems, teams, and external dependencies simultaneously. They also tend to occur under external pressure — contract renewals, cost reduction mandates, platform end-of-life — which creates schedule pressure that works against the operational discipline required to do them safely.

The Director's role in a migration program is to hold the governance line. That means:

- Requiring rollback plans to exist before cutover windows open, not after
- Enforcing data validation gates even when teams are confident and schedules are tight
- Sizing the canary wave to the learnings required, not to the schedule preference
- Communicating the no-rollback zone clearly and early to executive stakeholders

This is not risk aversion. An organization that can execute a complex migration with zero data loss, minimal downtime, and a tested rollback plan is demonstrably more capable than one that forces cutover under pressure and recovers from the resulting incident. The migration program is a capability demonstration, not just a technical project.

Hold the line.
