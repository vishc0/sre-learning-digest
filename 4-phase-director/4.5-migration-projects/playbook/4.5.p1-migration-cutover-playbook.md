# Migration Cutover Playbook

## Overview

A cutover is the moment production traffic shifts from the old environment to the new one. It is the highest-risk operation in a migration program. Most migration failures are not caused by bad architecture or poor planning — they are caused by poor cutover execution: incomplete pre-checks, unclear decision authority, or a rollback that was never actually tested.

This playbook governs cutover execution for a single application within a migration wave. It applies from 48 hours before the cutover window through post-cutover closure at T+24 hours.

**What this playbook does not cover**: Application-specific migration steps (data replication, schema transformation, service configuration). Those belong in the application's migration runbook. This playbook covers the sequence, gates, decision authority, and rollback framework that wrap around every application's runbook.

**Use this playbook for**: Every production cutover in a migration wave, regardless of application complexity. The discipline applies equally to a simple stateless service and a complex multi-tier application.

---

## Roles Required

Every cutover requires these roles filled by named individuals before the window opens. A role without a named person is a blocker.

| Role | Responsibility | Authority |
|------|---------------|-----------|
| Migration Lead | Owns cutover execution end-to-end; calls go/no-go at each gate; directs the technical team during the window | Decision authority for all technical steps |
| SRE On-Call | Monitors environment health; executes infrastructure-level steps; owns alerting response during the window | Escalation authority for infrastructure events |
| Rollback Decision Authority | Pre-delegated individual with authority to call rollback without requiring a vote | Single authority to call rollback — no consensus required |
| Stakeholder Comms Lead | Manages stakeholder notifications before, during, and after the cutover | Owns all external communication during the window |
| Application Team Lead | Available during the cutover window to answer application-specific questions and validate business logic | Subject matter authority for application behavior |

The Rollback Decision Authority and Migration Lead may be the same person for small, low-complexity cutovers. For Complexity Score 4-5 applications, they should be separate individuals to avoid the conflict between "I want this to succeed" and "I need to call rollback."

## T-minus 48 Hours: Pre-Cutover Preparation

These steps execute two business days before the cutover window. They are not optional and cannot be compressed into the day-of preparation.

### Step 1: Confirm the Cutover Window Is Still Valid

Check for conflicts that have emerged since the window was originally scheduled:

- Business calendar: no product launches, quarter-close events, or major campaigns scheduled within 24 hours of the cutover
- Change freeze calendar: confirm no freeze is active or scheduled during the window
- Upstream migration status: confirm all applications this application depends on have successfully migrated (or compatibility contracts are in place)
- Downstream notification: confirm all downstream system owners have been notified of the window and have not raised objections

If a conflict is found, postpone and reschedule before proceeding with T-48 preparation. Proceeding with a known conflict is a judgment error that escalates during the window.

### Step 2: Verify Target Environment Readiness

Run a full environment health check against the target environment:

- All services and components in a healthy state (not just reachable — healthy)
- Target environment capacity is provisioned for production load, not just test load
- SSL certificates valid and not expiring within 30 days of the cutover date
- DNS configuration reviewed and TTL values already reduced (if DNS flip is part of the cutover)
- Load balancer or service mesh configuration pre-validated against target endpoints

If the target environment health check fails at T-48, the cutover does not proceed until it passes. Document the failure, assign ownership, and re-run within 24 hours.

### Step 3: Run Final Data Synchronization Validation

For any application with persistent state, validate that the data in the target environment matches the source:

- Row counts by table or entity type: source versus target, within agreed tolerance
- Checksum or hash validation on critical data sets (financial records, identity data, configuration data)
- Referential integrity checks: no orphaned foreign keys in the target
- Last-synchronized timestamp: confirm replication or synchronization lag is within acceptable bounds

Document the validation results with timestamps. These become the baseline for post-cutover validation. If validation fails at T-48, do not proceed. Assign investigation ownership and re-run before the cutover window.

### Step 4: Confirm Rollback Infrastructure Is Staged and Accessible

Walk through the rollback procedure and confirm every dependency is in place:

- Old environment is fully operational and receiving no production traffic changes between now and the cutover
- Rollback scripts are accessible to the Rollback Decision Authority
- Load balancer or DNS configuration for traffic restoration is staged (commands or automation ready — not to be written during a live rollback)
- Database replication or snapshot is current enough to support a rollback to the pre-cutover state
- Rollback time estimate re-confirmed: the documented estimate must still be achievable within the maintenance window

### Step 5: Brief All Participants

Run a 30-minute pre-cutover brief with the full team. Cover:

- Cutover sequence (step-by-step, from C1 through completion)
- Each person's role and first action in the window
- Escalation path: who to contact if the Migration Lead is unavailable
- Rollback trigger criteria (read them aloud — not just "refer to the document")
- Communication channel for the window (bridge line, Slack channel)
- Hard stop time: the time at which the cutover must either be complete or rollback must begin to finish within the maintenance window

Document attendance. Any required role that is not represented at the brief is a go/no-go risk for the window.

### Step 6: Confirm Stakeholder Communication Plan

Confirm with the Stakeholder Comms Lead:

- Stakeholder notification list is current (includes business owners, dependent team leads, and executive sponsor for the wave)
- Notification template is ready for each state: "cutover starting," "cutover complete," and "cutover rolled back"
- Notification channel is confirmed (email, Slack, Teams, or equivalent)
- Escalation contact for business impact during the window is documented and confirmed available

## T-minus 1 Hour: Pre-Cutover Gate

One hour before the cutover window opens, the Migration Lead conducts a structured go/no-go poll. Every participant must respond. A NO from any participant triggers a postponement discussion before the window opens.

### Go/No-Go Poll

The Migration Lead contacts each required participant and collects a GO or NO response with brief rationale.

**Go/No-Go Criteria Checklist**:

- [ ] Target environment health check: all components healthy within the past 30 minutes
- [ ] Data validation: row counts and checksums match within agreed tolerance, validated within the past 4 hours
- [ ] Rollback infrastructure confirmed staged and accessible
- [ ] Rollback Decision Authority confirmed available and briefed
- [ ] Application Team Lead confirmed available for the duration of the window
- [ ] SRE On-Call confirmed on the bridge and monitoring dashboards are active
- [ ] Stakeholder notification "cutover starting" is ready to send
- [ ] No open Severity 1 or Severity 2 incidents in the environment being migrated from or to
- [ ] Change ticket is approved and the change window is active

**Handling a NO**:

If any participant calls NO, the Migration Lead collects the reason and evaluates:
- Can the blocking issue be resolved within 30 minutes without increasing risk? If yes, pause and resolve.
- Is the issue a risk the team can accept with documented justification? Requires Rollback Decision Authority and Migration Lead agreement, documented.
- Is the issue a genuine blocker? Postpone. The Migration Lead notifies the Stakeholder Comms Lead, who sends the postponement notification.

A postponement at T-1 is a successful risk management action. A cutover that proceeds through a known NO and fails is an execution failure.

## Cutover Execution

### Step C1: Open the Cutover Bridge

Open the conference bridge or incident channel designated for this cutover. All required participants join. The Migration Lead confirms all roles are present before proceeding.

Log the cutover start time. This timestamp anchors the maintenance window and rollback time calculations.

### Step C2: Send "Cutover In Progress" Notification

Stakeholder Comms Lead sends the pre-staged "cutover in progress" notification to all stakeholders. The notification includes:

- Application name
- Cutover start time
- Expected completion time
- Expected impact (downtime, if any; degraded state, if applicable)
- Contact for business impact questions during the window

### Step C3: Execute Application-Specific Cutover Steps

Execute the application's migration runbook, step by step. The Migration Lead owns the sequence. The SRE On-Call owns infrastructure steps. The Application Team Lead validates application behavior.

Discipline during execution:
- Read each step aloud before executing it
- Confirm completion of each step before calling the next
- Do not improvise. If the runbook does not cover a situation encountered during execution, stop, assess, and decide as a team whether to proceed or rollback. Do not invent steps under pressure.
- Assign one person to maintain a running log with timestamps during execution

### Step C4: Verify Each Major Step Before Proceeding

After each major step (service start, configuration change, schema migration), run the step's verification check before proceeding. Verification checks must be defined in the migration runbook before the cutover, not improvised during it.

If a verification check fails, do not proceed. Stop, assess root cause, and evaluate: is this recoverable within the maintenance window, or is rollback the right call?

### Step C5: Traffic Shift — Incremental Ramp

Shift production traffic to the new environment incrementally. Do not flip 100% of traffic in a single step unless the application has zero user-facing impact during the shift.

Standard incremental ramp:

| Traffic % to New Environment | Stabilization Period Before Next Increment |
|------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| 5% | 10 minutes |
| 25% | 15 minutes |
| 50% | 15 minutes |
| 100% | 30 minutes post-observation |

The stabilization period is non-negotiable. Its purpose is to detect failures that only manifest under load before the failure is at full scale.

For applications with no user-facing traffic (batch, async), the incremental ramp applies to job routing or message consumption, not HTTP traffic.

### Step C6: Run Cutover Validation Suite

After each traffic shift increment, run the pre-defined cutover validation suite. The validation suite must be defined before the cutover. If it was not defined before the cutover, you do not have a validation suite — you have improvised checking under pressure, which is not equivalent.

Validation suite components:
- Automated smoke tests against the new environment's endpoints
- Business transaction validation: a sample of end-to-end transactions completing successfully
- Data integrity spot-check: a sample of records accessible and correct in the new environment
- Downstream system health check: confirm dependent services are operating normally

## Go/No-Go at Each Traffic Shift Increment

Before each traffic shift increment, the Migration Lead evaluates the current state against the following thresholds. These thresholds must be defined and documented before the cutover window opens. Thresholds defined during the cutover are not valid — they are subject to confirmation bias.

**Error Rate**:
- Baseline: [establish from production metrics 7 days prior]
- Threshold: no more than [X]% above baseline for [Y] minutes
- If exceeded: stop the increment ramp; assess; rollback if not resolved within [Z] minutes

**P99 Latency**:
- Baseline: [establish from production metrics 7 days prior]
- Threshold: no more than [X]ms above baseline for [Y] minutes
- If exceeded: stop the increment ramp; assess; rollback if not resolved within [Z] minutes

**Business KPIs**:
- Define per application (examples: transaction success rate, order completion rate, API response validity rate)
- Baseline: [establish from production metrics prior to cutover]
- Threshold: [define acceptable deviation range]
- If exceeded: stop the increment ramp; escalate to Application Team Lead immediately

**Monitoring Check**:
- All dashboards actively receiving telemetry from the new environment
- No alerting gaps (no "no data" conditions on key metrics)

If any threshold is breached and not resolved within the stabilization period, do not proceed to the next increment. Evaluate rollback.

---

## Rollback Trigger Criteria

Rollback may be called at any point during the cutover window. Any one of the following conditions, if unresolved within the specified duration, triggers rollback:

- Error rate exceeds [threshold]% above baseline for [duration] minutes
- P99 latency exceeds [threshold]ms for [duration] minutes
- Data validation failure detected during or after traffic shift
- Business KPI outside acceptable range for [duration] minutes and the root cause is not identified
- Downstream system impact detected that cannot be attributed to expected behavior
- Migration Lead judgment: "something is wrong that I cannot explain and cannot prove is transient"

The last criterion is intentional. Unexplained system behavior during a cutover is a rollback signal, not a debugging opportunity. Debug after the system is restored, not while production traffic is on an unhealthy new environment.

**Who calls rollback**: The Rollback Decision Authority. No vote, no consensus, no escalation required. The authority is pre-delegated specifically to enable a fast, unambiguous rollback decision. If the Rollback Decision Authority is unavailable, the Migration Lead has backup authority.

## Rollback Execution

### Step R1: Rollback Decision Called

Rollback Decision Authority announces rollback on the bridge. Log the rollback decision timestamp.

Stakeholder Comms Lead sends the pre-staged "cutover rolled back" notification to all stakeholders immediately. Do not wait until rollback is complete to notify stakeholders.

### Step R2: Execute Rollback Runbook

Execute the application-specific rollback runbook. The rollback runbook must have been pre-staged and tested before the cutover. Steps in the rollback runbook execute in the documented order. No improvisation.

The SRE On-Call owns infrastructure rollback steps. The Application Team Lead confirms application state after each major rollback step.

### Step R3: Confirm Traffic Fully Restored to Old Environment

After rollback execution, verify that 100% of production traffic is routing to the old environment. Verification steps:

- Load balancer or service mesh configuration confirmed pointing to old environment
- Traffic metrics on old environment confirm expected request volume
- Error rates and latency on old environment within normal baseline
- New environment receiving zero production traffic

### Step R4: Post-Rollback Stabilization Check

Monitor old environment for 30 minutes post-rollback. Confirm:
- No residual impact from the cutover attempt
- All downstream systems operating normally
- Business KPIs within expected range

### Step R5: Stakeholder Update

After the stabilization check, Stakeholder Comms Lead sends a final rollback notification:

"Cutover for [Application Name] has been rolled back. [Application Name] is operating normally on the original environment. A post-mortem review will be conducted by [date]. The next cutover attempt will be scheduled after root cause analysis is complete."

Do not speculate on root cause in stakeholder communications until the post-mortem is complete.

---

## Post-Cutover Validation (T+1 Hour)

After the traffic is fully shifted to the new environment and the ramp is complete, run the post-cutover validation before closing the bridge.

- [ ] All automated smoke tests passing on new environment
- [ ] Monitoring dashboards showing healthy baselines (not elevated, not absent)
- [ ] Downstream systems confirmed operating normally (verify with downstream team leads)
- [ ] Business KPIs confirmed within expected range
- [ ] No active alerts on new environment
- [ ] Data integrity sample check complete and passing
- [ ] Migration Lead declares cutover complete and logs completion timestamp

After post-cutover validation is confirmed, send the "cutover complete" notification to all stakeholders. Close the bridge.

**Do not close the bridge before post-cutover validation is confirmed.** "It looks good" is not a validation.

---

## Post-Cutover Closure (T+24 Hours)

These steps execute within 24 hours of a successful cutover.

- [ ] Confirm new environment has been stable for 24 hours (no incidents, no rollback triggers)
- [ ] Decommission rollback infrastructure: remove the old environment's active traffic path (do not delete — keep accessible for 7 days post-cutover for emergency reference)
- [ ] Update CMDB: record new environment as the system of record for this application
- [ ] Update DNS, load balancer, and service mesh configurations to remove old environment entries
- [ ] Close the change ticket with completion notes and validation results attached
- [ ] Send final "cutover complete" communication to all stakeholders with stabilization confirmation
- [ ] Schedule post-cutover retrospective within 1 week (all required participants, 60 minutes)
- [ ] Log the cutover results in the wave tracking document: start time, completion time, rollbacks (if any), issues encountered, lessons to apply to subsequent cutovers

The post-cutover retrospective agenda covers: what worked, what did not, what would have triggered rollback if the team had been less experienced, and what changes are needed before the next cutover. Retrospective findings feed back into the wave plan for remaining applications.
