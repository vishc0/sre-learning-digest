# Vendor Exit Playbook

## Overview

A vendor exit is a migration with a contract ticking in the background. Everything about a platform migration applies — dependency mapping, cutover execution, rollback planning — but vendor exit adds legal, commercial, and relationship dimensions that purely technical migrations do not have. The contract governs your timeline, your data rights, and your costs. Failing to read it before making operational decisions is the most common and most expensive mistake in vendor exit programs.

This playbook governs a technology vendor exit from the decision to exit through formal relationship close-out. It applies when an organization is moving off a technology vendor's platform — SaaS, PaaS, managed service, or software — to an alternative.

**What makes vendor exit different from platform migration**:

- The current vendor is a counterparty, not a passive bystander. Their cooperation level affects your exit timeline, your data extraction quality, and your support during the transition.
- The contract defines your rights — including when you can leave, at what cost, and in what format you can retrieve your data. These rights are fixed at signing. Know them before you negotiate or act.
- The vendor has an incentive to slow your exit or make it expensive. This is not malice — it is business. Plan accordingly.
- Exit is irreversible from a relationship standpoint. Decisions made during exit affect future procurement leverage with this vendor and set precedent for how your organization manages vendor transitions.

## Roles

| Role | Responsibility | Decision Authority |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| Executive Sponsor (VP/Director) | Owns the exit decision; accountable to the business for transition risk and cost | Approves exit trigger, exit window, target technology selection, and formal vendor notification |
| Program Manager | Owns transition timeline and cross-functional coordination | Escalation authority for schedule conflicts; reports to Executive Sponsor |
| Technical Lead | Owns target architecture design, data migration, integration rebuild, and parallel run operations | Technical decision authority during transition execution |
| Legal/Procurement Lead | Reviews contract, manages notice obligations, oversees data deletion requirements and exit fees | Authority to sign off on contract interpretation and formal vendor communications |
| Vendor Relationship Owner | Manages the working relationship with the vendor during transition; coordinates data extraction requests | Authority to make commitments to the vendor on operational questions; does not have authority on commercial or legal positions |

These roles must be filled by named individuals before Phase 1 begins. A vendor exit with undefined ownership fails before it starts.

## Phase 1: Exit Decision and Contract Review (Months 1-2)

### Step 1.1: Document the Exit Trigger

Before any planning begins, formally document why the organization is exiting. The exit trigger determines the timeline pressure, the negotiating posture, and the acceptable cost of exit.

Common exit triggers and their implications:

| Trigger | Timeline Pressure | Negotiating Posture |
|---------|------------------|---------------------|
| Vendor-announced EOL | Fixed external deadline | Limited leverage; focus on data portability |
| Cost — price increase or better alternative | Self-determined | High leverage; vendor may offer retention pricing |
| Strategic technology change | Self-determined | Moderate leverage; depends on contract term remaining |
| Security or compliance failure by vendor | Urgent, may be contract-triggered | High leverage; contract may include remedy clauses |
| Vendor acquisition or financial instability | Variable | Depends on acquiring entity's posture |

Document the trigger with supporting evidence (EOL announcement, pricing notice, incident record). This documentation protects the organization if the vendor challenges the exit.

**Director checkpoint**: Executive Sponsor reviews and confirms the exit trigger and authorizes the exit program to proceed.

### Step 1.2: Legal and Procurement Review of the Current Contract

Legal/Procurement Lead conducts a full contract review before any external communication with the vendor. The review covers:

**Notice period**: How many days written notice are required to terminate? Who receives the notice? What form is required (certified mail, specific email address, or legal department)? Starting the notice clock incorrectly restarts it.

**Exit fees and penalties**: Are there early termination fees? Are there minimum spend commitments remaining in the term? Calculate the exact cost of exit at different dates.

**Data portability rights**: What is the organization contractually entitled to export? In what format? Is there a charge for bulk data export? Is there a timeline within which the vendor must provide exports after request? Many contracts are silent on format, which defaults to "whatever the vendor chooses" — a significant operational risk.

**Audit rights**: Can the organization audit the vendor's data deletion after exit? If so, in what form and timeframe?

**SLA during transition**: What service level obligations apply during the notice period? Vendors sometimes deprioritize exiting customers. Knowing the contracted SLA lets you enforce it.

**IP and license terms**: Who owns configurations, customizations, or data models built on the vendor's platform? What licenses expire at termination?

Produce a contract summary document: one page, covering each of the above points with the relevant contract section cited. This document is the reference for all subsequent exit planning. Do not rely on team members' recollections of contract terms.

### Step 1.3: Identify the Exit Window

Using the contract review, map out the timeline:

- **Earliest legal exit date**: the earliest date the organization can exit without penalty (after serving notice, fulfilling minimum commitments)
- **Latest viable exit date**: the date by which exit must be complete (EOL date, compliance deadline, or maximum acceptable run cost)
- **Optimal exit date**: the date that balances cost, operational readiness, and risk

The optimal exit date drives the transition timeline in Phase 2. Build backward from it.

Document the exit window with dates. If the latest viable exit date is less than 6 months away, escalate to the Executive Sponsor immediately. Compressed timelines require scope reduction, additional resources, or accepted risk — not wishful planning.

**Director checkpoint**: Executive Sponsor reviews and approves the exit window. This approval authorizes the transition timeline in Phase 2.

### Step 1.4: Determine Exit vs. Retention Posture

Before contacting the vendor, determine the organization's posture:

**Genuine exit**: The decision to leave is final. The goal is smooth execution. Communicate this posture clearly internally so that no one inadvertently signals uncertainty to the vendor.

**Negotiating leverage**: The organization might stay if the vendor meets specific conditions (price reduction, feature commitment, SLA improvement). If this is the posture, document the retention offer criteria before any conversation with the vendor. Vendors are skilled at reading negotiating positions. Going into a conversation without documented criteria results in accepting terms that were not pre-approved.

If the posture is "negotiating leverage," the Legal/Procurement Lead and Executive Sponsor must agree on the written criteria for what would cause the organization to stay. These criteria are confidential. They are not shared with the vendor.

If the exit decision is firm, skip this step and proceed to Phase 2.

## Phase 2: Transition Planning (Months 1-3)

### Step 2.1: Select the Target Technology

The Technical Lead leads target technology evaluation. The Executive Sponsor approves the selection.

Target technology options for a vendor exit:

| Option | Description | Best For | Risk |
|--------|-------------|----------|------|
| In-house build | Build equivalent capability internally | When vendor's functionality is undifferentiated and internal capability exists | Scope creep; build time underestimated |
| Open source equivalent | Adopt OSS alternative | When a mature OSS option exists and the team can operate it | Operational ownership; upgrade burden |
| Alternative vendor | Move to a competing vendor's platform | When switching cost is lower than build cost and OSS is immature | Re-creates vendor dependency |

Evaluate target options against:
- Functional coverage: does the target cover all capabilities currently used (not all capabilities the vendor offers — only the ones in use)
- Data import capability: can the target ingest the exported data format?
- Integration compatibility: does the target expose APIs compatible with existing integrations, or must integrations be rebuilt?
- Total cost of ownership over 3 years: include licensing, hosting, operational staffing, and migration cost

**Director checkpoint**: Executive Sponsor reviews and approves the target technology selection. This is a commercial and strategic decision, not only a technical one.

### Step 2.2: Build the Transition Timeline

Working backward from the optimal exit date identified in Step 1.3, construct the transition timeline. The timeline must include all phases of this playbook with realistic durations. Common timeline compression mistakes:

- Underestimating data extraction time (especially for large datasets or poorly documented export formats)
- Assuming the parallel run period can be shortened (it cannot — users need time to validate, and shortcuts become defects)
- Omitting time for integration rebuild and testing (integrations are always more complex than they appear)
- Not accounting for vendor responsiveness delays (data export requests can take weeks if not contractually time-bound)

Build the timeline with buffers: at minimum, 20% buffer on data extraction and parallel run phases. These are the phases most likely to encounter surprises.

Present the timeline with two dates: the target completion date and the hard deadline. If the target date slips, the team works to recover. If the hard deadline is approached, the Executive Sponsor is notified immediately.

### Step 2.3: Inventory All Integrations, Data Flows, and Workflows

The Technical Lead leads a comprehensive audit of everything connected to the current vendor's platform:

- API integrations: which internal systems call the vendor's APIs? What data do they send and receive?
- Inbound data flows: what data flows into the vendor's platform from external systems?
- Outbound data flows: what data flows out of the vendor's platform to other systems?
- Automated workflows: what business processes are automated within the vendor's platform? (These are often invisible to engineering but business-critical)
- User-facing workflows: what do end users do directly in the vendor's platform that will need to be replicated in the target?
- Reporting and analytics: what reports are generated from the vendor's platform? What are their audiences?

For each item, document: current state (how it works today), target state (how it will work in the new environment), and migration approach (rebuild, replace, or deprecate).

Do not assume integrations and workflows are fully documented. Pull API gateway logs, network traffic data, and interview heavy users. Undocumented integrations discovered late are the most common cause of vendor exit delays.

### Step 2.4: Assess Data Portability

For each data category in the vendor's platform, assess:

- **Export format**: what format does the vendor export in? CSV, JSON, XML, proprietary binary? Is the format documented? Is it complete (does it include all fields, or does the vendor silently exclude some)?
- **Export cost**: does the contract allow bulk data export at no charge, or is there a per-record or per-request fee?
- **Export timeline**: how long will a full data export take to generate? For large data sets, vendor-side export jobs can take days to weeks.
- **Historical data**: does the export include all historical records, or only active records? Many vendors archive old records and do not include them in standard exports.
- **Attachment and binary data**: is unstructured data (documents, images, attachments) exported separately? In what format?
- **Target ingestion**: can the target system ingest the exported format directly, or is transformation required?

Produce a data portability assessment table:

| Data Category | Volume | Export Format | Export Cost | Transform Required | Historical Included |
|--------------|--------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| [Category] | [rows / GB] | [format] | [$/record or included] | [yes/no] | [yes/no] |

### Step 2.5: Build the Full Transition Cost Estimate

The Technical Lead and Legal/Procurement Lead produce a comprehensive cost estimate for the exit. Incomplete cost estimates produce budget surprises that stop programs.

Include:

| Cost Category | Estimate | Notes |
|--------------|----------|-------|
| Data extraction (vendor fees) | | Per contract terms |
| Data transformation and ingestion | | Engineering effort |
| Integration rebuild | | Per integration count in Step 2.3 |
| Target technology licensing | | Year 1 and ongoing |
| Target environment infrastructure | | Hosting, compute, storage |
| Parallel run period (dual costs) | | Running both systems simultaneously |
| Training | | Users and operations staff |
| Exit fees or penalties | | Per contract terms |
| Contingency (20%) | | Non-negotiable; compression always costs |
| **Total** | | |

**Director checkpoint**: Executive Sponsor reviews and approves the transition cost estimate. If the cost exceeds the approved budget, the Executive Sponsor either secures additional budget or adjusts scope (timeline, target technology, or exit date). This decision cannot be deferred to execution.

## Phase 3: Data Extraction (Months 2-5)

### Step 3.1: Initiate Data Export Before Notifying the Vendor of Exit Intent

Begin data extraction before formally notifying the vendor of your intent to exit.

Rationale: After receiving formal notice, some vendors deprioritize support requests, restrict export functionality, or become less cooperative on data format questions. Extracting data while the relationship is operationally normal reduces this risk. This is not deceptive — you have a contractual right to your data. Exercise that right before it becomes contentious.

Data export requests should be made through the Vendor Relationship Owner using routine operational framing ("we need a full data export for backup and compliance purposes") unless the contract requires specific notice for bulk exports.

If the contract requires notification to initiate bulk export, consult Legal/Procurement before requesting.

### Step 3.2: Validate Exported Data Completeness and Format

When the vendor delivers the exported data, do not proceed to transformation until validation is complete.

Validation checks:
- Total record counts match expected volumes (compare against vendor's own reporting)
- All documented fields are present in the export (compare against the vendor's data model documentation)
- Sample records are readable and correctly structured
- Attachment and binary data is present and accessible
- Historical records are included at the agreed depth
- No fields are truncated or transformed in ways that change meaning

If validation reveals gaps, raise them with the Vendor Relationship Owner immediately. Document all discrepancies in writing. Do not accept "this is a known limitation" without written acknowledgment from the vendor. Missing data after exit is unrecoverable.

### Step 3.3: Run Data Transformation Pipeline

The Technical Lead owns the data transformation pipeline that converts exported data from the vendor's format to the target system's required format.

Transformation pipeline requirements:
- Idempotent: can be re-run without duplicating records
- Auditable: produces a log of every transformation applied
- Validated at output: post-transformation validation checks match input record counts and spot-check field values
- Version-controlled: the transformation code is in source control

Run the transformation pipeline on a subset first. Validate the subset output before processing the full data set.

### Step 3.4: Store Extracted Data in Vendor-Neutral Format

After transformation, store the original extracted data in a vendor-neutral, durable format in controlled storage (internal object storage, not dependent on either the old or new vendor).

This archive serves as the reference if post-exit discrepancies arise. Keep it for a minimum of 12 months post-exit, or per the organization's data retention policy, whichever is longer.

### Step 3.5: Verify All Data Categories Are Captured

Before closing Phase 3, verify against the data portability assessment from Step 2.4:

- [ ] All operational data categories extracted and validated
- [ ] All historical data categories extracted to agreed depth
- [ ] All configuration data exported (settings, templates, workflow definitions)
- [ ] All attachment and binary data extracted and accessible
- [ ] Audit logs extracted if required for compliance
- [ ] Transformation pipeline complete and validated
- [ ] Archive stored in vendor-neutral format in controlled storage

Any missing category is a blocker on Phase 5. Do not proceed to the final cutover with unresolved data gaps.

## Phase 4: Parallel Run Period (Months 4-8)

### Step 4.1: Stand Up Target System and Begin Parallel Operations

Stand up the target system with production-equivalent capacity and configuration. Begin operating both the vendor system and the target system simultaneously.

The parallel run period has two purposes:
1. Validate that the target system functions correctly under real workload before it becomes the system of record
2. Give users time to operate in the target system and surface gaps before the old system is gone

During the parallel run, the vendor system remains the system of record. Changes made in the vendor system are authoritative. Changes made in the target system are for validation purposes only, unless a specific workflow has been formally cut over.

### Step 4.2: Migrate Non-Critical Workflows First

Identify the lowest-risk, lowest-criticality workflows that can be moved to the target system first. Migration order follows the same dependency-first principle as platform migration: migrate workflows with no dependencies before workflows that depend on others.

Non-critical workflows first means:
- Internal-only workflows before customer-facing ones
- Low-volume workflows before high-volume ones
- Workflows with no compliance or regulatory obligation before regulated ones
- Workflows with easily reversible data before workflows with permanent state changes

Each workflow migrated to the target system during the parallel run is validated before the next workflow moves. This is a continuous validation of the target system under incremental real load.

### Step 4.3: Re-Wire Integrations Incrementally

For each integration identified in Step 2.3, re-wire it to point to the target system on a per-workflow basis. Do not re-wire all integrations simultaneously.

Re-wiring sequence:
1. Build and test the new integration endpoint on the target system in a non-production environment
2. Deploy the new endpoint in the target system's production environment
3. Run both endpoints in parallel (old vendor and target) if technically feasible, to validate equivalence
4. Switch the integration to the target endpoint
5. Validate integration behavior in production before proceeding to the next integration

Maintain a re-wiring tracker showing each integration's status: not started, in progress, re-wired, validated.

### Step 4.4: Train Users Before Cutting Over Critical Workflows

Users must be trained on the target system before their critical workflows are moved. Training before cutover, not at cutover, is the discipline.

Training requirements:
- All users who perform critical workflows have completed target system training (not just received access)
- Documentation for critical workflows in the target system is complete and accessible
- Support resources are available for the first 30 days after critical workflow cutover
- A feedback mechanism is in place for users to report gaps in the target system

Do not cut over critical workflows until the training completion rate for affected users is confirmed. A high adoption rate measured by login data is not the same as training completion.

### Step 4.5: Define Cutover Criteria for System of Record Transition

Document the specific, measurable criteria that determine when the target system is ready to become the system of record. These criteria must be defined and approved before the parallel run begins, not during it.

Example criteria (adapt to your context):
- All workflows in scope have been migrated and validated in the target system
- Re-wired integrations have been operating without errors for [N] days
- User training completion rate is [X]% or above for all critical workflow users
- No open defects rated Severity 1 or 2 in the target system
- Data consistency check between vendor system and target system passes within agreed tolerance
- Business stakeholder sign-off on target system readiness

When all criteria are met, proceed to Phase 5.

## Phase 5: Final Cutover and Vendor Notification

### Step 5.1: Execute Final Cutover

Execute the final cutover from the vendor system to the target system using the Migration Cutover Playbook. The vendor system is the source environment; the target system is the destination.

The cutover makes the target system the system of record. After this cutover, the vendor system is read-only reference only.

Before the cutover, confirm:
- All parallel run criteria from Step 4.5 are met
- A final data synchronization from the vendor system to the target is performed immediately before cutover
- The rollback plan is in place (rollback means reverting the system of record to the vendor system, which remains available during the post-cutover stabilization window)

### Step 5.2: Serve Formal Notice to the Vendor

After the final cutover is complete and the stabilization window has passed, serve formal notice to the vendor per the contract requirements.

The Legal/Procurement Lead owns this step. The notice must:
- Be in the form specified by the contract
- Be served to the entity and contact specified by the contract
- Start the contractual notice clock from the date of delivery, not the date of drafting
- Be retained with delivery confirmation (read receipt, certified mail confirmation, or equivalent)

Do not serve formal notice before the cutover is complete. Serving notice triggers the vendor's transition process and may reduce their cooperation if issues arise during cutover.

**Director checkpoint**: Executive Sponsor reviews and approves the formal vendor notice before it is sent. This is a commercial decision with contract implications.

### Step 5.3: Request Final Data Export for Archival

After serving notice, request a final data export from the vendor that captures any data created or modified after the Phase 3 extraction. This export covers the delta between the Phase 3 extraction date and the cutover date.

The final export request should be made through the Vendor Relationship Owner. Confirm the export scope, format, and delivery timeline with the vendor in writing.

### Step 5.4: Freeze Vendor System for Reference Access

After the final cutover, the vendor system becomes read-only. Establish the read-only access period with the vendor — this is typically 30-90 days, depending on the contract and business needs.

Communicate to all users that the vendor system is now read-only reference only. New records and changes must be made in the target system. Any updates made in the vendor system after cutover will not be replicated and will be lost at close-out.

### Step 5.5: Run Historical Data Validation

Validate that critical historical records are accessible and correct in the new system:

- Sample records from each historical period (last week, last month, last quarter, last year, beyond one year)
- Regulatory or compliance records that must be retained for specific periods
- Records referenced in any active legal matters or audits
- Records needed for tax or financial reporting

Document the validation results. If gaps are found, work through the Vendor Relationship Owner to retrieve them from the vendor system while it is still accessible.

## Phase 6: Vendor Relationship Close-Out (Final Month)

### Step 6.1: Confirm All Contractual Obligations Are Met

Legal/Procurement Lead confirms each contractual obligation is satisfied:

- [ ] All exit fees paid (final invoices settled)
- [ ] Notice period completed (calendar date confirmed)
- [ ] Data deletion certificates received (if required by contract or applicable regulation)
- [ ] Any minimum commitment amounts fulfilled or negotiated
- [ ] Return of vendor-owned assets (if any physical or licensed assets were provided)
- [ ] Outstanding support tickets resolved or transferred

Do not close the vendor relationship with open financial obligations. Unresolved invoices become leverage for vendors to complicate future reference requests or data access.

### Step 6.2: Retrieve All Vendor-Held Data

Before the vendor access is terminated, retrieve:

- Backup copies held by the vendor
- Audit logs beyond the standard retention period (if accessible and needed)
- Configuration exports (settings, templates, custom workflow definitions)
- Any reports or analytics not captured in the Phase 3 extraction

The Vendor Relationship Owner coordinates retrieval. Confirm receipt of all requested data before signing off on close-out.

### Step 6.3: Document Lessons Learned

Program Manager leads a close-out retrospective with the Technical Lead, Legal/Procurement Lead, and Vendor Relationship Owner. Document:

- What the organization underestimated at the start (timeline, cost, complexity, vendor cooperation)
- What contractual terms caused friction or risk, and what contract language would have been better
- What data portability gaps were discovered and how to require portability in future contracts
- What technical approaches worked well during the parallel run period
- What would be done differently for the next vendor exit

The lessons learned document is filed in the program archive and shared with the procurement team to inform future vendor contract negotiations. Vendor exit learnings are most valuable when they prevent the next exit from repeating the same problems.

### Step 6.4: Formal Sign-Off from Legal and Procurement

Legal/Procurement Lead issues a formal close-out sign-off confirming all contractual obligations are met and the vendor relationship is legally terminated.

**Director checkpoint**: Executive Sponsor reviews and countersigns the close-out sign-off. This confirms organizational acceptance that the vendor relationship is closed.

### Step 6.5: Archive Exit Documentation

Program Manager archives all exit program documentation in a durable, accessible location:

- Original contract and all amendments
- Contract review summary (from Step 1.2)
- Exit trigger documentation
- Transition timeline and actuals (planned vs. actual for each phase)
- Data portability assessment and validation records
- Integration re-wiring tracker
- All data export records and validation results
- Formal vendor notice and delivery confirmation
- Final data export records
- Data deletion certificates
- Lessons learned document
- Formal close-out sign-off

Retention period: minimum 7 years, or per the organization's records retention policy, whichever is longer. Regulatory investigations and legal disputes can arise years after a vendor relationship ends.

## Director's Checkpoints Summary

The following steps require Executive Sponsor (Director/VP level) review and approval before the program proceeds. These are the decisions with commercial, legal, and strategic implications:

| Step | Decision | Why Director-Level |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| 1.1 | Exit trigger confirmed; program authorized | Commits organizational resources to a significant program |
| 1.3 | Exit window approved | Sets the hard deadline that drives all subsequent planning |
| 2.1 | Target technology selection approved | Commercial and strategic decision with multi-year implications |
| 2.5 | Transition cost estimate approved | Budget authority and scope alignment |
| 5.2 | Formal vendor notice approved | Irreversible commercial action; triggers contractual timeline |
| 6.4 | Close-out sign-off | Confirms organizational acceptance of relationship termination |

Everything between these checkpoints is execution. The program team owns execution. The Director owns the decisions.
