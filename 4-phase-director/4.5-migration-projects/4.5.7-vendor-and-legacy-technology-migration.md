# Chapter 7: Vendor and Legacy Technology Migration

## Why This Chapter Matters

Vendor migration is the migration type that catches Directors off guard most often — not because the technical work is harder, but because the organizational and commercial dynamics are different from anything in a standard platform migration. The skills that make you effective at PCF-to-Kubernetes or on-prem-to-cloud migrations do not automatically transfer. This chapter builds the frame you need to lead vendor exit programs as a Director, not as a technical program manager.

---

## Vendor Migration vs. Platform Migration: The Structural Difference

Platform migrations are technically driven. The trigger is usually internal: a cloud strategy, a cost optimization target, a modernization roadmap. The timeline is negotiable. The relationship you're navigating is with your own organization.

Vendor migrations are contractually driven. The trigger is external: license expiry, vendor end-of-life announcement, cost escalation after an acquisition. The timeline has a hard external boundary that you do not set. And you are navigating two parallel relationships simultaneously — the commercial relationship with the vendor you are exiting, and the internal program to replace them.

This structural difference changes everything about how you lead:

- You cannot slip a vendor migration the way you can slip an internal platform migration. A license expiry date does not negotiate.
- Your vendor knows you are a customer in distress. They have leverage during the transition period — support responsiveness, audit timing, retention pricing — and they will use it.
- Budget approval for vendor exit must account for the commercial relationship cost, not just the technical migration cost. These are different line items.

A Director leading a vendor migration must hold both the technical program and the commercial relationship simultaneously. If you delegate the commercial side entirely to procurement and run the technical side in isolation, you will get surprised — by audits, by data extraction fees, by support SLA degradation at the worst possible moment.

## Legacy Vendor Exit: The Canonical Cases

### Oracle Licensing

Oracle's per-core, per-processor licensing model is one of the most aggressive in enterprise software. The licensing cost for a mid-size Oracle deployment can represent tens of millions of dollars annually. Oracle's audit team is proactive, not reactive — they audit customers who are migrating, not just customers who are under-licensing.

When you are leading an Oracle exit program, the technical target is usually PostgreSQL, Amazon Aurora, or a cloud-native managed database. The technical work looks straightforward on paper: migrate schema, migrate data, re-point application connections. The reality is different.

Oracle-specific SQL features, stored procedures, and PL/SQL logic are deeply embedded in application codebases — often written years ago by developers who no longer work for your company. Oracle-specific constructs do not translate directly to PostgreSQL syntax. The migration effort is not data movement — it is application re-development. An Oracle exit program for a mid-size enterprise is a 2-3 year effort, not a 6-month project.

Director-level point: Oracle licensing lock-in is intentional and deeply engineered. Every Oracle-specific feature an application uses is a line of migration cost. Before you commit to an Oracle exit timeline, invest in a proper application portfolio assessment that maps Oracle-specific dependencies per application. That assessment will recalibrate your timeline estimate significantly.

### IBM Mainframe Modernization

COBOL applications running on IBM z/OS represent 40 or more years of accumulated business logic. Insurance premium calculation engines. Banking settlement systems. Telecom billing logic. These systems process trillions of dollars of transactions daily because they are reliable, not because they are modern.

Mainframe modernization has three viable strategies:

- **Re-host on x86 Linux**: lift the COBOL workloads to commodity hardware without rewriting the code. Tools like Micro Focus or Rocket Software enable this. Preserves the logic, reduces hardware cost, does not eliminate the COBOL dependency.
- **Re-platform to cloud microservices**: decompose the mainframe workloads into modern services. High cost, high risk, multi-year effort. The result is modern — cloud-native, observable, scalable. The risk is in the decomposition.
- **Retain as system-of-record**: accept that the mainframe is the source of truth for certain data domains and build modern interfaces around it rather than replacing it. Common in banking. Unglamorous but often the right answer.

The risk that makes mainframe modernization uniquely dangerous: the business logic encoded in COBOL is often undocumented. The engineers who wrote it are retired or deceased. Modernization without documentation is archaeology — you are reverse-engineering decades of business rules from code, not from specifications.

Director framing: never approve a mainframe modernization without a discovery and documentation phase that produces a business logic inventory. Without that inventory, your team will discover business rules in production, not in testing.

### VMware and the Broadcom Displacement Wave

The Broadcom acquisition of VMware completed in late 2023. In the 12-18 months that followed, enterprise customers reported licensing cost increases of 3-5x for vSphere, vSAN, and NSX. This was not unexpected — Broadcom's acquisition model involves cost rationalization through customer consolidation — but the magnitude and speed surprised many enterprise IT organizations.

The result is a large wave of VMware exit programs currently underway across the industry. Migration targets include KVM and OpenStack for private cloud, AWS EC2 and Azure VM for public cloud, and Kubernetes directly for containerized workloads.

The T-Mobile context is relevant here: EKS-based container platforms displace both PCF workloads and VMware workloads. If you are building the case for Kubernetes investment, the VMware displacement argument is real and quantifiable — you can point to peer enterprises' licensing cost increases as supporting evidence.

## Open-Source Migration: The True Cost Equation

Replacing commercial tools with open-source equivalents is appealing on the business case: eliminate the license cost. The pattern is common — ServiceNow replaced by Jira Service Management, Splunk replaced by an OpenTelemetry plus Elasticsearch stack, Dynatrace replaced by Prometheus and Grafana.

The business case always looks clean until you account for the true cost equation:

**Commercial tool license cost vs. (OSS infrastructure cost + 2-4 FTE to operate + feature gap development)**

Open-source tools require engineering time to configure, operate, extend, and maintain. A Splunk license includes support, ongoing feature development, and the guarantee that the product continues to work when your infrastructure changes. A Prometheus plus Grafana stack requires someone who knows Prometheus, someone who can write PromQL, someone who manages the retention and scaling of the metrics backend, and someone who builds the dashboards that Splunk would have provided out of the box.

The second point is harder to surface in a business case: open-source migration is rarely a 1:1 feature replacement. Splunk's machine learning capabilities, SIEM integrations, and alert management are not replicated by a self-managed Elasticsearch cluster. The feature gap is real. Your stakeholders need to accept it before the migration begins, not discover it after.

Director action: before approving an open-source migration business case, require a feature gap analysis that documents what the organization is giving up, who accepts that gap, and what (if anything) will be built to close it.

## SaaS Replacement Migrations: The Hidden Complexity

Replacing one SaaS product with another looks like the simplest migration category. Both systems are cloud-hosted, both have APIs, both have customer support. The reality is that SaaS replacements carry complexity that is almost entirely non-technical.

The hidden costs that rarely appear in the initial business case:

**Data portability**: Is the incumbent SaaS exporting your data in a standard format or a proprietary one? Many SaaS vendors provide data export that is complete but formatted in ways that require significant transformation before import into the replacement system.

**Integration re-wiring**: Every integration the old SaaS provided must be rebuilt. Every ITSM ticketing integration, every SSO configuration, every API webhook, every Slack notification. These integrations were built incrementally over years. Rebuilding them on an accelerated timeline creates defect risk.

**License overlap**: You will pay for both systems during the parallel run period. For enterprise SaaS contracts, this can represent 3-6 months of double licensing cost. This overlap period is never optional — you cannot cut over a complex SaaS tool without running both systems in parallel through at least one full business cycle.

**Organizational change management**: SaaS tools change how people work. The workflow that 500 engineers use daily will be different in the new tool. This is not a training issue — it is an organizational change management program. Budget for it explicitly or you will absorb the cost as productivity loss during and after cutover.

## Hidden Costs That Never Appear in the Business Case

Every vendor exit business case submitted for budget approval will underestimate the total program cost. This is not dishonesty — it is the predictable result of discovery bias: you cannot enumerate costs you have not yet discovered, and discovery happens during execution.

The categories that consistently appear after budget approval:

- **License true-up costs**: Many vendors have audit rights that activate during renewal or exit. Expect an audit when the vendor detects migration activity.
- **Data extraction fees**: Some vendors charge for bulk data export. This is deliberate lock-in. It should appear in contract review before signing any new agreement with a vendor.
- **Integration re-development**: Every integration the vendor provided as a feature must now be built or sourced elsewhere.
- **Knowledge transfer**: Years of institutional knowledge about vendor-specific configuration, workarounds, and quirks lives in team members' heads. When those team members leave — and attrition during long programs is real — that knowledge leaves with them.
- **Organizational resistance**: Teams build professional identities and workflows around tools. Resistance to migration is a real cost, measured in delays, parallel operation extensions, and re-training cycles.

Director standard: add 30-40% to any vendor exit business case to account for hidden costs. The business case that wins budget approval is almost never the actual cost of the program. Your credibility as a Director depends on managing this expectation with leadership before program start, not defending cost overruns after.

## Managing the Vendor Relationship During Exit

The commercial dynamic during vendor exit is adversarial in structure, even when both parties maintain professional courtesy. The vendor knows you are leaving. They have options: accelerate audits, reduce support responsiveness, or offer attractive retention pricing designed to slow your exit.

The Director's commercial playbook during vendor exit:

- **Maintain the relationship until you no longer need it.** You need support from this vendor until the day of final cutover. Burning the relationship early creates risk in the migration.
- **Do not disclose your exit timeline to the vendor** until you are contractually required to do so, or until you have migrated enough workloads that their leverage is minimal.
- **Negotiate clean exit terms during any renewal discussion.** If a contract renewal is coming during your migration window, use that moment to negotiate data portability guarantees, audit right limitations, and exit assistance terms.

---

## Director's Lens

Vendor exit programs are 18-36 month programs, not projects. They require VP-level executive sponsorship, dedicated program management, and budget protection across multiple fiscal cycles.

The most common failure mode for vendor exit programs is not technical — it is organizational. The program runs out of budget or executive sponsorship before completion, leaving the enterprise in a worse position than when it started: half-migrated, paying for both old and new, with neither fully operational.

Your job as a Director is to secure the commitments that make completion possible before the program starts, not to manage the consequences of insufficient commitment after the program is underway.
