# Chapter 4: Your Domain Managers

> Your domain managers are not executors of your will. They are owners of a function. Your job is to make sure each domain is in the right hands, that the hands have what they need, and that you know when something is going wrong before it becomes your problem.

---

## How to Use This Chapter

Each section below covers one domain manager role in your organization. The structure is consistent:

- **What they own** — scope of accountability
- **What you expect from them** — cadenced deliverables
- **What you give them** — your obligations to them
- **How to tell if this domain is healthy** — specific green/amber/red signals
- **How to tell if this domain is failing** — observable symptoms, not vibes

Read these as operating contracts. You are not managing tasks. You are managing a portfolio of domains, each of which must function independently of your daily attention.

## 1. Incident Manager

### What They Own

The Incident Manager owns the end-to-end incident lifecycle: process design for detection through closure, the Incident Commander (IC) training program, the on-call rotation structure and engineer wellness, the post-incident review process, and the incident metrics framework. They do not run every incident — they own the system that ensures every incident is run well.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- MTTR trend vs. target (current week vs. 30-day rolling avg)
- On-call load: pages per engineer per day this week
- P1 count and whether any P1s have open action items from PIR

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: MTTR by tier, MTTA, alert-to-page ratio, IC certification rate, on-call engineer churn risk
- One process improvement shipped this month and its measured impact

**Quarterly:**
- Domain health assessment: are we trending toward or away from the targets?
- On-call roster sustainability review: who is at risk of burnout? Who is undertrained?
- IC program maturity update: are ICs making decisions or escalating everything?

### What You Give Them

**Weekly 1:1 focus:**
- Unblock: what are they waiting on from you, from peer teams, from tooling?
- Advise: share one thing you observed from this week's incidents (read the summaries — do not make them brief you)
- Develop: one coaching item per month, not per week; do not turn every 1:1 into feedback

**Decision authority:**
- Full authority to modify on-call structure, IC rotation, and P2/below process without your approval
- Authority to defer a post-incident review date when the team needs recovery time

**Air cover:**
- When engineering teams push back on IC authority during an incident, you back your IC — publicly, in writing if needed
- When another Director complains that your IC "took too long" to restore service, you own that conversation; your IM does not defend themselves to peer Directors

**Development:**
- Sponsored access to incident management community (VOID, SRE community, Flock Safety SRE Blog level)
- Quarterly review of whether they are growing toward Senior Manager scope

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| MTTR trend (P1/P2 combined) | Improving or flat at target | Flat but above target for 2+ months | Increasing for 3+ months |
| On-call load | < 2 pages/engineer/day avg | 2–3 pages/engineer/day | > 3 pages/engineer/day |
| Repeat incident type within 90 days | Zero | One recurrence | Two+ recurrences |
| IC decision reversal post-PIR | < 5% of incidents | 5–15% | > 15% |
| PIR completion within SLA | > 95% | 80–95% | < 80% |
| On-call engineer attrition | Zero in past 6 months | 1 departure citing burnout | 2+ departures citing on-call load |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- You learn about a P1 from your VP's Slack message, not from the incident bridge or your IM's escalation.
- IC decisions are being overridden by senior engineers during the incident ("just let me fix it") with no process consequence.
- The same category of alert — memory pressure, connection pool exhaustion, certificate expiry — pages the on-call rotation every week.
- Post-incident reviews are being written but no action items are tracked to completion.
- Your on-call engineers are calling in sick on Monday after weekend on-call shifts.

## 2. Problem Manager

### What They Own

The Problem Manager owns the permanent elimination of recurring failures. This includes the Known Error Database (KEDB), the problem P-score prioritization model, the relationship with engineering teams to drive fix delivery, trend analysis from incident data, and the proactive problem identification process (finding problems before they cause incidents). They do not fix problems. They own the process that ensures problems get fixed.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- Problems opened this week vs. closed this week (are we keeping up?)
- Top 3 open problems by P-score and current fix ETA
- Any P1 problems that have slipped their committed fix date

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: Mean Time Between Incidents (MTBI) for known problem areas, KEDB size trend, problems closed with permanent fix vs. workaround, engineering team fix delivery rate
- Repeat incidents attributable to open known problems (this is your most important number)

**Quarterly:**
- P-score model calibration: is the scoring model routing priority correctly?
- Trend report: are we making the org more reliable or just managing the same failures more efficiently?

### What You Give Them

**Weekly 1:1 focus:**
- Unblock: engineering teams de-prioritizing problem fixes is the #1 blocker; you own the conversation with peer Directors when this happens
- Advise: review the top 10 KEDB items monthly and ask "which of these will still be open in 6 months?" — that is where to focus
- Develop: coach toward business case writing; a good Problem Manager can get engineering investment by framing repeat incidents in cost terms

**Decision authority:**
- Authority to P-score problems and publish priority without engineering team sign-off (they can contest, not veto)
- Authority to escalate a stalled problem to your level without asking permission first

**Air cover:**
- When engineering leadership says "we don't have time to fix that problem, it only happens occasionally," you translate the business impact and hold the line. Your Problem Manager should not have to fight that battle alone.

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| MTBI trend for top-10 known problems | Improving | Flat for 2+ months | Declining |
| Problems open > 30 days without fix ETA | Zero | 1–3 | > 3 |
| Same root cause recurrence within 90 days | Zero | 1 | 2+ |
| KEDB entries with permanent fix | > 60% | 40–60% | < 40% |
| Engineering fix delivery on committed date | > 80% | 60–80% | < 60% |
| Proactive problems (found before incident) | > 20% of total | 10–20% | < 10% |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- The KEDB is growing every month but MTBI for known problem areas is not improving — the team is documenting failures, not eliminating them.
- The same RCA section ("root cause: insufficient memory allocation") appears in P1 post-incident reviews six months apart.
- P1 problems are open for over 30 days with no committed fix delivery date and no escalation from your Problem Manager.
- Problem reviews happen but are attended only by the Problem Manager — engineering teams have stopped showing up.
- Your Problem Manager talks about problems in terms of tickets open and closed, not in terms of incidents prevented.

## 3. Change Manager

### What They Own

The Change Manager owns the change lifecycle: the change policy framework, CAB structure and governance, change categorization standards, the pre-approval program for standard changes, change freeze management, and the change risk model. They are accountable for the change failure rate and for ensuring the CAB is a risk-filtering function, not a rubber stamp or a bureaucratic gate.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- Change volume: standard / normal / emergency breakdown and % emergency vs. last 4 weeks
- Change failure rate this week vs. 90-day rolling average
- Any changes in flight with elevated risk this coming weekend

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: change failure rate by type and by team, emergency change rate, change lead time, CAB meeting throughput, pre-approved change adoption rate
- One policy or process improvement shipped and its measured impact on failure rate or lead time

**Quarterly:**
- CAB effectiveness review: are the right people attending? Is the right risk being caught?
- Change category audit: are things categorized correctly or is the team gaming the model?

### What You Give Them

**Weekly 1:1 focus:**
- Unblock: when a team is chronically submitting emergency changes, your Change Manager needs peer Director support to enforce the process; own that conversation
- Advise: review the change failure rate by team — is the failure concentration in one team? That is a coaching conversation for their manager, not a change process failure
- Develop: coach toward influencing without authority; a Change Manager who only enforces policy will be marginalized; one who teaches risk thinking will be sought out

**Decision authority:**
- Authority to reject a change or defer it from a CAB window without your approval
- Authority to call an emergency CAB without advance notice when a P1 requires it

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emergency change rate | < 10% of total | 10–15% | > 15% |
| Change failure rate (rolling 90-day) | < 5% | 5–10% | > 10% |
| Change failure rate trend | Improving or flat at target | Flat above target for 2+ months | Increasing |
| CAB meeting duration vs. scheduled | Within 15 minutes | 15–30 minutes over | Routinely running double |
| Standard change pre-approval adoption | > 60% of eligible changes | 40–60% | < 40% |
| Changes failing in prod same week as approval | < 2% | 2–5% | > 5% |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- Emergency change rate has been above 15% for three months and your Change Manager has not escalated the pattern to you.
- CAB meetings run 90 minutes every week and approve everything submitted — CAB has become a scheduling ceremony, not a risk filter.
- The change failure rate has not moved meaningfully in three months despite the team reviewing failures in the weekly meeting.
- Teams are splitting large changes into smaller ones to avoid normal-change scrutiny — the categorization model is being gamed and your Change Manager has not flagged it.
- You hear "CAB never actually stops anything" from engineering leads — that is a signal the function has lost credibility.

## 4. SLM Lead (Service Level Management)

### What They Own

The SLM Lead owns the SLA/SLO framework: defining service targets, measuring performance in real time, reporting accuracy, breach management, and the customer-facing SLA review process. They are the interface between your operational performance and your customer commitments. They do not own making services reliable — they own knowing exactly how reliable they are and making sure the right people know.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- SLO compliance by Tier 1 service (red/amber/green)
- Any SLA breach this week (contractual level) and current customer communication status
- Error budget consumption rate for services approaching breach threshold

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: SLO attainment by service and tier, error budget burn rate, breach frequency, customer dispute frequency, measurement accuracy audit
- Review of any SLOs that have not been reviewed with customers in 12+ months

**Quarterly:**
- SLA review cycle: have all contractual SLAs been reviewed and confirmed as accurate with customers?
- Error budget policy review: are error budget consequences being enforced?

### What You Give Them

**Weekly 1:1 focus:**
- Unblock: data source gaps are the #1 blocker; when a service has no reliable SLO measurement, escalate to the platform team immediately — do not let this drift
- Advise: ask "which SLO are we most likely to breach this month?" — this surfaces where to focus Availability Manager attention

**Air cover:**
- Customer disputes about SLA data accuracy go through you, not your SLM Lead — they prepare the data; you handle the relationship

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| SLO compliance rate (Tier 1) | > 99.5% | 98–99.5% | < 98% |
| SLA breaches discovered after fact (not real-time) | Zero | 1/quarter | 2+/quarter |
| Customer disputes over SLA measurement accuracy | Zero | 1/quarter | 2+/quarter |
| SLOs without real-time measurement | Zero | 1–2 services | 3+ services |
| Error budget policy enforcement | Enforced consistently | Enforced with exceptions | Rarely enforced |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- Your SLM Lead tells you about an SLA breach after the customer already called your VP.
- SLO attainment is being reported from Splunk dashboards built by the SLM Lead without validation against the source systems — the number looks good but nobody trusts it.
- Customer disputes about measurement methodology are being handled by your SLM Lead without your involvement — they will lose these conversations because they do not have the relationship authority.
- Error budgets exist in a spreadsheet and are never used to make a deployment or feature decision.

## 5. Availability Manager

### What They Own

The Availability Manager owns reliability at the system level: capacity planning for Tier 1 services, MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures) trending, DR test planning and execution, failure mode analysis, and the availability roadmap. They are the person who says "this system will fail under these conditions" before the conditions occur. They work closely with the Performance Lead and the Architecture Lead — those roles generate inputs; Availability translates them into risk.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- Any Tier 1 service approaching capacity threshold (CPU, memory, connection pool, message queue depth — your RabbitMQ/Kafka context matters here)
- MTBF trend for Tier 1 services vs. target
- Status of any open DR action items

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: MTBF by Tier 1 service, capacity headroom by service, DR test completion rate, single points of failure identified vs. mitigated
- Capacity forecast for the next 90 days with assumptions stated

**Quarterly:**
- DR test results: what failed during the test? What was fixed? What is still open?
- Availability roadmap: what investments are needed to maintain or improve MTBF targets?

### What You Give Them

**Air cover:**
- When engineering teams deprioritize DR test prep ("we can't spare the capacity"), this escalates to you. A DR test that never runs is not a DR capability — it is a false assumption.
- Capacity investment requests require your sponsorship at the VP level; your Availability Manager cannot secure that budget alone.

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| MTBF trend (Tier 1 services) | Improving or flat at target | Flat below target 2+ months | Declining |
| DR test completion | Within 12 months, all Tier 1 | 13–18 months | > 18 months or never |
| DR test pass rate | > 90% of scenarios pass | 75–90% | < 75% |
| Capacity headroom (Tier 1 at peak) | > 30% | 15–30% | < 15% |
| Single points of failure with no mitigation plan | Zero | 1–2 | 3+ |
| Capacity forecasting accuracy (90-day) | Within 10% | 10–25% error | > 25% error |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- DR has not been tested in 18 months because "we never have a good window" — that is a planning failure, not a scheduling problem.
- A Tier 1 service fails due to connection pool exhaustion that the team knew was trending toward the limit but did not escalate.
- Capacity planning is done annually in a spreadsheet during budget season, not continuously — the first signal of a capacity problem is a P1.
- Your Availability Manager can tell you what MTBF is today but cannot tell you where it will be in 90 days.

## 6. Environment Manager

### What They Own

The Environment Manager owns the full environment lifecycle for non-production and production-adjacent infrastructure: environment provisioning and decommission, configuration trueup (QA mirrors prod), environment-to-environment promotion gates, environment availability SLOs for development teams, and config drift detection. The "works in QA, breaks in prod" failure mode is their problem to prevent.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- Environment trueup completion rate (are QA/staging configs current against prod?)
- Any environment unavailability this week that blocked a development team
- Config drift incidents this week (where environment delta caused a test failure or prod issue)

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: environment availability by environment, trueup completion rate, config drift incidents, environment provisioning lead time, environment-caused prod incidents
- Promotion gate pass rate by stage

**Quarterly:**
- Environment consolidation review: are we running environments that are no longer actively used?
- Tooling review: are IaC patterns (Terraform, Helm) current and consistently applied?

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Works in QA, not prod" incidents | Zero/month | 1/month | 2+/month |
| Environment trueup completion rate | > 95% | 80–95% | < 80% |
| Config drift incidents per month | 0–1 | 2–3 | 4+ |
| Environment availability for dev teams (business hours) | > 99% | 95–99% | < 95% |
| Environment provisioning lead time for a new env | < 1 business day | 1–3 days | > 3 days |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- Your P1 incident RCA reads "configuration mismatch between staging and production" for the third time this year.
- Development teams are maintaining their own environment configs in spreadsheets because they do not trust the official trueup process.
- Environment provisioning requires a ticket to the platform team and takes a week — teams are bypassing the process and building their own.
- Your Environment Manager knows the environment is drifted but has not escalated because there is no agreed process for what to do about it.

## 7. Performance Lead

### What They Own

The Performance Lead owns the performance engineering discipline: baseline establishment for Tier 1 services, performance gate design for CI/CD pipelines, regression detection, capacity-correlated performance modeling, and the performance testing library. They prevent performance regressions from reaching production. When a perf regression does reach production, they are the person who finds it.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- Any performance gates failed this week (and what happened — fix, exception, bypass?)
- Performance regressions found in production this week
- Baseline coverage status: what % of Tier 1 services have a current performance baseline?

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: perf gate pass rate, exception rate, bypass rate, regression catch rate (found in testing vs. found in production), baseline coverage by tier
- One performance improvement delivered (optimization, gate improvement, or baseline expansion)

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| Performance regressions caught before production | > 80% of detected | 60–80% | < 60% |
| Perf regressions found in production | 0–1/month | 2–3/month | 4+/month |
| Baseline coverage (Tier 1 services) | > 90% | 70–90% | < 70% |
| Performance gate bypass rate | < 5% | 5–10% | > 10% |
| Gate exception rate (bypass with approval) | < 10% | 10–20% | > 20% |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- Performance regressions are being found by customers, not by the performance gate.
- Tier 1 services do not have a current performance baseline — "we used to have one but it got stale."
- Performance gates are being bypassed routinely because they are "too slow" — the gates have become friction without function.
- Your Performance Lead reports gate pass/fail counts but cannot tell you which regressions the gates actually prevented.

## 8. Platform Architecture Lead

### What They Own

The Platform Architecture Lead owns the technical standards that your operations organization builds on: the Architecture Review Board (ARB) process, Architecture Decision Records (ADRs), the standards exception process, technology radar maintenance, and technical debt visibility. They are not the CTO. They govern the operational technology standards within your domain and are the voice of technical sanity when a team wants to introduce something that will create operational complexity.

### What You Expect from Them

**Weekly (3-bullet ops review format):**
- ADRs published or closed this week
- Any standards exceptions requested this week (what, by whom, and recommendation)
- ARB participation rate for the most recent review session

**Monthly:**
- Domain metrics report: open ADRs by age, standards exception rate (% of new systems requesting exception), ARB decision throughput, technology radar update status
- One standards artifact published or updated

**Quarterly:**
- Technology radar review: what is new, what is moving out, what is creating operational risk?
- Technical debt register: what are the top-10 technical debt items within your operational domain, and what is the cost of carrying them?

### Health Signals

| Signal | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| ADRs open > 60 days without decision | Zero | 1–2 | 3+ |
| Standards exception rate (new systems) | < 5% | 5–10% | > 10% |
| ARB participation rate | > 80% of stakeholders | 60–80% | < 60% |
| Technology radar last updated | < 6 months ago | 6–12 months | > 12 months |
| Technical debt items with no mitigation plan | < 20% of register | 20–40% | > 40% |

### How to Tell This Domain Is Failing

- Engineering teams are making architectural decisions without going through ARB because "ARB takes too long and doesn't add value."
- More than 10% of new systems are running on non-standard technology with approved exceptions — the standards are losing ground faster than they are being enforced.
- ADRs are being written but not decided — they sit in draft for months because the Architecture Lead cannot get the right people in the room.
- Your Architecture Lead can tell you what the standards are, but cannot tell you how many current systems actually comply with them.

## Running Your Manager Portfolio

### The Weekly Ops Review Pattern

Every domain manager gives you three bullets — not a status report, not a presentation. Three bullets:
1. This is where we stand vs. target.
2. This is the one thing that is off or at risk.
3. This is what I need from you, if anything.

If a manager cannot give you three bullets in under three minutes, their domain health is not being measured. Help them build the measurement, then require the format.

### The Monthly Domain Metrics Report

Each domain manager publishes a metrics report on the same day every month. You read it before the 1:1 — you do not make them brief you on data you could have read. The 1:1 uses the report as a starting point, not a substitute for preparation.

### The Quarterly Health Assessment

Each domain manager presents a health assessment against their domain's green/amber/red signals. This is your formal mechanism for surfacing domain risk before it becomes your VP's problem. Any domain in red for two consecutive quarters needs a written remediation plan.

---

> **WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE**
>
> Every domain manager can give you a 3-minute status on their domain without notes, because they are measuring it every day. Domain health is visible on a shared dashboard that you check before asking anyone a question. Managers escalate policy questions to you ("should our SLO window be rolling or calendar month?") and handle operational questions themselves ("we had a breach, here is how I handled it"). Your weekly ops review runs in 25 minutes because each manager is prepared. You learn about problems from your managers, not from your VP. A new team member can look at any domain's health dashboard and understand whether the domain is operating correctly.

---

> **WHAT FAILURE LOOKS LIKE**
>
> You learn about a domain problem from your VP's Slack message or a customer complaint — your manager knew and did not escalate. Domain managers solve today's problem but never address the pattern — the MTTR improves for one week, then reverts. Domain metrics either do not exist ("we don't really track that") or exist but are not trusted ("those numbers are never accurate"). Your domain managers come to your 1:1 with problem descriptions but no recommendations — they are waiting for you to tell them what to do. You find yourself attending domain meetings to stay informed because there is no dashboard, no report, and no consistent status format. You are managing seven domains in detail because the managers have stopped managing their own.
