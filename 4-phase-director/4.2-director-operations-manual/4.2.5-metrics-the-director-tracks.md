# Chapter 5: Metrics the Director Tracks

You do not manage by gut feel at the Director level. You manage by signals — specific numbers that tell you whether the organization is healthy before someone walks into your office with a problem. Your metrics portfolio has four cadences: daily (5 minutes), weekly (ops review), monthly (VP reporting), and quarterly (executive scorecard). Each cadence answers a different question. Daily: is anything on fire? Weekly: are we improving? Monthly: are we meeting commitments? Quarterly: are we winning?

If any metric requires you to open a ticket, ask an engineer, or wait for a report to be compiled, that metric is not instrumented — it is a gap. Fix it before you are asked about it in an executive meeting.

---

## Daily Metrics — The 5-Minute Morning Check

You run this every morning before your first meeting. It takes five minutes if your dashboard is built correctly.

**SLO burn rate** — Are any Tier 1 services burning error budget faster than the expected pace? Normal burn rate is 1× (you consume the budget evenly over the month). A 3× burn rate means you will exhaust your error budget in one-third the remaining time. That is your trigger to engage. You do not wait for the SLO to breach — you act when the burn rate tells you it will breach. At T-Mobile scale, a Tier 1 API gateway burning at 5× in the first week of the month will exhaust 35 days of budget in 7 days. That is a Director-level conversation, not a team-level one.

**Open P1 incidents — count and age** — How many are open right now? Any P1 that has been open for more than 2 hours has your attention. You are not running the bridge. But you are checking that your Incident Commander has it, that the right people are engaged, and that the VP is already aware. A P1 incident is not a technical event. It is a business event.

**Overnight change outcomes** — Did anything deployed in last night's change window fail or get rolled back? A failed change is not automatically a problem — rollbacks are healthy when they work correctly. What concerns you is a failed change with no rollback, a change that succeeded deployment but degraded a downstream service, or a change that was not captured in the change record. If your overnight failure rate is above 5%, your change process has a problem.

**On-call health** — Did any engineer get paged more than 3 times overnight? Chronic overnight paging is not an alerting problem. It is a system reliability problem that is draining your engineers and creating a future attrition risk. If the same person is paged 3+ nights in a row, you rotate the on-call schedule today — not next sprint.

## Weekly Metrics — Monday Ops Review

This is what you present in the weekly operations review. Every number has a target. If it is green, you move on. If it is yellow or red, you have a recommendation — not just a data point.

**MTTR trend (rolling 4 weeks)** — Tier 1 target: under 30 minutes. Tier 2 target: under 2 hours. The rolling window matters. A single bad week is an incident. A 4-week trend moving the wrong direction is a structural problem. When MTTR trends up, look first at toil — are engineers spending incident time on manual steps that should be automated? Then look at runbooks — are they current and complete?

**Incident count by severity** — P1 target: fewer than 2 per week. P2 target: fewer than 10 per week. P3 is directionally useful but not Director-level action unless volume spikes. If P1 count exceeds target, you want to know: same service, different services, or correlated (e.g., a shared dependency)? Pattern determines response.

**Change failure rate** — DORA elite target: under 5%. This is the percentage of changes that cause a degradation or require rollback. If you are above 10%, your deployment pipeline needs attention. If you are above 20%, your change process is broken and you need to declare it before someone else does.

**Open P1 problems — count and days open** — Target: fewer than 5 open P1 problems, none older than 30 days. Problems are not incidents. A problem is a known recurring or high-impact root cause that has not been permanently fixed. If you have 12 open P1 problems and the oldest is 90 days, your Problem Management program is funding future incidents.

**Environment incidents and MTTE** — Target: MTTE (Mean Time to Environment) under 4 hours. This is your non-production reliability signal. Developers blocked by broken environments are not shipping. Environment MTTE above 8 hours means your engineering teams are not working at full capacity. At enterprise scale, that translates directly to delayed releases and missed delivery commitments.

**SLO compliance by service** — Tier 1 target: 99.9% or higher. You look at this weekly not because you expect it to change dramatically week-to-week, but because a steady week-over-week decline is detectable before month-end breach — and you would rather raise it in Monday ops review than explain it to the VP on the 31st.

## Monthly Dashboard — VP Report

This is your formal upward communication. The VP does not need to know what MTTR means — they need to know whether the operations organization is meeting its commitments and what it will cost to maintain or improve them.

**Availability actual vs. target by tier:**

| Tier | Target | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| Tier 0 | 99.99% | Core network, payment processing — 52 minutes downtime/year maximum |
| Tier 1 | 99.9% | Customer-facing APIs, authenticated sessions — 8.7 hours/year maximum |
| Tier 2 | 99.5% | Internal tools, reporting, batch systems — 43.8 hours/year maximum |
| Tier 3 | 99.0% | Dev/test, non-critical tooling — 87.6 hours/year maximum |

Report actuals. If Tier 1 came in at 99.86%, say so and explain what moved it and what is being done about it.

**Error budget consumption by service** — This tells your VP which services are "spending" their reliability investment and which are coasting with headroom. A service that consumes 80% of its error budget every month needs engineering investment. A service at 20% budget consumption is over-engineered relative to its SLO — consider whether that engineering resource is better deployed elsewhere.

**DORA metrics — the four-number summary of engineering velocity:**

| DORA Metric | Elite Target | What It Means in Plain Terms |
|-------------|-------------|------------------------------|
| Deployment Frequency | On demand (multiple/day) | How often are you shipping safely? |
| Lead Time for Changes | Under 1 hour | How fast can a committed change reach production? |
| Change Failure Rate | 0–5% | What percentage of changes break something? |
| MTTR | Under 1 hour | How fast do you recover when they do? |

You are not expected to be at elite on all four from day one. But you need to know where you are and which dimension is the priority investment for this quarter.

**Problem backlog health** — P1/P2 open count, age distribution, and fix delivery rate. Target: 80% of P1 problems resolved within 30 days. If you are below 60%, your team is triaging problems but not closing them — which means the same incidents will recur.

**Change volume mix** — What percentage of your changes were normal, standard, or emergency? Emergency change target: under 10% of total volume. If emergency changes are 25% of your volume, your change process is a bottleneck — teams are routing around it instead of through it.

**SLA breach count and credits issued** — Report the number, the dollar value, and the affected customer segments. If the VP learns the credit amount from Finance before they hear it from you, you have a trust problem.

**On-call load** — Pages per engineer per week. Target: under 2 per day average across the on-call rotation. Above 3 per day on average is a burnout risk that will surface as attrition within 6 months.

**Team health** — Open headcount (unfilled roles), attrition risk flags (anyone you know is at flight risk), and PTO coverage gaps (who is the single-person-on-call for a critical system in July).

## Quarterly Scorecard — Executive Reporting

This is your board-level picture. Five numbers, each with a trend arrow, each with a one-sentence narrative.

**Operational Excellence Score** — A composite index you build from three equally weighted components: availability performance (40%), MTTR performance (30%), and repeat incident rate (30%). Normalize each to a 0–100 scale against your targets. Report the composite. If the composite is above 85, you are operating well. Between 70–85 is manageable with visible improvement trajectory. Below 70 is a risk flag that requires an improvement plan.

**Reliability Investment ROI** — What did incidents cost the organization this quarter (SLA credits issued + estimated engineer hours + customer impact estimate), and how does that compare to the prior quarter? If you invested in automation or reliability engineering, this is where you show the return. "We spent $400K on SRE tooling and avoided $1.8M in SLA credits" is a capital allocation argument, not a tech argument.

**Transformation velocity** — How many applications were modernized, migrated, or retired this quarter? How many legacy systems are still running past their target sunset date? Executives care about this because modernization reduces operational risk over time. A system running on EOL infrastructure is a liability that compounds.

**Architecture compliance** — What percentage of new systems deployed this quarter followed your architectural principles (observability standards, SLO definitions, runbook requirements, DR classification)? How many architecture exceptions were granted and why? If exception count is rising, your standards are either too strict or too slow.

**Team capability** — Headcount vs. plan, senior-to-junior ratio, key person risk count (how many services have only one engineer who fully understands them). Key person risk above 3 is a Director-level concern. Above 5 is an executive-level concern.

## Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

Most of the metrics above are lagging — they tell you what already happened. Three leading indicators predict future MTTR degradation before it shows up in the numbers. Act on these early.

**On-call load (pages per engineer per week)** — When on-call load creeps above 3 pages per day, engineers start writing worse runbooks, skipping postmortems, and missing the subtle signals that precede major failures. MTTR will degrade 4–8 weeks after on-call load starts rising. You see on-call load go up before MTTR moves. Address it first.

**Toil percentage** — The percentage of your team's operational capacity spent on manual, repetitive, automatable work with no lasting value. DORA research and Google's SRE literature both treat toil above 50% as unsustainable. At 40% toil, engineers stop improving systems because they are too busy maintaining them. Track this via your team's sprint velocity in reliability work vs. toil work. If toil is above 40%, your automation backlog needs to be treated as a reliability risk, not just an efficiency improvement.

**KEDB growth rate** — The Known Error Database (or your equivalent problem backlog) should be shrinking or stable. If it is growing by more than 2–3 entries per month net, you are identifying more problems than you are resolving. That gap compounds. Six months of growing KEDB means you have a backlog of recurring incidents waiting to happen. Track the net growth rate, not just the count.

## Your 6-Tile Morning Dashboard

You should be able to assess operational health in 5 minutes or fewer. Six tiles, each with a current value and a target. Green means clear. Yellow means watch. Red means act today.

| Tile | What It Shows | Green | Yellow | Red |
|------|---------------|-------|--------|-----|
| **P1 Count** | Open P1 incidents right now | 0 | 1, age < 2h | 1 aged > 2h, or any count ≥ 2 |
| **SLO Burn** | Highest burn rate across Tier 1 services | < 2× | 2–3× | > 3× |
| **Change Success Rate** | Last 24-hour window success % | > 95% | 90–95% | < 90% |
| **On-Call Load** | Pages/engineer last 24h (max across rotation) | < 3 | 3–5 | > 5 |
| **Open P1 Problems** | Count of open P1 Problem records | < 3 | 3–5 | > 5, or any > 30 days |
| **Repeat Incident Rate** | Rolling 30-day same-cause recurrence % | < 5% | 5–15% | > 15% |

This dashboard should be built in Splunk (search-driven tiles using ServiceNow data pulled via the ServiceNow add-on) or Datadog (with ServiceNow integration for problem/incident counts). It should load without manual data entry. If you are copying numbers from emails into a spreadsheet to build this view, you do not have a dashboard — you have a manual process that will fail when you need it most.

## What to Do When a Metric Goes Red

Each red metric has a specific response. Do not improvise.

**SLO burn rate > 3×** — Pull up the SLO definition for the burning service. Identify which error category is driving the burn (error rate, latency, or availability). Page the service owner. If burn rate is above 5× and the service is Tier 1, notify your VP within 1 hour — before the SLO breaches, not after.

**Open P1 > 2 hours** — Call the Incident Commander directly. Get a one-sentence status. If the bridge has the right people and a working theory, you step back. If the bridge is looping or has not identified a hypothesis, you escalate: bring in a senior architect or the service owner's engineering manager.

**Change failure rate > 10% in a week** — Stop discretionary changes for 48 hours. Conduct an emergency change review: pull the last 20 failed changes, identify common patterns (same deployment pipeline, same team, same environment, same type of change). Present findings to the Engineering VP by end of week.

**Open P1 problems > 5 or any > 30 days** — Your Problem Manager is either understaffed, under-empowered, or blocked on engineering capacity. Meet with them this week. For each problem over 30 days, you want: who is the owner, what is blocking closure, and what is the risk of this problem causing another incident before it is fixed.

**Repeat incident rate > 15%** — Your postmortem process is not producing action items that get done. Pull the last 10 repeated incidents. How many had postmortems? How many postmortems had action items? How many action items were completed? If the answer is "postmortems happened but action items sat in JIRA," you have a prioritization problem. Reliability action items must be first-class sprint work, not background tasks.

**On-call load > 5 pages/day/engineer** — Escalate to Engineering VP. You cannot fix chronic on-call load through operational heroics. You need engineering capacity redirected to reliability work. Make the case with data: at 5 pages/day, this engineer is spending 3–4 hours in incident response. That is not sustainable and is a retention risk.

> **What good looks like**
>
> All six morning tiles are populated automatically — no Slack messages sent to gather data, no spreadsheets refreshed, no waiting on a team member to pull a report. You open your dashboard at 8:05 AM, read it in 3 minutes, and know whether to go straight to your first meeting or make one call first. Your monthly VP report pulls from the same data sources as your daily dashboard — there is no "VP version" and "internal version." When a metric trends red, you are already preparing your narrative before anyone asks. The metrics tell the story before the problems become headlines.

> **What failure looks like**
>
> You arrive at the monthly ops review and someone asks what your MTTR was last quarter. You do not know because it takes your team three days to compile the report from four different systems — by hand. You have two sets of numbers: the ones you use internally (honest, sometimes uncomfortable) and the ones you present to the VP (polished, rounded, missing the bad weeks). When the VP asks a follow-up question based on the VP version, you cannot answer it because you are not sure which number is right. A P1 incident happened at 2 AM last Tuesday and you learn about it at 10 AM because no one thought to page you. Your repeat incident rate is "unknown" because no one has built the query that would calculate it.
