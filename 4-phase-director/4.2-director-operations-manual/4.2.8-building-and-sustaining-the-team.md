# Chapter 8: Building and Sustaining the Team

> "Your job is not to be the best engineer in the room. Your job is to build the room where the best engineering happens without you in it."

At Director scale — 40 to 100 engineers in scope — you cannot carry individual technical problems. Your leverage is structural: the team you build, the managers you develop, the conditions you create for people to stay and grow. Everything in this chapter is about making your org work when you are not watching.

---

## Recommended Team Structure

For a mid-size enterprise operations org (40–100 engineers in scope, multiple production platforms, 24x7 availability requirements), the following structure distributes accountability without creating fiefdoms.

```
Director of Operations
│
├── Incident & Problem Management Manager
│       Owns: incident command, problem management, KEDB,
│             on-call governance, blameless culture, RCA quality
│
├── Change & Release Management Manager
│       Owns: CAB, DORA metrics, change governance,
│             release readiness gates, environment management
│
├── Platform Reliability Manager
│       Owns: SLM, SLO/SLA tracking, availability reporting,
│             capacity planning, performance engineering
│
└── Architecture & Transformation Lead
        Owns: ARB, technical principles, portfolio modernization,
              toil reduction roadmap, platform standards
```

Each manager owns a domain end-to-end: they set the standards, run the processes, own the metrics, and escalate only what requires your authority. That last part — escalation discipline — is what you spend month 2 establishing (see Chapter 7, delegation charters).

The Architecture & Transformation Lead is not a manager in the people-management sense — this role owns technical direction and platform modernization without carrying a direct team. At T-Mobile or comparable enterprise scale, this is the role that carries your Kafka/RabbitMQ/EKS modernization work forward while your other three managers keep the lights on.

**Span of control target:** Each manager owns 8–15 engineers. Below 8 is a team lead, not a manager. Above 15 and you will lose visibility into individual performance before attrition signals reach you.

## Hiring Philosophy

You hire twice: once for the role, once for the org. Every hire either raises or lowers the bar for everyone already there. Mediocre hires do not maintain status quo — they pull the bar down because strong engineers notice and adjust their own expectations.

### The Three Non-Negotiables

Every operations hire at senior level and above must demonstrate all three. If one is absent, the hire fails regardless of technical depth.

**1. Incident experience under pressure**
Not "familiarity with incident management." Has this person been in the room when a P1 is at hour 3, leadership is asking for an ETA, two hypotheses have already been ruled out, and someone needs to make a call? Ask them to narrate the last P1 they commanded. What decision did they make that they still question? The answer to that second question tells you more than the answer to the first.

**2. Process thinking**
Can they design a repeatable system, not just solve a one-off problem? The test: ask them to describe a process they designed from scratch — not inherited, not improved, built. What did they consider? What did they throw out? What would they do differently? Operations leaders who only solve problems produce tribal knowledge that walks out when they do.

**3. Communication span**
Can they speak to an on-call engineer in the language of signals and runbooks, and then walk into the VP staff meeting and speak in the language of business impact and risk? Not two different people for two different audiences — one person who can shift register without losing precision. Ask them to explain a recent major incident to you as if you are the VP who needs to decide whether to declare a customer notification. Then ask them to explain it as if you are the engineer who needs to write the fix. Watch whether the substance changes or only the framing.

### Hiring Rubric

| Role | Must-Have | Nice-to-Have | Red Flags |
|------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Incident & Problem Manager | P1 command experience; RCA authorship; blameless culture in practice | ITIL certification; chaos engineering exposure | Blames individuals in postmortems; cannot separate symptom from cause |
| Change & Release Manager | CAB governance; DORA awareness; rollback planning | SAFe or Agile Release Train experience; feature flag experience | Every change is low risk; never challenged a change request |
| Platform Reliability Manager | SLO design; capacity modeling; Kubernetes or equivalent | Prometheus/Grafana; performance engineering; chaos tooling | Confuses SLA compliance with reliability; reactive only |
| Architecture & Transformation Lead | ARB or design review ownership; modernization delivery; hands-on platform depth | Cost engineering; FinOps; developer platform experience | Designs without delivering; cannot defend trade-offs under pressure |
| Principal SRE | On-call ownership; automation authorship; incident command | Kafka/RabbitMQ or message bus depth; Splunk or observability tooling | Cannot explain why a system fails; only knows happy path |

### What Not to Do

Do not hire your clone. If you are the pattern-recognizer who sees systemic risk from across the room, you need a detail-oriented domain owner who catches the individual change that will break at 2am. If you are the relationship builder who smooths cross-functional friction, you need a process designer who will not let consensus replace standards. Operational excellence requires cognitive diversity at the manager layer — it does not happen through uniformity.

Do not use whiteboard assessments for operations roles. Assess with scenarios drawn from real operational history. The assessment for a Principal SRE hire:

> "Walk me through the last P1 incident you commanded from declaration to close. I want the full story: who called it, what you knew and when, every hypothesis you chased, the decision that broke the incident open, and one thing you would do differently in the response. Then tell me what you changed in the system or process after it was over."

A candidate who cannot produce a specific, detailed answer to that question has not commanded real incidents at real scale.

## Developing Managers

Hiring managers is not a one-time act. A manager who was right for the role 18 months ago may have reached a ceiling, be developing toward the next level, or be burning out — and unless you have a deliberate development practice, you will not know which until it's too late.

### Quarterly Stretch Assignments

Every manager receives one assignment per quarter that takes them outside their domain. The goal is not to cross-train for coverage — it is to surface how they think when they are not the expert in the room.

Examples:
- The Incident Manager runs a Change governance retrospective for a quarter — they have to understand CAB quality from the change owner's perspective, not the responder's
- The Change Manager leads an architecture review — they are now defending technical decisions they did not make, to engineers who know more than they do
- The Platform Reliability Manager presents the quarterly scorecard to the executive team — they have to translate operational metrics into business language without your scaffolding

What you are measuring: How do they perform under unfamiliar pressure? Do they default to their domain expertise or do they adapt? A manager who cannot operate outside their home domain is a specialist, not a leader.

### Skip-Level 1:1s

Run monthly skip-level 1:1s with two to three individual contributors per month, rotating so you cover the full team quarterly. Keep it to 30 minutes. Tell the manager it is happening — no ambush. Ask:

1. "What is your manager doing well that I should know about?"
2. "What does your manager need to get better at?"
3. "What should I know about the state of this team that doesn't make it to the weekly ops review?"

The third question surfaces the information that is edited out of formal reporting. Not because managers lie — because they normalize problems they are living with. A team member who has been paged 12 nights in a row may not even mention it because it feels like the baseline. You need to know it is not.

Bring what you learn from skip-levels back to the manager in their next 1:1. Not as a gotcha — as data. "I heard from a few people on your team that the on-call rotation feels unsustainable. Walk me through what you're seeing and what you're thinking about it."

### Succession Planning — Annual, Not Optional

For each manager role, you answer three questions every year before the Q4 planning cycle:

1. If this person left in 90 days, who is the internal candidate?
2. What is that candidate's specific gap for the role today?
3. What is the 12-month development plan to close that gap?

If you cannot answer question 1 with a real name, you have a succession gap. If you cannot answer question 3 with specific actions, the development plan does not exist and the gap will not close.

Do not share succession plans downward — people who know they are "the backup" often either wait for the role or feel demoted when they do not get it. Keep the plan at director level. Use it to shape assignments, give exposure, and sponsor visibility — not to telegraph outcomes.

## Team Health: Leading Indicators

Team health problems that show up as attrition or performance collapse always had early signals. The following metrics are leading indicators — they tell you the problem is forming, not that it has already formed. Manage these aggressively and you will rarely need to manage a resignation letter.

### Team Health Dashboard

| Metric | Target | Measurement Method | Frequency | Escalation Trigger |
|--------|--------|-------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| On-call pages per engineer (4-week rolling avg) | < 2 pages/night | PagerDuty or equivalent — per-engineer report | Monthly | Any engineer > 2 pages/night triggers staffing or runbook review |
| On-call escalation concentration | No 2 engineers handle > 40% of total escalations | PagerDuty escalation routing stats | Monthly | Concentrated routing = single point of failure |
| Toil percentage | < 50% of eng time | 2-week self-report, quarterly | Quarterly | > 60% = team is in maintenance mode, not improving |
| New hire ramp time | < 90 days to first independent on-call shift | Tracked in hiring/onboarding tracker | Per cohort | > 90 days = onboarding is broken |
| Annual attrition | < 15% | HR data — rolling 12 months | Quarterly | > 15% triggers root cause review: staffing, compensation, or on-call |
| Open P1 problem age | Zero problems open > 45 days without a fix date | ITSM backlog query | Weekly ops review | Any P1 > 45 days without owner + date is escalated to director |
| Manager 1:1 coverage | 100% of direct reports met weekly | Manager self-report; spot-check via skip-levels | Monthly | Missed 1:1s are an early signal of manager overload or avoidance |

Track these in the same system you use for operational metrics. Team health is an operational metric — it has the same failure modes as platform health: silent degradation, threshold crossings, and cascading failures.

## Retaining Top People

Retention is not a compensation problem. It is a work-conditions problem that compensation cannot fix.

The primary reason SRE and operations engineers leave — not the stated reason, the actual reason — is toil. They feel like they are fighting the same fires they fought last quarter and the quarter before. The platform is not getting better; it is just staying the same level of broken with their continuous effort holding it in place. When a competitor offers them a role on a greenfield platform, they take it — not for the salary, but for the chance to stop treading water.

Your primary retention lever is visible, measurable progress on toil reduction. When engineers can see that the alert that used to page them at 2am is gone — automated away or fixed at the source — they feel like the work is moving in a direction. That feeling is worth more than a 10% compensation adjustment.

### Career Path Visibility

Make the tracks explicit, write them down, and review them in 1:1s.

Three tracks for operations engineers:

**1. Technical Track (Individual Contributor)**
SRE II → Senior SRE → Principal SRE → Distinguished Engineer (if your org has it)

Principal SRE criteria: owns the reliability design for a platform or domain; authors runbooks and operational principles others follow; can lead an architecture review; is consulted on major changes before they hit CAB; independently commands P1 incidents for any service in the portfolio.

**2. Architecture Track**
Senior SRE → Reliability Architect → Platform Architect

Architect criteria: designs systems for operability before they are built, not after; owns the ARB process for a domain; authors the technical principles the team operates by; drives modernization roadmaps.

**3. Management Track**
Senior SRE → Tech Lead → Operations Manager → Director

Not every Principal SRE wants to manage. Never assume the track. Ask directly: "Where do you want to be in 18 months — deeper into the technical track, or are you interested in the management path?" Have the conversation annually, not once.

### Recognition — Making the Invisible Visible

Operations work has the worst recognition problem in technology. When everything is working, no one notices. When something breaks, everyone notices the breaking. The people who prevented 50 P1s through good capacity planning, runbook quality, and automated remediation are invisible — unless you make them visible.

Specific practices:
- In the monthly VP status report, name the individuals whose work produced measurable outcomes. Not "the team improved MTTR" — "Patel's runbook redesign for the payment gateway cut MTTR on that service from 48 minutes to 9 minutes over the quarter."
- In the Weekly Ops Review, close any discussion of a metric improvement with the name of who drove it.
- When a major incident is resolved under target MTTR, send a specific acknowledgment to the incident commander and the on-call engineer within 24 hours — before the postmortem, not after.

Recognition is not a soft skill. It is an operational practice that directly reduces attrition.

## The Toil Reduction Program

Toil is not a feeling. It is a measurable operational condition with a target and a budget.

**Definition**: Toil is manual, repetitive, automatable work that scales with system load rather than with engineering judgment. Restarting a service because no one has fixed the memory leak is toil. Reviewing 300 CAB entries because the risk scoring is manual is toil. Running the same Splunk query every Monday morning because no dashboard exists is toil.

**The target**: < 50% of engineering time on toil and maintenance; > 50% on improvement, automation, and new capability.

**How to measure it**: Do not ask engineers to log time continuously — that is itself toil. Run a 2-week measurement sprint quarterly. Ask teams to self-report by category: planned improvement work, unplanned reactive work, maintenance (scheduled but manual), toil (repetitive and automatable). Aggregate per team. The precision does not need to be perfect — you are looking for order-of-magnitude signals, not audit-grade accuracy.

### Toil Reduction as OKR

Pair every quarter's toil measurement with a targeted reduction OKR:

```
Objective: Reduce operational toil and reclaim engineering capacity

KR1: Toil percentage drops from 68% to 55% by end of Q2
     (baseline from Q1 measurement sprint)
KR2: Automated remediation handles 3 alert categories that currently 
     require manual intervention (specific alerts named in the KR)
KR3: Monthly false-positive alert volume reduced by 40% through 
     runbook-driven tuning (from current 120 false positives/month 
     to < 72)
```

**The toil backlog**: Create a dedicated backlog for toil reduction items. Track them in the same system as feature work. Prioritize by impact × frequency: a daily manual process that takes 30 minutes is higher priority than a monthly one that takes 2 hours, even though the monthly one has more total minutes — because the daily process also interrupts flow and compounds fatigue.

Make the backlog visible in the Weekly Ops Review. If the toil backlog has not had a closed item in two weeks, ask why — is it being deprioritized, is the work blocked, or is no one accountable for it?

---

> ### What Good Looks Like
>
> Your managers make independent decisions — at the level defined in their delegation charters — without calling you first. You find out by reading the weekly ops summary, not by being in the room. Toil is decreasing quarter over quarter and the engineers on the teams can tell you which specific automated remediations they shipped that made it decrease. Every manager role has at least one named internal successor with a documented development plan. No engineer on any team is averaging more than 2 pages per night over a 4-week rolling period. When a strong engineer announces they are leaving, you are surprised — not because the signals were absent, but because the team health indicators were green and the retention conversations were happening. Annual attrition is below 12%. The last two external hires hit full independent contribution within 75 days.

---

> ### What Failure Looks Like
>
> The same two people absorb every on-call escalation above tier 1. Everyone on the team knows who they are. Those two people are 14 months from leaving, and when they leave, you will have a knowledge cliff, not a staffing gap — because the knowledge was never distributed. Your engineers report spending 70–75% of their time on toil, which means the platform is not getting better under your watch — it is staying the same level of broken through heroic maintenance. Good engineers who joined 18 months ago are posting their resumes, because they were promised improvement work and received maintenance work. You have no succession plan for any manager role. If the Incident Manager walks out tomorrow, you are the Incident Manager. The team health dashboard does not exist — you are managing by the feeling in the room, which is the last signal to change and the hardest to act on.
