# Chapter 7: The 90-Day Director Plan

> "You inherit the org as it is. You leave it as it should be. The 90 days in between are the only time you get to observe before everyone adjusts their behavior for you."

The 90-day window is a finite resource. Spend it wrong and you will spend the next 18 months trying to recover credibility you never built. Spend it right and your team knows who you are, your VP sees you as stable and adding value, and you have replaced inherited assumptions with data-driven direction.

This chapter gives you a week-by-week operating plan, the deliverables that prove you did the work, and the warning signs that tell you when you are drifting off course.

---

## Phase 1 — Days 1–30: Listen and Learn

The single most expensive mistake a new Director makes in month one is confusing motion for progress. Scheduling townhalls, reorganizing team Slack channels, rebranding the on-call rotation — none of that is listening. Listening is structured, intentional, and produces a written artifact you will use in Phase 3.

### Week 1: Direct Report 1:1s

Meet every direct report individually. One hour each. You are not here to introduce yourself — you are here to learn. Lead with questions. End with silence long enough that they fill it.

**The five questions you ask every direct report, in order:**

1. "What's the biggest operational pain point right now — not someday, today?"
2. "What decision has been sitting in this org waiting for someone to make it?"
3. "What would you change if you had the authority to change it tomorrow?"
4. "What wakes you up at night about this org that doesn't make it to the status reports?"
5. "Where do we have single points of failure — people, not systems?"

The last question surfaces your highest-risk human dependencies before an incident surfaces them for you. If the same name appears in three different managers' answers to question 5, that person is both your most valuable team member and your most dangerous bus-factor risk. Act on that information by week 4.

**What you are listening for:**

- Themes that appear across multiple people — these are systemic issues, not individual grievances
- Problems framed as "we've always done it this way" — these are stale decisions waiting to be revisited
- Anything someone says, then pulls back — "never mind, that's not really a big deal" — probe that statement; they are editing for your benefit
- Who gets mentioned in multiple conversations as either a problem or a hero — that signal is useful and almost never appears in a status report

Take notes during the session. Verbatim quotes from these conversations will anchor your State of the Org document in month 1.

### Week 2: Shadow Every Domain

You cannot manage what you have not observed. In week 2, you sit in on — you do not run — four operational rituals:

| Ritual | What You Observe | Specific Question You Are Answering |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| Active incident bridge call | Who calls the room to order? Who defers? Who escalates? | Does the team run the incident, or does the incident run the team? |
| CAB meeting | Rigor of change review, quality of risk assessments, who blocks, who rubber-stamps | Is this governance or theater? |
| Problem review (postmortem) | How blame is handled, whether root causes are structural or anecdotal, action item quality | Is blameless culture practiced or just stated? |
| SLA review with a customer or business stakeholder | How the team frames misses, whether the data is trusted, how commitments are made | Does the team own accountability, or do they explain it away? |

You are not grading people. You are gathering data. Take specific notes: who spoke, what was said, how long it took, what decision came out of it. You will reference these observations in the State of the Org document.

### Week 3: Data Review — Last 6 Months

Pull the operational data and read it as a story, not a scorecard. You are looking for trends, not snapshots. Request this data in raw form if possible — pre-packaged dashboards have already been filtered for what the team thought was important.

**The five data streams you examine:**

**1. MTTR Trend**
Target: P1 MTTR < 30 minutes, P2 MTTR < 2 hours. Look at the rolling 6-month trend. A worsening trend means one of three things: the same services are failing repeatedly (systemic reliability problem), the same people are handling incidents (staffing and knowledge distribution problem), or the response process is breaking down under scale (process maturity problem). Which one it is determines your intervention.

**2. Incident Count and Composition**
Split by severity. What percentage are P1 vs. P2? What services or components dominate? If 60% of your P1s trace to three services and those three services are not in anyone's reliability improvement roadmap, you have found your first action item.

**3. Change Failure Rate**
Target: < 5% of changes cause an incident or require rollback. Industry benchmark is < 5%; above 10% signals a governance failure. Pull the last 50 failed changes and look for a pattern: same team, same service type, same time-of-week. Pattern = systemic gap. Randomness = execution variance that training and process can address.

**4. Problem Backlog Age**
Any P1 problem open more than 45 days without a documented fix date and owner is a leadership failure — not the team's leadership failure, a systemic accountability failure. Count them. For each one: when was it opened, who owns it, what is the current status, why does no fix date exist? This becomes part of your month-2 stabilization work.

**5. On-Call Load**
Pull pager volume per engineer over the rolling 4-week period. Any engineer averaging more than 2 pages per night is a flight risk — burnout is not a warning, it is an exit in progress. Any team where two people are handling 70%+ of escalations has a knowledge distribution problem that one bad week will turn into a staffing crisis.

### Week 4: The State of the Org Document

Write it now. You will not have this clarity again — in 30 more days, you will have started to normalize what you observed. The State of the Org is a private working document, not a presentation. It is how you prove to yourself that you understand what you inherited.

**State of the Org Document Structure:**

```
1. What I Inherited
   - Team structure: headcount, tenure distribution, open roles
   - Current operational performance: the 5 data streams with your assessment
   - The three highest operational risks I see (be specific — not "reliability" but 
     "the payment gateway has no runbook, one SRE owns all tribal knowledge, and 
     the MTTR for that service is 4x the org average")

2. What Is Working
   - Specific practices, specific teams, specific individuals — be named and be fair

3. What Is Broken
   - Systemic issues, not individual performance gaps
   - Backed by the week 3 data and the week 1–2 observations
   - Ordered by consequence: if nothing changes, what fails first?

4. What I Don't Know Yet
   - Explicit unknowns — questions you could not answer from 4 weeks of data
   - Where you need more time before forming a view

5. My Tentative Hypotheses
   - What you think is true but have not yet confirmed
   - Labeling something a hypothesis forces you to test it in month 2 rather than 
     act on it as fact
```

## Phase 2 — Days 31–60: Stabilize

Month 2 is where you shift from observer to actor — but the actions you take are narrow, specific, and high-confidence. You are not yet transforming; you are stabilizing. The org needs to see that decisions get made under your watch before they will trust your direction.

### Identify the Top 3 Operational Risks

From the State of the Org document, surface the three risks that will make you look bad in month 3 if they materialize. These are not the three most important long-term risks — those go in the 12-month roadmap. These are the risks that, if they fire in the next 60 days, will define how your VP sees your first quarter.

For each risk, document it in a simple risk table:

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Action | Owner | Due Date |
|------|-----------|--------|------------------|-------|----------|
| | L / M / H | L / M / H | Specific, not generic | Named | Day XX |

Assign yourself as owner of at least one. That signal matters — you are not delegating all risk out of your name.

### Establish Your Operating Rhythm

By day 45, the following rituals are non-negotiable and running on schedule:

| Ritual | Cadence | Duration | Your Role | Who Attends |
|--------|---------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Weekly Ops Review | Monday, weekly | 60 min | Facilitator | All domain managers |
| Direct Report 1:1 | Weekly | 45 min each | Coach / decision-maker | Individual DRs |
| Monthly Metrics Review | Last week of month | 90 min | Accountable owner | DRs + VP |
| Quarterly Business Review | Quarterly | 2 hours | Presenter | VP, peers, key stakeholders |

Do not wait for the calendar to be perfect. Run the first Weekly Ops Review in week 2 with whatever agenda you have. The ritual matters more than the perfect content.

**Weekly Ops Review agenda (strict — do not let it drift):**

1. Open incidents and problem status: 15 min — any P1 open, any P1 closed this week, MTTR on closed incidents
2. Change pipeline: 10 min — what's going out this week, what failed last week
3. Risks and blockers: 10 min — what needs a decision at director level
4. Team health: 5 min — on-call volume, any escalation to address
5. Parking lot / announcements: 5 min

Total: 45 minutes of content, 60 minutes allocated. The 15 minutes of buffer is for decisions, not for running over on metrics recitation.

### Make Your First Visible Decisions

Between day 31 and day 60, make at least 5 decisions that are specific, communicated, and named. Not directional statements — decisions. A decision has: what was chosen, what was not chosen, who it affects, when it takes effect.

Examples of the right kind of early decision:
- "We are moving the CAB meeting from Wednesday to Tuesday to allow more time before the change freeze on Friday. Effective next week."
- "The problem backlog is too long. Any P1 problem open more than 45 days without a fix date gets escalated to this operating review by the owning manager — starting Monday."
- "On-call rotations will be reviewed quarterly by the Incident Manager and presented to me. If any engineer's 4-week rolling average exceeds 2 pages per night, the on-call roster expands or the incident volume gets addressed. Non-negotiable."

These decisions are not dramatic. They do not require organizational change. They demonstrate that your presence has weight.

### Clarify Delegation and Issue Delegation Charters

By day 60, every domain manager has a written delegation charter that answers three questions:

1. What decisions do you make independently without informing me first?
2. What decisions do you make and then inform me within 24 hours?
3. What decisions require my input or approval before you act?

Until you issue these charters, your managers will default to checking everything with you — and you will become the bottleneck you were hired to clear. Issue the verbal version in month 2 1:1s. Confirm in writing by day 60.

## Phase 3 — Days 61–90: Set Direction

Month 3 is where you stop being new. By day 90, you have an opinion about where this org needs to go, and you have presented it to your VP. Everything you do in month 3 should produce a durable artifact — a roadmap, a set of principles, a Q1 goal sheet — that exists whether or not you are in the room.

### Present the 12-Month Operational Roadmap

This is your most important deliverable. It answers: what will be measurably better in 12 months, and how?

**Roadmap structure for the VP presentation:**

```
Section 1: Where We Are (2 slides — the data, not the story)
  - Current MTTR, SLO compliance, change failure rate, toil percentage
  - The 3 highest risks you identified in month 1, and current status

Section 2: Where We Need to Be (1 slide — the target state)
  - Specific numeric targets for 12 months from now
  - Example: "MTTR for P1 < 20 min (from 38 min today), 
    change failure rate < 5% (from 11% today), 
    toil < 50% of eng time (from ~65% today)"

Section 3: How We Get There (3 slides — the plan)
  - 3–4 major initiatives, each with: owner, timeline, success metric
  - Dependencies on resources, headcount, or cross-functional support

Section 4: What You Need from Me (1 slide — explicit ask)
  - Budget, headcount approval, executive air cover for a specific decision
  - Do not leave this implicit — you need a VP who will say yes or no, 
    not one who nods and disappears
```

### Identify and Deliver the Quick Win

By day 45 you identified a specific problem from month 1 that you can demonstrably resolve by day 90. By day 90, you stand up in the Weekly Ops Review and close it.

The quick win is not a technical achievement — it is a leadership signal. Choose something that was a real pain point, that at least two people mentioned in week 1 1:1s, and that you can close with a combination of decision-making and targeted effort — not a 6-month engineering project.

Examples: eliminating a recurring false-positive alert that was paging engineers 3x/night; publishing a runbook for the top-3-incident-volume service that had none; setting up the quarterly on-call review process that previously did not exist.

## 90-Day Plan: Week-by-Week Table

| Week | Primary Activity | Key Deliverable |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Direct report 1:1s — all 5 questions | Notes: themes, quotes, named risks |
| 2 | Shadow ops rituals — incident, CAB, PM review, SLA review | Observation notes per ritual |
| 3 | 6-month data review — MTTR, incidents, CFR, problem backlog, on-call | Data summary with your assessment |
| 4 | Write State of the Org | State of the Org document (private) |
| 5 | Identify top 3 risks; first risk mitigations in flight | Risk register: 3 items, owners, dates |
| 6 | First Weekly Ops Review running; 1:1 rhythm established | Operating rhythm live |
| 7 | Peer relationship meetings — 1:1 with every functional peer | Cross-functional pain point map |
| 8 | Delegation charter conversations with all DRs | Verbal delegation charters issued |
| 9 | Quick win execution in flight; draft 12-month roadmap | Quick win: in progress |
| 10 | Delegation charters in writing; stakeholder map complete | Written charters signed off |
| 11 | Roadmap finalized; Q1 goals drafted with each domain manager | Roadmap deck ready for VP |
| 12 | VP roadmap presentation; quick win delivered | 12-month roadmap presented; quick win closed |

## 90-Day Deliverables Checklist

- [ ] State of the Org assessment — written, 2–3 pages, completed by day 30
- [ ] Operating rhythm established — Weekly Ops Review running by day 14
- [ ] All direct reports have written delegation charters — signed off by day 60
- [ ] Stakeholder map complete — all first substantive meetings done by day 60
- [ ] Quick win identified — in flight by day 45
- [ ] Quick win delivered — closed by day 90
- [ ] 12-month operational roadmap — presented to VP by day 90
- [ ] Q1 goals by domain — documented with each domain manager by day 90
- [ ] At least 10 named, communicated decisions made in days 1–90

---

> ### What Good Looks Like
>
> By day 90, you have replaced every inherited assumption about this org with a data-backed assessment. Your team knows your name and your decision-making style — because you have made at least 10 visible, specific decisions and they were communicated to the people they affected. Your domain managers know exactly what they are authorized to decide without calling you, because you wrote it down. Your VP has seen a 12-month roadmap that contains specific numeric targets — not "improve reliability" but "reduce P1 MTTR from 38 minutes to 20 minutes by Q3." The first Weekly Ops Review ran in week 2 and has not missed a week since. Two of the five risks you identified in month 1 are closed or actively mitigated. The quick win is delivered and the person who did the most work to close it was named publicly by you.

---

> ### What Failure Looks Like
>
> Day 90 and you are still in listening mode — still "getting up to speed," still scheduling introductory meetings, still saying "I need a few more weeks before I can give direction." Your domain managers are checking every decision with you because no delegation charter was ever issued — your inbox is full and your team has slowed down to your approval speed. You have not presented anything to your VP beyond a status update, which your predecessor was already sending. The same P1 problem backlog that existed on day 1 exists on day 90 — nothing has been closed, no owner has been named, no escalation has happened. Your team knows you are nice and well-intentioned. They do not yet know what you stand for or what you will not tolerate. That ambiguity is already costing you.
