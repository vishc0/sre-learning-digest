# Chapter 3: Decision Rights and Delegation

> You are the Director of Operations. Your job is not to make more decisions — it is to ensure the right decisions are made at the right level, with the right context, every time.

---

## The Core Problem You Will Inherit

When you step into this role, you will be handed a team that is accustomed to escalating everything upward. Your predecessor may have been the type who wanted to be in every meeting, approve every change, and personally lead every P2. That felt like control. It was actually a bottleneck.

Your domain managers are capable. Your job is to define where their authority ends and yours begins — then defend that line. A Director who approves every change is not protecting the org; they are destroying their managers' decision-making muscle and creating a single point of failure.

---

## The Decision Framework

Every decision made in your organization belongs in one of four buckets. If you cannot classify a decision, that is a signal it lacks a clear owner.

**Tier A — Director Decides Alone**
Operational decisions within your budget authority, team structure changes within approved headcount, process changes scoped to your domain, vendor escalations within existing contract terms, performance feedback short of PIP.

**Tier B — Director Decides with Peer Alignment**
Cross-domain changes that impact another Director's services or teams, shared resource allocation (shared on-call pools, shared tooling budget), policy changes that affect more than one team, decisions that set precedent for how two orgs interact.

**Tier C — Director Decides with VP Approval**
Headcount adds or backfill above a vacancy, budget requests above your spend threshold, Tier 1 architectural changes with customer impact, SLA changes with contractual implications, any policy that binds the whole organization, and anything with legal or regulatory downstream risk.

**Tier D — Delegated to Domain Manager**
Day-to-day domain decisions within the domain charter, Incident Command for P2 and below, change approvals within CAB authority, problem prioritization within the P-score model, team scheduling and sprint planning.

## Delegation Principles

### Delegate the Decision, Not the Task

The difference between these two is the difference between a capable manager and a sophisticated dispatcher.

When you say "handle the CAB prep," you have delegated a task. When you say "you are accountable for the quality of changes approved through CAB, including deciding what gets deferred," you have delegated the decision. The first produces someone who executes steps. The second produces someone who thinks.

Before you delegate, ask: "Am I giving them the authority to be wrong and own the consequence?" If the answer is no — if you will override them the moment you disagree — you have not delegated. You have just added a layer of theater.

### Delegate with Context: Transfer the Why, Not Just the What

When you hand off a decision space, your manager needs to know:
- What outcome this decision is meant to protect
- What constraints are non-negotiable (regulatory, contractual, architectural)
- What the last three decisions in this space looked like and why
- When to escalate instead of deciding

A domain manager who knows the "why" will make better decisions in situations you never anticipated. One who only knows the "what" will freeze the moment something novel appears.

### Stay Informed Without Staying in Control

Visibility is not control. The following are the same mistake made at different altitudes:

- Attending the CAB meeting yourself is not visibility — it is participation that crowds out your manager.
- Reviewing every change ticket is not visibility — it is auditing your manager's work, which signals distrust.
- Asking "did you approve that?" for every change is not visibility — it is shadow approval authority.

Visibility looks like this: a weekly dashboard showing change failure rate, emergency change percentage, and CAB throughput. You review it. If something moves outside the control band, you ask your manager one question: "What's driving this?" You do not show up at the next CAB.

## Micromanagement vs. Visibility: The Line

### Micromanagement (Stop Doing These)

1. Attending the CAB meeting as a participant when your Change Manager is the chair. Your presence changes who speaks.
2. Reviewing change tickets before they go to CAB. That is your Change Manager's gate, not yours.
3. Paging an IC during an active P2 to ask what is happening. Read the incident bridge. If you must speak, ask "do you need anything from me?"
4. Editing your Problem Manager's RCA before it goes to the customer. Give feedback, then let them publish.
5. Approving time-off for individual contributors who report to your domain managers. That authority is theirs.

### Visibility (Keep Doing These)

1. Reviewing the weekly change failure rate metric — not the individual changes.
2. Reading the P1 incident summary 24 hours post-resolution without asking the IC to walk you through it verbally unless something needs escalation.
3. Checking whether MTTR is trending correctly at the monthly ops review.
4. Asking your domain managers in 1:1: "Is there anything you are waiting on from me or someone else?" — then unblocking it.
5. Reviewing the decision log quarterly and noting whether the pattern of decisions reflects the principles you set.

## Escalation Threshold Clarity

Your domain managers will escalate to you. The problem is that without explicit thresholds, they will escalate either too much (every decision) or too little (only after a crisis). Define the threshold for each domain. Publish it in the team wiki.

| Domain | Escalate to Director If |
|---|---|
| Incident | Any P1 active; P2 with Tier 1 customer impact; IC decision being challenged by a stakeholder; on-call engineer refuses page |
| Problem | P1 RCA disputed by engineering team; problem open > 30 days without a fix delivery commitment; same root cause repeating within 60 days |
| Change | Emergency change required for Tier 0/1 service; change failure causes P1; CAB cannot reach consensus on a high-risk change |
| Environment | Prod environment unavailable; environment config drift causes P1; DR failover being triggered |
| SLM | Customer formally disputes SLA data; measured breach with contractual penalty implications; SLO target being proposed for revision |
| Performance | Perf regression in Tier 1 service reaching production; baseline coverage below 70% with a release upcoming; gate bypassed without approval |
| Architecture | Standards exception requested for a Tier 0/1 system; ADR open > 60 days without decision; ARB quorum cannot be reached on a blocking decision |
| People | Any team member approaching PIP; retention risk for a key engineer; grievance filed against a team member |

The rule behind these thresholds: **escalate when the scope, risk, or stakes exceed what the domain charter covers**. P2s are inside the domain. P1s are yours. Disputes within a team are the manager's. Disputes that cross domains or involve external parties come to you.

## The Decision Log

Every significant Tier A or Tier B decision gets a log entry. Not a paragraph — a record.

**Format:**
```
Date: YYYY-MM-DD
Decision: [one sentence]
Context: [what prompted this, what constraints existed]
Options considered: [at least two, even if one was obviously wrong]
Decision made: [what was chosen]
Rationale: [why this option over the others]
Outcome: [fill in 30-90 days later]
```

You will review this log quarterly. The review has three questions:
1. Are there categories of decision where I keep being surprised by the outcome? (This means my assumptions were wrong.)
2. Are there categories of decision where my managers never escalate? (This could mean they are handling it well — or hiding problems.)
3. Has the same decision been made differently in two similar situations? (This means the principle was not clear enough.)

The decision log is not an accountability document. It is your institutional memory. When someone asks "why did we do that?" in 18 months, you have an answer. When a new Director inherits your role, they understand your reasoning.

If you are making decisions in Slack and not logging them, you are building an organization that cannot learn from itself.

## Decisions That Are Never Delegated

Regardless of how capable your domain managers are, the following never leave your authority:

- **Tier 1 outage declaration**: You can delegate the IC process, the bridge management, the communication cadence. You cannot delegate the final call that a Tier 1 outage is occurring. That declaration triggers contractual, regulatory, and reputational consequences. It is yours.
- **Headcount decisions**: Who is on your team and at what level. You can involve your managers in the process, but the decision and the accountability are yours.
- **Termination authority**: This includes performance-based separation and involuntary RIFs. Never delegate this. Even if a manager initiates a PIP, you own the endpoint decision.
- **Any decision with legal or regulatory implication**: Data breach escalation path, compliance audit responses, contract breach acknowledgment. These leave your domain the moment you delegate them.
- **Decisions that contradict a stated principle**: If you have said "we never suppress monitoring to clear an alert," and a situation arises where someone wants to do that, the exception must come to you. Not because you will necessarily say no — but because exceptions to principles must be consciously made by the person accountable for the principle.

---

> **WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE**
>
> Your domain managers make 95% of decisions inside their charter independently, without asking you first. You learn about P2 incidents from the summary, not the bridge. Your decision queue has fewer than five active items in any given week. Your decision log has consistent rationale that other people could reconstruct your reasoning from. Managers escalate policy questions to you ("should we change how we categorize Tier 2 changes?") but not operational ones ("should I approve this Tier 2 change?"). A new manager joining your org can read the rights matrix and know exactly what authority they have on day one.

---

> **WHAT FAILURE LOOKS LIKE**
>
> Your domain managers ask for permission before making P3 decisions. You are on the CAB bridge because "I like to stay close to changes." You approve every change above a certain risk score because you do not trust the process your team runs. Your decision log is empty because "I make decisions in the moment and communicate them in Slack." Your managers have stopped proposing solutions and started asking "what do you want me to do?" You are working 60-hour weeks and every week feels the same. You find out about a problem from your VP, not your manager. Your managers' managers are making decisions because the managers are waiting for you.

---

## Summary: The Three Rules

1. **Define the line before the incident, not during.** Your managers cannot make confident decisions in a crisis if they have never been told where their authority ends.
2. **Visibility is a dashboard, not a meeting.** If you need a human to tell you what is happening, your instrumentation has failed.
3. **The cost of micromanagement is not your time — it is your managers' confidence.** Every decision you take back trains them to not decide. You will not be able to buy back that confidence after the fact.
