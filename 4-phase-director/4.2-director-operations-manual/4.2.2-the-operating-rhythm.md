# Chapter 2: The Operating Rhythm

---

## Why Rhythm Is Not Optional

A Director without a deliberate operating rhythm becomes a reactive function. Every day is shaped by whoever sends the loudest Slack message, whichever VP stopped by, and which incident surfaced. This is not leadership — it is institutional pinball.

The operating rhythm is how you impose structure on a role that has no natural structure. It is the mechanism by which your team has predictable access to you, your VP has predictable signal from you, and you have protected time to think, synthesize, and steer.

This chapter documents the full Director's calendar: daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annual. It is not aspirational. It is the minimum operating cadence for a Director managing a 50–200 person SRE or Operations organization at enterprise scale.

---

## The 20% Rule

Before the calendar, the single most important structural decision you will make: **protect 20% of your weekly working time as unscheduled Director Focus time.**

At 45 hours per week, that is 9 hours. Block it in 90-minute increments — Tuesday 3:00–4:30pm, Thursday 7:00–8:30am, Friday 2:00–3:30pm. Label the calendar block "Director Focus — Do Not Schedule." Treat it as a hard meeting. Decline overlapping requests. Do not use it for heads-down work you should have delegated. Use it for:

- Reading a post-mortem from another company and thinking about whether your systems have the same failure mode
- Working through a problem with no immediate deadline
- Writing the thinking that drives next quarter's direction
- Responding to things that need a real answer, not a quick reply
- Noticing what is not on your calendar that should be

When your calendar is 100% booked with standing meetings and reactive calls, you are operating on what your organization did, not on what it should do next. The 20% is where the Director lives.

---

## The Async-First Principle

Status is async. Decisions are synchronous.

Most of the communication in your organization — status updates, incident summaries, weekly metrics, change outcome reports — should be written and consumed asynchronously. You should not be in a meeting to hear that the SLO compliance was 99.2% last week. That should be in a written report you read in 8 minutes on Tuesday morning.

Reserve synchronous time — actual meetings, actual calls — for three things only:

1. **Decisions**: A call where the output is a documented decision that could not be reached async because it requires debate, clarification, or real-time judgment
2. **Development**: A 1:1 or coaching conversation where the value comes from the live exchange, not the content
3. **Alignment**: A conversation where understanding needs to be confirmed, not just transmitted — typically used for significant changes in direction or when you detect that written communication has created confusion

Everything else: async. Written. Documented. Searchable.

If your weekly ops review is a status recitation where each manager reads numbers aloud, that meeting should not exist. Numbers go in a written brief. The meeting is for the three items above.

## Daily: 30–45 Minutes

### Morning Dashboard Review (15 minutes, before 8:30am)

Open your operational dashboard before you open Slack or email. The sequence matters. If you read Slack first, your attention will be shaped by other people's framing of events before you have seen the raw signal.

What to check, in order:

**1. Open P1/P2 incidents**
Are there active incidents? If yes: is there an IC? Is there a bridge? Is the domain manager aware? You are not joining the bridge — you are confirming the system is running the incident correctly.

**2. Overnight change outcomes**
What changes were deployed between 6pm and 6am? What was the outcome? Any rollbacks? Any open incident traces that link to a change? This takes 3 minutes if your change management tooling is instrumented correctly. If it takes 20 minutes, you have a tooling problem to fix.

**3. SLO burn rate trend**
Not the current snapshot — the 7-day trend. A 99.3% SLO that trended from 99.7% over the past week is a different situation than a stable 99.3%. You want to catch degradation before it becomes a miss, not report the miss after the fact.

**4. New KEDB entries**
What known errors were added overnight? This surfaces repeat issues that are not yet generating P1/P2 tickets. The KEDB is your early warning system for systemic problems that have not yet crossed the severity threshold.

### Splunk / Observability Morning Check (5–10 minutes)

Three queries. Run them in this order. Adjust the index and sourcetype for your specific environment.

**Query 1 — Incident volume by severity, last 24 hours:**
```
index=incidents earliest=-24h
| stats count by severity
| sort -count
```
Baseline: P1 = 0 or 1, P2 < 5, P3 < 20 for a mid-size enterprise. If P2 count is above baseline, flag for the ops review.

**Query 2 — SLO compliance trend, rolling 7 days:**
```
index=slo_metrics earliest=-7d
| timechart span=1d avg(compliance_pct) by service
| where avg(compliance_pct) < 99.5
```
This surfaces only the services trending below target. If this query returns zero rows, your services are healthy. If it returns rows, those services need problem records.

**Query 3 — Overnight change success rate:**
```
index=change_records earliest=-12h latest=now
| stats count(eval(outcome="success")) as success,
        count(eval(outcome="rollback")) as rollback,
        count(eval(outcome="failed")) as failed by change_window
```
Target: rollback rate < 2%, failed rate < 1%. Anything above that gets documented in the ops review.

These three queries tell you the operational health of your organization in under 10 minutes. If you cannot run them in 10 minutes, your observability tooling is insufficiently instrumented or organized — which is itself a director-level problem to solve.

### Slack / Email Triage (10 minutes)

One pass. Not continuous monitoring throughout the day.

Triage rules:
- **Respond immediately**: your VP, a P1 situation that the system is not handling, a peer VP flagging something that requires your involvement before 10am
- **Scheduled slot (today)**: domain managers with questions or asks that need an answer today but not in the next 90 minutes
- **Weekly ops review**: anything that is a status update or a problem that can wait until Monday
- **Async written response (this week)**: anything that warrants a real answer but has no urgency — write the response in a quiet block, not between meetings

Do not triage continuously. Do not have Slack notifications on during your morning dashboard review. Reactive attention fragmenting is the mechanism by which Directors become day-long responders instead of weekly rhythm-setters.

### Daily Standup (if applicable, 15 minutes)

As a Director, your role in a standup is **listener, not presenter.** You are there to hear what is blocked and whether it requires you to act. You are not there to give status or to drive the conversation.

If your standup has become a status broadcast where everyone recites yesterday's work, cancel it and replace it with an async standup in Slack. Standups that do not surface blockers have no value as synchronous events.

## Weekly: The Backbone of Director Operations

### Weekly Calendar Block Overview

| Time | Monday | Tuesday | Wednesday | Thursday | Friday |
|------|--------|---------|-----------|----------|--------|
| 7:00–8:30 | Director Focus | Director Focus | — | Director Focus | — |
| 8:30–9:00 | Dashboard + Splunk | Dashboard + Splunk | Dashboard + Splunk | Dashboard + Splunk | Dashboard + Splunk |
| 9:00–10:00 | Weekly Ops Review | 1:1 Block | 1:1 Block | Cross-Functional Sync | Director Focus |
| 10:00–11:00 | — | 1:1 Block | 1:1 Block | — | — |
| 11:00–12:00 | — | — | — | — | — |
| 12:00–13:00 | Lunch (protected) | Lunch (protected) | Lunch (protected) | Lunch (protected) | Lunch (protected) |
| 13:00–14:30 | — | Director Focus | — | — | Metrics Review + VP Status |
| 14:30–16:00 | — | — | Director Focus | — | — |
| 16:00–17:00 | — | — | — | Peer sync (ad-hoc) | — |

Blocks labeled "—" are available for ad-hoc scheduling, reactive response, and project work. They are not free-for-all. They are managed capacity.

---

### Monday: Weekly Ops Review (50 minutes)

**Attendees**: You + all domain managers. No engineers unless presenting. No observers.

**Required pre-read (sent Sunday night by each domain manager)**: Written brief, maximum one page, covering: incidents this week by severity, open P1/P2 problems and their fix target dates, change calendar for the coming week, environment status, any risks that need Director awareness.

**Agenda** (50 minutes total, documented decisions by minute 45):

| Slot | Time | Owner | Purpose |
|------|------|-------|---------|
| Incident review | 10 min | Domain managers | Severity ≥ P2 this week. What was it, what was root cause, is a problem record open? |
| Open problems | 10 min | Domain managers | All P1/P2 open problems. Fix date, confidence, blocker if any. |
| Change calendar | 5 min | Change manager or domain lead | What is deploying this week? High-risk items flagged. |
| Environment status | 5 min | Domain managers | Degraded environments, maintenance windows, trueup completion rate. |
| Risks and asks | 10 min | All | What does each manager need from the Director this week? Cross-domain conflicts? |
| Director direction | 5 min | You | What are you prioritizing this week? What decisions have been made? What context does the team need? |
| Decisions documented | 5 min | You | Read back decisions made in this meeting. Confirm owners and dates. |

The meeting ends at 50 minutes. If it is running over, you are in the wrong level of detail. The ops review is not a troubleshooting session. It is a risk and decision meeting. Technical troubleshooting happens offline.

---

### Tuesday and Wednesday: 1:1 Blocks

You hold a 30-minute 1:1 with each direct report once per week. If you have six domain managers, that is three hours across Tuesday and Wednesday. Schedule them back-to-back in blocks to preserve focus.

**The 1:1 agenda is theirs, not yours.** They own the preparation. You bring three standing questions:

1. What is blocking you that is above your authority to unblock?
2. What decision do you need from me this week?
3. What are you worried about that you have not put in a written report yet?

Your job in the 1:1 is to listen and ask follow-up questions. If you are talking more than 30% of the time, you are running the wrong meeting.

Use the final 5 minutes of each 1:1 to explicitly state: the unblock you committed to, the decision you made, and whether you need anything from them before next week. Write it down before you leave the call.

**Development conversations** belong here, but not every week. Reserve one 1:1 per month per direct report for a deeper development conversation: what are they working on that is stretching them, what feedback do you have from what you observed this month, where do you want them to grow in the next quarter.

---

### Thursday: Cross-Functional Sync

**Attendees**: You + peer VP of Engineering + VP of Product + CISO or Security Director. Rotating optional attendees based on what is active.

**Purpose**: Surface what is coming at your organization from adjacent teams before it arrives. Changes in product roadmap that will land platform load on your SRE teams. Security mandates that need to be absorbed. Engineering platform migrations that will require SRE support. Organizational changes that will create on-call dependencies.

**Format**: 45 minutes, structured as:
- What is each org shipping in the next 4 weeks that the others need to know about? (15 min)
- What are the open cross-functional risks? (15 min)
- What decisions or escalations need to be made above this group? (10 min)
- What do we commit to each other before next Thursday? (5 min)

If this meeting has no decisions or commitments, it should not be a meeting. Convert it to a written weekly status exchange between the group.

---

### Friday: Metrics Review + VP Status (30 minutes)

**Part 1 — Metrics Review (15 minutes)**: Pull your operational scorecard. You should be able to do this in 15 minutes because your domain managers produced the underlying data as part of the pre-read for Monday's ops review. You are not compiling data — you are reviewing aggregated signal and making editorial decisions about what to escalate to your VP.

Metrics reviewed every Friday:

| Metric | Target | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| SLO compliance (weighted avg) | ≥ 99.5% | SLO dashboard |
| MTTR (P1 incidents) | ≤ 45 min | Incident tooling |
| MTTR (P2 incidents) | ≤ 4 hours | Incident tooling |
| Change failure rate | ≤ 2% | Change management tooling |
| Rollback rate | ≤ 1.5% | Change management tooling |
| Open P1 problems > 30 days | 0 | Problem management |
| Open P2 problems > 60 days | ≤ 3 | Problem management |
| On-call pages per engineer per week | ≤ 5 | PagerDuty / alerting platform |
| Mean alerts per on-call shift | ≤ 8 | PagerDuty / alerting platform |

**Part 2 — VP Status Write (15 minutes)**: Write your weekly status to your VP. It should be 200 words or fewer. Format, every week, no exceptions:

```
Week of [date]

Highlights:
1. [Positive operational outcome or initiative progress. Specific, with numbers.]
2. [Second highlight. Preferably in a different domain than #1.]
3. [Third highlight or a decision made that the VP should know was made.]

Risks:
1. [Risk heading: description of what it is, likelihood, and what you are doing about it.]
2. [Second risk. If you have no risks, flag that explicitly: "No material risks this week."]

Decision needed from VP:
[One decision, clearly framed: context, options considered, your recommendation, and the specific ask.
  If none needed: "No decision needed this week."]
```

This status write takes 15 minutes because you have been watching the data all week. If it takes 90 minutes, you are not watching the data or you are writing too much. Trim until it is 200 words.

## Monthly: The Accountability Layer

Monthly rituals exist to catch trends that weekly snapshots miss and to fulfill accountability to external stakeholders.

### SLA Compliance Report (first week of month)

Prepare and present to: Customer Success VP, your VP, and any customer-facing account teams with SLA contractual commitments.

Content:
- Compliance by service tier (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3 — or however your org classifies)
- Month-over-month trend per service
- Any SLA breaches: root cause, customer impact, remediation taken
- Forecast for next month based on in-flight problems

Format: a written brief with a one-page appendix of charts. Present in 20 minutes. Leave 20 minutes for questions. Do not read the brief — they can read it. Use the synchronous time for the questions and decisions.

### Problem Management Review (second week of month)

All open P1/P2 problems reviewed in a 60-minute session with domain managers. For each open problem:
- What is the RCA status? (complete / in progress / blocked)
- What is the fix? (identified / in development / deployed / verified)
- What is the target completion date? Is it still accurate?
- Is this problem generating recurring incidents? If yes, is there an interim mitigation?

Target state: zero P1 problems open > 30 days. Zero P2 problems open > 60 days without an active fix in the development queue.

If you are consistently missing these targets, you have either a problem-quality problem (RCAs are too shallow to produce actionable fixes) or a capacity problem (fixes are identified but there is no engineering capacity to deliver them). Both require Director-level action: one is a process problem, one is a resource conversation with the VP of Engineering.

### Change Quality Review (second week of month)

DORA metrics review. Four numbers:

| DORA Metric | Target (mid-size enterprise) | Your current |
|-------------|------------------------------|--------------|
| Deployment frequency | ≥ 10/week per team | — |
| Lead time for changes | ≤ 1 week | — |
| Change failure rate | ≤ 2% | — |
| Mean time to restore | ≤ 1 hour (P1) | — |

Beyond DORA: CAB efficiency. What percentage of changes went through standard CAB vs. emergency CAB? Standard should be ≥ 85%. If emergency CAB is running high, you have a planning problem upstream — changes are not being planned far enough ahead.

### Environment Status Review (third week of month)

A full health check of all pre-production and production environments:
- Any environment with a persistent degraded state (not fully healthy for > 7 days)
- Trueup completion: are all environments consistent with their declared baseline? Target: ≥ 95% of environments complete trueup within the defined window
- MTTE (Mean Time to Environment Recovery) for any environment incidents that occurred this month

### Team Health Review (last week of month)

Numbers you review and discuss with your domain managers:

| Metric | Target | Action if missed |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| PTO utilization (team avg) | ≥ 80% of accrued by Q-end | Individual conversations with managers who have teams below 60% |
| On-call load equity | Max 2× spread between highest and lowest engineer | Rebalance rotation immediately |
| Open headcount > 60 days | 0 | Escalate to HR and your VP |
| Manager attrition risk | Track proactively | Retain via development conversation and comp review |

## Quarterly: Strategy and Accountability to Leadership

### Q1 and Q3: Portfolio and Architecture Review

All application health scores reviewed by domain. Any application below the defined health threshold gets a plan: remediation, sunsetting, or risk acceptance with executive sign-off.

Architecture review: all open Architecture Decision Records (ADRs), standards exceptions granted in the past quarter, and ARB (Architecture Review Board) decisions pending. Your job here is not to make the architectural decisions — it is to ensure the decision-making process is working and that operational risk is represented in architectural choices.

### People Review (every quarter)

With each of your domain managers, a structured 45-minute conversation:
- What is their current performance trajectory? (not rating — trajectory)
- What are their development goals for the next quarter?
- Who on their team is a retention risk?
- Who on their team should be on a development track for the manager's role?
- Is there anything I am doing or not doing that is limiting your effectiveness?

Succession planning is a quarterly output, not an annual one. You should know at all times which of your domain managers could step up if you needed to promote or backfill one. If you cannot answer that question in 90 seconds, you have not been developing your bench.

### Budget vs. Actuals Review (every quarter)

Work with your Finance partner. Review:
- Headcount: planned vs. actual (including open reqs)
- Tooling/licensing: on-track vs. variance
- Project spend: any project materially over or under budget, and why
- Forecast to end of year: are there decisions required to stay on budget?

You present this to your VP at the quarterly review. No surprises. If you are going to miss a budget line, surface it in the monthly cycle, not the quarterly cycle.

### Board / Executive Operational Scorecard (Q1 and Q3)

You will typically support your VP in preparing an operational update for the executive leadership team or board. The format varies by organization. What does not vary: lead with business impact, not operational metrics. Translate:

- "MTTR improved from 72 to 38 minutes" → "Customer-impacting incident duration cut by 47%, reducing potential SLA credit exposure by $X annually"
- "Change failure rate dropped from 6% to 1.8%" → "Engineering velocity increased — 96 fewer failed deployments per year — reducing emergency response cost and customer impact events"

Your VP will often be able to give you the business context needed for this translation. Provide the operational facts; request the business translation framework.

## Annual: Structure and Investment

### Budget Planning (Q3 of current year, for next year)

Build your annual budget from the ground up, not from last year's numbers plus a growth percentage. Start with: what does the organization need to achieve next year (from the strategic plan), and what resources — headcount, tooling, training, contractors — does that require?

Present to your VP with three scenarios: baseline (maintain current capability), growth (scale to meet planned demand), and transformation (invest in capability uplift). Let the VP choose the envelope. Then own the execution of what is chosen.

### Technology Radar Update

Once per year, produce or update a technology radar for your organization. Four quadrants: Adopt (standard, use this), Trial (promising, run a structured pilot), Assess (worth evaluating, not ready), Hold (do not start new work on this, migrate away). Circulate to your domain managers and the VP of Engineering. Revisit quarterly to see if any items have moved.

### Team Structure Assessment

Once per year, step back and ask: is the team structure I have the right structure for the next 12 months? Changes in platform architecture, product scope, or headcount may have made the current structure suboptimal. Do not restructure reactively. Assess intentionally.

### Director Personal Review

What you review about yourself, once per year:

- Did I meet the three Director job metrics? (Direction clarity: can my managers explain our operating principles without prompting? Obstacle removal: what blockers did I resolve that my managers could not? Development: what did each of my managers achieve that they could not do 12 months ago?)
- Am I spending time at Director altitude or am I slipping into Principal SRE mode? (Look at your calendar for the year. Count the hours in executive/strategic conversations vs. operational/technical conversations.)
- What is the one capability gap in my organization that I need to close in the next year?
- What is the one capability gap in myself that I need to close in the next year?

---

> ### What Good Looks Like
>
> Your calendar has predictable open blocks every week. Your team knows that you are available in defined windows and focused in defined windows. They plan accordingly.
>
> The weekly ops review ends in 50 minutes. The last 5 minutes are a read-back of decisions. Your domain managers leave knowing exactly what was decided and who owns what next step.
>
> You write the Friday VP status in 15 minutes because the data was compiled during the Monday ops review and updated through the week. It is a synthesis exercise, not a data-gathering exercise.
>
> Your monthly SLA report is presented with confidence because you have been watching the SLO burn rate every day. There are no surprises in the numbers — you already addressed the risks mid-month.
>
> When you take a week off, your domain managers know what decisions they can make, what decisions need to wait, and what would trigger them to call you. They make the right calls. You return to a one-page summary of what happened.

---

> ### What Failure Looks Like
>
> Your calendar is 100% booked. There are no open blocks. Every request gets slotted into the next available 30-minute gap, which is often three days away. You have no time to think.
>
> Every day starts with a Slack thread requiring you to find context, reconstruct what happened overnight, and make three decisions before your first meeting. You are reactive before 9am.
>
> The monthly SLA report takes three days to compile because the data lives in five different places and no one person owns any of it. The report is late. Your VP notices.
>
> You are in meetings from 9am to 5pm four days per week. You respond to email at 10pm. You have not had a development conversation with a domain manager in six weeks. You tell yourself this is temporary.
>
> The quarterly budget review surfaces a surprise: one of your teams has been running 15% over headcount budget because a contractor engagement was extended without proper approval. You are learning about this now.

---

The operating rhythm is not bureaucracy. It is the mechanism by which you replace reactive noise with structured signal. An organization without operating rhythm is an organization that is always surprised. An organization with operating rhythm surfaces risks early, makes decisions at the right level, and compounds its operational maturity quarter over quarter.

Build the rhythm in your first 90 days. Protect it every week after that. The 20% Director Focus time is the first thing your calendar will try to surrender to reactive demand — it is the last thing you should give up.
