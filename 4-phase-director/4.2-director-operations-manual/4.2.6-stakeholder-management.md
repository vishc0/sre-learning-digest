# Chapter 6: Stakeholder Management

Your technical expertise got you to this role. It will not keep you in it. What keeps you in it is the network of relationships you maintain — the VP who trusts your read of operational risk, the Engineering VP who co-owns the change process with you, the Product VP who negotiates SLOs knowing you will deliver on them. These relationships are not soft skills. They are operational infrastructure. When a Tier 1 service fails at 2 AM and you need to make a decision in 10 minutes, you rely on the relationship you built with the Engineering VP over the previous 6 months. You cannot build trust during an incident.

This chapter maps every significant stakeholder, tells you what they need from you, what you need from them, and how often you communicate. It also covers the two communication rules that the Director-level role cannot operate without: no surprises and lead with impact.

---

## Stakeholder Map

| Stakeholder | Role | What They Need from You | What You Need from Them | Cadence | Health Indicator |
|-------------|------|------------------------|------------------------|---------|-----------------|
| VP/CTO | Your direct manager | Operational status, risk flags, decision requests — all before they hear it elsewhere | Decision authority on resource allocation, strategic priorities, air cover when you need to hold a position | Weekly written status + async escalation as needed | They ask you for input before making ops decisions, not after |
| VP of Engineering | Peer | Shared change process ownership, reliability OKRs, architectural alignment | Engineering capacity for reliability work; joint authority on change freeze | Weekly cross-functional sync | You are presenting joint data in the same meeting, not competing narratives |
| VP of Product | Peer | Change freeze dates and criteria; release readiness sign-off; SLO negotiation | Release schedule visibility; product roadmap signals that affect operational risk | Bi-weekly standing meeting | They bring you into release planning before the release is scheduled, not after |
| Security / Compliance | Peer or above-peer | Monthly operational security posture; audit evidence package; advance notice of audit findings | Audit schedule, compliance requirements that affect architecture, findings before they escalate | Monthly briefing + as-needed on audit | You are not surprised by audit findings; you helped prepare them |
| Finance | Functional peer | Monthly budget actuals vs. plan; quarterly investment cases for headcount or tooling | Approved budget clarity; cost model support for SLA credit exposure reporting | Monthly actuals + quarterly investment review | Finance calls you for data, not the other way around |

## Your VP/CTO: Manage Up, Not Up-Down

Your VP's job is to make resource decisions, set strategic direction, and represent operations credibly to the executive layer above them. Your job is to give them what they need to do that job without being asked.

**Weekly written status format** — Send this every Monday morning, before your VP's first meeting of the week. Three parts, always in this order:

```
OPERATIONS STATUS — [Week of DATE]

HIGHLIGHTS (3 max):
1. [What went well and why it matters]
2. [Metric that moved in the right direction]
3. [Initiative that progressed]

RISKS (2 max):
1. [What you are watching and what you are doing about it]
2. [What could become a problem if not addressed]

DECISION NEEDED (1 max):
[The specific decision, the two options, your recommendation, and the deadline for the decision]
If no decision needed this week: "No decision needed. Monitoring [X]."
```

This format is not a status report. It is a structured transfer of situational awareness. Your VP reads it in 90 seconds and knows whether operations needs their attention. If your weekly status is a two-page narrative, you are writing for yourself, not for them.

**Escalation timing** — The rule is simple: your VP hears bad news from you before they hear it from anyone else. This is non-negotiable. If a Tier 1 service is at risk of missing its monthly SLA, you notify by day 15 — not day 31. If a P1 incident is affecting an enterprise customer, you notify within 2 hours of incident declaration — not after resolution. If a security finding surfaces in an audit, you call before the written report lands. The cost of early notification is a slightly uncomfortable conversation. The cost of late notification is a trust gap that takes months to close.

## VP of Engineering: The Co-Owner Relationship

You and the VP of Engineering share ownership of two things: the change process and the reliability bar. Neither of you can deliver either alone. You own the operational process — change windows, change review, rollback authority, incident command. They own the engineering capacity — who builds the reliability features, who refactors the fragile systems, who builds the runbooks.

**What to align on explicitly:**

- Joint definition of "release ready" — what criteria must a release meet before your operations team will approve the deployment window? Get this in writing. When a Product team asks whether their release is ready and you and the Engineering VP give different answers, you have lost credibility with both of them.
- Change freeze criteria — who has authority to declare a change freeze? How long can it last without executive approval? What categories of change are exempt? You draft this; the Engineering VP co-signs it. Both of you communicate it the same way.
- Reliability OKRs — at least one OKR per quarter should be jointly owned: "Reduce Tier 1 MTTR from 45 minutes to under 30 minutes." When an OKR is owned by only one side, the other side deprioritizes the work that would make the OKR achievable.

**What to watch for** — If you find yourself presenting different operational metrics than the Engineering VP presents in the same meeting, you have a data alignment problem that will undermine both of you. Agree on the sources before the meeting. One definition of MTTR, one ServiceNow query, one number on the slide.

## VP of Product: SLO Negotiation and Release Alignment

The VP of Product controls the release schedule. You control the change window. These two things will conflict — and when they do, you need a relationship that makes the conflict resolvable without an escalation to the CTO.

**SLO negotiation** — SLOs are not purely a technical decision. The Product VP defines what "acceptable availability" means for the customer experience. Your job is to tell them what it costs to deliver it. "99.9% on this service requires redundant deployments in three availability zones and an active-passive failover configuration. Engineering estimate is 6 weeks of work and $40K/month in additional infrastructure. Do you want to commit this service to 99.9% or negotiate to 99.5% for this quarter?" That is how you negotiate. Not "what SLO do you want?" — that question produces an unrealistic answer every time.

**Release readiness sign-off** — Define the gate criteria once, in writing, and apply them consistently. Release readiness should not be a judgment call in a Friday afternoon meeting. It should be a checklist: observability instrumentation present, runbook updated, rollback plan documented, SLO risk assessment completed. You sign off when the checklist is complete. You do not sign off when it is not, regardless of the release pressure. This is the most important place to hold the line.

**Change freeze communication** — You own the freeze criteria. Product owns the release schedule. Align these at the start of each quarter. Major holidays, peak traffic periods, and quarter-end periods should be discussed in a planning meeting, not debated during a freeze. If the Product VP is surprised by a change freeze, you did not communicate early enough.

## Security and Compliance: Be Their Best Witness

The Security and Compliance function is not your adversary. They are your organizational risk management partners. Your operational posture — your change management records, your incident documentation, your configuration standards — is their audit evidence. If you maintain clean operational records, their audit preparation is easy. If you do not, their audit preparation reveals your gaps.

**Monthly security posture briefing** — A 30-minute standing meeting. You bring three things: current patch compliance percentage by tier (target: 100% of Tier 0/1 systems patched within SLA), recent change audit trail (any emergency changes that bypassed standard review), and open security findings from any system or tool with an assigned remediation date. They bring open audit findings, upcoming audit windows, and any new compliance requirements that affect your architecture.

**Audit readiness position** — You should be able to produce a change log, an incident history, and a configuration baseline for any Tier 1 system within 24 hours of a request. If that takes a week, your operational documentation practice is a compliance risk. In regulated telecom environments, the FCC and SOX auditors are not interested in "we had to gather the data." They expect the data to exist.

## Finance: Speak Their Language

Finance approves your headcount, your tooling budget, and your infrastructure investments. They do not respond to technical arguments. They respond to return on investment, cost avoidance, and risk quantification.

**Monthly actuals** — Provide actual spend vs. approved budget, by cost center. Highlight any variance above 10% with a one-line explanation. Do not make them ask. Do not send the raw numbers without context.

**Quarterly investment case format** — When you need new headcount or tooling, bring Finance a business case with three components:

1. What is the current operational cost of not having this? (Incident hours × fully-loaded engineer cost + SLA credit exposure)
2. What does the investment cost? (One-time + recurring)
3. What is the payback period? (Break-even at what reduction in incident cost or credit exposure?)

A request for 2 additional SREs should be presented as: "We are currently spending 1,200 engineer-hours per quarter on toil that 2 additional SREs would eliminate. At a fully loaded cost of $200K/year per SRE, the investment pays back in 18 months against avoided overtime and attrition replacement cost alone. SLA credit avoidance is additional upside."

## Enterprise Customers: QBR Preparation and Breach Communication

**Quarterly Business Review (QBR) preparation** — You present to enterprise customers quarterly. The format: actual availability vs. contracted SLA by service, MTTR for any incidents affecting that customer, upcoming changes or improvements, and service improvement commitments for next quarter. Do not present raw operational data. Translate it: "Your payment processing API was available 99.94% of the time this quarter against a 99.9% SLA. You had headroom. In Q3, we are deploying active-active redundancy which will increase the target to 99.95%."

**SLA breach communication** — Director-level communication for any Tier 1 breach affecting an enterprise customer. Communicate before the credit calculation is finalized. "We owe you notification that our order management API breached its SLA this month. You will receive a credit of $X per your contract. Here is a summary of what happened and what we are doing to prevent recurrence." Customers tolerate failures. They do not tolerate finding out from their invoice.

## Vendors: Hold the Contract Line

Your major vendors — cloud provider, observability platform, messaging infrastructure — have SLAs in their contracts. Most enterprises do not track them with the same rigor the vendor does. You will.

**Quarterly vendor review agenda** — Pull the vendor's SLA performance data against their contracted targets. Identify any months where they missed. Prepare your SLA credit claim before the meeting. Present your usage trends for capacity planning. Discuss open support tickets and their resolution time against contracted support SLAs.

**Escalation protocol** — Define this before you need it: at what point does a vendor support ticket escalate to your vendor account manager? At what point do you invoke the SLA credit clause? At what point do you escalate to the vendor VP? The answers depend on severity and duration. A cloud provider P1 outage affecting your Tier 0 services that has been open for 4 hours with no resolution ETA escalates to their VP. Do not wait for your account manager to offer this.

## The "No Surprises" Protocol

Every stakeholder in your map should hear bad news from you before they hear it from anyone else. This is a specific decision tree, not a vague aspiration.

| Event | Who is notified | Timeline | Method |
|-------|----------------|----------|--------|
| P1 incident declared | VP/CTO | Within 30 minutes of declaration | Direct message or call |
| P1 incident affecting enterprise customer | Account Executive + VP/CTO | Within 1 hour of declaration | Call, not message |
| Tier 1 SLO on track to breach before month end | VP/CTO | By day 15 of the month | Written status update |
| Change failure rate above 10% in a week | VP of Engineering | Within 48 hours | Verbal + written summary |
| Security finding surfacing in audit | CISO + VP/CTO | Day of discovery | Call |
| SLA credit being issued to enterprise customer | Account Executive + Finance + VP/CTO | Before credit is finalized | Written notification |
| Engineer attrition risk identified | VP/CTO | Within 1 week of identification | 1:1 conversation |
| Budget forecast tracking above 10% variance | Finance | Before month close | Written forecast update |

The common thread: you are always the first call, never the last. Stakeholders who learn about problems from sources other than you will start going around you — to your engineers, to your peers, to your vendors — because they will not trust your reporting to be current.

## The "Lead with Impact" Translation

Engineers report root causes. Executives need customer impact and business cost. Before you walk into any executive meeting, translate every technical metric you plan to present.

| Technical Metric | What You Would Say to Your Team | What You Say to the Executive |
|-----------------|--------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| MTTR improved from 45 min to 28 min | MTTR is down 38% this quarter | We restored Tier 1 services 17 minutes faster on average this quarter, which avoided approximately $1.4M in SLA credits based on our credit rate schedule |
| Change failure rate dropped from 12% to 4% | CFR is at DORA elite level | Failed deployments dropped by two-thirds. Engineering teams shipped 3 times more frequently without the rollback tax that was consuming 8% of their sprint capacity |
| On-call pages per engineer dropped from 8/day to 2/day | On-call load is within target | Engineer overnight interruptions are down 75%. This directly reduces attrition risk in a team where turnover would cost $180K per replacement in recruiting and ramp time |
| Repeat incident rate is 18% | Problem Management program is not working | One in five incidents we respond to is a problem we have seen before. We are funding those incidents instead of fixing them. I need to prioritize Problem Management remediation work in next quarter's capacity planning |
| Error budget consumption for payment API is at 85% with 2 weeks left in the month | Payment API is going to breach its SLO this month | We will breach our SLA with [Customer X] this month on the payment API. I am notifying you now so we can prepare the communication. The credit exposure is approximately $[X]. Here is what caused it and what we are doing. |

You are not dumbing down the technical reality. You are respecting that your audience's job is to make resource and priority decisions — and they cannot make those decisions from raw operational metrics. Give them the translation.

---

> **What good looks like**
>
> Your VP stops you in the hallway to say "I got your weekly status — no questions, looks solid." Enterprise customers bring you into their planning conversations because they trust your reliability data. The VP of Product calls you before scheduling a major release, not after. Security Compliance includes you in audit prep because your operational documentation is clean. When a P1 incident starts, you send one Slack message to notify your VP, because you already know they check that channel and trust your framing. Stakeholders ask you for status rather than hunting for it through your engineers or your peers. No VP or customer is surprised by a breach or failure — they were already briefed by you.

> **What failure looks like**
>
> Your VP hears about a Tier 1 outage from an enterprise customer's account team before you call them. You spend the first 15 minutes of your monthly executive review defending data accuracy — "that number is actually slightly different if you look at the ServiceNow view vs. the Datadog view" — instead of driving decisions. The VP of Product schedules a major release for the same week as a critical system migration without telling you, because the two of you have never aligned release and change calendars. Your Finance contact emails you asking for last month's actuals because the report is two weeks late. A security audit finding surfaces that you knew about but did not escalate because you did not think it was material — and it turns out the CISO had a different definition of "material." You have two versions of your MTTR number: the one you tell your engineers and the one on the VP slide, slightly different because one counts environmental incidents and one does not.
