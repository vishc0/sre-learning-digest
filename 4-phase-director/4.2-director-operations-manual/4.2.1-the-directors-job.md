# Chapter 1: The Director's Job

---

## The Promotion That Changes Everything (Except What You Think It Does)

The title change to Director is straightforward on paper. The cognitive shift required is not. Most Directors who wash out in their first year don't fail because they lack technical depth — they fail because they keep operating from their old identity. They stay the Principal SRE in a Director's chair, and the organization suffers the consequences at scale.

This chapter is about what actually changes, what you are now responsible for, and — critically — what you must stop doing.

---

## From Executor to System Designer

As a Principal SRE or Senior Manager, your primary value was execution quality. You were the person who understood the platform cold, wrote the runbook that saved the incident, held the most context in the room, and could be dropped into any crisis and improve it. Your leverage was personal. Your impact was direct.

As a Director, your primary value is **system design** — designing how work gets done, not doing the work yourself.

Here is the analogy that makes this concrete.

As a Principal SRE, you are the Incident Commander on a P1 bridge. You are running the call, driving the timeline, making the technical calls, documenting the decisions. When the incident closes, you were the one who fixed it.

As a Director, you are the person responsible for ensuring that every P1 — all of them, simultaneously, whether you are on the bridge or not — runs that way. You are not the IC. You are the architect of the incident response system: the training, the runbooks, the escalation thresholds, the on-call design, the SLO policy that defines when something becomes a P1 in the first place. You are accountable for the outcome of every incident your teams work, whether you ever heard about them or not.

That shift — from "I fix the incident" to "I design the system that fixes incidents" — is the Director's job. If you do not make this transition, you become a bottleneck. You become the single point of failure you spent years designing out of production systems.

## The Three Jobs of a Director

Strip away everything else and you have three jobs. Everything in your calendar, every decision you make, every meeting you attend, should map to one of these.

### Job 1: Set Direction

You decide what your organization is optimizing for, and you communicate it clearly enough that your team can make hundreds of daily decisions without checking with you. This is not a vision statement. It is a set of operating principles that resolve trade-offs.

Examples of real direction:
- "We bias toward reliability over velocity when they conflict in production systems. Staging is different."
- "An on-call engineer should never be woken up more than twice per week. If they are, the alert is the problem, not the engineer."
- "Every P2 gets a problem record opened by 8am the next business day. No exceptions."

When you set direction poorly, every trade-off becomes an escalation to you. When you set it well, your managers resolve trade-offs at their level and you hear about it in the weekly review.

### Job 2: Remove Obstacles

Your domain managers are blocked every day by things outside their authority: cross-team dependencies, vendor escalations, budget approvals, platform constraints, executive interference, conflicting priorities from peer organizations. These blocks are invisible to the VP above you and unsolvable by the manager below you. You sit exactly at the altitude where you can see them and have the authority to resolve them.

This is not the same as solving their problems for them. You are removing structural obstacles so your managers can execute. When a VP of Engineering keeps pulling your team's engineers into architecture discussions and disrupting sprint commitments, that is your problem to resolve with the VP — not your manager's.

### Job 3: Develop People

Your team's ceiling is determined by the ceiling of the managers under you. A domain manager who cannot run a weekly ops review without your presence limits how much you can delegate. A domain manager who can run the org for two weeks without you gives you strategic leverage.

Development is not training. It is structured exposure: putting your managers in situations slightly beyond their current capability, observing what happens, and debriefing on what they should do differently. You attend their staff meetings to observe how they handle conflict, not to handle it for them. You debrief after incidents, asking "what would you have done differently at the 30-minute mark?" You introduce them to your peers in Product and Engineering so they build the relationships that make future work easier.

## The Accountability Shift: You Own Outcomes, Not Tasks

As a Principal SRE, accountability was granular and direct. You owned the migration. You owned the runbook. You owned the change window.

As a Director, accountability is outcome-based and indirect. You own the SLA compliance number, the MTTR trend, the change failure rate, the headcount health of your org. None of those are things you personally produce. All of them are yours.

This is the part of the Director role that surprises most first-time Directors. When your team's SLO burn rate spikes for three consecutive weeks because one domain manager is not running problem reviews, the VP does not hold the domain manager accountable — they hold you accountable. You are the system. When the system produces bad output, you are the variable.

This means your job is not to be informed about everything that is happening. Your job is to ensure the right things are happening at the right level, and to know when they are not — early enough to correct before it becomes your VP's problem.

## Information Asymmetry: The Director's Navigation Problem

You now sit at a structural disadvantage that did not exist as a Principal SRE: you have more organizational context than your team AND less tactical detail than your team. You know things your managers do not — budget constraints, headcount decisions pending, strategic pivots being discussed at the VP level, partnership risks with other organizations. Your managers know things you do not — which engineer is actually struggling, what is really causing the on-call fatigue, what the vendor account team said in the side conversation.

You must design explicit information flows in both directions.

**Downward**: Hold a weekly operating rhythm (Chapter 2) that ensures your team receives the organizational context they need to make good decisions. Do not let organizational information die at your level.

**Upward from your team**: Design your 1:1s and your ops reviews so that the information you actually need (risks, early signals, things going wrong before they become P1s) surfaces routinely. Your managers will not volunteer problems unless they trust that you will help them, not punish them. Build that trust deliberately.

**Upward to your VP**: You are accountable for translating your organization's operational reality into the language the VP operates in: strategy, risk, investment, and outcomes. Not ticket volume. Not incident count. Risk-adjusted operational health, investment required to sustain or improve it, and the decisions you need from the VP to move forward.

## What You Stop Doing

This section is the one most first-time Directors resist reading. Read it anyway.

### Stop reviewing every change yourself

You set the change management policy. You ensure the policy is being followed. You review the aggregate outcome — change failure rate, rollback frequency, CAB throughput. You do not sit in CAB reviewing individual changes. That is your Change Manager's or your domain managers' job. If you are reading change records at 11pm to give approval, you have not built a change process — you have built a single point of failure.

### Stop being the primary escalation point for on-call

Your on-call engineers should escalate to their on-call managers. Their on-call managers escalate to the domain manager. The domain manager escalates to you when the incident is a P1 with executive exposure or requires a cross-domain decision. If you are being paged directly by on-call engineers, you have a management chain problem. Fix the chain, not your pager.

### Stop attending all CAB meetings

CAB is an execution-level process. Attend quarterly to review how the process is working. Attend when a high-risk change for a critical platform is being reviewed and your judgment is genuinely needed. If you are at every CAB, you are consuming time that should go to the three Director jobs, and you are preventing your managers from owning their domain's change process.

### Stop being the technical SME in architecture discussions

You can be in the room for architecture decisions with significant operational risk. You should not be the person doing the whiteboard design. Your job in an architecture discussion is: "What are the operational implications? What does this cost us in on-call load? Does this align with our reliability principles?" You are there to represent operational risk, not to architect the system. If you are the smartest technical person in the room in these discussions, you need to develop your architects and tech leads faster.

### Stop firefighting as your primary operating mode

Every Director has reactive days. The goal is that reactive days are the exception, not the rhythm. When firefighting is your daily mode, it means either: the operating cadence is broken (no one is running problem reviews, so fires keep recurring), or you have not delegated enough to absorb the routine load.

## The Air Cover Role

One of the Director jobs that rarely gets written down: you absorb the organizational weather.

Every organization has internal politics, competing priorities, executive pressure, and budget tension. These forces land on your organization from above and from peers. Your job is to ensure as little of that turbulence as possible reaches your engineers and managers. They should be focused on executing well — not on navigating internal politics or second-guessing organizational direction every time leadership changes messaging.

This is not about filtering information. It is about translating organizational noise into clear direction for your team. When the VP says "we need to accelerate the platform migration," your engineers do not need to hear about the business pressure behind that. They need to hear: "We have shifted the migration to Q2. Here is the revised scope. Here is what changes in your work this sprint."

You take the anxiety. You convert it to clarity. You hand your team clarity.

## Your Relationship with the VP/CTO Above You

Your VP/CTO does not want to manage your team. They want to trust that your team is managed. There is a significant difference.

What your VP needs from you:

**Predictability.** If your ops review is every Monday and your weekly status lands every Friday, your VP can plan around you. If you are reactive and inconsistent, you create work for them.

**Clear asks.** Every time you escalate to your VP, come with: the situation, what you have already tried, and the specific decision or resource you need from them. Do not bring problems without proposed paths. Do not bring ten things that need their attention — prioritize and bring three.

**No surprises.** A P1 incident that becomes a customer SLA breach is acceptable. A P1 incident that becomes a customer SLA breach that your VP hears about from the customer VP before you brief them is a trust-destroying event. Your VP should never hear about a significant problem from someone other than you first.

**Operational outcomes, not operational activity.** Your VP does not care that the team ran 47 change windows this quarter. They care that change failure rate dropped from 8% to 3% and is on track for the 2% annual target. Report outcomes. Lead with impact.

## The Accidental Manager Trap

The most common failure mode for Directors promoted from Principal SRE or Senior Manager: they continue to operate like the senior individual contributor they were, creating a management vacuum at the Director level.

The signs:

- You are the person who resolves technical ambiguity in your org's critical meetings, because your managers are not yet empowered or capable enough to do it.
- Your skip-levels (your managers' direct reports) come to you for direction because they have learned you are faster and more decisive than their manager.
- You have more context about the state of your organization's systems than any of your domain managers do individually.
- Your team's operational health has no meaningful variance whether or not you are on vacation.

That last one is the test. If your organization degrades meaningfully when you take a week off, you have not built a Director-level system. You have built a Principal-SRE-shaped dependency on yourself. Your job is to make yourself unnecessary for the day-to-day operation of the organization, so your energy can go to the things only you can do: setting direction, removing structural obstacles, and developing the people who run the execution.

---

> ### What Good Looks Like
>
> Your team handles P2 incidents start to finish without notifying you. You learn about them in the Monday ops review.
>
> Your domain managers bring you two or three options when they need a decision, not a problem statement. They have already done the analysis.
>
> Your weekly ops review runs in 50 minutes and ends with three documented decisions. The conversation is forward-looking — this week's risks, not last week's fires.
>
> Your VP sends you a message on Friday: "Good status update. Clear, no surprises." That is it. That is the signal.
>
> You take a full week of PTO and return to find the org running at its normal cadence. The domain managers resolved three meaningful issues while you were gone and documented the decisions for you.

---

> ### What Failure Looks Like
>
> You are paged or Slacked for every P2. Your domain managers have learned that you will take over if they wait long enough.
>
> Your domain managers open every 1:1 by asking "what should I do about X?" You are the decision-maker for calls they should own.
>
> You are the only person in your organization with a complete picture of what all domains are doing. No one manager knows what the other domains are doing or how they are performing.
>
> Your calendar shows two or more direct operational meetings per day (CAB, incident bridges, architecture sessions) and you spend less than two hours per week in development conversations with your managers.
>
> When you took two weeks off last year, there were two significant escalations that required you to Slack in from vacation to resolve. You told yourself it was exceptional. It happened again this year.

---

The Director's job is not harder than the Principal SRE's job — it is differently hard. The Principal SRE's challenge is depth: knowing the system better than anyone. The Director's challenge is breadth and abstraction: designing systems of people and process that run reliably without your personal intervention. The skills that made you excellent as a Principal SRE are not liabilities — but they will become liabilities if you apply them at the wrong altitude.

The chapters that follow are about operating at the right altitude, every day, deliberately.
