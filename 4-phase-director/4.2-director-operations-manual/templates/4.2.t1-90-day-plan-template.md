# 90-Day Director of Operations Plan

**Usage**: Start filling this in on Day 1. The goal tables drive your weekly 1:1 agenda with your VP. The State of the Org document (at the bottom) is the deliverable you produce at the end of Day 30 — do not skip it.

**Principle**: The first 30 days are for listening, not deciding. The next 30 days are for establishing and making a few visible decisions that prove you understand the environment. The final 30 days are for committing to a forward plan.

## Days 1–30: Learn Everything, Commit to Nothing

**Phase objective**: Build a complete picture of what you inherited — people, systems, processes, metrics, relationships — without changing anything yet. Exception: if something is actively on fire, stabilize it, but do not reorganize around it.

### Goals Table — Days 1–30

| # | Goal | Description | Success Criteria | Status | Target Date |
|---|------|-------------|-----------------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Meet all directs | 1:1 with each direct report. Learn their domain, their top concern, and their read on the team's biggest risk. Take notes. Do not make promises. | All direct report 1:1s completed. Notes filed. | [ ] Not Started | Day 10 |
| 2 | Shadow all domains | Attend each domain's ops review, incident bridge (at least 1), and team standup for at least one week. Observe; do not run. | All four domain operational rhythms observed. At least 1 incident bridge attended per domain. | [ ] Not Started | Day 20 |
| 3 | Complete data review | Pull 90 days of metrics across: MTTR, incident counts, SLO compliance, change failure rate, on-call load, problem backlog age. Build a baseline. | Data review document complete with 90-day trending data for all key metrics. Baseline established. | [ ] Not Started | Day 25 |
| 4 | Meet all stakeholders | 1:1 with each stakeholder on the Stakeholder Map (Engineering VP(s), Product VP, Security Lead, Finance BP, key customers). First meeting is listening only. | All Tier 1 stakeholders met. Stakeholder Map draft complete. | [ ] Not Started | Day 30 |
| 5 | Write State of the Org | Synthesize everything learned into the State of the Org document (template at bottom of this file). Share with VP. | State of the Org document reviewed and accepted by VP. Director has VP alignment on top 3 risks. | [ ] Not Started | Day 30 |

## Days 31–60: Establish, Decide, Delegate

**Phase objective**: Set the operating rhythm. Make the first visible decisions — ones that demonstrate you understand what matters and have the authority to act. Issue delegation charters so your managers know what they own.

## 3. Top 3 Risks I Identified

*Be specific. Name the risk, state why it matters, and state what happens if it is not addressed.*

1. **Risk: [e.g., On-Call Burnout — Platform Team]**
   - **What it is**: [e.g., Platform Reliability team averaging 8.4 overnight pages per engineer per week vs. target of 6. Two engineers have raised informal attrition signals in the past 60 days.]
   - **Why it matters**: [e.g., Platform team carries the on-call load for the two Tier 0 services. If one engineer exits, the remaining team cannot sustain the rotation without pulling engineers from other domains, which cascades.]
   - **What happens if unaddressed**: [e.g., High probability of attrition event within 60–90 days. Recovery from that event would take 6–9 months. High likelihood of SLA breach during the gap.]

2. **Risk: [e.g., P1 Problem Backlog — 7 Open vs. Target of 3]**
   - **What it is**: [e.g., Seven P1 problems open; four have been open more than 30 days. Three of the seven are driving repeat incidents — directly inflating the repeat incident rate to 18%.]
   - **Why it matters**: [e.g., Each unresolved P1 problem is a loaded gun. The repeat incidents from these three problems have consumed 40% of the on-call load in the past quarter.]
   - **What happens if unaddressed**: [e.g., Repeat incident rate stays elevated, on-call load stays elevated, attrition risk increases. Closed loop: the problem backlog and the burnout risk are feeding each other.]

3. **Risk: [e.g., Standard Change Catalog Stale — 14 Months]**
   - **What it is**: [e.g., The standard change catalog (pre-approved changes that bypass CAB) has not been updated in 14 months. As a result, 38% of changes going through CAB are routine deployments that should be pre-approved.]
   - **Why it matters**: [e.g., Each unnecessary CAB review adds an average of 3 days to deployment lead time. This is measurably suppressing deployment frequency and is the primary driver of the 6.2% change failure rate (last-minute changes introduced to work around the delay).]
   - **What happens if unaddressed**: [e.g., DORA metrics continue to lag peers. Engineering teams route around CAB through emergency change process (currently 5% of changes), inflating the emergency change rate and the audit risk that comes with it.]
