# Chapter 8 — Team Health and Retention

> **Audience**: Director of Operations and domain managers.
> **Purpose**: A measurement and action framework for diagnosing team health before it becomes attrition. Healthy operations teams are built, not found. This chapter gives you the metrics, the warning signals, and the specific interventions.

---

## Section 1 — Team Health Metrics

These six metrics, tracked consistently, give you an early warning system for team health problems before they show up as resignation letters. Each metric has a target, a warning threshold, a red threshold, and a measurement method.

---

### On-Call Load

**What it measures:** The volume of interruptions per engineer per week, averaged over a rolling 4-week period.

| Band | Pages/Engineer/Week | Action |
|------|---------------------|--------|
| Green | < 5 | No action required. Monitor monthly. |
| Amber | 5–10 | Investigate the source. Triage alert tuning. Plan load reduction. |
| Red | > 10 for > 2 consecutive weeks | Immediate action. See Section 3. |

**How to measure:** Export from PagerDuty or VictorOps. Pull pages per person per week for the rolling 4-week window. Do not use team averages alone — averages hide outliers. One engineer at 18 pages/week and five at 2 pages/week averages to 5 and looks green; the engineer at 18 is at critical risk.

**The trap to avoid:** Measuring alert count instead of page count. An alert that fires 100 times without paging anyone is noise but not load. A page at 3am that requires 45 minutes to resolve is load, regardless of how fast MTTA looked in the report.

---

### Toil Percentage

**What it measures:** The fraction of each engineer's working time spent on undifferentiated operational work — work that is repetitive, manual, interruptive, and doesn't permanently improve the system.

The SRE definition is precise: toil is work tied to running a production service that tends to be manual, repetitive, automatable, tactical, devoid of enduring value, and scales linearly with service growth.

| Band | % of Team Time on Toil | Action |
|------|------------------------|--------|
| Green | < 50% | Acceptable. Monitor quarterly. |
| Amber | 50–65% | Reliability improvement work is constrained. Plan reduction. |
| Red | > 65% | Reliability work has effectively stopped. Escalate to VP. |

**How to measure:** Quarterly engineer survey (5-question, anonymous — see Section 4 for survey instrument) plus manager estimation from ticket categorization. Tag tickets in JIRA as `toil` or `reliability-investment`; run a count at end of quarter.

**Why 50% is the target, not zero:** Some toil is unavoidable in operations. The goal is not to eliminate it — it's to cap it, make it visible, and ensure it's trending down over time. An org at 45% toil that is actively reducing is healthier than one at 30% toil that has stopped measuring.

---

### Incident Response Concentration

**What it measures:** Whether incident command (IC) capability is spread across the team or concentrated in 2–3 engineers.

| Metric | Target | Red Flag |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Engineers capable of IC'ing a P2 incident | ≥ 5 | < 3 |
| Engineers capable of IC'ing a P1 incident | ≥ 3 | < 2 |
| % of P2+ ICs run by top 2 engineers | < 40% | > 80% |

**How to measure:** Pull IC history from your incident management tool (ServiceNow, PagerDuty, or your ticketing system). Count unique ICs per severity level for the trailing 90 days. Sort by person. If the top 2 engineers account for more than 80% of P2+ IC runs, you have a knowledge concentration problem.

**Why this matters beyond the obvious:** The two engineers running 80% of your P1s are your single biggest flight risk. When they leave — and eventually they will — your incident response capacity collapses. Concentration is a retention risk masquerading as a capability metric.

---

### PTO Coverage Integrity

**What it measures:** Whether the team can function when any single engineer takes 2 consecutive weeks off without degrading incident response or creating coverage gaps.

**The test:** For each person on your team, answer: who covers their function when they're out? Is that person identified by name? Have they been briefed? Has the coverage been tested (even in a tabletop exercise)?

| Signal | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Named backup exists and has been briefed | Green |
| Named backup exists but has never covered the role | Amber |
| "We'd figure it out when it happens" | Red |
| "We'd call them on vacation if something happened" | Critical — address immediately |

**Red flag phrase to listen for:** "We'd call them on vacation if something happened." If you hear this about any engineer for any critical function, you have a coverage crisis. That engineer's vacation time is not real. Their work-life separation is broken. Their departure risk is high.

---

### New Hire Ramp Time

**What it measures:** Days from a new hire's start date to their first meaningful contribution.

**Definition of meaningful contribution (L3+ SRE):**
- IC'd a P3 incident solo, OR
- Written a runbook that was reviewed and published, OR
- Contributed a finding to a problem investigation

**Target:** < 45 days from start date to first meaningful contribution for L3+ SRE roles.

**How to track:** Log start date and first-contribution date for every new hire. If your org is < 45 days on average, your onboarding program is working. If you're averaging 60+ days, find the bottleneck (access delays, unclear Day 1–30 structure, buddy program gaps, runbook quality).

**Why this matters for retention:** New hires who don't contribute meaningfully within 60 days are at elevated attrition risk in months 3–6. The primary driver is a feeling of incompetence or irrelevance — "I've been here 2 months and I still haven't done anything real." Structured onboarding solves this.

---

### Knowledge Bus Factor

**What it measures:** For every critical system or operational function, how many engineers can perform it without relying on someone else.

**Definition:** Bus factor = the number of team members who, if simultaneously unavailable, would cause the team to be unable to perform a critical function. Named after the morbid thought experiment of "how many people need to be hit by a bus before this stops working."

| Tier | System Type | Target Bus Factor | Red Flag |
|------|-------------|-------------------|----------|
| Tier 1 | Revenue-critical, customer-facing | ≥ 3 | = 1 |
| Tier 2 | Internal platform, business operations | ≥ 2 | = 1 |
| Tier 3 | Dev tools, internal support | ≥ 1 | = 0 (no one knows) |

**How to measure:** For each Tier-1 system, ask: who can diagnose a production failure in this system without asking for help? Who can perform a controlled failover? Who can review and approve a change to its infrastructure? The number of people who can do all three is the bus factor.

**At T-Mobile scale:** A bus factor of 1 on a RabbitMQ broker cluster, a Kafka topic configuration, or an EKS cluster's networking layer is not acceptable. If one person knows how the TIBCO EMS routing rules were configured and they leave, you have an incident that no one can fully diagnose. Map this quarterly.

## Section 2 — The Three Retention Risks for Operations Engineers

Operations engineers don't leave because of compensation alone. The three patterns below account for the majority of voluntary attrition in SRE and platform engineering teams. Each has a specific signature and a specific intervention.

---

### Risk 1: The Toil Trap

**What it sounds like in 1:1s:**
- "All I do is respond to alerts."
- "Nothing ever gets permanently fixed."
- "I've been cleaning up after the same system for 18 months."
- "I know how to fix this properly, but there's never time because there's always a fire."

**Root cause:** No effective Problem Management program. Incidents get resolved but not investigated for root cause. Alert tuning never happens — new alerts are added every quarter but old ones are never retired. Feature work always takes priority over reliability investment, so the same failure modes recur indefinitely. Engineers who care about quality watch the same problems happen repeatedly and conclude that the org doesn't care — and eventually they stop caring too, or they leave.

**Specific interventions:**

1. **Implement Problem Management with teeth.** A problem record is only valuable if it has an owner, a target resolution date, and is reviewed weekly by someone with authority to prioritize it. A problem record that sits open for 6 months with no movement is worse than no problem record — it documents that the org knows the problem and chose not to fix it.

2. **Set a toil budget and publish it.** "No engineer spends more than 50% of their time on toil" is a policy, not a preference. When an engineer is at 60%, the manager's job is to remove toil from their plate — not to tell them to manage their time better.

3. **Tie error budget policy to recurring incident elimination.** If a service is burning its error budget, feature releases for that service pause until the reliability work is done. This is the only mechanism that reliably creates organizational pressure to fix the root cause of recurring incidents rather than just resolving each instance.

4. **Make toil visible in leadership reporting.** When the monthly metrics report shows "toil percentage: 62% — up from 58% last quarter," executives can see the trend. Invisible toil is deprioritized. Named, measured, trending toil is a leadership conversation.

5. **Celebrate reductions.** When an automation project eliminates 5 hours of weekly manual work, recognize it publicly. "The team automated the certificate rotation process — that's 5 hours per week back to reliability work starting this quarter." Engineers who build automation want to see it valued.

---

### Risk 2: The Growth Stall

**What it sounds like in 1:1s:**
- "I've been a Senior SRE for 3 years. I'm good at my job. But I don't know what 'next' looks like for me here."
- "I asked my manager about promotion 6 months ago and I haven't heard anything since."
- "The only way to grow here seems to be to become a manager, and I don't want to be a manager."

**Root cause:** No published career ladder. Promotions are opaque (decided by managers in a room, with no transparent criteria). Stretch assignments don't exist — senior engineers get loaded with toil instead of development work because they're the most capable at handling it. The most common error is rewarding reliability with more toil: "you're so good at this, so here's more of it."

**Specific interventions:**

1. **Publish the career ladder and make it specific.** Vague criteria ("demonstrates leadership") are useless. Specific criteria ("led the post-incident review for at least 2 P2 incidents in the past 6 months, with documented action items and follow-through") give engineers a clear target. See Chapter 5 for the career ladder framework.

2. **Every engineer has a documented growth plan, updated quarterly.** The plan has: 1 skill to develop, 1 stretch assignment, and 1 milestone for the next 6 months. The manager's job is to create the conditions for the plan to execute — not just to write it and forget it.

3. **Create stretch assignments systematically.** A stretch assignment is a project or responsibility slightly above the engineer's current level, with a defined scope and a deadline. Examples: leading a postmortem, designing the monitoring architecture for a new service, contributing to an Architecture Decision Record, owning the on-call rotation redesign project. These should not be assigned ad-hoc — track them.

4. **Create rotation opportunities.** A 3–6 month rotation to a different team (SRE rotating into Architecture, Platform Engineering rotating into a product team) builds skills, expands networks, and breaks the growth stall pattern. Make rotations available and publicize that they exist.

5. **Track time-in-level for every engineer.** Pull this monthly. Flag anyone who has been at the same level for more than 3 years without a documented promotion plan. That engineer is either being managed poorly or is a flight risk. In either case, the Director needs to know.

---

### Risk 3: Invisible Work

**What it sounds like in 1:1s:**
- "Nobody notices this work until something breaks. When it breaks, everyone knows SRE. When it doesn't break, nobody knows we exist."
- "I spent three months on a reliability project that saved the company from a major outage, and nobody outside this team knows it happened."
- "Leadership only talks about us when we're the problem, not when we're the reason things are working."

**Root cause:** Operations work is invisible by nature. Success in SRE looks like nothing happening — no outages, no data loss, no customer impact. This is the opposite of feature work, where success looks like shipping something visible. The engineer who quietly prevented 10 outages this quarter is harder to celebrate than the engineer who shipped a new feature. Over time, this invisibility erodes the sense that the work matters.

**Specific interventions:**

1. **Monthly operations report to VP/executive level.** Include a "reliability investments" section that frames operational work in terms of outcomes: "The circuit breaker implementation in the payment service prevented 3 potential P2 incidents this quarter based on the failure patterns we observed in staging. Estimated impact avoided: [X hours of customer-facing downtime]." This reframes SRE from "the team that fixes outages" to "the team that prevents them."

2. **Director actively advocates in leadership forums.** When the weekly leadership review happens, the Director's job is to name wins from the operations team: "The team shipped 47 changes this quarter with a 98.5% change success rate. That's not an accident — it's the result of the pre-change review process we implemented in Q1." If the Director doesn't say this, no one else will.

3. **Attribute prevented incidents.** When a performance gate in the deployment pipeline catches a memory leak before it reaches production, document it: "Performance gate caught [issue] in [service] — would have caused estimated P2 in production within [timeframe]. Fixed before release." Track these monthly. Report them quarterly. Engineers who know their prevention work is documented and visible are more likely to invest in it.

4. **Make PIR action items visible to leadership.** When a P1 PIR generates 10 action items and the team completes all 10 within 30 days, that is a significant delivery. Track PIR action item completion rates and report them. "We generated 34 PIR action items this quarter and completed 31 — 91% close rate within SLA." That number belongs in the VP-level monthly report.

## Section 3 — The On-Call Health Dashboard

Run this dashboard weekly. It takes 15 minutes if the data sources are connected. It takes longer only if you're pulling manually — automate the pull if you run it more than twice.

### Metrics to Track Weekly

| Metric | What You're Looking For |
|--------|-------------------------|
| Alert volume (week-over-week) | Is total alert count trending up, flat, or down? |
| Pages per engineer per week | By person, not average. Who is carrying the most load? |
| Alert-to-action ratio | Of all pages, what % require a human to do something vs. auto-resolve or become noise? |
| MTTA trend (4-week rolling) | Mean time to acknowledge, rolling average. Trending up = fatigue or runbook gap. |
| % of pages outside business hours | What fraction of pages hit engineers outside 8am–6pm local? |
| Alert debt | New alerts added this week minus alerts closed or tuned. Positive = accumulating debt. |

### Interpreting the Signals

**Alert volume trending up 10%+ month-over-month:** The system is degrading or growing without a corresponding investment in stability. Don't tune alerts to suppress the signal — investigate the source. Is a service failing more frequently? Is load increasing? Has a new deployment introduced instability?

**One engineer getting 3x the pages of other rotation members:** The rotation is broken. This is not that engineer's fault. Fix the schedule — check whether they're covering additional shifts, whether their services alert at disproportionate rates, or whether they're being defaulted to for escalations outside the rotation. Address within 1 week.

**Alert-to-action ratio > 50%:** More than half of all pages require a human to do something. This is the primary driver of on-call burnout at volume. Actionable alert rate > 50% means the alert configuration is too aggressive or the system is genuinely unstable. Triage: which alerts are firing most frequently and what action do they require? Start there.

**MTTA trending up over 4 weeks:** Engineers are acknowledging pages more slowly than before. This is a fatigue signal. It can also indicate that engineers don't know what to do with an alert — a runbook gap. Check both: has on-call load increased recently (fatigue), and are there alerts without corresponding runbooks (knowledge gap)?

**> 30% of pages outside business hours:** The system is not stable during peak or off-peak usage. Investigate: are these alerts clustering in the overnight window (batch jobs, scheduled processes) or randomly distributed? Random distribution suggests chronic instability; clustering suggests a specific batch process or scheduled job that needs a reliability investment.

**Alert debt positive for 3+ consecutive weeks:** You are accumulating alert debt. Every new alert added without removing or tuning an old one increases the cognitive load of the on-call engineer. Run an alert tuning sprint: identify the top 10 highest-volume alerts, review their action rate, and either tune the threshold, convert to informational, or eliminate the alert if it requires no human action.

## Section 4 — The Director's Monthly Health Review

Run this on the first Monday of each month. Block 90 minutes. Do not cancel it; this is how you see problems before they become crises.

### The Review Sequence

**Step 1 — On-call health dashboard (15 minutes)**
Pull last month's pages per engineer, alert volume trend, MTTA, and alert-to-action ratio. Are any metrics in amber or red? If yes, which domain? Which specific engineer or service?

**Step 2 — Delegation charter metrics (15 minutes)**
Review the 4 metrics tracked in each manager's delegation charter (from Chapter 6). Are they trending in the right direction? Where is the delta between target and actual largest?

**Step 3 — Time-in-level review (5 minutes)**
Pull the time-in-level list for every engineer. Flag anyone at the same level for > 3 years without a documented promotion plan. Flag anyone who was promoted > 6 months ago and hasn't received updated growth targets.

**Step 4 — New hire ramp status (5 minutes)**
For any engineer who started in the last 90 days: what is their first-contribution date? Are they on track for the < 45-day target? If anyone is past 45 days without a first contribution, meet with their manager this week.

**Step 5 — Visibility check (5 minutes)**
Ask yourself: is anyone on the team invisible to me? Who have I not heard from, directly or through their manager, in more than 2 weeks? Silence from an individual contributor can indicate high load with no bandwidth to surface it, disengagement, or a management gap. If someone has been invisible for > 2 weeks, schedule a skip-level 1:1 within 10 business days.

**Step 6 — Toil percentage review (15 minutes)**
Review toil percentage for each domain. Is any domain above 60%? If yes, what is the specific source? Is there a problem record open for it? If not, open one.

**Step 7 — Action assignment (10 minutes)**
Identify one health risk to address this month. Assign it explicitly: who owns it, what is the deliverable, what is the due date. This is tracked in your standing management tracker, not in memory.

### The Monthly Action Log

Maintain a simple running log: Month, Health Risk Identified, Owner, Due Date, Status. Review the previous month's action at the start of the next review. If it's not closed, understand why before adding a new action.

This log is the difference between a health review that drives change and one that generates conversation. The conversation is not the output — the action is.
