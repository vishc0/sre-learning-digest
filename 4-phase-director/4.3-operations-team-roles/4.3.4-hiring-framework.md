# Chapter 04 — Hiring Framework for Operations Roles

**Audience**: Director of Operations  
**Purpose**: A working hiring guide — not HR boilerplate. Use this to screen resumes, design interview panels, evaluate candidates against role-specific rubrics, and construct competitive offers.

---

## Section 1: Hiring Principles

These five principles govern every hiring decision you make on this team. They are not aspirational — they are filters. If a candidate fails on any one of them, no amount of technical skill closes the gap.

### 1. Hire for judgment in ambiguity

Operations roles encounter novel failure modes daily. The RabbitMQ cluster behavior you saw last quarter will not repeat in exactly the same form. Your people need to reason through situations that no runbook anticipated — while a VP is on the bridge and the queue depth is climbing.

Memorized procedures are table stakes. What you are hiring for is the ability to assess an unfamiliar situation, form a reasonable hypothesis, execute a conservative test, and adjust. This is not a teachable skill in 90 days. Screen for it explicitly.

**How to detect it**: Ask about the most unusual incident they ever worked. Push past the resolution — ask how they knew which hypothesis to test first, what they ruled out, and what they would do differently. People with genuine judgment will narrate their reasoning. People without it will narrate their actions.

### 2. Hire for communication — both directions

Operations bridges engineering and business. Your managers must translate a JVM heap exhaustion into business risk language for a VP and must translate a VP's "we cannot go down again" into a concrete SLO with an error budget for an engineer. Both directions must be fluent.

A manager who is technically sharp but communicates poorly upward will fail at stakeholder management. A manager who communicates well upward but cannot earn the trust of engineers will fail at execution. You need both.

**How to detect it**: In the Director interview (Stage 5), give the candidate a technical scenario and ask them to explain it to you as if you are the VP of Customer Experience. Then ask them to explain the same scenario to a new SRE. Both explanations should be accurate, but the framing, vocabulary, and level of detail should be completely different. Anyone who gives you the same speech twice either does not understand their audience or does not understand the content.

### 3. Hire for calm under pressure

Past behavior predicts future behavior. Hypotheticals reveal what people aspire to; incident history reveals what they actually did.

You want candidates who describe high-pressure situations with specificity, who can recall what they were thinking in a P1 at 3 AM, and who talk about those situations without either catastrophizing or minimizing. The candidate who says "it was pretty intense but we just worked through it" and cannot tell you what the first five minutes looked like did not own the incident — they survived it.

**How to detect it**: Never ask "how would you handle a P1?" Ask "walk me through the last P1 you commanded — what happened in the first five minutes?" The specificity of the answer is the signal. Vague answers mean they were not the decision-maker. Specific answers — who they called, what they pulled up first in Splunk, what the first hypothesis was — mean they were in the chair.

### 4. Hire for humility

Operations people who are too proud to escalate create disasters. The classic failure pattern: a senior engineer who is certain they know what is wrong, spends two hours debugging in the wrong direction, and finally escalates when the situation has degraded from recoverable to critical.

Humility in operations is not self-deprecation. It is the operational discipline to say "I have been on this for 20 minutes and I am not converging — I need a second set of eyes" before pride lets a recoverable incident become a P1 bridge call.

**How to detect it**: Ask them about a time they were wrong during an incident — specifically, a time they had a hypothesis that turned out to be incorrect. Candidates with genuine humility will describe this with clarity and sometimes with humor. They will tell you what they thought, why they thought it, and what the actual root cause was. Candidates who cannot name a time they were wrong in an incident are either very new to operations or not self-aware. Both are risks.

### 5. Hire for systems thinking

Second-order effects are the primary source of surprise in complex systems. Restarting a Kafka consumer group fixes the lag but may cause a downstream ordering system to receive a burst of out-of-sequence messages. Scaling up an EKS node group reduces latency but may surface a Kubernetes API server rate limit you did not know existed at that node count.

People who see only the immediate action and not the downstream consequence will fix one thing and break another. This is particularly dangerous in middleware environments where message ordering, exactly-once delivery guarantees, and connection pool limits create cascading dependencies.

**How to detect it**: Give the candidate a simple proposed action ("we are going to restart the TIBCO adapter to clear the connection backlog") and ask them: "what else might change when you do that?" Strong candidates will walk through at least two downstream effects. They will ask about queue depth, in-flight messages, and whether the receiving system has a reconnect throttle. Weak candidates will say "the adapter comes back up and the connections re-establish."

## Section 2: Standard Interview Structure (All Roles)

Every operations hire goes through this five-stage process. Do not compress stages to move faster — the risk of a wrong hire in operations is measured in P1 incidents, not hiring cycle time.

| Stage | Format | Duration | Conducted By | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Resume screen + recruiter phone screen | 30 min | Recruiter | Baseline qualifications, compensation alignment, availability |
| 2 | Technical screen | 60 min | Domain manager (IPM-Mgr, CRM-Mgr, PRM, or ATL depending on role) | Domain knowledge depth, tool fluency, hands-on experience validation |
| 3 | Behavioral interview | 60 min | Senior team member (Sr-SRE or peer manager) | STAR format, incident history, collaboration patterns, escalation judgment |
| 4 | Domain scenario exercise | 60–90 min | Panel of 2 (domain manager + one technical peer) | Live or take-home case — assesses applied judgment, not recalled knowledge |
| 5 | Director interview | 45 min | You | Culture alignment, judgment, communication quality, long-term fit |

**Your role in Stage 2–4**: You do not conduct these. You design them. For each role you open, define the scenario used in Stage 4 before the first resume review — not after. Hiring managers who design the scenario while reviewing candidates unconsciously tune it to the candidates they have already seen, which introduces bias.

**Stage 5 — what you are assessing**: You already have the technical signal from Stage 2 and the behavioral signal from Stage 3. Your job in the Director interview is to assess three things: (1) whether this person can communicate clearly in an unstructured conversation, (2) whether their stated motivations for the role match what the role actually demands, and (3) whether they ask questions that reveal how they think about the work. A candidate who asks no questions in a Director interview is either checked out or over-rehearsed — both are signals worth noting.

## Section 3: Role-Specific Hiring Frameworks

### Incident & Problem Management Manager

**Must-have competencies:**
- Has commanded at least three P1 incidents as the named Incident Commander — not "was on the bridge," but ran the bridge. Can describe their protocol for the first five minutes.
- Has facilitated a blameless post-incident review where the team did not want to do it. Knows how to manage defensiveness in a PIR room.
- Runs on data: measures MTTR trend, recurrence rate, and IC coverage, not just whether incidents got resolved.
- Maintains composure with a VP on the bridge who is asking the same question repeatedly. Has been in this situation and has a method for handling it.

**Nice-to-have competencies:**
- Experience with ITIL v4 or an equivalent structured framework (valuable as shared vocabulary, not as a substitute for judgment).
- Has built or materially improved a KEDB — not just used one.
- Experience with alert fatigue remediation — has reduced alert volume without reducing signal quality.

**Red flags:**
- "We just fix it and move on." — No PIR culture means problems recur. This is a non-starter.
- Cannot name an incident management metric they tracked at their last job. If they never measured it, they never managed it.
- Describes their role in every major incident as "I led the team" without being able to name a specific decision they made and why. This is the "we" problem — they were present, not in charge.
- Blames engineers when incidents happen. The PIR is not a performance review.

**Interview Questions:**

**Q1: "Walk me through the last P1 you commanded. What did you do in the first five minutes?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Vague timeline. Describes what the team did, not what they personally decided. Cannot recall specific actions. "We got everyone on a call and started looking at it." |
| 2 | Clear sequence of events. Names who they called and why. Mentions parallel tracks for mitigation and comms. Uses "I" correctly for decisions they made. |
| 3 | Crisp first-five-minute protocol. Names their first action (acknowledge → declare → assign IC → open bridge → notify stakeholders). Describes parallel tracks explicitly. Names what they delegated and the reasoning behind each delegation. Can state the mitigation hypothesis they worked first and why. |

**Q2: "How do you run a PIR when the team is burned out and wants to move on?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Sets a meeting. Sends an agenda. Hopes people show up. |
| 2 | Mentions psychological safety and blameless culture. Uses language from Accelerate or equivalent. Acknowledges the emotional state of the team. |
| 3 | Specific facilitation technique. Runs a pre-PIR survey to collect timeline data asynchronously so the meeting is not reconstruction work. Starts with the timeline as shared fact before moving to causes. Uses structured cause-mapping (5 Whys or equivalent) rather than open-ended discussion. Asks every participant for one thing they would do differently — ensures the room hears from everyone, not just the loudest voice. Does not allow the PIR to become a blame session by redirecting system-level causes explicitly. |

**Q3: "Show me the metrics you used to measure the health of your incident management program at your last job."**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | MTTR and alert count. Volume metrics only. No trend analysis. |
| 2 | MTTR, MTTA, recurrence rate. Understands that MTTR alone is insufficient. Can describe what a healthy trend looks like. |
| 3 | MTTR trend over rolling 90 days, recurring incident rate by service, IC coverage breadth (what percentage of incidents were handled by a named IC vs. ad-hoc), alert-to-action ratio (what fraction of alerts resulted in a human action), SLO breach rate correlated with incident volume. Can explain why each metric was chosen and what decision it informed. |

**Q4: "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a VP's decision during a P1 — what did you do?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Never had this experience, or deferred immediately without surfacing a concern. |
| 2 | Raised the concern once and then deferred. Describes the outcome diplomatically. |
| 3 | Surfaced the concern with specific technical data ("if we restart the service now, we risk losing the in-flight messages in the queue — here is what I recommend instead and why"). Accepted the VP's decision after being heard and executed it without undermining it. Can describe what they would do differently in hindsight. The key signal: they advocated with data and then committed. |

### Change & Release Management Manager

**Must-have competencies:**
- Has run a CAB (Change Advisory Board) — not attended one, run one. Can describe their scoring rubric for change risk assessment.
- Fluent in DORA metrics: deploy frequency, change failure rate, MTTR, and lead time for changes. Can describe how these metrics influenced a decision they made.
- Has assessed risk for infrastructure-level changes — Kubernetes cluster upgrades, message broker topology changes, or equivalent. Not just application deployments.
- Has experience with blue/green or canary release patterns — can describe the rollback trigger conditions they defined and why.

**Nice-to-have competencies:**
- Has implemented a change freeze process and enforced it when developers pushed back.
- Experience with automated change risk scoring (integrating with CI/CD pipeline to flag high-risk changes before CAB submission).
- Has defined a Standard Change catalog — the list of pre-approved, repeatable changes that bypass CAB.

**Red flags:**
- "We follow the process, so we're safe." — Process compliance is not risk management. This candidate will approve bad changes because they filled out the right form.
- Cannot explain the difference between a Standard Change and a Normal Change. This is table-stakes vocabulary.
- Has never rejected a change. Either they have never managed a real CAB or they approve everything, which makes the CAB meaningless.
- Describes DORA metrics as "things the leadership team tracks" rather than tools they use to improve their team.

**Interview Questions:**

**Q1: "Walk me through how you scored change risk at your last organization. What factors went into the assessment?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | "We looked at impact and urgency." Generic. Could not be wrong but reveals no system. |
| 2 | Describes a structured rubric: blast radius, rollback complexity, testing evidence, deployment window, past change failure rate for this system. Uses a scoring matrix, not a gut check. |
| 3 | Structured rubric plus calibration: explains how the rubric was built (using past change failures as training data), how it was weighted (systems with more dependencies get higher blast radius scores), and how edge cases were adjudicated. Can name a change that scored low risk but turned out to cause an incident — and explains what the scoring model missed and how they updated it. |

**Q2: "Tell me about a change you rejected at CAB and what happened afterward."**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Cannot recall a specific rejection, or describes one where the requester immediately agreed. |
| 2 | Names a specific change, the risk factors that led to rejection, and the outcome. The requester pushed back; they held the position. |
| 3 | Specific change with technical detail. Explains the risk factors clearly. Describes a structured conversation with the requester: what additional information or testing would change the decision. The change was either rescheduled with additional controls or abandoned. Describes what they communicated to the requester and why, because change managers who reject without explanation create adversarial relationships with delivery teams. |

**Q3: "How do you use DORA metrics to drive improvement — give me a specific example."**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Describes DORA metrics conceptually. Cannot name a specific improvement they drove using the data. |
| 2 | Names a specific metric (e.g., change failure rate) that was elevated, identifies a contributing factor (e.g., insufficient pre-deployment testing for a specific service category), and describes a process change they implemented. |
| 3 | Full improvement cycle: baseline measurement, hypothesis about the cause, intervention design, re-measurement, and outcome. Can describe what the data looked like before and after. Understands that DORA metrics are lagging indicators and describes what leading indicators they tracked to know the intervention was working before the lagging metric improved. |

**Q4: "An engineering team wants to bypass CAB for an emergency fix during a P1. What do you do?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Approves it because it is an emergency. No process. |
| 2 | Routes it through an emergency change process with expedited approval. Documents it. Reviews it in the next CAB. |
| 3 | Explains the emergency change protocol: specific approval chain (Director + domain manager), documented risk acceptance, mandatory post-implementation review within 48 hours. Distinguishes between "emergency" (unplanned production issue requiring immediate action) and "urgent" (someone did not plan ahead). Can describe a time they pushed back on a team claiming emergency status when the situation did not meet the definition. |

### Senior SRE (Incident Command Focus)

**Must-have competencies:**
- Has been the named Incident Commander for a P1 at scale — not "part of the team," the IC. Can articulate the first five minutes of their IC protocol without being prompted.
- Has designed monitoring for a service from scratch — not consumed an existing dashboard but made instrumentation decisions: what to instrument, what alert thresholds to set, and why.
- Technical depth in at least one platform layer: Kubernetes/EKS, enterprise messaging (Kafka, RabbitMQ, TIBCO), or distributed systems observability (Splunk, Prometheus, OpenTelemetry).
- Can explain a complex incident in plain language to a non-technical audience without losing accuracy.

**Nice-to-have competencies:**
- Has written a runbook and knows what makes a runbook actually usable at 3 AM vs. theoretically correct.
- Experience with chaos engineering or game-day exercises — has broken things intentionally to test recovery.
- Has trained junior SREs in incident command — can teach the discipline, not just execute it.

**Red flags:**
- Cannot describe a specific incident with technical depth. "We fixed it" without mechanism. If they cannot explain what the root cause was and how they verified it, they were not doing root cause analysis — they were doing recovery and hoping.
- Treats monitoring as consumption ("I check the Splunk dashboards") rather than design ("I defined these metrics because these are the signals that matter for this service").
- Has never been IC — has always been "on the team." At Senior SRE level, this is a gap.
- Describes on-call as something that happens to them rather than something they own.

**Interview Questions:**

**Q1: "Walk me through a production incident where you were IC. What was happening, and what did you do in the first five minutes?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Vague description of the incident. Cannot recall specific actions or decisions in sequence. Describes what the team did collectively. |
| 2 | Clear timeline. Names the service affected, the symptom observed, who they brought in and why. Describes mitigation actions in order. Can state what the root cause turned out to be. |
| 3 | Full IC narrative: symptom observed, declaration criteria met, bridge opened, parallel tracks assigned (comms, investigation, mitigation), first hypothesis formed with reasoning, hypothesis tested, result, adjustment. Names specific commands run or dashboards checked. Can describe what they ruled out and why. Knows the difference between mitigation (stopped the bleeding) and resolution (fixed the root cause). |

**Q2: "Tell me about a service you instrumented from scratch. How did you decide what to monitor?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Added some dashboards and alerts. Cannot explain the selection logic. |
| 2 | Describes USE method (Utilization, Saturation, Errors) or RED method (Rate, Errors, Duration) applied to the service. Can name the signals they chose and why they matter. |
| 3 | Instrumentation design with tradeoffs: why they chose certain SLIs, what they deliberately did not alert on (and why not alerting on it was safe), how they set thresholds (not arbitrary — based on baseline behavior and SLO targets), and what they changed after the first month of production data. Can describe a false positive they tuned away and a false negative they caught by adding a signal. |

**Q3: "Describe a time your hypothesis during an incident was wrong. How did you figure that out, and what did you do next?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Cannot recall being wrong. Or: describes a time someone else was wrong. |
| 2 | Names a specific incident, the incorrect hypothesis, the evidence that disproved it, and the pivot to the correct hypothesis. |
| 3 | Full reasoning narrative: what made the wrong hypothesis plausible, what evidence they checked first, what the disconfirming signal was, how long they spent on the wrong track, and what they learned about their own diagnostic process from the experience. Strong candidates will tell you how this changed their IC approach going forward. |

**Q4: "What does a good on-call week look like, and what does a bad on-call week look like?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Good = quiet. Bad = lots of pages. No depth. |
| 2 | Good = alert quality is high (pages are actionable), bad = alert fatigue (woken up for things that self-resolved). Understands that quiet and healthy are not the same. |
| 3 | Good on-call week: every page required human action, each action had a clear runbook or a clear decision, no page woke them more than once for the same reason, they had time to improve one thing they noticed during the rotation. Bad on-call week: repeat pages for the same issue (no problem management), alerts that resolved themselves (noise that erodes trust), no time for improvement because the rotation was 100% reactive. Can describe how they would fix a bad on-call pattern — not just survive it. |

### Platform Architect

**Must-have competencies:**
- Has written real ADRs — not templates, actual decision records that capture context, options considered, trade-offs, and the chosen approach. Can defend one in detail under challenge.
- Has made and owned a technology decision at platform scale — not recommended one, made one. Understands what it means to be accountable for a choice that affects multiple teams.
- Deep experience in enterprise middleware: Kafka, RabbitMQ, or TIBCO at production scale. Can describe the operational characteristics of their chosen platform (partition rebalancing behavior, connection pool limits, memory model) not just the feature set.
- Kubernetes at scale: has managed multi-cluster deployments, understands etcd and the API server as reliability constraints, has worked through node autoscaler and cluster-autoscaler interaction.

**Nice-to-have competencies:**
- Has led a technology migration — from one platform to another — including the coexistence period and cutover strategy.
- Experience with service mesh (Istio, Linkerd) and the operational overhead trade-offs.
- Has presented architecture decisions to an executive audience and translated technical trade-offs into business risk language.

**Red flags:**
- Architecture by intuition, not data. Cannot cite trade-offs in a previous decision they made. "We went with Kafka because it is the best option" without being able to articulate what alternatives were considered and why they were rejected.
- Has only recommended technology choices — has never been accountable for operating the platform after the choice was made. Architects who do not operate develop blind spots that become your team's incidents.
- Cannot explain why they would or would not use a specific technology in a specific context. Everything is a "it depends" without the substance of what it depends on.
- Dismisses operational concerns as "that is an ops problem." In a platform architect role, there is no separation — the architecture is the operations.

**Interview Questions:**

**Q1: "Walk me through an ADR you wrote. What decision were you making, what options did you consider, and why did you choose what you chose?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Describes a technology choice. Cannot articulate the options that were not chosen or why. "We went with X because it met our requirements." |
| 2 | Names two or three options, describes the evaluation criteria, and explains the decision. Can articulate one or two trade-offs of the chosen approach. |
| 3 | Full ADR narrative: the context that forced the decision, the options with their specific trade-offs (not just pros and cons — actual operational characteristics at the scale they were running), the evaluation criteria weighted by business priority, the chosen option and its consequences, and what has happened since. Can name something the ADR got wrong and describe how they would write it differently today. |

**Q2: "Tell me about a time a technical decision you made caused an operational problem. What happened?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Cannot name one. Either they have not made consequential decisions or they do not acknowledge their role in outcomes. Both are risks. |
| 2 | Names a specific decision and the resulting operational problem. Describes the remediation. Is factual, not defensive. |
| 3 | Specific decision with technical depth — what assumption the decision was built on, how the production environment violated that assumption, what the operational impact was (incident count, performance degradation, cost), and how the architecture was corrected. Can describe what they added to their decision-making process as a result. The ability to learn from architectural mistakes is the signal — not the absence of mistakes. |

**Q3: "You are evaluating whether to migrate from RabbitMQ to Kafka for an enterprise integration platform. Walk me through your assessment."**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Kafka is better because it scales better. Generic. No trade-off analysis. |
| 2 | Describes the key differences: Kafka's log-based model vs. RabbitMQ's queue-based model, consumer group semantics vs. competing consumers, retention behavior, ordering guarantees. Identifies use cases where each is stronger. |
| 3 | Full assessment framework: current workload characteristics (message rate, payload size, consumer count, ordering requirements), existing consumer patterns (are they pull or push, do they need exactly-once semantics), operational maturity of the team for Kafka-specific failure modes (partition rebalancing, consumer lag monitoring, log compaction), migration complexity (coexistence period, consumer migration order, cutover risk), and total cost of ownership. Can describe what a phased migration would look like and what the highest-risk moment in the migration is. |

**Q4: "How do you keep platform standards current in an organization where teams move faster than the governance process?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Enforces the standards through review gates. Teams work around them. Does not acknowledge the tension. |
| 2 | Acknowledges the tension. Describes making standards easy to adopt (golden paths, templates, reference architectures) rather than relying solely on enforcement. |
| 3 | Full governance model: standards with fast lanes (pre-approved patterns that teams can adopt without review), exception process that is lightweight and documented (so teams who need to deviate can do so quickly and transparently), regular refresh cadence for standards to prevent them from becoming stale, and feedback loops from teams to the architecture function so standards evolve based on real usage. Can describe a standard they retired because it was no longer serving the teams. |

### Performance Engineer

**Must-have competencies:**
- Has run a soak test — not a load test, a soak test. Minimum 4-hour sustained load to surface memory leak and resource exhaustion patterns. Can describe the difference in what each test type reveals.
- Can read a JVM heap dump or garbage collection log. Knows what a G1GC pause spike looks like and what causes it. Applicable to TIBCO adapter processes and Kafka consumer JVM configurations.
- Knows Little's Law (L = λW — the number of requests in a system equals the arrival rate times the average time in system) and can apply it to size thread pools, connection pools, or queue depths.
- Has set a performance gate in a CI/CD pipeline — a specific threshold on response time, throughput, or error rate that blocks a deployment if exceeded.

**Nice-to-have competencies:**
- Has done memory profiling with a tool (VisualVM, JProfiler, async-profiler) not just GC log analysis.
- Experience with distributed tracing (Jaeger, Zipkin, or OpenTelemetry) to isolate latency at the service boundary vs. internal processing.
- Has built a performance baseline from production traffic replay, not synthetic load.

**Red flags:**
- Treats load testing as "run JMeter and see what breaks." No hypothesis, no baseline, no degradation model. This is performance testing theater — it generates reports but not signal.
- Cannot explain what thread pool exhaustion looks like in a GC log or a Splunk query. This is a fundamental skill for enterprise middleware performance engineering.
- Has never set a performance gate — only runs tests after deployment to validate. This puts the performance signal after the risk, not before it.
- Conflates latency and throughput. Cannot describe a scenario where latency is acceptable and throughput is not, or vice versa.

**Interview Questions:**

**Q1: "Walk me through a performance problem you diagnosed. What were the symptoms, what did you investigate first, and what did you find?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | "Latency was high, we added more capacity." No diagnosis. |
| 2 | Describes a specific symptom, the investigation toolchain used (Splunk, JVM metrics, thread dumps), and the finding. Can name the root cause. |
| 3 | Full diagnostic narrative: symptom with specific metrics (p99 latency at Xms, error rate Y%), initial hypotheses ranked by likelihood, investigation sequence with specific commands or queries used, finding with technical explanation (e.g., "G1GC pause storms caused by a 4GB heap running at 85% occupancy with a humongous object allocation rate exceeding the region size"), remediation applied, and the before/after metric comparison. Can describe what would have happened if they had not caught it before the next release. |

**Q2: "Explain Little's Law and show me how you have used it in your work."**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Has heard of it. Cannot explain it or apply it. |
| 2 | Can state L = λW correctly and explain the variables. Can describe a theoretical application. |
| 3 | Can apply it to a specific sizing problem they solved: "Our Kafka consumer was processing 500 messages per second, each taking an average of 200ms to process. Little's Law tells us we need L = 500 × 0.2 = 100 concurrent threads in steady state. We were configured for 50, which explained the lag accumulation under peak load." Can extend the reasoning to what happens at 2x load and what the thread pool ceiling should be given available heap. |

**Q3: "How do you design a performance gate that is strict enough to catch regressions but loose enough not to block legitimate releases?"**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Sets a threshold. Does not describe how they chose it or how they maintain it. |
| 2 | Describes baselining from production data, setting thresholds at a percentage above the baseline (e.g., p95 latency must not exceed 120% of baseline), and a review process for adjusting the gate when the system changes. |
| 3 | Full gate design: baseline methodology (rolling 30-day P95 from production, not synthetic), threshold selection (P95 because P99 is too noisy for CI gates, not P50 because that misses tail latency regressions), confidence interval requirements (test must run long enough to stabilize — at least 200 warm-up requests before measurement begins), separate gates for different traffic profiles (read-heavy vs. write-heavy endpoints), and an exception workflow for releases where a regression is understood and accepted with documented risk. Can describe a gate they tuned after it generated a false positive that blocked a valid release. |

**Q4: "You are asked to performance-test a new Kafka consumer before it goes to production. Walk me through your test plan."**

| Score | Answer Profile |
|---|---|
| 1 | Sets up JMeter or similar, runs load, checks if it fails. |
| 2 | Defines test scenarios (normal load, peak load, sustained load), identifies the metrics to collect (consumer lag, processing latency, GC behavior, thread pool utilization), and sets pass/fail criteria before running the test. |
| 3 | Full test plan: baseline test (current production consumer behavior replicated in a perf environment), load test at 1x and 2x expected peak (to establish headroom), soak test at sustained peak for 4+ hours to detect memory growth patterns, chaos test (what happens when the upstream Kafka broker partition count changes during consumption), specific metrics tracked (consumer lag per partition, end-to-end message latency including queue time, JVM heap usage over time, GC pause frequency and duration, thread pool active count vs. max), pass criteria for each test type, and a documented rollback criterion if the new consumer does not meet the gate within the test window. |

## Section 4: Offer Construction Guidance

### Level Mapping

Mapping a candidate's current title to your internal level is not a one-to-one translation — titles vary wildly across organizations. Use scope and impact, not label:

| Internal Level | Scope | What They Own |
|---|---|---|
| IC Level 3 (Senior) | Team or domain | Executes complex work independently; consults on design; mentors L2s |
| IC Level 4 (Staff/Principal) | Multi-team | Drives cross-team initiatives; defines technical direction for a domain; influences strategy |
| Manager Level 1 | Team (5–10 people) | Runs day-to-day operations for one function; accountable for team-level metrics |
| Manager Level 2 | Multi-team (10–20 people) | Manages managers; owns a department function (e.g., all of Incident & Problem Management) |
| Director | Organization (20–50 people) | Sets the operating model; accountable to VP; owns budget and headcount |

**Practical rule**: if a candidate was a "Senior SRE" at a three-person startup, they are not your Senior SRE equivalent — the scope difference is too large. If they were a "Staff Engineer" at a company with 500+ engineers and a complex platform, that may map to Principal or Manager L1 depending on whether they led people. Ask scope questions, not title questions.

### Compensation Benchmarking

Anchor compensation to market percentile based on the impact level of the role, not the candidate's current salary. Using current salary as the anchor perpetuates pay inequity and anchors to a number that may reflect a prior employer's budget constraints.

| Impact Level | Market Anchor | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| High operational impact (IPM-Mgr, CRM-Mgr, PRM) | P75 | These roles carry 24/7 accountability and are hard to replace quickly — P75 reflects the cost of losing the hire within 12 months |
| Technical specialist (Sr-SRE, SRE-Perf, PlatArch) | P75–P90 depending on specialization depth | Deep Kafka/Kubernetes/TIBCO expertise is scarce; going below P75 means competing with market for retention immediately after hire |
| Standard operations (ChgMgr, RelEng, EnvEng) | P50–P75 | Broader talent pool; P50 is defensible if the total comp package (equity, benefits, flexibility) is competitive |
| Analyst track (ProbAnal, SLA-Anal, AppPortAnal) | P50 | Typically earlier-career roles with defined growth paths; P50 is appropriate with clear Level 3 to Level 4 progression documented |

Get current market data from your HR compensation team or an external benchmarking source (Radford, Levels.fyi for engineering, or Mercer for operations roles) before extending any offer. Do not rely on the candidate's stated expectations as the market signal — they may be under-anchored.

### Equity Considerations for Operations Roles

Operations roles are not typically equity-heavy, but equity matters for retention. Guidelines:
- For Manager L1 and above: include equity in the offer. The vesting cliff creates retention incentive through the highest-risk departure window (months 12–18).
- For IC roles: equity is a differentiator, not a requirement. If budget is constrained, a front-loaded sign-on bonus can compensate for a below-market equity package for a candidate who has unvested equity at their current employer.
- For all roles: never make equity the primary compensation story. Operations professionals who depend on equity for income will leave when equity is underwater. Base compensation must be competitive on its own.

### What to Include in the Offer Call vs. the Offer Letter

**Offer call** (you or the hiring manager conducts this — not the recruiter): communicate the level, the role title, total compensation including base, bonus target, and equity structure, start date flexibility, and the reason you personally want this person on the team. This is the persuasion call — the letter is the paperwork. If you are excited about the hire, say so directly and specifically. "I think the way you described your PIR facilitation approach is exactly what this team needs" lands better than "we are very excited to extend this offer."

**Offer letter** (HR produces): base salary, bonus structure and payout schedule, equity grant and vesting schedule, benefits summary, start date, conditions of employment (background check, employment authorization). The letter should not contain surprises — anything in the letter that was not covered in the call creates friction and erodes trust before day one.

### Common Negotiation Scenarios

**"My current employer is offering me a counter-offer."**  
Acknowledge it. Do not panic and immediately increase your offer. Ask what the counter-offer changed and whether money was the only factor in their decision. If they were planning to leave for reasons beyond compensation, a counter-offer from the current employer does not change those reasons. If money is genuinely the gap, determine whether your offer can be adjusted within band. If it cannot, be honest: "We are at the top of the band for this level. If compensation is the deciding factor, I want you to make the right decision for yourself. What I can tell you is what makes this role and this team different from what your current employer can offer." Then make that case concretely.

**"I need time to decide."**  
Two weeks is a reasonable window. Beyond two weeks, the probability of close drops significantly — they are either interviewing elsewhere or genuinely undecided (which is its own signal). Set a specific date: "I can hold the offer open until [date]. I want to give you space to decide, and I also want to be fair to the other candidates who are waiting." This is not a pressure tactic — it is honest about the situation.

**"Can you increase the base?"**  
Before answering, ask: "Is base the primary concern, or are there other aspects of the offer you want to discuss?" Sometimes the base question is a proxy for a different concern (title, flexibility, start date, total comp). If it is genuinely about base: check whether you are within band. If yes and you have room, you can move — but anchor the move to something the candidate provided in the process ("based on your performance engineering background, we want to bring you in at the higher end of the band"). Do not offer the increase without a reason — free money with no justification reads as either desperation or sloppy compensation management.

**"I have another offer I am considering."**  
Ask about the other offer — not the specific numbers, but the role type, team size, and scope. This gives you signal about what the candidate is optimizing for. If they are choosing between your role and a similar one, the decision is often about team and manager, not just package. If they are choosing between your role and something fundamentally different (e.g., a startup equity play vs. an enterprise stability play), you are not really competing — they are making a career direction decision and your offer is one input. Clarify what they are deciding and address that decision directly.
