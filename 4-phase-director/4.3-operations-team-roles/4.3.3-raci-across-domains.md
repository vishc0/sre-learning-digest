# Chapter 03 — RACI Across Operational Domains

**Audience**: Director of Operations  
**Purpose**: Working reference for accountability mapping across all operational functions. Use this to onboard new managers, resolve ownership disputes, and identify coverage gaps before they become incidents.

---

## RACI Key

| Letter | Meaning | Rule |
|--------|---------|------|
| **R** | Responsible | Does the work. One or more people can hold R. |
| **A** | Accountable | Owns the outcome. Exactly one person per activity — no exceptions. |
| **C** | Consulted | Must be asked before action is taken. Two-way communication. |
| **I** | Informed | Told after action is taken or at defined checkpoints. One-way. |

**R/A** in one cell means that role both does the work and owns the outcome — acceptable when the individual contributor and accountable owner are the same person.

## Reading the RACI — Common Questions

### What to do when two people are both marked A

**This is not allowed.** The RACI model defines accountability as singular — if two people are both marked A for an activity, you have no accountability. In a P1 at 2 AM, two accountable owners will either both escalate (overlap and noise) or both assume the other is handling it (gap and disaster).

Resolution steps:
1. Identify the activity and the two roles claiming A.
2. Ask: "Who gets the phone call if this goes wrong at midnight?" — that person is A.
3. Ask: "Who signs off on the outcome in the QBR?" — that person is also A. They should be the same person.
4. Demote the other role to C or I depending on how deeply they need to be involved.
5. Update this document and publish the change to both parties on the same day. Unresolved accountability disputes rot quietly and surface loudly.

If the two parties genuinely share accountability (joint team ownership, matrix org), you have an organizational design problem, not a RACI problem. Escalate to whoever owns the org structure and get a decision within two weeks.

### What to do when nobody is marked R

**This is a gap.** An activity with an A and no R means someone is accountable for something that nobody is doing. Common causes: a role was removed from the org and the RACI was not updated, a new activity was added to scope but not assigned, or the activity was assumed to be handled by automation.

Resolution steps:
1. Verify the activity is actually happening — if it isn't, you have an operational gap, not just a documentation gap.
2. Identify who has the skills and capacity to own execution.
3. Assign R, communicate the assignment directly to that person, and confirm they understand what "Responsible" means — they do the work, they deliver the output, they raise blockers proactively.
4. If no one has capacity, the activity is either deprioritized (with documented risk acceptance) or funded as a new hire requirement.

Do not assign R to a team — assign it to a role. "The SRE team" cannot be R. "Sr-SRE" can.

### How to use the RACI in a new hire's onboarding

The RACI is a working document for onboarding, not a trophy document. Use it in three stages:

**Week 1 — orientation pass.** Walk the new hire through every row where their role appears. For each row: explain what the activity is in plain language, describe what "done" looks like, and name one or two people they can call if they are unsure. Do not assume they know what "KEDB entry" or "trueup execution" means — define it.

**Week 2 — shadow pass.** Have the new hire shadow someone executing each activity where their role is R. For activities that occur infrequently (e.g., QBR preparation, EOL risk assessment), use a recorded walkthrough or documented runbook as a substitute.

**Week 3 — confirmation.** Ask the new hire to narrate back what they are Responsible for, what they are Accountable for, and who they Consult versus Inform for their top three activities. Any gaps in their narration identify areas needing additional coaching.

After 30 days, give the new hire edit access to this document and ask them to flag any row where the RACI does not match what is actually happening in practice. Real operations drift from documented process — the new hire's fresh perspective is your best audit tool.
