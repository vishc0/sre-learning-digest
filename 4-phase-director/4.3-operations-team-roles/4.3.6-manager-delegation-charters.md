# Chapter 6: Manager Delegation Charters — Operations Domain Managers

## What a Delegation Charter Is and Why It Exists

A delegation charter is an explicit written contract between you — the Director of Operations — and each of your domain managers. It defines, in writing and with no ambiguity, three things: what decisions the manager makes alone, what decisions require you to be notified, and what decisions require your involvement before anything moves.

Without a charter, every decision becomes a negotiation. A manager who is unsure whether they need your approval will either ask too often — becoming a bottleneck to your inbox — or decide too often on their own and occasionally make a call that should have involved you. Both failure modes erode trust. The charter eliminates the negotiation by making the boundary explicit before any specific situation arises.

A charter also creates accountability in both directions. When you sign off on a charter with a manager, you are committing to stay out of decisions they own. Overriding a manager's charter decision in the moment — without a specific, articulable reason to escalate — undermines their authority on the team and trains them to wait for your direction rather than act independently. The charter only works if you honor it as seriously as they do.

**Key operating principles for all charters in this document:**

- "Full authority" means the manager decides and executes. They do not draft a proposal for your review. They act, then include the outcome in their standard reporting.
- "Notifies Director same business day" means a Slack message or short email with the decision and brief rationale — not a meeting request.
- "Consults Director before deciding" means the manager brings a recommendation and their reasoning. You respond with either approval or a redirect. The default is to approve their recommendation unless you have specific information they do not.
- "Escalates to Director" means you make the call. The manager provides you with the information you need to decide and executes whatever you decide.
- These charters are reviewed annually and re-signed. They are also reviewed and updated after any major structural change: a reorg, a new Tier-1 system onboarding, a significant incident that exposed a gap in the charter, or a change in the manager's team composition.
- These charters are not performance review documents. A manager who makes a decision within their full authority that turns out to be wrong is not in violation of their charter. They exercised their authority; the outcome was a learning. A manager who makes a decision that should have been escalated is in violation of the charter, regardless of whether the outcome was good.
- Review and re-sign annually. Re-open the charter immediately if the team structure changes, a new Tier-1 system is added to the portfolio, or a significant incident reveals an unaddressed ambiguity.

## Charter 1: Incident and Problem Management Manager

**Charter effective date**: _______________
**Manager name**: _______________
**Director name**: _______________
**Next scheduled review**: _______________

---

### Mission

Own the organization's ability to detect, contain, and learn from every incident before it repeats. The measure of success is not incident count — incidents happen. The measure of success is that every incident is contained to its minimum blast radius, every significant incident produces a learning the organization acts on, and no incident recurs because an action item was ignored.

---

### Full Authority — Decides Alone, No Director Involvement Required

You decide and execute without asking. These outcomes appear in your weekly summary.

- Declare P2 and P3 incidents: severity classification, impact statement, opening of the incident bridge
- Assign Incident Commanders for P2, P3, and P4 incidents — including rotating assignments for on-call engineers
- Close problem records for P2, P3, and P4 issues, once problem management criteria are met
- Approve runbook content and authorize publication to the team knowledge base
- Adjust on-call rotation assignments within approved headcount — you decide who is primary, secondary, and overflow, and when rotations change
- Schedule, facilitate, and close PIRs for P2, P3, and P4 incidents
- Enter, update, and retire records in the Known Error Database (KEDB)
- Approve overtime for on-call engineers, within the budget envelope approved quarterly

---

### Decides and Notifies Director Same Business Day

You make the decision and execute it. You send a same-day notification with the decision and brief rationale. No approval required — this is informational.

- Changes to the on-call escalation policy: any change to who gets called, in what order, and under what conditions
- Any decision to waive a mandatory PIR — must document the reason (e.g., incident fully resolved by runbook with no novel failure mode, no customer impact)
- Changes to incident severity definitions — what qualifies as a P1, P2, P3, or P4
- Partnership decisions with other team leads: joint runbooks, shared escalation paths, combined on-call pools with peer teams

---

### Consults Director Before Deciding

You bring a recommendation and your reasoning. Director responds with approval or redirect. You do not act until you have a response, unless the situation is time-sensitive — in which case, you flag the urgency in your message and act after a defined wait window (4 hours for non-urgent, 1 hour for time-sensitive).

- Adding a new tool to the incident management toolchain — new ITSM integrations, new alert routing platforms, new on-call management software
- Proposing a structural change to the on-call rotation model — moving from follow-the-sun to regional, changing rotation length, shifting from team-based to function-based on-call
- Any change that materially affects another team's processes — if your decision requires another team to change their behavior, you consult first

---

### Escalates to Director — Director Decides

You bring the situation with your recommended course of action and the information needed to decide. Director makes the call and you execute it.

- P1 incident declaration: you bring the severity recommendation; Director approves or adjusts
- Any personnel action: performance improvement plan, formal warning, termination recommendation, promotion recommendation — all require Director involvement before any action is communicated to the employee
- Headcount additions or reductions: you can propose, never unilaterally decide
- Budget requests outside the approved quarterly envelope
- Disputes with peer Directors or VP-level stakeholders that have not resolved through direct conversation

---

### Key Metrics You Own

These are the numbers you are accountable for. They appear in your weekly and monthly deliverables and are reviewed in your quarterly Director 1:1.

| Metric | Target | Review Cadence |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| MTTA — P1 | < 5 minutes | Weekly |
| MTTA — P2 | < 15 minutes | Weekly |
| MTTR — P1 | < 2 hours | Weekly |
| MTTR — P2 | < 4 hours | Weekly |
| PIR completion rate (P1 and P2) | 100% within 5 business days | Weekly |
| Recurring incident rate | < 10% of monthly incident volume | Monthly |
| On-call load | < 5 pages per engineer per week (rolling 4-week average) | Weekly |
| KEDB coverage | ≥ 80% of recurring incidents have a KEDB entry | Monthly |

A "recurring incident" is any incident caused by a failure mode that already has an open problem record or a closed problem record without a verified fix.

---

### Weekly Deliverable to Director

Due by end of day Monday, covering the prior week. Format: short written summary in Slack or email, no more than one page.

- Total incident count by severity (P1, P2, P3, P4)
- MTTR by severity — actual vs. target
- Top 3 repeat offenders: services or failure modes that produced more than one incident in the week
- Open PIR action items aging more than 10 business days — owner, blocker, expected close
- On-call health score: pages per engineer (worst, median, best), any rotation coverage gaps

---

### Monthly Deliverable to Director

Due by the 5th business day of the following month. Format: written document, 2–3 pages.

- Full incident metrics with trend lines: MTTA, MTTR, incident volume, severity distribution — current month vs. trailing 3 months
- Problem management status: all open problems, age of each, assigned owner, expected close date; any problem open more than 60 days requires an explanation
- PIR action item closure rate: how many action items were opened in the month, how many closed, how many are overdue
- On-call rotation health assessment: rotation load distribution, engineer burnout risk flags, proposed adjustments for next quarter

---

### When Director Steps In

Even within full authority decisions, you need to know when your Director is going to show up without being asked.

- **Active P1 incident**: Director joins the incident bridge as executive sponsor — not as IC, not to direct technical response. Director is there to field external escalations, protect the IC from political interference, and be available to make resource decisions the IC cannot make alone. You remain IC.
- **MTTR trend degraded 20% or more over 4 consecutive weeks**: Director initiates a working session to review. Not a performance conversation — a diagnostic to find the root cause together.
- **Recurring incident rate exceeds 15% for two consecutive months**: Director initiates a problem management review. The hypothesis is that problem records are not being closed with verified fixes, or that action items are not producing durable solutions.
- **Team member not performing to expectations**: Director is involved before any formal action is initiated. The first conversation between you and the employee about performance is preceded by a conversation between you and your Director about the facts and the plan.
- **External stakeholder raises a concern directly to Director**: Director shares the concern with you and the two of you align on response before Director replies to the stakeholder. Director does not route around you.

## Charter 2: Change and Release Management Manager

**Charter effective date**: _______________
**Manager name**: _______________
**Director name**: _______________
**Next scheduled review**: _______________

---

### Mission

Own the velocity and safety of every change reaching production. The objective is to maximize deployment frequency and minimize change-induced incidents — these are not in tension when the process is well-designed. DORA elite performance (multiple deployments per day, change failure rate below 1%) is the aspirational target. Governance is not a gate that slows change; it is a quality signal that enables faster, safer change.

---

### Full Authority — Decides Alone, No Director Involvement Required

- Approve Standard Changes: any change type in the approved Standard Change catalog ships without additional review
- Reject RFCs at the peer review gate: if an RFC does not meet submission criteria, send it back to the submitter with specific remediation requirements
- Chair CAB sessions: set the agenda, facilitate the review, record the disposition of each RFC
- Declare change freeze for non-emergency deployments during defined risk periods: major holidays, planned peak traffic events (Black Friday, year-end close), platform maintenance windows
- Set CAB scheduling and meeting cadence: weekly, bi-weekly, or adjusted for volume
- Approve deployment pipeline configurations for non-Tier-1 services: build steps, test gates, deployment targets, rollback triggers
- Manage the release calendar for all non-Tier-1 changes: scheduling, conflict resolution, dependency ordering

---

### Decides and Notifies Director Same Business Day

- Changes to the Standard Change catalog: adding a new standard change type, removing one that is no longer safe to pre-approve, modifying the criteria for an existing type
- Emergency changes initiated outside the normal CAB window: any change deployed under the Emergency Change process, with the reason and outcome
- Change freeze declarations lasting more than 5 consecutive business days — these affect business velocity broadly enough that Director needs visibility
- Deployment pipeline outages that affect release velocity for more than 4 hours: what failed, what was bypassed (if anything), restoration timeline

---

### Consults Director Before Deciding

- Changes to the CAB governance model: composition of the CAB, voting rights, quorum requirements, authority of CAB to reject vs. recommend
- Tooling additions to the CI/CD pipeline that are net-new to the organization: a new testing gate, a new deployment tool, a new approval workflow
- Any change to the RFC process that affects SLA commitments to business partners

---

### Escalates to Director — Director Decides

- Changes affecting Tier-1 services: Director is notified before the deployment window opens and approves the go/no-go decision for any Tier-1 change outside standard change classification
- Any situation where a business stakeholder — at VP level or above — is pressuring the team to bypass CAB for a specific change: Director is the one who responds to that stakeholder, not you
- DORA metrics degrading more than 20% over 4 consecutive weeks: Director initiates a joint diagnostic
- Emergency changes required by an active P1 incident affecting Tier-1 services: Director approves before the emergency change is applied
- All personnel actions

---

### Key Metrics You Own

| Metric | Target | Review Cadence |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Deployment frequency | ≥ 1 per week per service (target: daily) | Weekly |
| Change failure rate | < 5% (target: < 1%) | Weekly |
| MTTR from failed change | < 1 hour | Weekly |
| Lead time for change | < 1 week for non-emergency | Monthly |
| CAB throughput | 0 RFCs waiting > 5 business days for CAB review | Weekly |
| Emergency change rate | < 10% of total change volume | Monthly |

Change failure is defined as any change that requires rollback, hotfix, or produces an unplanned incident within 24 hours of deployment.

---

### Weekly Deliverable to Director

Due by end of day Monday, covering the prior week.

- Total change volume: standard, normal, emergency — counts and percentages
- Deployments completed in the week: by service tier
- Change failure count and brief root cause for each failure
- RFCs pending review more than 5 business days: submitter, service, age, blocker
- DORA snapshot: deployment frequency, change failure rate, MTTR from failed change vs. target

---

### Monthly Deliverable to Director

Due by the 5th business day of the following month.

- DORA metrics trend: current month vs. trailing 3 months, with commentary on directional movement
- Change failure root cause analysis: aggregate themes across the month's failures — not individual incident write-ups, but pattern analysis
- CAB throughput report: RFCs submitted, approved, rejected, returned for revision; average time from submission to disposition
- Standard Change catalog review: proposed additions, removals, or criteria changes for the following month
- Release calendar for the next 30 days: scheduled deployments, known risk windows, change freeze dates

---

### When Director Steps In

- **Change failure rate exceeds 10% for two consecutive weeks**: Director initiates a joint review of the change pipeline, test gates, and RFC quality. The hypothesis is a systemic quality gap, not a random bad week.
- **VP or business stakeholder is bypassing CAB governance**: Director handles the stakeholder conversation. You document the bypass attempt, the outcome, and share it with Director same day.
- **Tier-1 system change is being expedited without completing proper review**: Director is notified immediately. Director decides whether to allow the expedite or hold the change. You do not allow a Tier-1 emergency change to proceed without Director awareness, even under time pressure.

## Charter 3: Platform Reliability Manager

**Charter effective date**: _______________
**Manager name**: _______________
**Director name**: _______________
**Next scheduled review**: _______________

---

### Mission

Own the reliability floor of the platform. Every Tier-1 service meets its SLO. The DR plan is tested on schedule and works when it matters. Performance degrades predictably — never catastrophically and never without warning. Toil is tracked, not accepted as permanent.

The reliability manager is not the one who responds to incidents — that is Incident Management. You are the one who ensures the conditions that cause incidents are being systematically addressed, and that when a catastrophic failure happens, the business can recover within committed timeframes.

---

### Full Authority — Decides Alone, No Director Involvement Required

- Approve SLO definitions for Tier-2 and Tier-3 services: what the SLI is, what the target is, what the measurement window is
- Declare an error budget freeze for any service: when a service has consumed its error budget, feature deployments to that service stop until the budget is restored. You make this call.
- Approve DR test plans for all services: test scope, test date, test criteria for pass/fail, recovery procedure
- Own and maintain BCP documentation for all services in scope: procedure accuracy, contact lists, decision trees
- Schedule and conduct quarterly performance reviews for non-Tier-1 services: define the scope, run the test, report outcomes
- Approve load test designs for non-Tier-1 services: acceptance criteria, traffic profiles, pass/fail thresholds
- Set alert thresholds for all services in scope: what fires, at what level, with what urgency
- Approve additions to Splunk dashboards: new panels, new saved searches, new alerting rules

---

### Decides and Notifies Director Same Business Day

- Error budget freeze declaration: the name of the service, the budget consumed, the date of the freeze, the expected recovery timeline
- DR test outcomes for any service: pass or fail, with a one-paragraph summary if failed — Director needs to know test results the day they occur, not in the monthly report
- Performance gate failures that are blocking a planned release: what failed, what service, what the remediation path is
- SLO breach for Tier-2 services: Director needs to know the same day a Tier-2 SLO is breached, with initial impact assessment

---

### Consults Director Before Deciding

- SLO changes for Tier-1 services: raising or lowering a target requires Director concurrence because Tier-1 SLOs are typically reflected in customer commitments
- Any change to the DR test schedule or scope: if a test is being postponed, skipped, or reduced in scope, Director needs to agree before you communicate the change to the team
- New availability targets for services being onboarded: first-time SLO assignment for a service new to the portfolio requires Director alignment

---

### Escalates to Director — Director Decides

- SLO breach for Tier-1 services: Director is notified immediately, before the external communication goes out
- Error budget exhausted for a Tier-1 service: Director decides whether to continue deploying (accepting risk), freeze, or accelerate the reliability fix
- DR test failure for any Tier-1 service: Director needs to make the call on re-test timeline, interim compensating controls, and whether to communicate the failure to business stakeholders
- Business continuity risk: if the BCP cannot be activated within the committed RTO due to documentation gaps, unavailable recovery systems, or key-person dependency — Director needs to know and decide on the emergency response
- All personnel actions

---

### Key Metrics You Own

| Metric | Target | Review Cadence |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| SLO compliance — Tier-1 | 100% of Tier-1 services meeting SLO monthly | Monthly |
| Error budget consumption — mid-month check | < 80% of monthly error budget consumed by mid-month | Bi-weekly |
| DR test completion rate | 100% of Tier-1 DR tests executed on quarterly schedule | Quarterly |
| DR test success rate | ≥ 95% of tests meet RTO/RPO targets | Quarterly |
| Toil percentage | < 50% of SRE team time on undifferentiated operational work | Monthly |
| Performance gate pass rate | ≥ 95% of releases passing performance gate without rework | Monthly |

Toil is defined as manual, repetitive operational work that does not contribute to improving the reliability posture — it keeps the lights on but does not make the lights more reliable. Work that could be automated and has not been is toil. Alert noise that requires human triage but could be suppressed or auto-remediated is toil.

---

### Weekly Deliverable to Director

Due by end of day Monday, covering the prior week.

- SLO compliance snapshot: current achievement for each Tier-1 service, error budget remaining, trend (improving/stable/degrading)
- Error budget status per Tier-1 service: budget consumed this month to date, projected end-of-month status
- Open availability risks: any identified threat to SLO compliance without a remediation plan in place
- Toil tracking snapshot: estimated toil percentage for the week, top 3 toil sources, any automation work completed or in progress

---

### Monthly Deliverable to Director

Due by the 5th business day of the following month.

- Full SLA report: SLO achievement for every service in scope, any breach details with customer impact assessment, comparison to prior month
- DR readiness status: which tests are current, which are overdue, any failed tests and remediation status, gaps in DR documentation
- Performance engineering summary: load test results for the month, capacity forecasts, any services approaching capacity limits
- Toil reduction progress: toil percentage trend over trailing 3 months, automation projects completed, automation projects in flight, estimated toil hours saved by completed automation

---

### When Director Steps In

- **Tier-1 SLO breach occurs or is imminent (less than 20% error budget remaining)**: Director joins a working session to assess the situation. Director may reach out to the business owner of the affected service. You remain the accountable owner of the recovery — Director is providing executive air cover.
- **DR test failure for a Tier-1 service**: Director leads the response planning, not just receives a report. This is a material business risk and requires Director-level decision-making on interim controls and communication.
- **Toil percentage exceeds 60% for two consecutive months**: Director treats this as a team health and retention risk. A team spending 60% of its time on manual operational work is a team that will burn out and leave. Director initiates a joint plan to address the root cause — which is usually a combination of technical debt, under-investment in automation, and scope that has grown beyond team capacity.

## Charter 4: Architecture and Transformation Lead

**Charter effective date**: _______________
**Manager name**: _______________
**Director name**: _______________
**Next scheduled review**: _______________

---

### Mission

Own the long-term technical health of the platform. Every architectural decision is deliberate and documented. Every aging system has a funded migration path before it reaches End of Life. The portfolio never accumulates hidden risk — risk is visible, rated, and being actively managed or consciously accepted.

The Architecture and Transformation Lead is the organizational conscience on technical debt. Incident Management tells you what broke today. This role tells you what is going to break in 18 months if nothing changes.

---

### Full Authority — Decides Alone, No Director Involvement Required

- Chair ARB (Architecture Review Board) sessions: set the agenda, facilitate the review, record the decision and rationale for each submission
- Approve architectural standards for non-Tier-1 patterns: message queue configuration standards for RabbitMQ and Kafka, Kubernetes deployment patterns, Splunk integration standards, API design standards for non-Tier-1 services
- Approve ADRs for non-Tier-1 architectural decisions: the decision record is approved and added to the ADR register
- Maintain and publish the application portfolio inventory: service list, health scores, tier classifications, technology stack, and support status
- Assign EOL risk ratings to systems: Green (current, fully supported), Yellow (approaching EOL within 24 months), Red (EOL within 12 months or already unsupported)
- Approve Transformation Engineer work plans: sprint commitments, milestone definitions, technical approach for modernization projects within existing budget and scope
- Reject ARB submissions that do not meet the submission criteria: send back with specific remediation requirements, do not let incomplete submissions consume ARB time

---

### Decides and Notifies Director Same Business Day

- Any architecture decision that introduces a net-new external dependency: a new SaaS platform, a new cloud service, a new vendor integration — even if it is for a non-Tier-1 service
- EOL risk rating changes from Green or Yellow to Red for any system in the portfolio: Director needs to know the same day a system is rated High EOL risk
- ARB rejections of a proposed architecture: Director should know when a submitted design was turned back, even for non-Tier-1 systems — this is a signal about engineering quality or process compliance

---

### Consults Director Before Deciding

- Architectural standards changes that affect Tier-1 systems: any change to the standard configuration or pattern for RabbitMQ, TIBCO EMS, Kafka, EKS, or Splunk in Tier-1 contexts — these affect reliability posture and may affect change processes
- New technology adoption decisions: a net-new tool, platform, or framework being recommended for adoption across the organization — Director must agree before the recommendation is published
- Modernization project scope or timeline changes: if a transformation project's scope is expanding, timeline is slipping more than 4 weeks, or technical approach is fundamentally changing from the approved plan

---

### Escalates to Director — Director Decides

- ARB approval for Tier-1 architecture changes: any architectural decision affecting Tier-1 services requires Director sign-off before the ADR is approved and the change proceeds
- Any system in the portfolio reaching EOL within 12 months without an approved migration plan: Director must approve either a funded migration plan or a conscious risk acceptance decision — this cannot be left unresolved
- Architectural risk that creates regulatory or security exposure: if an architectural decision creates a compliance gap (PCI, SOX, FCC, CPNI in a T-Mobile context) or a material security vulnerability, Director is notified immediately
- Transformation project budget requests: any request for incremental funding beyond the approved annual envelope
- All personnel actions

---

### Key Metrics You Own

| Metric | Target | Review Cadence |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| ARB review SLA | 100% of submissions reviewed within 10 business days | Weekly |
| ADR coverage | ≥ 90% of architecture decisions above L3 complexity have an ADR | Monthly |
| Portfolio health — Green rated | ≥ 80% of systems rated Green | Monthly |
| EOL risk management | 0 systems at EOL without an approved migration plan | Monthly |
| Transformation milestone on-time rate | ≥ 75% of scheduled milestones delivered on time | Monthly |

L3 complexity is defined as: any architectural decision that affects more than one service, introduces a new integration pattern, changes a data flow, or creates a new external dependency. Single-service configuration changes that follow an existing approved pattern are below this threshold.

---

### Weekly Deliverable to Director

Due by end of day Monday, covering the prior week.

- ARB queue status: submissions received, reviews completed, decisions recorded, any submissions pending more than 5 business days with the identified blocker
- New architectural risks identified: any system, integration, or pattern identified as a risk during the week — brief description, risk rating, proposed next step
- Transformation project status: RAG rating for each active project, milestone progress, blockers requiring Director attention

---

### Monthly Deliverable to Director

Due by the 5th business day of the following month.

- Portfolio health scorecard: full inventory with health ratings, technology stack summary, tier distribution, change from prior month — any system that changed rating receives a brief explanation
- EOL risk register: all systems rated Yellow or Red, age of the rating, assigned migration owner, migration plan status, projected EOL date
- ADR register update: ADRs added or superseded in the month, ADR coverage percentage by tier
- Transformation program status: all active projects with milestone status, budget consumption, projected completion, risks and mitigations
- Standards compliance summary: any services found operating outside approved architectural standards, remediation status, owner

---

### When Director Steps In

- **Tier-1 system reaches High EOL risk without a funded migration plan**: Director escalates to VP if funding is not available within the Operations budget. This is not a situation that can age on a risk register — it is a Director-level commitment to resolve.
- **ARB process is being bypassed for a Tier-1 change**: Director handles the stakeholder conversation with whoever is bypassing governance. You document the bypass, the rationale given by the bypassing party, and the architectural risk created. Director decides whether to retroactively approve or require remediation.
- **A transformation project is Red for two consecutive months**: Director initiates a project recovery review with you, the project owner, and any relevant stakeholders. Two consecutive Red months on a transformation project is a signal that either the scope is wrong, the resources are wrong, or the plan is wrong — and those are Director-level decisions to fix.
