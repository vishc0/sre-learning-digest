**Purpose**: Authoritative definitions of every role on the operations team — mission, ownership, decision rights, metrics, and success/failure signals

## 1. Director of Operations — L8 / Senior Director Equivalent

### Mission
Own the reliability, availability, and operational health of the platform so that every engineering team can ship with confidence and every business stakeholder can trust the numbers.

### Owns
- Final accountability for all 10 operations domains: Incident, Problem, Change, Release, Service Level, Availability, Continuity, Environment, Performance, Architecture Governance, Application Portfolio
- The operations team org structure, headcount plan, and hiring decisions
- Escalation path for all SEV1 incidents when the Incident Manager is unavailable
- Quarterly SLA commitments to executive stakeholders
- Operations budget: tooling, headcount, contractor capacity
- Cross-team relationships with engineering VPs, product leadership, and security/compliance

### Day-to-day
- Run the weekly Operations Review with the four managers and Architecture Lead — hold each lead accountable to their metrics before the meeting ends
- Review and sign off on CAB recommendations for Normal changes that touch Tier 0 services
- Review PIRs for all SEV1 incidents within 48 hours of publication — look for systemic patterns, not individual blame
- Represent operations in architecture design reviews for new platform initiatives — catch operational complexity before it is built
- Conduct monthly 1:1s with each direct report focused on team health, career trajectory, and strategic blockers — not status updates
- Escalate operational risk to engineering leadership and business stakeholders with specific data, not general concerns
- Approve headcount requisitions and make final hiring decisions for all L5+ roles on the team

### Decides Alone
- Incident escalation to business leadership during active SEV1s affecting customer-facing Tier 0 services
- Hiring decisions for all manager and technical lead roles
- Emergency exceptions to Standard Change process for novel failure scenarios not covered by existing runbooks
- Prioritization trade-offs between operational stability work and transformation project delivery
- When to invoke a formal DR exercise outside the quarterly schedule

### Escalates
- SEV1 incidents that require a public communications decision (customer notification, regulatory notification) — escalate to VP/SVP
- Headcount decisions above the approved budget — escalate to VP with a written business case
- Architectural changes that require decommissioning a system depended on by another business unit — escalate to VP with risk assessment
- Compliance or security findings discovered through operational review — escalate to CISO / legal with timeline and remediation plan

### Key Metrics
- Mean Time to Recover (MTTR) for Tier 0 services: target under 30 minutes for SEV1
- SLO compliance rate across all Tier 0/1 services: target 99.5%+ over rolling 90 days
- Change Success Rate: percentage of Normal and Standard changes that complete without incident — target above 98%
- On-call burden: average pages per engineer per week — target below 3 sustained over 90 days
- Open problem tickets older than 30 days without a resolution date: target zero for Tier 0/1-impacting known errors
- Team attrition: voluntary departure rate — target at or below company engineering average

### Interfaces
- **Engineering VPs and Delivery Teams**: SLO data, change approvals, architecture reviews
- **Business Stakeholders**: SLA reporting, incident communications, platform health narratives
- **Security / Compliance**: audit support, vulnerability remediation timelines, access reviews
- **Vendors**: RabbitMQ/TIBCO support contracts, EKS support, Splunk enterprise licensing
- **HR**: headcount planning, performance management, compensation reviews

### Success Indicators
- SEV1 incidents produce PIRs within 48 hours, and the actions in those PIRs are completed within the agreed timeline — every time, not most of the time
- The CAB runs efficiently: Normal change approval cycle time is under 5 business days, Emergency changes are approved in under 30 minutes
- SLA reports are published on time, contain no surprises for business stakeholders, and include trend data — not just point-in-time numbers
- Team members are being promoted from within; the org is producing its own next generation
- Engineering teams describe the operations team as an accelerator, not a bottleneck

### Failure Indicators
- More than one SEV1 per month where the PIR concludes "unknown root cause" — the Problem Management function is not working
- CAB meetings running long regularly because changes are poorly documented — Change Management is not setting standards
- Business stakeholders are learning about platform incidents from their own monitoring before the operations team notifies them — incident communication process is broken
- On-call engineers are paging you directly because they cannot reach their manager — escalation paths are unclear
- You are the only person who knows how to make a specific operational decision — you have not delegated clearly enough

## 2. Incident & Problem Management Manager — L6 / Senior Manager

### Mission
Build and run the system that detects, responds to, and permanently resolves production failures — so that on-call is sustainable and the same failure never causes the same outage twice.

### Owns
- End-to-end incident response process: detection through resolution through post-mortem
- On-call rotation design, schedule, and engineer welfare
- Known Error Database (KEDB): currency, completeness, accessibility
- Runbook library: coverage, review cadence, quality standards
- Problem Management process: RCA facilitation, problem ticket lifecycle, permanent fix tracking
- PIR process and publication — not just for SEV1s; SEV2s that recur more than twice in 90 days also require a PIR

### Day-to-day
- Review all active incidents at the start of each shift and confirm Incident Command is assigned
- Meet weekly with Senior SREs and the Problem Analyst to review the problem backlog — any ticket older than 30 days without a remediation date gets escalated
- Audit on-call rotation fairness monthly: pages-per-engineer, time-of-day distribution, weekend distribution
- Review and approve all PIRs before publication — confirm root cause is stated with evidence, not opinion
- Run a monthly runbook review: pull a random sample of 5 runbooks, verify they are current and accurate
- Chair the post-incident review meeting for all SEV1s — not to assign blame, but to extract systemic learning
- Escalate to the Director any incident pattern that suggests a structural reliability problem (e.g., the same Kafka consumer group lagging causes three SEV2s in one month)

### Decides Alone
- On-call rotation schedule and assignment
- Runbook approval: a runbook goes live only when this manager signs off
- Severity classification of an active incident (SEV1 vs. SEV2 vs. SEV3)
- Whether a problem ticket requires a permanent fix or is acceptable as a documented known error with a workaround
- Whether a PIR requires a follow-up review meeting or if the written artifact is sufficient

### Escalates
- Any SEV1 that has been active for more than 60 minutes without a clear resolution path — escalate to Director
- Any on-call engineer who is showing burnout indicators (unresponsive pages, declining runbook quality, expressed distress) — escalate to HR with Director visibility
- A problem ticket where the permanent fix requires cross-team architectural work that this team cannot drive alone — escalate to Director for executive sponsorship
- A runbook gap on a Tier 0 service discovered during an active incident — escalate to Director as a risk item the same day

### Key Metrics
- MTTR for SEV1 incidents: target under 30 minutes
- MTTR for SEV2 incidents: target under 2 hours
- PIR completion rate: 100% of SEV1s and recurring SEV2s have a published PIR within 48 hours of resolution
- KEDB completeness: every production known error in the last 12 months has a KEDB entry with a workaround
- On-call burn rate: average pages per engineer per week — target below 3 over 90-day rolling window
- Runbook coverage: at least 90% of Tier 0 and Tier 1 services have a reviewed runbook younger than 90 days

### Interfaces
- **Senior SREs**: daily — they are the execution arm; this manager is the system designer
- **Problem Analyst**: weekly — problem backlog review, RCA progress, KEDB quality
- **On-Call Coordinator**: weekly — rotation schedule, runbook reviews, engineer welfare
- **Change & Release Manager**: when a PIR produces a change request — hand off the RFC with full context
- **Platform Reliability Manager**: SLO data during incidents; SLO breach data feeds the problem backlog
- **Director**: weekly ops review, escalations, major incidents

### Success Indicators
- On-call rotation is staffed 100% of the time with no unplanned gaps for three or more consecutive months
- The same failure mode does not appear in two PIRs within a six-month window
- Engineers on the team describe their on-call rotation as manageable and professionally developmental — not exhausting
- KEDB is cited by Tier 1 and Tier 2 engineers outside the ops team as a useful resource — it has external consumers

### Failure Indicators
- PIRs are being written more than a week after the incident — the process has no enforcement
- The KEDB has entries that have not been reviewed in more than six months
- Multiple on-call engineers have escalated to you that they feel unsafe acknowledging pages late at night — the rotation is too heavy
- SEV1 incidents regularly conclude with "cause under investigation" in the PIR — root cause discipline is not present
- The Director finds out about a SEV1 from a business stakeholder before this manager notifies them

## 3. Change & Release Management Manager — L6 / Senior Manager

### Mission
Make production change fast, safe, and predictable — so delivery teams ship confidently and the platform does not regress.

### Owns
- Change Advisory Board (CAB): process, cadence, quality standards, and final approval authority for Normal changes
- Standard Change library: templates, approval workflow, and review cadence
- Emergency Change process: fast-path criteria, approver list, retroactive documentation standards
- Release pipeline governance: deployment model standards, pipeline failure thresholds, rollback requirements
- Non-production environment management: parity standards, reservation system, and environment lifecycle policy
- Change success rate tracking and improvement

### Day-to-day
- Chair the weekly CAB meeting — review every Normal change RFC for completeness, risk assessment, and rollback plan before approving
- Review the Standard Change library monthly — retire any template that has not been used in 90 days; flag any recurring Normal change that should be templated
- Review all Emergency Changes within 24 hours of completion — confirm the fast-path was appropriate and that retroactive documentation is complete
- Monitor release pipeline failure rates weekly — any pipeline with a failure rate above 5% gets a root cause investigation within that week
- Audit non-production environment parity quarterly — compare staging configuration against production; log every delta as a risk item
- Partner with the Incident Manager when a change-induced incident occurs — the CAB process is only as good as the feedback loop from incidents

### Decides Alone
- Approval or rejection of any Normal change RFC at CAB
- Classification of a change as Standard, Normal, or Emergency
- Whether a Standard Change template is retired from the library
- Mandatory rollback trigger criteria for a release in progress
- Environment reservation priority when two teams conflict on non-prod scheduling

### Escalates
- A delivery team that is bypassing the change process repeatedly — escalate to the Director with evidence
- An Emergency Change request that arrives during a SEV1 but would require taking a second production system offline — escalate to the Director for a risk decision
- A change-induced SEV1 where the rollback plan documented in the RFC was ineffective — flag to the Incident Manager and Director as a process failure, not just an incident
- CAB meeting attendance falling below 50% of required stakeholders for three consecutive weeks — the process is losing organizational support; escalate to Director

### Key Metrics
- Change Success Rate: percentage of changes completed without causing an incident — target above 98%
- CAB cycle time: average days from RFC submission to CAB approval — target under 5 business days for Normal changes
- Standard Change ratio: percentage of all changes classified as Standard — target 70%+
- Emergency Change rate: percentage of all changes classified as Emergency — target below 5%
- Rollback rate: percentage of changes requiring rollback — target below 2%
- Environment parity gap: number of configuration differences between staging and production identified in quarterly audit — target under 10

### Interfaces
- **Change Manager**: daily — the IC who runs the operational mechanics of CAB
- **Release Engineers**: daily — pipeline governance and deployment model
- **Environment Engineer**: weekly — non-prod parity and environment scheduling
- **Incident Manager**: when a change causes an incident — the handoff must be clean and fast
- **Delivery teams**: at CAB — they submit RFCs; this manager approves or rejects
- **Architecture Lead**: for major Normal changes that touch architecture boundaries — consult before CAB, not at CAB

### Success Indicators
- Delivery teams describe the change process as clear and fast — not bureaucratic
- Emergency changes are rare and always legitimate — not a workaround for teams that do not want to wait for CAB
- Release pipeline failure rates are trending down quarter over quarter
- Every incident PIR that cites a change as a contributing factor has a corresponding CAB process improvement action item

### Failure Indicators
- Delivery teams are submitting changes directly to production without an RFC because "CAB takes too long" — the process is a bottleneck, not an accelerator
- Emergency change volume is above 10% — teams have learned that Emergency is the fast path, not the exception path
- Production incidents are being caused by changes that passed CAB review — the review criteria are insufficient
- The Standard Change library has not had a new template added in more than six months — the team is not learning from patterns

## 4. Platform Reliability Manager — L6 / Senior Manager

### Mission
Engineer the platform to be reliable before the next incident, not after it — through SLO governance, DR readiness, and performance architecture.

### Owns
- SLO definitions for all Tier 0 and Tier 1 services — authorship, approval process, and error budget governance
- SLA reporting to business stakeholders — the report, its data sources, and its publication schedule
- DR program: runbook authorship standards, annual test cadence for Tier 0, quarterly test planning
- BCP (Business Continuity Plan) contributions from the operations team perspective
- Load testing as a pre-release gate for Tier 0/1 changes
- Availability architecture review for all new services before ARB sign-off

### Day-to-day
- Review SLO dashboards daily — flag any Tier 0 service consuming more than 50% of its monthly error budget by mid-month
- Prepare the monthly SLA report with the Service Level Analyst — review the data, add narrative context for any month where SLOs were breached
- Schedule and facilitate quarterly DR tests — assign test owners, prepare test criteria, and publish findings reports within 72 hours
- Review load test results for any release touching a Tier 0 or Tier 1 service — sign off before the Change Manager approves the RFC
- Attend architecture reviews for new services with significant availability requirements — add the HA design review as a prerequisite before the service goes to ARB

### Decides Alone
- Whether an SLO target is achievable given current platform architecture (escalates to Director if target requires architectural investment)
- Whether a release needs a load test — does not defer to the delivery team's judgment on this
- Whether a DR test result constitutes a pass or a fail
- Whether a service's availability architecture is acceptable for its assigned tier classification

### Escalates
- An SLO breach that is systemic (more than three Tier 0 services breached in the same month) — escalate to Director with a root cause summary, not just numbers
- A DR test finding that reveals the platform cannot meet its RTO for a Tier 0 service — escalate to Director and Architecture Lead immediately
- A load test result that fails but the delivery team is pushing to ship anyway — escalate to Director for a risk decision
- An availability architecture review finding that a proposed new service introduces a single point of failure in a Tier 0 dependency chain — escalate to Architecture Lead and Director before ARB

### Key Metrics
- SLO compliance rate: percentage of Tier 0 and Tier 1 services meeting SLO target over rolling 90 days — target 99.5%
- Error budget burn rate: no Tier 0 service should exhaust its monthly error budget before day 25 of the month
- DR test pass rate: 100% of scheduled DR tests completed; pass/fail results published within 72 hours
- Time to DR decision (TTDD): from SEV1 incident start to explicit DR invocation or explicit DR stand-down decision — target under 15 minutes for Tier 0
- SLA report timeliness: published within 5 business days of month close — every month

### Interfaces
- **SRE-Availability and SRE-Performance**: daily — they are the execution capacity for this manager's strategy
- **Service Level Analyst**: weekly — SLA data production and SLO dashboard accuracy
- **Incident Manager**: when a SEV1 is in progress — SLO data and error budget status are inputs to incident severity decisions
- **Architecture Lead**: availability architecture reviews — this manager reviews the operational implications; the Architect reviews the design
- **Business stakeholders**: monthly SLA review — this manager presents the data and owns the narrative

### Success Indicators
- No Tier 0 service has exhausted its error budget more than twice in a rolling 12-month period
- DR tests reveal gaps that are resolved before the next test — the test-and-improve cycle is functioning
- Business stakeholders receive the SLA report before asking for it — every month
- Load testing catches a performance regression before production every quarter — the gate is providing signal

### Failure Indicators
- Error budgets are exhausted early in the month regularly without a formal escalation being triggered — the SLO governance process is not being enforced
- DR tests are being canceled or rescheduled more than once — organizational priority for DR is not being maintained
- Load testing is being skipped because the delivery team says "it's a minor release" — the gate is not being held
- SLA reports contain errors that business stakeholders catch before the operations team does — data quality and review process are broken

## 5. Architecture & Transformation Lead — L7 / Staff / Principal Equivalent

### Mission
Shape the technical direction of the platform so that operational complexity decreases over time — not increases — as the platform grows.

### Owns
- Architecture Review Board (ARB) process: chairs meetings, sets review criteria, maintains decision records
- Architecture principles library: the documented standards for how services are built, deployed, and integrated
- ADR (Architecture Decision Record) repository: every significant architectural decision recorded with context, options considered, and rationale
- Technical debt inventory: a categorized and prioritized list of architectural debt items across the platform
- Modernization roadmap: the multi-quarter plan to migrate legacy services and retire deprecated patterns
- Application portfolio health: works with the Portfolio Analyst on health score methodology

### Day-to-day
- Chair the weekly or bi-weekly ARB meeting — review proposed designs against architecture principles; approve, reject, or request revision with written rationale
- Maintain the architecture principles library — at minimum quarterly review; update when a new pattern is approved or an old pattern is deprecated
- Write or review ADRs for all significant architectural decisions — an ADR is required for any decision that would take more than a sprint to reverse
- Review technical debt submissions from engineering teams — categorize, prioritize, and maintain the backlog
- Consult on new service designs before they reach ARB — catch misalignments early, when they are cheap to fix
- Present the modernization roadmap to senior leadership quarterly — with specific progress metrics, not narrative progress

### Decides Alone
- Whether a proposed architecture conforms to documented principles — approved or requires revision
- Whether a technical debt item is categorized as Critical, High, Medium, or Low
- Whether an existing ADR requires revision in response to new information or a changed standard
- What constitutes a "Standard Architecture" decision (fast-path, no ARB required) vs. a "Non-Standard Architecture" decision (full ARB review required)

### Escalates
- An architectural proposal from a senior engineering team that would violate a core platform principle and the team is unwilling to revise — escalate to Director and engineering VP jointly
- A technical debt item that creates compliance or security exposure — escalate to Director with a remediation timeline
- A modernization project that is blocked by organizational resistance (team won't migrate from TIBCO to Kafka because of resourcing) — escalate to Director for executive sponsorship
- Any new service that would create a new single point of failure in a Tier 0 dependency chain — block at ARB and escalate to Director before approving

### Key Metrics
- ARB cycle time: average days from design submission to ARB decision — target under 10 business days
- ADR currency: percentage of ADRs reviewed within the last 12 months — target 100% for active decisions
- Architecture principles violations found post-deployment (escaped standards) — target zero for Tier 0/1 services
- Modernization project completion rate: percentage of roadmap items completed within the committed quarter — target 80%
- Technical debt items in "Critical" category older than 90 days without a remediation plan — target zero

### Interfaces
- **Platform Architect**: primary technical partner — the Architect does the deep technical work; this Lead provides the governance framework
- **Transformation Engineers**: direct reports — they execute the modernization roadmap
- **Portfolio Analyst**: direct report — they provide the data that informs the portfolio strategy
- **Delivery engineering leads and Staff Engineers**: ARB participants — influence-based relationship, not hierarchical
- **Director**: weekly — strategy alignment, escalations, and cross-team roadmap conflicts
- **Platform Reliability Manager**: availability architecture reviews — collaborative peer relationship

### Success Indicators
- ARB is described by engineering teams as valuable, not bureaucratic — teams bring proposals early because the feedback is useful
- The same architecture anti-pattern does not appear in two ARB submissions within 90 days — the principles library is being read and applied
- Modernization projects are completing on schedule and the platform is measurably simpler (fewer legacy middleware adapters, fewer EOL runtimes in production) each quarter
- Technical debt items in the Critical category are being resolved, not just added — the inventory is shrinking

### Failure Indicators
- ARB submissions are being withdrawn before a decision because teams find it faster to build without approval — the ARB is not providing value fast enough
- ADRs are being written after architectural decisions are already implemented — governance is reactive, not proactive
- The modernization roadmap is unchanged after two consecutive quarters — no projects are completing and the backlog is growing
- Architecture principles violations are being discovered in production during incidents, not in ARB reviews

## 6. Senior SRE — L5, Incident Command Focus

### Mission
Lead incident response for Tier 0 and Tier 1 production failures and build the runbook and knowledge base that makes the next incident faster to resolve.

### Owns
- Incident Command for all SEV1 and SEV2 incidents during on-call shift
- Runbook authorship and review for the services in their ownership domain
- On-call rotation participation — sets the standard for what excellent on-call behavior looks like
- Mentorship of junior on-call engineers — including shadowing, paired on-call, and PIR coaching
- Splunk saved search library — maintains and publishes alert searches and triage queries for the team

### Day-to-day
- Respond to PagerDuty pages within 5 minutes during on-call shift — acknowledge, assess severity, and either resolve or escalate within 15 minutes
- Write or update at least one runbook per sprint for a service in their domain
- Review and comment on PIRs written by junior engineers — ensure root cause is evidence-based, not assumed
- Build and maintain Splunk dashboards and saved searches for the services they own — these are not personal tools; they are team resources
- Participate in weekly Problem Review to identify whether any current KEDB items need a deeper investigation
- Pair with the On-Call Coordinator monthly to audit rotation health and runbook coverage

### Decides Alone
- Severity classification of an incoming page (SEV1 vs. SEV2 vs. SEV3) for incidents they command
- Whether to page additional engineers during an active incident
- Whether a runbook is accurate enough to publish or requires another review cycle
- Whether to apply a known workaround from the KEDB or escalate to the Problem Analyst for a deeper investigation

### Escalates
- A SEV1 that has been active for more than 20 minutes without a clear resolution vector — escalate to Incident Manager
- A new failure mode not covered by any runbook or KEDB entry — flag to Incident Manager during incident and create a problem ticket afterward
- An engineer on the on-call rotation who is not responding within SLA — escalate to On-Call Coordinator and Incident Manager immediately
- A recurring incident pattern (same alert, same resolution, same service, third time in 30 days) — escalate to Problem Analyst with a formal problem ticket

### Key Metrics
- Mean time from page to acknowledgment: target under 5 minutes during on-call hours
- Runbooks authored or reviewed per sprint: target minimum 2
- PIR quality score (peer-reviewed against a defined rubric): target 4/5 or above
- Incidents commanded where root cause was identified before resolution: target 80% (some incidents resolve before cause is known — this is acceptable if documented)

### Interfaces
- **On-call team**: during incidents — Incident Command is the authority in the room
- **Problem Analyst**: post-incident — hands off investigation to the Analyst after resolution; the Analyst owns the long-tail RCA
- **Incident Manager**: daily during on-call; weekly in team standup
- **Delivery engineering teams**: during incidents — they are subject matter experts who the Senior SRE coordinates, not who coordinate the Senior SRE

### Success Indicators
- On-call engineers under their mentorship describe them as calm, structured, and decisive during incidents
- Their runbooks are being used by other engineers without needing clarification — they are written for strangers, not for themselves
- The incidents they command consistently produce PIRs with specific, evidence-based root causes within 48 hours

### Failure Indicators
- Runbooks they own are outdated (older than 90 days) or contain broken steps
- They are frequently the only person who can resolve a specific type of incident — they have not transferred knowledge
- PIRs they author consistently list "root cause: under investigation" without a follow-up problem ticket
- Pages during their on-call shift are not acknowledged within SLA

## 7. SRE - Availability — L4/L5

### Mission
Design, test, and validate that Tier 0 and Tier 1 services can survive component failures and meet their recovery time objectives.

### Owns
- DR runbooks for assigned Tier 0 and Tier 1 services
- Annual DR test execution for assigned services
- HA architecture review contribution — flags availability gaps in new service designs
- Pod Disruption Budget (PDB) and HPA configuration review for Kubernetes workloads
- BCP documentation contributions for platform-layer dependencies (EKS cluster failure, RabbitMQ cluster loss, TIBCO EM failure)

### Day-to-day
- Review Kubernetes cluster events and node health dashboards at least twice per shift — look for eviction pressure, pending pods, and resource constraint warnings before they become incidents
- Execute DR runbook walkthroughs (tabletop or live) at least once per quarter per assigned service
- Review HPA and PDB configurations on all Tier 0 services after any cluster upgrade or node pool change
- Contribute to availability architecture reviews: for any new service that will be Tier 0 or Tier 1, review the proposed deployment model and flag SPOFs before ARB
- Maintain DR runbooks: after each DR test or each incident that triggers a failover, update the runbook with what actually happened vs. what the runbook said

### Decides Alone
- Whether a DR runbook step is correct and current
- Whether a Kubernetes HPA configuration is appropriate for a service's load profile
- Whether a DR test passes or fails based on defined criteria

### Escalates
- A Kubernetes node pool that is under eviction pressure but not yet causing a production impact — escalate to Incident Manager as a pre-incident risk
- A DR test finding that reveals RTO cannot be met — escalate to Platform Reliability Manager immediately
- An HA design review finding that a proposed service introduces a SPOF — escalate to Platform Reliability Manager and Architecture Lead before the ARB meeting

### Key Metrics
- DR runbook coverage: 100% of assigned Tier 0 services have a tested DR runbook
- DR test completion: all scheduled DR tests completed within the quarter without rescheduling
- Kubernetes cluster health: zero uncorrected HPA misconfiguration findings per quarter
- RTO validation: assigned services can demonstrate RTO compliance in DR tests — not just in documents

### Interfaces
- **Platform Reliability Manager**: primary — receives direction on priorities and test schedule
- **On-call rotation**: participates in on-call as a rotation member; brings availability expertise to active incidents
- **Architecture Lead and Platform Architect**: availability architecture reviews — provides the operational perspective on HA design
- **Delivery engineering teams**: DR runbook walkthroughs require the service owner to participate — this SRE facilitates

### Success Indicators
- DR tests reveal issues that are fixed before the next test — the test-and-fix cycle is working
- Kubernetes cluster eviction events are caught and resolved before they page anyone — proactive, not reactive
- Delivery teams describe DR review as valuable input — not a checkbox exercise

### Failure Indicators
- DR tests are passing on paper but the runbook steps have never been executed live
- Kubernetes eviction events are being discovered during incident triage — not caught by proactive monitoring
- DR runbooks are more than six months old and have not been tested

## 8. SRE - Performance Engineer — L4/L5

### Mission
Ensure the platform can handle production-scale load before a release ships, and identify performance bottlenecks before they become availability incidents.

### Owns
- Load testing design and execution for all Tier 0 and Tier 1 releases
- Performance baseline library: documented throughput, latency, and resource consumption baselines for all Tier 0 services
- Capacity planning analysis for growth projections — feeds the quarterly infrastructure budget request
- Splunk dashboards for performance metrics: JVM heap, Kafka consumer lag, RabbitMQ queue depth, EKS CPU/memory trends
- Performance regression tracking: when a release degrades a performance baseline, owns the regression investigation

### Day-to-day
- Run load tests for any release that touches a Tier 0 or Tier 1 service — produce a written result report before the Change Manager approves the RFC
- Monitor Kafka consumer lag across production topics daily — a consumer group that is lagging by more than 10 minutes on a Tier 0 topic is a pre-incident signal
- Review RabbitMQ queue depth trends weekly — identify queues that are growing without a corresponding increase in consumers
- Update performance baselines after each major release — a baseline that is 6 months old is no longer a baseline; it is historical data
- Contribute to capacity planning: quarterly, produce a projection of infrastructure needs for the next two quarters based on growth trends

### Decides Alone
- Whether a load test result meets the pass criteria for a release
- Whether a Kafka consumer lag trend is within acceptable bounds or requires investigation
- Whether a performance baseline requires a formal update

### Escalates
- A load test failure where the delivery team is pushing to proceed anyway — escalate to Platform Reliability Manager for a risk decision
- Kafka consumer lag on a Tier 0 topic that has been growing for more than 30 minutes without an obvious cause — escalate to Incident Manager as a potential pre-incident
- A capacity projection that shows the platform will hit resource limits in the next quarter without additional infrastructure — escalate to Platform Reliability Manager and Director with data

### Key Metrics
- Load test coverage: 100% of Tier 0 and Tier 1 releases have a load test result before CAB approval
- Performance regression catch rate: percentage of performance regressions caught in pre-production vs. in production — target 90%+ caught pre-production
- Kafka consumer lag SLO: Tier 0 consumer groups at less than 5-minute lag 99.5% of the time
- Capacity projection accuracy: quarterly projections within 20% of actual consumption

### Interfaces
- **Platform Reliability Manager**: primary direction; load test results and capacity projections reviewed here first
- **Release Engineers**: coordinates load test scheduling with release pipeline timing
- **Delivery engineering teams**: the service owner provides load test parameters; this engineer designs and executes the test
- **On-call rotation**: participates in rotation; brings performance diagnostics expertise to active incidents

### Success Indicators
- Performance incidents in production are consistently traced back to conditions not tested in load testing — and each one produces a new load test scenario going forward
- Kafka consumer lag is surfaced as a warning before it becomes a page — detection happens before impact
- Delivery teams consult this engineer early in sprint planning for capacity guidance — not just at release time

### Failure Indicators
- Load tests are being designed by delivery teams rather than this engineer — test quality and independence are not being maintained
- Performance baselines are stale (more than 6 months old) for Tier 0 services
- Kafka consumer lag incidents are being discovered via customer reports rather than platform monitoring

## 9. Change Manager — L4/L5

### Mission
Operate the change governance machinery — RFC intake, CAB facilitation, Standard Change management — so that every production change is trackable, reviewable, and recoverable.

### Owns
- RFC intake process: reviews every submitted RFC for completeness before it reaches CAB
- CAB meeting facilitation: prepares the agenda, runs the meeting, documents approvals and rejections
- Standard Change library: adds new templates, retires obsolete ones, ensures every template has a named owner
- Emergency Change documentation: ensures every Emergency Change is retroactively documented within 24 hours of completion
- Change calendar: maintains the forward-looking schedule of approved changes by environment and service tier

### Day-to-day
- Review all RFC submissions daily — bounce any RFC that is missing a rollback plan, test results, or an impact assessment back to the submitter with specific feedback
- Prepare the CAB agenda every week — include risk level, estimated downtime, rollback complexity, and a recommendation (approve/defer/reject) for each RFC
- Maintain the change calendar — block Tier 0 service changes during blackout windows (end of quarter, major marketing events)
- After CAB, notify all RFC submitters of the decision with a written rationale for any rejection
- Audit Emergency Changes weekly — every Emergency Change gets a formal review within 24 hours of completion

### Decides Alone
- Whether an RFC is complete enough for CAB consideration — can return it to the submitter without escalating
- Whether a Standard Change template is appropriate for a requested change — can approve a Standard Change without convening CAB
- Whether a change should be blocked from the change calendar due to a blackout window conflict

### Escalates
- An RFC from a Tier 0 service owner that has a High-risk rating and no tested rollback plan — escalate to Change Manager (manager) before CAB
- A team that is submitting changes consistently with incomplete documentation — escalate to Change & Release Management Manager with evidence for a process conversation
- A proposed Emergency Change during an active SEV1 that would require taking a second Tier 0 service offline — escalate to Director immediately

### Key Metrics
- RFC rejection rate at intake (bounced before CAB): target below 20% — if higher, the RFC template is not clear enough
- CAB meeting duration: target 60 minutes or less for a standard weekly agenda of 10–15 changes
- Emergency Change retroactive documentation completion: 100% within 24 hours
- Change calendar accuracy: no changes deployed outside of scheduled windows without an approved Emergency Change record

### Interfaces
- **Change & Release Management Manager**: daily — primary direction; escalations go here
- **Delivery teams**: primary external interface — they are the RFC submitters
- **Release Engineers**: coordinates deployment windows with pipeline schedules
- **Incident Manager**: Emergency Change requests during active incidents flow through both simultaneously

### Success Indicators
- CAB meetings start and end on time with clear decisions for every RFC on the agenda
- Standard Change library is growing — new templates are being added because teams see the value of the fast path
- Delivery teams describe the RFC process as clear and predictable — they know exactly what information to provide

### Failure Indicators
- CAB meetings regularly run long because RFCs are poorly formed — RFC quality is not improving despite feedback
- Standard Change templates are rarely used because teams don't know they exist or find them harder than submitting a Normal change
- Emergency Change documentation is frequently missing or completed days after the change — the retroactive process has no teeth

## 10. Release Engineer — L4

### Mission
Build, maintain, and improve the pipelines and deployment tooling that take code from repository to production safely and repeatably.

### Owns
- CI/CD pipeline build and maintenance for assigned service domains
- Deployment automation: scripted deployments for Kubernetes/EKS workloads, canary release tooling, blue/green deployment configurations
- Pipeline observability: metrics, alerts, and dashboards that surface pipeline health before a release fails
- Deployment runbook documentation: how to deploy, how to roll back, and how to verify success for each pipeline they own
- Integration testing gates: the automated test stages that run as part of the pipeline before production deployment

### Day-to-day
- Monitor pipeline health dashboards daily — a pipeline with a build failure rate above 5% gets investigated that shift
- Execute or oversee production deployments for changes on the change calendar
- Write and maintain deployment runbooks — after every deployment that required manual intervention, update the runbook
- Review pipeline changes proposed by delivery teams — approve or request revision based on defined pipeline standards
- On-call rotation participation — brings deployment and rollback expertise to active incidents

### Decides Alone
- Whether a pipeline failure requires a rollback or can be resolved with a forward fix
- Whether a proposed pipeline change from a delivery team conforms to deployment standards
- Whether a deployment should be paused mid-flight based on error rate monitoring

### Escalates
- A deployment that is causing a production impact and the rollback is not completing within the expected window — escalate to Incident Manager immediately
- A pipeline design from a delivery team that would bypass required security or quality gates — escalate to Change & Release Management Manager
- A production deployment that has been paused for more than 30 minutes pending a decision — escalate to Change Manager for a formal decision record

### Key Metrics
- Pipeline failure rate: target below 5% per pipeline per week
- Deployment success rate: target above 98% of planned deployments complete without rollback
- Mean time to rollback: from the decision to roll back to rollback completion — target under 10 minutes for Tier 0 services
- Pipeline coverage: 100% of Tier 0 services have an automated deployment pipeline with a documented rollback step

### Interfaces
- **Change Manager**: coordinates deployment windows with the change calendar
- **Delivery engineering teams**: builds and maintains the pipelines that deliver their code
- **Environment Engineer**: non-production pipeline runs require environment availability; coordinates scheduling
- **On-call rotation**: participates in rotation; primary expertise is deployment-related incidents

### Success Indicators
- Rollbacks are executed in under 10 minutes because the pipeline automation makes it a one-command operation
- Delivery teams can execute their own deployments via the pipeline with this engineer in an advisory role — not a dependency role
- Pipeline failures are caught before they page anyone because the pipeline health monitoring fires first

### Failure Indicators
- Deployments require manual intervention on a regular basis — the pipeline is not reliable
- Rollback procedures are not documented or are not tested
- Pipeline changes from delivery teams are being deployed without review — governance is not being maintained

## 11. Environment Engineer — L4

### Mission
Maintain non-production environments at production parity so that integration, staging, and performance testing environments can be trusted to predict production behavior.

### Owns
- Non-production environment provisioning: dev, integration, staging, performance test environments on Kubernetes/EKS
- Configuration parity: tracks delta between each non-production environment and production; flags drift within 24 hours
- Environment reservation calendar: manages scheduling of environments for testing sprints and release dry-runs
- Environment lifecycle policy: when environments are rebuilt, patched, or retired
- Environment-specific Splunk dashboards: non-prod log routing and monitoring configuration

### Day-to-day
- Run a daily configuration comparison between staging and production — any delta becomes a tracked item; Tier 0 service deltas are flagged immediately
- Manage the environment reservation queue — confirm no two teams are scheduled for the same environment simultaneously
- Rebuild non-production environments on a defined cadence (at minimum monthly for staging) — environments that are never rebuilt accumulate configuration drift that is invisible until a release fails
- Coordinate with Release Engineers when a pipeline run reveals a non-prod environment issue
- Patch non-production Kubernetes nodes and maintain version alignment with the production EKS cluster within one minor version

### Decides Alone
- Non-production environment configuration changes that bring parity closer to production
- Environment reservation priority for non-conflicting requests
- Whether an environment rebuild is required immediately vs. can wait for the scheduled cadence

### Escalates
- A configuration delta between staging and production that has been open for more than 48 hours without resolution — escalate to Change & Release Management Manager
- An environment that is being held by a team beyond their reservation window and blocking another team — escalate to Change & Release Management Manager
- A non-production Kubernetes cluster that is more than two minor versions behind production — escalate as a risk item

### Key Metrics
- Configuration parity score: percentage of Tier 0 service configurations identical between staging and production — target 95%+
- Environment availability: target non-production environments available 99% of scheduled working hours
- Environment rebuild cadence: staging rebuilt at minimum monthly; other environments per defined schedule
- Parity delta resolution time: configuration deltas identified and resolved within 48 hours

### Interfaces
- **Release Engineers**: pipeline runs depend on environment availability — primary daily collaboration
- **Change Manager**: environment reservation requests come through the change calendar
- **Delivery teams**: primary consumers of non-production environments — manages their access and scheduling
- **Platform Reliability Manager**: performance test environments are managed by this engineer; load tests are run by the Performance SRE

### Success Indicators
- Production incidents are never traced back to a staging-to-production configuration difference that the Environment Engineer was aware of but did not resolve
- Environment reservation conflicts are rare because the calendar is well-managed
- Delivery teams trust that staging behavior predicts production behavior — they do not hedge with "well, staging is different"

### Failure Indicators
- Configuration drift between staging and production is discovered during a release, not during a parity audit
- Environments are rebuilt only when they break — not on a scheduled cadence
- Teams are bypassing environment reservations because the calendar is not enforced

## 12. Problem Analyst — L4

### Mission
Transform incident data into permanent fixes — so that every known error has a documented workaround and the platform is measurably less likely to fail in the same way twice.

### Owns
- Known Error Database (KEDB): every entry's accuracy, workaround quality, and review date
- RCA facilitation: after a SEV1 or recurring SEV2, leads the structured root cause investigation
- Problem ticket lifecycle: from creation through root cause identification through permanent fix delivery tracking
- PIR action item tracking: after a PIR is published, owns the follow-up to confirm action items are completed on schedule

### Day-to-day
- Review KEDB entries weekly — any entry without a reviewed workaround in 90 days gets flagged; any entry where the permanent fix was supposed to be delivered but isn't gets escalated
- Facilitate RCA sessions after all SEV1 incidents — prepare the timeline, bring the Splunk evidence, and structure the discussion to reach a specific root cause, not a general category
- Update problem tickets with investigation progress weekly — a problem ticket with no update for 14 days is a stale ticket that will never be resolved
- Track PIR action items in JIRA — weekly update, flag anything behind schedule to the Incident Manager
- Build and maintain the RCA template library — different failure categories (Kafka consumer group lag, RabbitMQ memory alarm, EKS pod eviction, TIBCO adapter timeout) have different investigation frameworks; maintain one per failure category

### Decides Alone
- Whether a problem ticket is classified as "Known Error with Workaround" (KEDB entry, no active investigation required) vs. "Under Investigation" (active RCA ongoing)
- Whether an RCA has reached a specific enough root cause to close the investigation phase
- Whether a KEDB entry's workaround is accurate enough to publish to the on-call team

### Escalates
- An RCA investigation that has been open for more than 30 days without a root cause — escalate to Incident Manager with a summary of what has been ruled out
- A permanent fix that requires architectural change (e.g., the root cause is that TIBCO is a SPOF for a Tier 0 service) — escalate to Architecture Lead and Director with the problem ticket and business case
- A PIR action item assigned to a delivery team that is more than two sprints past due — escalate to the Incident Manager for an executive push

### Key Metrics
- KEDB completeness: 100% of known errors in the past 12 months have a KEDB entry with a tested workaround
- RCA completion rate: 90% of SEV1 problem investigations reach a specific root cause within 30 days
- PIR action item completion rate: 85% of PIR action items completed within the agreed timeline
- Repeat incident rate: same failure mode causing two or more SEV1s within 90 days — target zero

### Interfaces
- **Senior SREs**: primary handoff — they resolve the incident; this analyst takes over the investigation
- **Incident Manager**: weekly problem review; escalations flow here
- **Delivery engineering teams**: RCA sessions require their participation as domain experts — this analyst facilitates, they provide the system knowledge
- **Architecture Lead**: when root cause points to an architectural debt item — the formal handoff from problem management to transformation planning

### Success Indicators
- The KEDB is used by on-call engineers without prompting — it surfaces in incident Slack threads as a reference, not as an afterthought
- RCA sessions produce a specific root cause statement ("the TIBCO adapter pool exhausted because the connection timeout was set to 30 seconds and the downstream service was responding in 45 seconds") not a category ("connectivity issue")
- The same incident type does not appear in the problem backlog twice with two separate investigation threads — problems are consolidated

### Failure Indicators
- PIR action items are marked complete but the fix was never actually deployed — the tracking is nominal, not real
- RCA sessions produce vague root causes that are not actionable ("intermittent network issue")
- KEDB entries are being created but not updated after the permanent fix is delivered — the database is growing but not being maintained

## 13. Service Level Analyst — L3/L4

### Mission
Produce the data that tells the business and the engineering teams whether the platform is meeting its commitments — and surface the signal before the stakeholders ask for it.

### Owns
- SLO tracking dashboards: built and maintained in Splunk or equivalent; one per Tier 0 and Tier 1 service
- Monthly SLA report: the authoritative document published to business stakeholders — not a raw data dump; a formatted, narrative-supported report
- Error budget consumption tracking: alerts when any Tier 0 service consumes more than 50% of its monthly error budget by mid-month
- SLO definition documentation: the written record of what each SLO measures, how it is calculated, and who approved it

### Day-to-day
- Check SLO dashboards each morning — flag any Tier 0 service with anomalous error budget consumption to the Platform Reliability Manager before the standup
- Collect SLA data at month-end from each monitoring source — Splunk for application-layer SLOs, EKS metrics for infrastructure SLOs, RabbitMQ and Kafka consumer metrics for middleware SLOs
- Draft the monthly SLA report and deliver to the Platform Reliability Manager for review by day 3 of the following month
- Maintain the SLO definition document — when a service's SLO target changes, this document is the record of what changed, when, and why
- Respond to ad-hoc SLA queries from business stakeholders — produce a written response with source data, not a verbal answer

### Decides Alone
- Whether a data anomaly in the SLO dashboard is a monitoring artifact or a genuine SLO breach — escalates if uncertain
- Whether a stakeholder's ad-hoc SLA query requires a new dashboard or can be answered from existing data

### Escalates
- Any Tier 0 service consuming more than 50% of its monthly error budget before day 15 — escalate to Platform Reliability Manager immediately
- A discrepancy between what the SLO dashboard shows and what an engineering team claims about their service's availability — escalate to Platform Reliability Manager for resolution before the next SLA report is published
- A request to change an SLO target without a formal review process — escalate to Platform Reliability Manager

### Key Metrics
- SLA report timeliness: published within 5 business days of month close — every month without exception
- Dashboard accuracy: zero errors in monthly SLA data confirmed by stakeholders after publication
- Error budget alert lead time: Tier 0 error budget alerts fire before any business stakeholder notices an SLO trend

### Interfaces
- **Platform Reliability Manager**: primary direction; SLA report reviewed here before publication
- **Business stakeholders**: monthly SLA report recipient; ad-hoc query responder
- **Senior SREs and Platform engineers**: data source validation — when SLO data looks wrong, these are the subject matter experts
- **On-call team**: during incidents, provides real-time error budget status when asked

### Success Indicators
- Business stakeholders receive the SLA report before they ask for it — every month
- SLO dashboards are accurate enough that engineering teams reference them in their own sprint planning
- Error budget alerts are firing early enough that the Platform Reliability Manager can take corrective action before a breach occurs

### Failure Indicators
- SLA reports contain errors that are caught by business stakeholders — not by the operations team
- SLO dashboards are trusted by no one because they have been wrong before without correction
- Error budget alerts are firing but no one is acting on them — the signal exists but the process to respond is broken

## 14. Platform Architect — L7

### Mission
Define the technical standards that make the platform buildable, operable, and scalable — and ensure those standards are applied consistently across every service.

### Owns
- Architecture standards documentation: the written rules for how services are deployed on EKS, how they use RabbitMQ/Kafka/TIBCO, how they expose APIs, and how they handle failure
- ARB technical review: provides the deep technical assessment within the ARB process; the Architecture & Transformation Lead chairs, this Architect evaluates
- Technical debt classification: categorizes debt by architectural impact — not just age or team preference
- Reference architecture library: documented, approved patterns (e.g., "Standard EKS microservice deployment pattern," "RabbitMQ producer-consumer pattern for Tier 1 services")
- ADR authorship: writes the technical content of ADRs; the Architecture Lead signs off

### Day-to-day
- Attend ARB meetings and provide technical evaluation of each submitted design — written assessment, not verbal only
- Maintain reference architecture documents — update when a new pattern is approved or a deprecated pattern is retired
- Consult with delivery teams on architecture questions before they formalize a design — this is where the highest-value influence happens
- Review new service designs for operational operability: can it be monitored in Splunk, can it be deployed via pipeline, does it expose health endpoints, is its dependency list manageable
- Write ADRs for all significant decisions — focus on documenting the options considered, not just the decision made

### Decides Alone
- Whether a proposed design conforms to documented architecture standards
- Whether a technical debt item warrants Critical, High, or Medium classification

### Escalates
- A design that violates core standards and the submitting team has valid technical justification for the violation — escalate to Architecture & Transformation Lead for a standards exception decision
- A reference architecture that has been superseded by a better pattern but changing it would require migrating 10+ existing services — escalate to Architecture Lead and Director for a roadmap decision

### Key Metrics
- ARB technical assessments completed within 5 business days of submission — 100%
- Reference architecture documents reviewed within the past 12 months — 100%
- Architecture violations found in production (escaped ARB) — target zero for Tier 0/1 services

### Interfaces
- **Architecture & Transformation Lead**: primary reporting relationship; primary collaboration
- **Delivery engineering leads and Staff Engineers**: ARB participants and consulting clients
- **Platform Reliability Manager**: technical standards for availability architecture
- **Senior SREs**: operational feedback loop — what patterns are hard to operate gets fed back into standards

### Success Indicators
- Reference architectures are being adopted by delivery teams without being mandated — the patterns are good enough that teams choose them
- ARB technical assessments are described as detailed and useful by delivery teams — not as rubber stamps or arbitrary rejections

### Failure Indicators
- ARB rejections are appealed and overturned regularly — the technical assessment is not rigorous enough or the standards are not current
- Reference architectures are more than 18 months old and have not been updated to reflect current EKS versions or current middleware versions

## 15. Transformation Engineer — L5

### Day-to-day

- Write migration runbooks before executing the first production migration — the runbook must be reviewed by the Platform Architect before production work begins
- Engage with delivery teams who own the services being migrated — the migration succeeds only if the owning team understands and accepts the new operational model
- Document every technical decision made during a migration as a lightweight ADR — the Architecture Lead reviews
- Report project status weekly to the Architecture & Transformation Lead — include: what was completed, what is blocked, and what the next milestone is

### Decides Alone
- Technical implementation choices within an approved migration pattern
- Whether a migration milestone is complete (subject to Architecture Lead review)
- Whether a migration discovery reveals a complication that changes the scope estimate

### Escalates
- A migration that encounters a technical blocker that cannot be resolved within the sprint — escalate to Architecture & Transformation Lead with a specific blocker description and options
- An owning team that is not engaging with the migration and is blocking progress — escalate to Architecture & Transformation Lead
- A scope change that would increase the migration timeline by more than 50% — escalate to Architecture Lead and Director before adjusting the roadmap

### Key Metrics
- Migrations completed per quarter: per the modernization roadmap commitments
- Defect rate post-migration: production incidents attributable to migration work in the 60 days post-completion — target zero for SEV1

### Interfaces
- **Architecture & Transformation Lead**: primary direction; project status; escalations
- **Platform Architect**: technical review of migration runbooks and implementation decisions
- **Delivery engineering teams**: the service owners who will operate the migrated service
- **Change Manager**: migration deployments go through CAB; this engineer prepares the RFCs

### Success Indicators
- Migrations complete within the committed timeline with no SEV1 incidents in the 60-day post-migration window
- Owning teams describe the migration as well-managed and the knowledge transfer as complete

### Failure Indicators
- Migrations are completing but the owning teams are calling the operations team for support on the migrated service 90 days post-go-live — knowledge transfer did not happen
- Migration runbooks are written after the first production migration, not before

## 17. On-Call Coordinator — L3/L4

### Mission
Run the operational mechanics of on-call — rotation schedule, runbook currency, alert routing, and engineer welfare — so that on-call is sustainable and coverage is never a surprise.

### Owns
- On-call rotation schedule: published at minimum 4 weeks in advance; no gaps
- Runbook library: tracks coverage, review dates, and accuracy for every runbook in the team's domain
- Alert routing configuration: PagerDuty (or equivalent) escalation policies, alert-to-runbook mapping, and on-call contact validation
- On-call metrics reporting: pages-per-engineer, time-of-day distribution, weekend distribution — monthly report to Incident Manager
- Runbook review process: assigns reviewers, tracks review completion, flags any runbook older than 90 days without a review

### Day-to-day
- Publish the on-call schedule 4 weeks in advance — confirm every shift has a primary and a backup engineer assigned
- Review alert routing configuration weekly — confirm every alert has a current runbook linked, every escalation policy has a valid contact, and there are no stale alert definitions

- Produce the monthly on-call metrics report — pages-per-engineer, weekend pages, time-of-day distribution, and acknowledgment latency by engineer
- Support on-call engineers who request schedule swaps — process the swap, update the system, and notify all parties

### Decides Alone
- On-call schedule swap approvals when both parties agree and coverage is not affected
- Whether an alert routing configuration change is a minor update (can deploy immediately) or a significant change (requires Incident Manager review)

### Escalates
- An on-call engineer who is consistently missing acknowledgment SLAs — escalate to Incident Manager immediately
- A runbook with no assigned owner and no review in more than 90 days — escalate to Incident Manager to assign an owner
- A schedule gap that cannot be filled by the normal rotation — escalate to Incident Manager, who will escalate to the Director if necessary
- An on-call engineer who reports they are unable to safely continue their rotation due to health or personal circumstances — escalate to Incident Manager same day

### Key Metrics
- On-call schedule coverage: 100% of shifts have a primary and backup assigned, published 4 weeks in advance
- Runbook review currency: zero runbooks older than 90 days without a completed review for Tier 0 and Tier 1 services
- Alert-to-runbook mapping: 100% of production alerts for Tier 0/1 services have a linked, current runbook

### Interfaces
- **Incident Manager**: primary direction; escalations flow here

- **All on-call engineers**: schedule management, swap requests, metrics feedback
- **PagerDuty / on-call platform**: primary tool; owns the configuration

### Success Indicators
- On-call schedule is published 4 weeks in advance with zero gaps — every month
- On-call engineers describe the rotation as fairly distributed — no one is carrying disproportionate weekend burden

### Failure Indicators

- Pages-per-engineer data is produced but never acted on — the report exists but the operational response does not
