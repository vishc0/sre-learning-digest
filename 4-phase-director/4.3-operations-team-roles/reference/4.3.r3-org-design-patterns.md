# Org Design Patterns — Operations Teams

Three reference structures for operations organizations at different company scales. Use these to pressure-test your own org design, identify where you're over- or under-staffed for your scale, and communicate the rationale for structural decisions to your Director or VP.

These are patterns, not prescriptions. The right structure depends on your product complexity, deployment frequency, reliability requirements, and team maturity — not just headcount. Use the scaling triggers to know when to move from one pattern to the next.

---

## Pattern 1: Small Ops Team

**Scale**: 15–30 engineers in operations, supporting a 200–500 person company
**Reporting**: Director of Operations (or a Senior Manager in this role) likely reports to VP Engineering or CTO. May not have a dedicated Director title at this size.

### Structure

```
Director of Operations (or Sr. Manager — may be player-coach)
├── Senior SRE / Team Lead (x2) — Incident Command + SLO tracking
├── SRE (x3–5) — incident response, on-call rotation
├── Change Manager (x1) — change governance, CAB chair
├── Release Engineer (x1) — pipeline maintenance, deployments
└── Platform Architect (x1) — part-time ARB chair, ADR governance, tech roadmap
```

**Total headcount**: 8–10 people including the Director. Every person covers multiple functions.

### What's Collapsed Relative to the Medium Pattern

| Function | Who Owns It in Small Pattern | Notes |
|----------|-----------------------------|----|
| Problem Management | Senior SREs rotate the PM function | No dedicated Problem Analyst. PIRs run by whoever IC'd the incident, coordinated by a Senior SRE. Risk: problem records slip when on-call load is high. |
| Environment Engineering | Release Engineer | Owns both pipeline and environment management. Works at small scale. Breaks when environment count exceeds ~20 or deployment frequency exceeds 10/day. |
| Service Level Analysis | Senior SREs produce SLA/SLO reports | No dedicated analyst. Reports are ad hoc or templated from a shared spreadsheet. Accurate but not scalable. |
| Application Portfolio Management | Platform Architect maintains the portfolio | Single spreadsheet or wiki page. Works if portfolio is < 50 services. |
| On-Call Coordination | Director or designated Senior SRE | No coordinator role. Rotation managed in PagerDuty directly. Works until rotation has > 8 people across multiple domains. |
| Architecture review | Platform Architect alone | No ARB committee at this size — one person does lightweight architecture review. Decisions documented in ADRs stored in Confluence or Git. |
| Transformation / Program | Director with Platform Architect support | No dedicated transformation function. Improvements happen as part of capacity planning, not a dedicated program. |

### What This Means Operationally

- Every Senior SRE wears 2–3 hats. This is expected and sustainable — at this size, specialization creates brittleness, not efficiency.
- The Director is hands-on. Expect to IC P1 incidents, participate in CAB, write runbooks, and review ADRs. This is normal for the scale. If a Director at this size is purely strategic, the team will notice.
- Change governance is lightweight: a weekly CAB with 3–4 participants, no subcommittees, no formal Change Advisory Board tooling beyond a shared calendar and a standard change request template.
- On-call burnout is a real risk at this size. With 5–7 engineers in the rotation, each person is on call roughly every 5–7 weeks. This is manageable only if page volume is controlled. Monitor pages per engineer per week actively.

### Scaling Triggers — What to Add First

These triggers are sequential — address #1 before #2, and #2 before #3. Hiring the wrong function first is a common mistake.

1. **When actionable on-call pages exceed 8 per engineer per week for 4+ consecutive weeks**: Add an SRE before anything else. The team is at capacity. Adding any other role while the on-call rotation is burned out will not help — the new hire will spend their ramp time watching burned-out teammates.

2. **When change failure rate exceeds 8% in any rolling 90-day window**: Add a dedicated Change Manager with a structured CAB process. At this failure rate, the cost of changes failing exceeds the cost of the governance overhead.

3. **When the Director is regularly ICing P1 incidents because there's no one else with the depth**: Hire a Senior SRE immediately. A Director who is the primary IC for P1s is a single point of failure for leadership continuity — every P1 pulls the Director away from everything else.

4. **When the application portfolio exceeds 50 services or the team is struggling to understand service dependencies**: Consider adding a Platform Architect or promoting the existing one and giving them headcount for a junior engineer.

5. **When headcount reaches 15 operations engineers and more than 2 domains exist**: Move to Pattern 2. A Director managing 15 ICs directly across 4 domains is at the limit of effective span of control.

## Pattern 2: Medium Ops Team

**Scale**: 30–75 engineers in operations, supporting a 500–2,000 person company
**Reporting**: Director of Operations reports to VP of Engineering or VP of Technology. A VP of Operations may exist but is not required at the lower end of this range.

### Structure

```
Director of Operations
├── Incident & Problem Management Manager
│   ├── Senior SRE / IC Team Lead (x1–2)
│   ├── SRE — Primary IC (x2–3)
│   └── Problem Analyst (x1)
│
├── Change & Release Management Manager
│   ├── Senior Change Manager (x1)
│   ├── Change Analyst (x1)
│   ├── Release Engineer (x2)
│   └── Environment Engineer (x1)
│
├── Platform Reliability Manager
│   ├── Senior SRE — Reliability / SLO (x1)
│   ├── SRE — Reliability (x2)
│   ├── Performance Engineer (x1)
│   └── Service Level Analyst (x1)
│
└── Architecture & Transformation Lead
    ├── Senior Platform Architect (x1)
    ├── Platform Architect (x1)
    ├── Application Portfolio Analyst (x1)
    └── Transformation Engineer (x1)
```

**Total headcount**: Director + 4 managers + 16 ICs = 21 minimum. Scales to 35 with 2–3 additional ICs per domain as needed.

**Manager span**: Each manager owns 4–7 direct reports. The Director owns 4 direct reports (the managers). This is the right structure for this scale — the Director is no longer player-coach.

### Key Differences from Pattern 1

- **Every domain has a dedicated manager**. The Director's job shifts from operations execution to strategy, cross-domain coordination, and people development of 4 managers. The Director no longer ICs incidents or attends CAB by default — they review metrics and step in on exceptions.
- **Problem Management is a full-time function** with a dedicated analyst. PIRs are not ad hoc — the Problem Analyst tracks every open problem record, drives action item completion, and produces the recurring problem management report.
- **Architecture has a team**, not one person. Architecture review is a structured ARB process with documented attendance, agenda, and ADR output.
- **On-call rotation has minimum 5 per domain** for primary IC. At this size, a rotation smaller than 5 means someone is on call more than once every 5 weeks — a burnout risk.
- **Service Level Analysis is dedicated**. An analyst owns SLO tracking, error budget reports, and the monthly SLA dashboard for stakeholders. SLOs are no longer maintained as a side task by SREs.

### Scaling Triggers — When to Move to Pattern 3

1. **When any single manager's direct reports exceed 10**: Split the team or add a Team Lead layer. A manager with 10+ direct reports cannot give each person meaningful 1:1 time and will lose visibility into individual performance.

2. **When the portfolio exceeds 150 services**: Add a dedicated Application Portfolio Analyst. At 150+ services, the portfolio cannot be maintained as a side function — decisions about modernization, decommission, and investment require full-time tracking.

3. **When DORA metrics have been stagnant for 6+ months despite improvement efforts**: Add a Release Engineer with dedicated focus on pipeline optimization, or a Transformation Engineer focused on DORA-targeted improvements. Stagnant DORA at this scale usually means the improvement work keeps getting deprioritized by operational demand.

4. **When Tier-1 incidents require VP-level involvement weekly**: This is a reliability signal, not a communication signal. Weekly VP-level P1 escalations indicate an SLO or reliability gap. Add SRE capacity in Platform Reliability before adding communication process.

5. **When Director headcount (direct manager reports) reaches 5–6**: Consider adding a Senior Manager layer. A Director managing 6 domains directly has span-of-control issues and is likely doing tactical work that a Senior Manager should own.

6. **When total operations engineers exceed 50**: Evaluate whether the VP layer is needed. Move to Pattern 3.

## Pattern 3: Large Ops Team

**Scale**: 75–200 engineers in operations, supporting a 2,000+ person company
**Reporting**: VP of Operations exists and owns the function. Directors report to the VP and are peers, not reports of a single Director.

### Structure

```
VP of Operations
│
├── Director of Operations — Reliability
│   ├── Platform Reliability Senior Manager
│   │   ├── SRE — Reliability Team (8–10 engineers)
│   │   └── SRE — Availability Engineering Team (6–8 engineers)
│   └── Performance Engineering Manager
│       └── Performance Engineering Team (8–10 engineers)
│
├── Director of Operations — Delivery
│   ├── Change & Release Management Senior Manager
│   │   ├── Change Management Team (5–7 engineers/analysts)
│   │   └── Release Engineering Team (6–8 engineers)
│   └── Environment Engineering Manager
│       └── Environment Engineering Team (8–10 engineers)
│
├── Director of Operations — Incident & Problem
│   ├── Incident Management Senior Manager
│   │   └── Incident Management Team (10–15 engineers/coordinators)
│   └── Problem Management Manager
│       └── Problem Management Team (5–8 analysts/engineers)
│
└── Director of Architecture & Transformation
    ├── Architecture Manager
    │   └── Architecture Team (6–8 architects)
    └── Transformation Program Manager
        └── Transformation Team (8–12 program managers/engineers)
```

**Total headcount**: VP + 4 Directors + 8 Senior Managers/Managers + 60–90 ICs = 75–110 minimum for this pattern to make sense. The upper end of this pattern can reach 200 operations engineers.

### Key Differences from Pattern 2

- **VP layer exists**. The VP owns strategy, executive stakeholder relationships, budget advocacy, and cross-Director coordination. The VP does not manage incidents, attend CAB, or own domain metrics. The VP reviews Director-level metrics and steps in on escalations.
- **Each domain is now a full team**, not a function within a shared team. Incident Management is its own team with its own manager. Problem Management is its own team — no longer a sub-function of Incident Management. This separation matters: when Problem Management reports to the Incident Manager, problem records get deprioritized in favor of active incidents.
- **Problem Management is structurally separated from Incident Management**. This is the most common structural failure at medium scale that forces the move to large-pattern: when the same manager owns both incident response and problem investigation, problem investigation loses every week.
- **Environment Engineering is its own team**. At this scale, the number of environments (dev, test, staging, performance, prod-like, etc.) across hundreds of services requires dedicated ownership. An IC within Release cannot own this alongside their pipeline responsibilities.
- **Transformation is a full program management function**. At this scale, a Transformation Engineer running improvement work is insufficient. You need PMs who can manage cross-team programs with milestones, dependencies, and executive visibility.
- **Architecture has a team and a structured ARB**. The ARB is a standing committee with scheduled sessions, documented attendance requirements, and an ADR standard enforced by the Architecture Manager.

### When to Restructure from Pattern 2 to Pattern 3

These are structural decisions, not headcount decisions. The trigger is always "this structure is causing the org to fail at something it should be good at."

1. **Headcount reaches 50 operations engineers**: The Director span of control becomes untenable. Add a second Director layer. The VP may or may not exist yet — a VP layer is often added at the 75–100 engineer mark.

2. **One Director is managing more than 4 managers**: Split the Director span. Each Director should own no more than 3–4 direct managers to maintain meaningful engagement with each domain.

3. **Problem Management is consistently deprioritized by the Incident Management manager**: Separate them structurally. This is not a people problem — it's an incentives problem. An Incident Manager's job is measured on MTTR. Problem analysis doesn't help MTTR in the short term, so it will always lose when the same person owns both.

4. **Release Engineering and Environment Engineering are blocking each other's roadmaps**: Separate them. When a Release Engineer is pulled into environment incidents, the deployment pipeline work stops. At this scale, these are different disciplines requiring different expertise.

5. **Architecture decisions are taking more than 4 weeks to get a disposition**: The ARB is under-resourced or improperly staffed. A full Architecture team with a structured ARB should be able to disposition 90% of requests within 2 weeks.

### VP-Level Operating Model

The VP in this pattern operates differently from a Director:

- **Reviews Director-level metrics** at a monthly cadence, not weekly domain metrics. The VP should not be reading incident counts — the Directors summarize those.
- **Owns the operations budget** and advocates for headcount with the CTO/CPO. Directors propose; VP approves and defends.
- **Is the escalation point for cross-Director disputes**. If two Directors disagree on a shared process, the VP decides or facilitates the decision.
- **Represents operations at the executive level**: participates in quarterly business reviews, board presentations involving reliability or delivery data, and executive incident post-mortems.
- **Sets the 2–3 year vision** for the operations organization. Directors set the 12-month operational roadmap within that vision.

## Choosing the Right Pattern

| Question | Small (P1) | Medium (P2) | Large (P3) |
|----------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| Operations engineer count | 8–20 | 20–50 | 50–200 |
| Company size | 200–500 | 500–2,000 | 2,000+ |
| Director is hands-on IC? | Yes, expected | No, exception | No, never |
| Problem Management dedicated? | No — shared function | Yes — dedicated analyst | Yes — dedicated team |
| Architecture as a team? | No — one architect | Yes — 3–4 people | Yes — 6–8 people |
| VP layer needed? | No | Maybe at upper end | Yes |
| On-call rotation minimum | 4 per domain | 5 per domain | 6+ per domain |
| Change governance structure | Lightweight CAB (1 hr/week) | Formal CAB with subdomains | CAB + domain-specific review boards |

> **Common mistake**: trying to run Pattern 1 management practices at Pattern 2 scale. The most frequent failure mode: a Director who was effective as a player-coach at 10 engineers tries to remain hands-on at 30 engineers, becomes the bottleneck on every important decision, and inadvertently prevents their managers from developing authority. When you cross the threshold from Pattern 1 to Pattern 2, the Director's job description fundamentally changes — even if the title doesn't.
