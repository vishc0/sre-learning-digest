## Section 3: Domains We Own

The following domains are under this team's primary ownership. "Primary ownership" means: we set the process, we govern the outcomes, we are accountable to leadership for results. Partner teams may execute within these domains, but we own the standard.

- **Incident Management** — detecting, containing, and resolving service disruptions. Includes on-call rotation, severity classification, bridge coordination, and stakeholder communication.
- **Problem Management** — eliminating the root causes of recurring incidents. Includes PIR authorship, root cause analysis, and tracking corrective actions to closure.
- **Change & Release Management** — governing all changes reaching production. Includes RFC intake, Standard/Normal/Emergency change classification, CAB, and change failure tracking.
- **Service Level Management** — defining, tracking, and reporting SLOs and SLAs. Includes SLO negotiation with product and engineering, error budget tracking, and monthly compliance reporting.
- **Availability & Continuity Management** — DR planning, Business Continuity Planning (BCP), HA architecture review, and failover exercise execution. Includes Tier-1 runbook ownership for disaster scenarios.
- **Environment Management** — non-production environment provisioning, governance, and decommissioning. Includes namespace and cluster lifecycle management across lower environments (Dev, QA, SIT, Staging).
- **Performance Engineering** — load testing, JVM/container tuning, performance gates in the release pipeline, and capacity planning for Tier-1 systems.
- **Application Layer Taxonomy** — service classification (Tier-1/2/3), dependency mapping, ownership registry, and topology documentation. Source of truth for what is critical and who owns it.
- **Architecture Principles & Governance** — Architecture Review Board (ARB) participation, Architectural Decision Records (ADRs), standards and patterns library, and exception management.
- **Application Portfolio & Transformation** — portfolio health tracking, end-of-life identification, modernization roadmap contribution, and technical debt visibility reporting.

## Section 4: Operating Principles

These are the non-negotiables. They govern how we work when things are ambiguous, when we disagree, and when we are under pressure. They are not aspirational — they are expected. A violation of these principles is a coaching conversation, not an oversight.

---

**1. Accountability without clarity is theater.**

Every person knows exactly what they own. Every domain has one primary owner. Every Tier-1 system has a named reliability owner. "Shared ownership" is a synonym for nobody owns it. If you cannot name the owner of something in thirty seconds, we have a gap — and closing it is the Director's job, not something we live with.

---

**2. Blameless by default.**

Systems fail. Processes break. Humans make mistakes under pressure. We investigate what failed, not who failed. Post-Incident Reviews are learning tools, not performance reviews. No PIR names a person as a root cause. The root cause is always a system, a process, a gap in tooling, or a missing safeguard. Individuals who surface failures faster, speak up in bridges, and write honest PIRs are the people we promote — not the ones we penalize.

---

**3. Data before opinion.**

No recommendation without a metric behind it. No escalation without a trend. No "I think the system is slow" without a Splunk query or a latency dashboard to back it. When we go to leadership, we bring numbers. When we escalate, we bring evidence. When we push back on a change, we cite the change failure rate or the error budget. Opinion is a starting point for a question — not a conclusion.

---

**4. Toil is debt.**

Every hour spent on undifferentiated operational work — manual restarts, alert noise, runbook steps that should be automated — is an hour not spent making the system more reliable. We track toil. We report toil percentage monthly. We fund toil reduction before we accept new operational scope. A team that is 70% toil cannot improve. If we are past 50%, it is the Director's job to escalate it as a capacity risk, not to ask the team to absorb more.

---

**5. On-call is a team sport.**

No single person is the only one who can respond to a critical failure. Minimum rotation size for any Tier-1 system: 5 engineers. Bus factor floor: 3 — meaning at least 3 people must be capable of independently resolving the most common failure modes for every Tier-1 system. Anything below this threshold is a documented risk that the Director owns and is actively working to remediate. Heroism in on-call is a system design problem, not a badge of honor.

---

**6. Escalate early, not late.**

If you are not sure whether to escalate, escalate. The cost of an unnecessary escalation is a brief interruption. The cost of a missed escalation is a P1 that ran unchecked for 40 minutes while one person tried to solve it alone. When you escalate, state three things: the situation, what you have already tried, and what you need from the escalation target. "Just wanted to give you a heads-up" is not an escalation — it is noise. A clear ask is always the right move.

---

**7. Runbooks are alive.**

A runbook that is not updated after every incident is a liability, not an asset. After every P1 or P2 resolution, the person who resolved the incident reviews the runbook and updates it before the incident ticket is closed. If the runbook did not exist, they write a draft within 48 hours. The Domain Manager reviews runbook coverage quarterly. An outdated runbook with a date stamp older than 90 days from the last related incident is flagged for immediate review. We do not trust runbooks we have not maintained.

---

**8. Change is risk with a plan.**

Every change creates risk. The question is not "can we avoid risk" but "have we understood and planned for it." CAB is not a bureaucratic bottleneck — it is a structured risk conversation between people who have context. Standard Changes that have been tested, documented, and shown low failure rates move fast. Normal Changes get scrutiny. Emergency Changes get post-review. A change that bypasses CAB without Director authorization is a process violation — not an acceptable response to urgency. Build the fast path correctly; do not circumvent the path.

---

**9. Visibility is advocacy.**

The Director reports reliability work monthly to VP level. Operations success is invisible by default — the absence of incidents is not noticed; only their presence is. We make our work visible. Prevented incidents count — document near-misses. Toil reduced counts — report the hours saved by automation. SLO compliance counts — publish the number, not just incidents. If the organization does not know what this team prevents, they cannot fund what this team needs. Visibility is not self-promotion; it is professional obligation.

---

**10. We improve together.**

Senior engineers mentor. Managers develop their direct reports. The team's collective capability is a shared responsibility — no one person's growth comes at the expense of another's visibility. When someone is stuck, we ask how to help — not whose problem it is. Knowledge that lives in one person's head is a risk. We document, we pair, we run game days, and we rotate responsibilities deliberately so that expertise spreads. The team that improves together is the team that survives organizational change intact.

## Section 5: Decision-Making Model

Decision authority is explicit. When the right level is unclear, escalate up one level. A 5-minute Slack message to confirm is never the wrong call. Defaulting to the wrong level — either over-escalating every minor call or under-escalating decisions that have broad impact — is a calibration conversation for your manager.

### Decision Authority by Level

**Individual IC — no approval needed:**
- Routing a new alert or incident within their assigned domain
- Following an existing, approved runbook step-by-step
- Updating Splunk dashboards, alerts, or saved searches they own
- Logging a new problem record following a P3/P4 incident they worked
- Triaging a new RFC as Standard, Normal, or Emergency per the published change classification matrix
- Updating a runbook after an incident they resolved (per Principle 7)

**Senior SRE / Team Lead — decides within their domain:**
- IC assignment for P3 and P4 incidents
- Closing a P3 incident (after verifying resolution criteria)
- Recommending a new Standard Change to the Change Manager for CAB review
- Approving a peer's runbook update for accuracy and completeness
- Calling a technical bridge for a P3 event without manager approval
- Adjusting alert thresholds within approved operating ranges (documented change)

**Domain Manager — decides within their domain with Director awareness:**
- P2 incident declaration and managing the P2 bridge
- Problem record closure after corrective action verification
- Standard Change approval (delegated from CAB, per approved delegation charter)
- On-call rotation adjustments for P3/P4 coverage
- Escalating a Normal Change to Emergency status under active incident conditions
- Accepting or deferring a new operational responsibility from a partner team (with Director notification)
- Hiring panel decisions for IC-level candidates within their domain (with Director sign-off on offer)

**Director — decides at organizational level:**
- P1 incident declaration
- CAB override for Emergency Changes under active P1 conditions
- Any Tier-1 architecture change that alters system behavior in production
- Cross-domain resource reallocation (moving an SRE across domains for more than one sprint)
- Personnel actions: performance improvement plans, role changes, terminations
- Any decision escalated from a manager that crosses domain boundaries
- Accepting new organizational scope from VP
- Budget commitments within Director authority

**VP — decides at business level:**
- Organizational restructuring (team splits, merges, reporting line changes)
- Budget commitments exceeding Director's delegated authority
- Regulatory or external compliance decisions that require executive sign-off
- External vendor or partner commitments at contract level
- Any decision the Director has escalated as beyond their authority or risk tolerance

**CTO / CIO — decides at enterprise level:**
- Business continuity decisions affecting multiple divisions
- Board-level reporting obligations (outages with customer or regulatory impact)
- Enterprise-wide architectural pivots

**Meeting hygiene rules:**
- Every meeting has a written agenda distributed at least 24 hours in advance. No agenda = meeting is cancelled or rescheduled by the owner.
- Notes are published to Confluence within 24 hours. Decisions captured in notes are binding.
- Recurring meetings with no agenda for two consecutive occurrences are reviewed by the Director for cancellation or restructuring.
- Attendees who are blocked or no longer relevant to a recurring meeting surface it to the owner — not by silently skipping.

## Section 7: Communication Norms

### Channel Rules

- **#incidents** — all active P1/P2/P3 incident communication. No DMs for incident coordination. The channel is the bridge and the record. @here is authorized for P1/P2 only.
- **#ops-team** — internal team communication, questions, FYIs, team announcements. Non-urgent.
- **#ops-leadership** — Director + Domain Managers only. Cross-domain coordination, leadership decisions, pre-escalation discussion.
- **#changes-cab** — all RFC submissions and change coordination. Change Manager moderates.
- **[PROJECT OR DOMAIN CHANNELS]** — domain-specific coordination. Director has view access to all channels.

### Response Time Expectations

| Channel / Mode | Urgency | Expected Response Time |
|---------------|---------|----------------------|
| #incidents (P1/P2) | Critical | Within 5 minutes during business hours; within 15 minutes during on-call hours |
| Direct Slack message (tagged, actionable) | High | Within 1 hour during business hours |
| #ops-team general message | Normal | Within 4 hours during business hours |
| Email (internal) | Low | Within 1 business day |
| Email (external stakeholder) | Varies | Within 4 hours; same day at minimum |

### Escalation Communication Standard

When escalating — in any direction, at any level — always provide:

1. **What is happening** — current state, observable facts
2. **What you have tried** — steps already taken, results observed
3. **What you need** — specific ask from the escalation target

"FYI there's an issue" is not an escalation. An escalation without a specific ask is a notification. Notifications are fine; do not label them escalations.

### Decision Documentation Rule

Any decision made in a verbal conversation, Slack thread, or informal meeting that affects team operations, process, or individual responsibilities must be documented in Confluence within 24 hours by the most senior person in the conversation. Verbal decisions that are not documented are not decisions — they are intentions.

### P1 Communication Protocol

| Action | Timing | Owner |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Post P1 declaration in #incidents | Immediately on P1 call | Incident Commander |
| Slack DM to Director | Within 5 minutes of declaration | On-call Domain Manager |
| Director Slack DM to VP | Within 15 minutes of P1 declaration | Director |
| First stakeholder status update in #incidents | Within 15 minutes of declaration | Comms Owner (designated on bridge) |
| Subsequent status updates | Every 15 minutes until resolved | Comms Owner |
| VP verbal brief (call or Slack voice note) | If P1 exceeds 45 minutes | Director |
| Written timeline to VP | Within 24 hours of resolution | Director |

## Section 8: Escalation Path

### Standard Escalation Chain

```
Individual IC
    │
    ▼ (immediate issues, technical guidance, IC-level decisions)
Team Lead / Senior SRE
    │
    ▼ (domain-level decisions, resource needs, P2 declaration)
Domain Manager
    │
    ▼ (org-level decisions, cross-domain, P1 declaration, CAB override)
Director of Operations
    │
    ▼ (budget, staffing, external stakeholder, regulatory)
VP of Engineering / VP of Operations
    │
    ▼ (business continuity, board-level, enterprise-wide impact)
CTO / CIO
```

**Rule**: Escalate up one level at a time. Skip levels only when the situation is a declared P1 and the intervening level is unavailable despite a documented attempt to reach them.

### P1 Incident Escalation Flow

```
On-call SRE detects or is alerted to P1-severity event
    │
    ▼
On-call SRE opens #incidents bridge, posts situation
    │
    ▼
Domain Manager notified (Slack DM + bridge tag) — within 5 min
    │
    ▼
Director notified by Domain Manager (Slack DM) — within 5 min of manager notification
    │
    ▼
Director notifies VP (Slack DM) — within 15 min of P1 declaration
    │
    ▼
Comms owner sends 15-min stakeholder updates (to #incidents + distribution list)
    │
    ▼
Director joins bridge as executive sponsor (if > 30 min or customer impact declared)
    │
    ▼
P1 resolved → Director sends written timeline to VP within 24 hours
    │
    ▼
PIR assigned and completed within 5 business days
```

### Escalation Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Over-escalation**: Taking every minor P3 to the Director without attempting resolution at manager level first
- **Under-escalation**: Running a P2 for 90 minutes without notifying the Director
- **Silent escalation**: Looping in someone senior over Slack without telling the person being escalated past
- **Reverse-engineering escalation**: Declaring something a P3 to avoid escalation when it is clearly a P2

## Section 9: How We Measure Success

The Director tracks and reports these metrics monthly to VP level. Metric definitions and data sources are documented in [CONFLUENCE LINK — Ops Metrics Definitions]. Dashboards are published in [SPLUNK DASHBOARD LINK / DATADOG LINK].

### Monthly VP-Level Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target | Source |
|--------|-----------|--------|--------|
| Tier-1 SLO Compliance | % of Tier-1 services meeting their monthly SLO | 100% of Tier-1 services | [Splunk / Datadog SLO dashboard] |
| MTTR — P1 Incidents | Mean time from P1 declaration to verified resolution | < 2 hours | Incident tracking tool |
| MTTR — P2 Incidents | Mean time from P2 declaration to verified resolution | < 4 hours | Incident tracking tool |
| Change Failure Rate | % of production changes causing a P1 or P2 within 24 hours | < 5% | CAB / change tracking tool |
| On-Call Load | Average pages per on-call engineer per week, per rotation | < 5 pages/engineer/week | PagerDuty / on-call platform |
| Toil Percentage | % of team hours spent on toil (manually tracked, monthly survey) | < 50% of total team time | Manager-reported, monthly |
| PIR Completion Rate | % of P1/P2 incidents with a completed PIR within 5 business days | 100% | Incident tracking tool |
| DORA — Deployment Frequency | How often code deploys to production | [HIGH band: multiple per day; MEDIUM: once per week] | CI/CD pipeline |
| DORA — Lead Time for Changes | Time from code commit to production | [HIGH: < 1 hour; MEDIUM: < 1 week] | CI/CD pipeline |
| DORA — Change Failure Rate | See above | < 5% | CAB / change tool |
| DORA — Mean Time to Restore | See MTTR P1 above | < 2 hours | Incident tool |

### Metric Escalation Rule

If any metric in the table above is **red for two consecutive months**, the Director presents a written remediation plan to VP within the same reporting cycle. The plan includes: root cause of the metric trend, three specific corrective actions with owners and due dates, and the expected timeline to return to green. A metric that is yellow for three consecutive months is treated as red.

### Metric Color Definitions

| Color | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| Green | At or better than target |
| Yellow | Within 10% of target threshold; trending toward target |
| Red | Beyond 10% of target threshold; OR trending away from target for two consecutive months |
