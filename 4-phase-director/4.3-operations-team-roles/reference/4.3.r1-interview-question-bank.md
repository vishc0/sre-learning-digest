# Interview Question Bank — Operations Roles

Organized by role and competency area. For each question: what it actually assesses, what a 1 / 2 / 3 answer looks like, and follow-up probes to use when the first answer is surface-level.

**Rating scale**:
- **1** — Weak or no evidence of the competency. Candidate cannot articulate the concept or gives a vague, generic answer.
- **2** — Adequate. Developing competency. Knows the concept but cannot demonstrate depth or has never applied it at the level the role requires.
- **3** — Strong. Exceeds expectations for this question. Demonstrates the competency with specific evidence, nuance, and transferable insight.

**How to use this bank**: Pick 4–6 questions per interview. Do not ask all of them — you will run out of time and the candidate will feel like they're taking a test. Choose the questions that probe the competencies you most need evidence for in this specific role. Prepare your rubric before the interview, not during it.

**A note on STAR format**: These questions are written to elicit STAR-style responses (Situation, Task, Action, Result). If a candidate answers without specifics — "I would do X" instead of "I did X" — use the follow-up probe: "Can you give me a specific example of when you did that?"

## Incident Command

*Relevant roles: Senior SRE, Incident Manager, Platform Reliability Manager, IC Team Lead*

---

### Q1: "Walk me through the last P1 incident you commanded. What did you do in the first 5 minutes and why?"

**Assesses**: Structure under pressure, parallel thinking, communication instinct, whether they have an actual first-5-minutes protocol or they just "jump in."

**What a 1 looks like**: Vague timeline. "We all jumped on it together and started troubleshooting." No clear sequence. Cannot articulate why they did things in the order they did. Uses "we" throughout with no clear personal ownership of any decision.

**What a 2 looks like**: Declares the incident, assembles the team, mentions stakeholder notification. Has a rough sequence but can't articulate the priority logic — they do it in the right order but don't know why that order is right.

**What a 3 looks like**: Crisp and specific sequence with rationale: declare severity → assign roles (IC, scribe, comms lead) → open the bridge → notify stakeholders on a parallel track → state the first hypothesis in the first 3 minutes → assign parallel mitigation tracks. Can explain the logic: you notify stakeholders early not because you have answers but because silence costs more credibility than "we're investigating." Can articulate why scribe and comms are separated from the IC role — cognitive load distribution. Does not conflate "troubleshooting" with "incident command."

**Follow-up probe**: "What was the first thing you were sure about — and the first thing that turned out to be wrong — in that first five minutes?"

---

### Q2: "Tell me about a time you had to close a P1 bridge before the root cause was fully known. How did you make that decision?"

**Assesses**: Judgment in ambiguity, risk tolerance calibration, ability to distinguish "service restored" from "problem solved," stakeholder communication discipline.

**What a 1 looks like**: "I wouldn't close the bridge until we knew the root cause" (demonstrates they confuse incident closure with problem closure, which will keep bridges open unnecessarily and burn out responders). OR: "I just closed it when things felt stable" (no defined criteria, pure intuition).

**What a 2 looks like**: Closes the bridge when service metrics return to normal. Flags that the RCA continues in a follow-up meeting. Communicates "service restored" to stakeholders.

**What a 3 looks like**: Has defined criteria for "stable enough to close" — for example: error rate below SLO threshold for 15 consecutive minutes, no new alert firings, lead engineer confirms no active mitigation in progress. Communicates to stakeholders at bridge close with explicit language: "Service is restored. Root cause is not yet confirmed. Problem ticket [#] is open. We will provide a root cause update by [specific time]." Understands the difference between incident closure (restore service) and problem closure (understand why). Has done this before and can name a specific incident.

**Follow-up probe**: "What exact words did you use when you communicated to stakeholders at bridge close? What did you tell them you still didn't know?"

---

### Q3: "How do you handle it when a subject matter expert on the bridge disagrees with your mitigation approach during an active incident?"

**Assesses**: Leadership style under pressure, ego management, speed vs. consensus calibration, ability to hold authority while remaining open to correction.

**What a 1 looks like**: "I make the call because I'm the IC and we don't have time to debate" — authoritarian without process. OR: "I defer to the expert because they know the system better than I do" — abdicates IC authority, invites paralysis when two experts disagree.

**What a 2 looks like**: Hears the disagreement, makes a call after a brief discussion, logs the disagreement for the PIR. Manages the moment but can't articulate how they decide when to switch vs. proceed.

**What a 3 looks like**: Time-boxed structured check: "In 30 seconds, tell me why your approach is better and what the risk is of my current approach." If the argument is compelling and the risk of switching is low, switches and acknowledges the input. If the argument is not compelling or the cost of switching mid-incident is high (e.g., partial rollback already in progress), proceeds and explicitly acknowledges the disagreement: "I hear you, we're proceeding with the current path — please log your concerns in the bridge channel and we'll revisit in the PIR." Always comes back to it in the PIR — does not bury the disagreement. Can articulate the meta-principle: a 5-minute bridge debate during a P1 costs more than a 95%-right decision made in 30 seconds.

**Follow-up probe**: "Have you ever switched your mitigation approach mid-incident based on a disagreement? Walk me through what changed your mind and what the outcome was."

---

### Q4: "What metrics did you use at your last org to know if your incident management program was actually healthy — not just active?"

**Assesses**: Data-driven thinking, SRE maturity, ability to distinguish activity metrics from outcome metrics.

**What a 1 looks like**: Names MTTR and alert volume. May mention "number of incidents." These are activity metrics — they tell you things are happening, not whether the program is working.

**What a 2 looks like**: MTTR, MTTA, recurring incident rate. Understands that trend matters, not just current value. May mention error budget.

**What a 3 looks like**: Treats metrics as a system, not a list. Names: MTTR trend direction (improving, stable, degrading — not just current value), MTTA, recurring incident rate (same root cause recurrence within 30 days), IC coverage breadth (are 2 people doing 80% of IC duty?), PIR completion rate (did we close the PIR within the SLA?), on-call load per engineer (are we burning people out?), alert-to-action ratio (what percentage of alerts require no action — this is your noise ratio), error budget consumption rate. Can explain why each metric matters: recurring incident rate tells you whether problem management is working. IC coverage breadth tells you whether you have a bus factor problem. Alert-to-action ratio tells you whether your monitoring is calibrated or just making noise.

**Follow-up probe**: "If you could only track 3 of those metrics because your tooling was limited, which 3 would you keep and why?"

---

### Q5: "Describe a P1 incident where the initial hypothesis was completely wrong and the team had already taken action based on it. What happened next?"

**Assesses**: Intellectual discipline, ability to pivot without ego investment in the wrong hypothesis, structured thinking under pressure.

**What a 1 looks like**: Either can't remember a specific example (everyone in production has one of these — if they can't remember, they weren't paying attention) or describes the incident as a sequence of things that happened without reflecting on the decision quality.

**What a 2 looks like**: Describes the incident, acknowledges the wrong hypothesis, describes what they eventually found to be the actual cause. May mention that the wrong action didn't make things worse.

**What a 3 looks like**: Names the specific wrong hypothesis and why it seemed right at the time (what data pointed to it). Names the point at which they recognized it was wrong and what data triggered that recognition. Names the actual root cause. Reflects on what it cost: time lost, whether the wrong action extended the incident or caused a secondary issue. Names one thing they added to their diagnostic process or runbook afterward to catch that class of error faster next time. The mark of a mature IC is not that they're always right on the first hypothesis — it's that they know how to recognize when they're wrong and pivot without wasting time.

**Follow-up probe**: "At what point did you make the decision to abandon the first hypothesis? What was the signal?"

## Change and Release Management

*Relevant roles: Change Manager, Release Engineer, Change & Release Management Manager*

---

### Q6: "Describe a change that went wrong in production. Walk me through what happened during the rollback and what specifically changed about your change process afterward."

**Assesses**: Learning from failure, process improvement instinct, honesty about failures (candidates who claim all their changes succeed either haven't done enough changes or aren't telling the truth).

**What a 1 looks like**: "We caught it quickly and rolled back" with no detail about what the failure was, how long the rollback took, what the rollback plan was, or what changed afterward. Vague lessons learned: "we reviewed our process."

**What a 2 looks like**: Describes the change and the failure mode. Mentions what the PIR found. Names one thing that changed about the process. May not be able to describe whether the process change prevented a recurrence.

**What a 3 looks like**: Describes the change in enough detail that you understand what it was intended to do. Names the failure mode specifically (e.g., "the deployment script didn't handle the case where an existing connection pool wasn't drained before the new version started, causing a connection exhaustion on the new nodes for the first 4 minutes after deploy"). Describes the rollback: was there a rollback plan before the change? How long did it take? Was the rollback time within the expected window? Names 2–3 specific process changes made afterward — and can say whether a subsequent similar change tested those improvements.

**Follow-up probe**: "How long did the rollback take? Was that within your expected rollback window? What would have happened if the rollback had taken twice as long?"

---

### Q7: "What's the difference between a risk assessment and a process checklist in your CAB process, and why does that difference matter?"

**Assesses**: Depth of change governance understanding. Candidates who've run CAB at scale understand this distinction. Candidates who've only followed a process do not.

**What a 1 looks like**: "They're basically the same thing — the checklist is how we verify the risk assessment." Or: defines the checklist correctly but treats risk assessment as just a more thorough checklist.

**What a 2 looks like**: Understands that checklists are compliance verification and risk assessment is judgment. Can distinguish the two conceptually.

**What a 3 looks like**: Articulates the fundamental difference: a checklist verifies that known risk categories have been addressed — it's a systematic scan against a predefined list. A risk assessment surfaces unknown or novel risks that no checklist covers — it requires judgment, experience, and context about what's different about this change. The best CABs use both: the checklist handles standard categories (rollback plan exists, testing done, notification sent, change window approved), while the open-ended risk discussion handles: "What's novel or unusual about this change that our checklist doesn't account for?" Can name a specific instance where a change passed all checklist items but a risk assessment discussion caught something the checklist missed.

**Follow-up probe**: "Have you ever approved a change that passed your checklist but still failed in production? What did the checklist miss? Did you update the checklist afterward or was it something that wouldn't belong on a checklist?"

---

### Q8: "Explain DORA metrics to a VP who isn't technical and convince them why these metrics should matter to the business."

**Assesses**: Communication across audiences, business alignment, ability to translate technical quality signals into business outcomes.

**What a 1 looks like**: Gives a technical explanation of what DORA is. Uses jargon: "deploy frequency is how often we push to prod." Doesn't connect to business outcomes. The VP would walk away informed but not convinced.

**What a 2 looks like**: Translates each metric into business terms. Makes the connection between deploy frequency and time to market. Mentions change failure rate as a cost signal.

**What a 3 looks like**: Opens with the business problem, not the metrics: "There are four things that determine whether engineering is delivering value at the speed the business needs, and whether it's doing so reliably. DORA gives us a way to measure all four." Then translates each: Deploy frequency = how quickly we can respond to what customers need. Lead time for changes = how long from a decision to a customer-visible outcome. Change failure rate = how often we create new problems when we try to improve things. MTTR = how quickly we recover when something breaks. Then connects to business outcomes: "High DORA = more features delivered per quarter, fewer incidents per release, faster recovery when things go wrong — which means lower revenue impact per incident." Can name a dollar value for MTTR in their context: "At our scale, a P1 hour cost us approximately $X in direct transaction loss, plus engineer time, plus customer trust." Does not claim DORA is perfect — acknowledges gaming risk and notes that these metrics work as a system, not individually.

**Follow-up probe**: "At your last company, which DORA band were you in for each metric? What was the single biggest structural blocker to moving up one band in your weakest metric?"

---

### Q9: "How do you handle a change request that is technically compliant with your process but feels risky in a way you can't quantify yet?"

**Assesses**: Judgment and confidence to act on intuition when data is incomplete, constructive friction, ability to communicate risk without blocking progress.

**What a 1 looks like**: "I approve it because it passed the process." (Process compliance without judgment — creates a false sense of safety.) OR: "I reject it because it doesn't feel right." (Blocks without communicating actionably — creates adversarial relationship with change requestors.)

**What a 2 looks like**: Asks clarifying questions in CAB to surface the concern. May request a delay to get more information.

**What a 3 looks like**: Has a structured approach to unquantifiable risk: calls it out explicitly in CAB — "I can't point to a specific checklist failure, but something about this change concerns me. Here's what I'm uncertain about: [specific question]. Before I approve, I want to understand [specific thing]." Treats their intuition as a hypothesis to test, not a veto. Gives the change requestor a specific action: "Get me the answer to [question] and I'll approve this in 24 hours." Documents the concern in the change record regardless of the outcome. If the answer is satisfactory, approves and notes what resolved the concern. If the answer is unsatisfactory or cannot be provided, has the confidence to hold the change with a clear explanation of why and what would change the decision.

**Follow-up probe**: "Has your intuition ever been wrong in this kind of situation — where you slowed down a change that turned out to be fine? How did you handle the relationship with the team whose change you delayed?"

## RCA and Problem Management

*Relevant roles: Problem Analyst, Senior SRE, Incident Manager, Platform Reliability Manager*

---

### Q10: "Walk me through how you would facilitate a PIR for a P1 that the team is exhausted and burned out from — the incident ran 14 hours and ended at 3am."

**Assesses**: Facilitation skill, psychological safety, blameless culture in practice (not just in principle), pragmatic process discipline.

**What a 1 looks like**: "I'd schedule a meeting and we'd go through what happened." No acknowledgment of the human condition — a team that just ran a 14-hour P1 at 3am is not in a condition to do good PIR analysis the next morning. No process adaptation.

**What a 2 looks like**: Mentions blameless culture. Focuses on what happened vs. who caused it. May delay the PIR by a day. Still structures it as a single synchronous meeting.

**What a 3 looks like**: Specific facilitation adaptations for a burned-out team: (1) Pre-PIR async survey sent within 24 hours of bridge close — people contribute timeline events, contributing factors, and questions while memory is fresh, without requiring a meeting; (2) Give the team 48 hours before the PIR meeting — not more, because memory degrades and context scatters; (3) Structure the PIR meeting around the objective timeline first (facts only, no interpretation), then contributing factors (what conditions made this failure possible? — not who caused it), then root causes, then action items; (4) Separate "contributing factors" from "root causes" explicitly — the team will conflate them otherwise; (5) Circulate a draft of the PIR document before the meeting so nobody is surprised by the narrative; (6) End with action items that are specific (what), assigned (who), and time-bounded (when). The facilitator's job is to protect the psychological safety of the room while ensuring the process produces something actionable.

**Follow-up probe**: "What do you do specifically when someone in the PIR starts attributing the incident to an individual — 'this happened because [person] made a mistake'?"

---

### Q11: "How do you distinguish a problem from an incident, and why does that distinction matter operationally?"

**Assesses**: ITIL/ITSM conceptual clarity, process discipline, understanding of why problem management exists as a separate function.

**What a 1 looks like**: "Incidents are handled in real-time, problems are handled after." Partially correct but misses the conceptual precision that makes the distinction operationally useful.

**What a 2 looks like**: Incident = active disruption to service. Problem = underlying cause under investigation. Can describe the sequence: incident first, problem investigation after. May know what KEDB is.

**What a 3 looks like**: Precise and operational: an incident is a disruption to service — the symptom. A problem is the unknown underlying cause — what you don't yet know about why the incident happened. You can resolve an incident (restore service) while the problem is still open — the root cause is unknown. You can have a problem without a current incident: proactive problem management means identifying failure-likely conditions in monitoring or in a new deployment before they cause an incident. The Known Error Database (KEDB) is the set of problems where the root cause is known but a permanent fix hasn't been deployed yet — so when the incident recurs, the IC has a documented workaround. Can explain why keeping these separate operationally matters: if the incident manager also owns problem closure, problem closure loses to incident response every week. This is the structural argument for separating Incident Management from Problem Management as teams scale.

**Follow-up probe**: "At your last org, what happened to problem records after the incident was closed? Who owned them and what was the average time to close a problem record? What happened to ones that were never closed?"

---

### Q12: "Describe a recurring incident — same root cause happening more than once. What was the failure in the problem management process, and what did you do about it?"

**Assesses**: Problem management effectiveness in practice, ability to diagnose process failures not just technical failures, accountability for systemic improvement.

**What a 1 looks like**: "We fixed it permanently the second time." Does not address why it recurred, does not address the process failure.

**What a 2 looks like**: Describes the incident, acknowledges the recurrence, names the corrective action. May not address why the first corrective action didn't stick.

**What a 3 looks like**: Describes the recurrence with specificity: what was the root cause the first time? What corrective action was taken? Why did it recur — was the corrective action implemented incorrectly, incompletely, or in only one of several affected instances? Then names the process failure: was the problem record closed before the fix was deployed? Was the fix only applied to one environment? Was the KEDB entry not propagated to ICs in other regions? Then describes the systemic fix: what changed in the problem management process to prevent the same category of failure from recurring?

**Follow-up probe**: "After the second occurrence, did you change how you close problem records — what criteria are required before a problem can be marked resolved? What does 'done' mean now that it didn't mean before?"

## Architecture and Platform

*Relevant roles: Platform Architect, Architecture & Transformation Lead, Senior SRE*

---

### Q13: "Walk me through an architectural decision you made. How did you document it, and have you ever revisited it and changed your mind?"

**Assesses**: ADR discipline, systematic decision-making, intellectual humility, whether they treat architecture decisions as reversible hypotheses or irreversible pronouncements.

**What a 1 looks like**: "We decided to use [technology] and it worked out well." No mention of alternatives considered, trade-offs evaluated, or documentation. Cannot name what would have led to a different decision.

**What a 2 looks like**: Describes the decision, mentions that trade-offs were considered, may have written some documentation. May not have revisited the decision.

**What a 3 looks like**: Can describe all five components of a good ADR: (1) Context — why was a decision needed at this moment? (2) Options considered — at least 3, not just "we could do A or B"; (3) Trade-offs evaluated for each option — not just "A is better" but specific: latency, operational complexity, team skill match, vendor risk, reversibility; (4) Decision and rationale — specifically why this option over the others in this context; (5) Consequences — what becomes easier, harder, or different because of this decision. Then — the mark of maturity — describes revisiting the decision 12+ months later: what did they find? Did the context change (the thing that made this decision right may no longer be true)? Would they make the same decision today? Bonus: names one architectural decision they'd make differently with hindsight and what they learned from the delta between the original context and what actually happened.

**Follow-up probe**: "How do you handle it when a junior engineer challenges an ADR you wrote? How do you distinguish 'this junior engineer has spotted something I missed' from 'this junior engineer doesn't yet have the context to understand the trade-offs'?"

---

### Q14: "How would you assess whether a 10-year-old application should be modernized, replaced with something new, or kept running as-is?"

**Assesses**: Portfolio management thinking, pragmatism, ability to resist both "rewrite everything" bias and "if it ain't broke" bias.

**What a 1 looks like**: "It depends." Without a framework. Restates the question as the answer.

**What a 2 looks like**: Mentions business value, technical debt, and cost as factors. May have a rough framework but can't apply it to a specific scenario or walk through the decision.

**What a 3 looks like**: Structured evaluation across four dimensions: (1) Business value — is this application still strategic? What does it enable? What is the cost of it being unavailable for 1 hour? 1 day? (2) Technical health — what's the bus factor (how many people understand this system)? Is it on a supported stack? What's the annual maintenance cost? Is it accruing technical debt at a rate that's compounding? (3) Risk profile — what's the failure mode? Is it on EOL infrastructure? Is it a compliance risk? (4) Economics — what's the cost to keep vs. cost to change? What's the TCO over 3 years for each path? Then applies a decision matrix: keep-as-is if business value is high and technical health is acceptable; modernize if business value is high and technical health is degrading; replace if a better tool exists and migration cost over 3 years is lower than maintenance cost; decommission if business value has dropped to low and another system covers the use case.

**Follow-up probe**: "Have you ever recommended decommissioning an application that people in the business still emotionally valued even though it was no longer serving its original purpose? What resistance did you face and how did you navigate it?"

---

### Q15: "How do you prevent architecture review from becoming a bottleneck? Describe how your ARB or design review process worked at your best previous organization."

**Assesses**: Process design thinking, ability to distinguish governance from bureaucracy, understanding that architecture review should be an accelerant not a blocker.

**What a 1 looks like**: "We had a weekly meeting where we reviewed designs." No distinction between what required review and what didn't. No discussion of throughput or turnaround time.

**What a 2 looks like**: Describes tiered review (small changes don't require full ARB). Mentions turnaround time targets. Can explain the criteria for what triggers a review.

**What a 3 looks like**: Describes a deliberate design to minimize bottleneck risk: (1) Clear tiering — not all changes require ARB review; the criteria for what triggers a review are documented and unambiguous; (2) Self-service for standard patterns — if a team is following an established, documented architecture pattern, they submit an ADR noting the pattern and it auto-approves without ARB; (3) SLA for ARB review — disposition every submission within [X] business days; (4) ADR-first culture — teams are expected to write the ADR before the ARB meeting, not during it; (5) Async review option — for non-controversial decisions, asynchronous review with a comment-and-approve mechanism rather than a synchronous meeting; (6) Architects embedded in teams — the best architecture review happens upstream, when an architect is part of the design conversation, not downstream as a gate. Measures ARB throughput: average days from submission to disposition, percentage of submissions returned for revision vs. approved on first submission.

**Follow-up probe**: "What happens in your ARB process when a team disagrees with the ARB's decision? What's the escalation path and who has the final call?"

## Performance Engineering

*Relevant roles: Performance Engineer, Senior SRE — Reliability, Platform Architect*

---

### Q16: "What's the difference between a load test, a soak test, a stress test, and a spike test — and when would you run each?"

**Assesses**: Performance engineering depth and vocabulary precision.

**What a 1 looks like**: "Load test is high users, soak test is longer running." Cannot distinguish stress from load, or spike from stress.

**What a 2 looks like**: Knows load and soak. Can define stress. May not know spike or may conflate it with load.

**What a 3 looks like**: Precise definitions and specific use cases:
- **Load test**: Validates that the system handles expected peak concurrency without degradation. Run: before launch and before any change touching the critical path. Tells you: does the system perform acceptably at the traffic level we expect on our busiest day?
- **Soak test**: Validates behavior under sustained load over time. Run: before launching any JVM-based service or any change touching connection management, caching, or thread management. Tells you: what degrades when the system runs hard for 4–8 hours? Catches memory leaks, connection pool exhaustion, GC pressure accumulation, file handle leaks.
- **Stress test**: Finds the breaking point. Run: when you need to know where your headroom ends. Apply increasing load until the system fails, then analyze the failure mode. Tells you: at what concurrency does the system fail, and how does it fail (graceful degradation vs. hard crash)?
- **Spike test**: Simulates sudden traffic bursts — e.g., a marketing campaign goes live and traffic triples in 60 seconds. Tests autoscaling response time, circuit breaker behavior, and whether the system can absorb the shock without cascading failure. Tells you: how does the system behave when traffic doesn't ramp gradually?

**Follow-up probe**: "Have you ever run a soak test that found something a load test missed? What was it and how long into the soak test did it surface?"

---

### Q17: "How do you read a JVM heap dump? Walk me through your process when you receive one."

**Assesses**: JVM performance debugging depth. This is a depth-probe question — not everyone on an operations team needs this, but a Performance Engineer or Senior SRE dealing with JVM services (Spring Boot, TIBCO EMS, Kafka brokers, etc.) should have this.

**What a 1 looks like**: "I'd send it to the dev team." May acknowledge they'd use a tool like MAT without knowing what to look at.

**What a 2 looks like**: Knows what a heap dump is and can use Eclipse MAT or VisualVM to open it. Can navigate to the histogram. Knows they're looking for unexpectedly large objects but can't describe the diagnostic flow in detail.

**What a 3 looks like**: Structured diagnostic flow: (1) Start with the histogram — what object types are consuming the most memory by retained heap? Look for anything where instance count × average size is disproportionate relative to what the application should be doing. (2) Check shallow heap vs. retained heap for suspicious objects — shallow heap is the memory the object itself uses; retained heap is everything that would be freed if this object were garbage collected. A small object with enormous retained heap is a leak anchor. (3) Navigate to the dominator tree — identifies the objects holding references that are preventing garbage collection. The dominator tree shows you the chain from GC root to the object that should have been collected. (4) Common findings they've seen: String or byte array accumulation (encoding or serialization issue), event listener leaks (listeners registered but never deregistered), cache entries not expiring (TTL misconfiguration or cache invalidation bug), thread-local storage accumulation in thread pools. Can describe a specific real heap dump finding from their own experience.

**Follow-up probe**: "What's the difference between a memory leak and a memory pressure problem? Have you seen both? How did the symptoms differ before you pulled the heap dump?"

### Q18: "Describe how you would set up performance monitoring for a new service that processes messages from a Kafka topic. What metrics would you track and what alerts would you set?"

**Assesses**: Observability design thinking for event-driven architectures. Directly relevant at T-Mobile scale with Kafka and RabbitMQ.

**What a 1 looks like**: "I'd monitor CPU and memory." Generic infrastructure metrics without any event-processing context.

**What a 2 looks like**: Mentions consumer lag. May mention throughput. Knows that lag is the leading indicator of a consumer falling behind.

**What a 3 looks like**: Full observability stack for a Kafka consumer:
- **Consumer lag** (partition-level, not just topic-level): The leading indicator. Alert: lag growing for 5+ consecutive minutes on any partition. Red alert: lag exceeding [X messages] where X is derived from the SLA for message processing latency.
- **Message processing rate**: Messages consumed per second per consumer instance. Alert: rate drops below baseline by 20%+ — indicates processing slowdown before lag becomes visible.
- **Processing time per message**: P50, P95, P99. Alert: P99 processing time exceeds [threshold] — indicates a slow processing path or a specific message type that's expensive.
- **Consumer group rebalance events**: Frequent rebalances indicate consumer instability (crashes, network issues, processing timeouts). Alert: more than 2 rebalance events in 10 minutes.
- **Dead letter queue (DLQ) accumulation**: Messages routed to DLQ because processing failed. Alert: any DLQ growth — DLQ messages are data loss in slow motion.
- **Error rate by error type**: Deserialization errors, business logic exceptions, downstream dependency timeouts — track separately to distinguish application errors from infrastructure errors.
- **Offset commit latency**: How long between consuming and committing the offset. High commit latency means your at-least-once guarantee is being stretched — in a crash, you'll reprocess more messages.

**Follow-up probe**: "How do you test this monitoring setup before the service goes to production? How do you know your alert thresholds are right?"

## Appendix: General Interview Guidance

### Calibration notes for interviewers

**On "would you" answers**: Any answer that describes what the candidate would do hypothetically (vs. what they did) scores no higher than a 2. The question bank is designed for past-tense answers. If a candidate consistently gives future-tense answers, use the probe: "Can you give me a specific example of when you actually did that?"

**On STAR format**: The goal is not to hear every STAR element in sequence. The goal is observable evidence that the competency has been applied in a real context. A candidate who gives a crisp 2-minute answer with a real situation and a clear outcome is stronger than a candidate who delivers a 5-minute formulaic STAR with no specificity.

**On calibrating 3s**: A 3 should be genuinely exceptional for this question. If you're giving most candidates a 3, recalibrate. A 3 means: this candidate has demonstrated a level of competency that would satisfy a strong performer at the target level for this role. A 2 means they're developing and would need support to perform this at level. A 1 means a real gap that should influence your hire/no-hire recommendation.

**On follow-up probes**: Use them. The first answer is often the rehearsed version. The follow-up probe reveals whether there's depth behind the surface response or whether the candidate has memorized good answers without the experience to back them up.

**On comparing candidates**: Score independently, then compare. Do not discuss scores with other interviewers before the debrief — you will anchor to the first person's opinion and lose the value of independent assessment.

### Preparing rubrics before the interview

For each question you select, write down before the interview:
1. What a 3 looks like for this specific role and level (not the generic definition above — tailor it to your context)
2. The one thing you most want to hear in a strong answer
3. The follow-up probe you'll use if the answer is surface-level

Do not read from the rubric during the interview. Use it to calibrate your scores after.
