# Chapter 5: Competency Levels — SRE Individual Contributor and Management Tracks

This chapter defines the competency framework for the Operations organization. It is the authoritative reference for promotion decisions, compensation banding, performance reviews, and role assignments. Every level is defined by what the engineer can do independently — not what they have been exposed to or trained on.

Each level definition answers three questions: What can this person do without supervision? What are they not yet trusted to do alone? How do you verify they are actually ready to advance?

---

## Part A: SRE Individual Contributor Track

### Level 1 — Associate SRE

**Role summary**: An Associate SRE is a developing engineer learning the operational environment. They execute defined procedures and escalate when procedures do not resolve the issue. They do not improvise.

**What they can do independently:**

- Follow a runbook to resolution for all documented failure modes in their assigned domain
- Acknowledge an alert, confirm severity, and route the incident to the correct team within 15 minutes
- Query Splunk dashboards and Datadog or Grafana panels for predefined metrics — they know which dashboard to open for which symptom, and can read the output
- Execute standard operational tasks: restart a pod via kubectl, drain a node, clear a dead-letter queue in RabbitMQ using documented procedure
- Open, update, and close P3 and P4 incident tickets with accurate timeline and impact data
- Escalate to a Senior SRE within 10 minutes when runbook steps do not resolve the issue — this is a required behavior, not optional

**What they cannot yet do:**

- Serve as Incident Commander for any incident, including P4
- Write a runbook from scratch — they can fill in gaps on an existing runbook under review
- Make architectural or configuration decisions outside documented procedures
- Facilitate a post-incident review or contribute as primary author on an RCA
- Interpret a service dependency map without guidance
- Tune or modify alert thresholds

**How you verify readiness — proof points required before promotion conversation begins:**

- Has resolved 20 or more P3 or P4 incidents using runbooks, without requiring escalation mid-execution (escalating within the first 10 minutes when runbook fails does not disqualify — that is correct behavior; calling for help because they didn't try is different)
- Has written at least one runbook — even a simple three-step restart procedure — reviewed and approved by a Senior SRE
- Can name the top 10 services in their domain, describe what each does, and identify the downstream service that breaks if each one fails
- Has completed on-call shadowing for at least one full rotation

**Promotion criteria to Level 2:**

- Minimum 18 months in role; can accelerate to 12 months if all proof points above are met and the manager and skip-level both agree
- Can IC a P4 incident solo — opens the bridge, drives to resolution, closes the ticket without being coached
- No "needs improvement" rating in any competency dimension in the last two review cycles
- Manager must document which proof points are met and which are met with exceptions

### Level 2 — SRE

**Role summary**: An SRE owns their domain's operational health at the P3/P4 level. They do not need to be told what to do in a known failure scenario — they act, then communicate. They begin building institutional knowledge, not just consuming it.

**What they can do independently:**

- Serve as Incident Commander for P3 and P4 incidents — open the bridge, assign actions, drive to resolution, file the summary
- Write runbooks for known failure modes in their assigned domain, including dependency context and rollback steps
- Read and interpret service dependency maps for their assigned domain — can explain upstream and downstream blast radius of a given failure
- Perform a basic RCA using 5 Whys methodology and produce a written output that holds up in a PIR meeting
- Monitor their own services using Splunk and Datadog: create saved searches, adjust panel thresholds, suppress noise for known maintenance windows
- Participate actively in on-call rotation as a primary responder (not shadow)
- Contribute meaningfully in CAB and change review meetings — can assess risk of a change in their domain

**What they cannot yet do:**

- IC a P1 or P2 incident without a Senior SRE or Manager on the bridge
- Design monitoring from scratch for a net-new service (they can extend existing monitoring)
- Lead a complex RCA that requires FMEA, fault tree, or Fishbone analysis
- Approve RFCs or represent their team at CAB as the accountable approver
- Mentor an L1 without guidance from a Senior SRE on approach

**How you verify readiness — proof points required before promotion conversation begins:**

- Has IC'd 10 or more P3 incidents solo, with call recordings or post-call notes that demonstrate command of the bridge
- Has written 5 or more runbooks, at least 2 of which were authored from scratch (not extended from existing)
- Has completed one 5 Whys RCA as primary author — published to the team's knowledge base and not materially revised by a senior reviewer
- Is a functioning member of the on-call rotation with no rotation gaps due to capability concerns in the trailing 6 months

**Promotion criteria to Level 3:**

- Minimum 24 months in L2; can accelerate to 18 months if all proof points are met
- Has IC'd at least one P2 incident with a Senior SRE in an advisory role (not driving) — the L2 must have been unambiguously in command
- Has actively mentored at least one L1 engineer — held regular 1:1s, reviewed their runbook, coached them through an incident
- Manager documents specific incidents and RCAs as evidence; this is not a checkbox review

### Level 3 — Senior SRE

**Role summary**: A Senior SRE is the operational backbone of the team. They handle the hardest incidents without escalation, build the knowledge that L1/L2 engineers execute, and extend the monitoring and reliability posture of the domain. They are trusted to represent the team's technical position in most internal forums.

**What they can do independently:**

- IC a P1 incident independently, including multi-service cascades — RabbitMQ queue storms, TIBCO EMS broker failures affecting downstream Kafka consumers, EKS node pool exhaustion affecting multiple namespaces
- Design and implement monitoring from scratch for a new service or integration: define SLIs, write Splunk alerts, build Grafana dashboards, establish baseline thresholds, document runbook hooks
- Facilitate a complex RCA using FMEA, Fishbone, or fault tree analysis as the primary facilitator — produces a written output that meets the organization's PIR standard without revision
- Review and approve RFCs submitted for changes in their domain — accountable approver at the domain level
- Lead the on-call rotation as primary coordinator: own schedule health, resolve coverage gaps, escalate rotation load concerns before they become burnout
- Mentor L1 and L2 engineers with a structured approach: hold regular 1:1s, track their proof points, provide written feedback for performance reviews
- Represent the team's technical position in domain-level design reviews and cross-team working groups

**What they cannot yet do:**

- Define reliability patterns, SLO frameworks, or error budget policy for the whole organization — they can propose; they cannot ratify
- Represent technical risk at VP or executive level as the sole voice from Operations — they support the Director or Staff SRE in those forums
- Set organizational standards in areas outside their domain without Director sponsorship
- Approve headcount, budget, or tooling at the organizational level

**How you verify readiness — proof points required before promotion conversation begins:**

- Has IC'd at least 3 P1 incidents and can reference each one by incident number, with documented timelines showing command of the bridge
- Has designed monitoring for 2 or more new services or integrations — not extended existing dashboards but built from SLI definition to alert runbook
- Has facilitated at least one FMEA or fault tree RCA and produced a PIR document that was accepted without material revision
- Has delivered at least one lunch-and-learn or internal training session — not a brown-bag share, but a structured knowledge transfer with materials

**Promotion criteria to Level 4 (Staff SRE):**

- Exceptional delivery on all proof points above — not just checked, but outstanding
- Has driven a reliability improvement that produced a measurable reduction in incident rate or MTTR: must be quantified and attributable to their specific work
- Demonstrated org-wide impact: other teams have adopted a pattern, runbook, standard, or tool that this engineer originated
- This promotion requires Director and VP concurrence — it is not solely a manager decision

---

### Level 4 — Staff / Principal SRE

**Role summary**: A Staff SRE operates at organizational scope. They do not own a domain — they own the reliability posture of the entire platform. Their work sets the floor that every domain team builds on. They are a technical authority in executive forums, not just in engineering reviews.

**What they can do independently:**

- Design reliability patterns and standards for the entire organization: SLO framework, error budget policy, toil classification standard, on-call health model
- Represent technical risk to VP and C-suite audiences — delivers clear risk narratives with business impact framing, not raw technical metrics
- Define SLO frameworks and error budget policy across all tiers of service: sets the methodology, not just the targets
- Lead a major reliability transformation project spanning multiple months and multiple teams — owns the plan, drives the execution, reports progress to Director and VP
- Own the SRE career ladder and mentorship program at the organizational level: defines the competency framework (this document), coaches managers on how to apply it, identifies high-potential engineers
- Evaluate and select tooling at organizational scope — brings a structured evaluation with criteria and trade-offs, not a personal preference
- Identify systemic reliability risks that no individual domain team can see because each team only sees their slice

**Key differentiators from Level 3 — this is where promotion panels focus:**

| Dimension | Level 3 — Senior SRE | Level 4 — Staff SRE |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Scope | Domain (one team's services) | Organization (all services, all teams) |
| Audience | Team leads, peer engineers, domain stakeholders | VP, Director, C-suite, external partners |
| Direction | Executes and refines existing standards | Sets standards; others execute them |
| Mentorship | Coaches 1–2 individuals | Coaches managers; shapes the program |
| Impact horizon | 30–90 days (incident, RCA, project) | 6–24 months (platform transformation, reliability model) |
| Failure mode | Misses the pattern across incidents | Sets a wrong standard that the whole org follows |

A Staff SRE who is doing excellent L3 work should not be promoted. The distinction is scope and direction, not proficiency.

## Part B: SRE Management Track

The management track is parallel to — not superior to — the IC track. An L4 Staff SRE and an Engineering Manager are peers. A Principal SRE and a Director are peers. Neither track is the "winner" — they serve different functions and require genuinely different strengths.

### Associate Manager / Team Lead

**Typical entry**: Promoted from L3 Senior SRE. This is a working manager role — they still carry an operational load, but they have formal accountability for 3–5 direct reports.

**What this role owns:**
- Runs weekly 1:1s with every direct report — not optional, not delegated
- Conducts quarterly competency reviews using the framework in Part A
- Owns the team's on-call rotation health: ensures coverage, tracks load, surfaces burnout risk before it is a crisis
- Triages first-line people issues: performance concerns, interpersonal friction, disengagement — escalates to their manager before taking formal action
- Contributes to hiring panels but does not own hiring decisions

**What this role does not yet own:**
- Final hiring or separation decisions — requires manager concurrence
- Team OKRs — proposed by this role, set with manager input, approved by Director
- Budget or tooling decisions at team level

**Signal that an L3 IC is ready for this transition:**
The individual is actively mentoring 2 or more people and seeking out that responsibility rather than having it assigned. They are comfortable not knowing the answer and saying so. They derive satisfaction from other people's growth, not only from their own incident command or technical output. They do not retreat to IC work when the people work gets difficult.

Not everyone should manage. An L3 who is exceptional at technical work and has no interest in people accountability should be encouraged toward Staff SRE, not pushed into management because "that's what comes next."

---

### Engineering Manager (L6)

**Scope**: 5–10 direct reports. Owns a domain or functional area end-to-end.

**What this role owns:**
- Domain delivery, reliability metrics, and team health — all three, not just the technical metrics
- Hiring into and managing out of the team, with Director visibility and approval for terminations
- Weekly domain review: runs the meeting, owns the agenda, escalates blockers to Director
- Cross-functional representation: speaks for the team to peer Engineering Managers, Product partners, and business stakeholders
- Performance reviews and compensation recommendations for all direct reports

**What this role does not yet own:**
- Organizational OKRs or strategy — provides input, does not set
- Headcount plan without Director concurrence
- Tooling decisions that affect other domains

---

### Senior Engineering Manager (L6+)

**Scope**: Manages other managers, or manages a larger IC team of 10–20 people with significant cross-team dependencies.

**What this role owns:**
- A full functional domain with dependencies on other teams — owns the accountability chain across those dependencies
- Participates in Director-level planning meetings as a contributing voice, not just a status reporter
- Drives headcount and budget forecasting for their domain; presents recommendations to Director
- Coaches their managers on people leadership, not just technical delivery

---

### Director of Operations (L8)

**Scope**: Manages 3–5 Senior Managers. Owns the entire operations function.

**What this role owns:**
- Organizational OKRs: sets them, owns the outcome, reports progress to VP
- Headcount plan and hiring strategy for the whole function
- Tooling strategy and budget envelope — makes or approves all significant tooling decisions
- Represents Operations at executive level: VP reviews, board-level operational risk, customer escalations
- Holds accountability when the organization fails — not in the sense of blame, but in the sense of "the Director is the one who explains what went wrong and presents the plan to prevent recurrence"

## Part C: Career Path Visualization

### The Two Paths

Both paths begin at Associate SRE. The fork happens — if it happens at all — at Senior SRE.

```
INDIVIDUAL CONTRIBUTOR PATH
----------------------------
Associate SRE        (0–2 years)
    ↓
SRE                  (2–4 years)
    ↓
Senior SRE           (4–7 years)
    ↓
Staff SRE            (7–12 years)
    ↓
Principal SRE        (12+ years, by exception)
    ↓
Distinguished Engineer  (rare; org-defining impact)

MANAGEMENT PATH
---------------
Associate SRE        (0–2 years)
    ↓
SRE                  (2–4 years)
    ↓
Senior SRE           (4–7 years)
    ↓ [fork decision]
Team Lead            (6–8 years)
    ↓
Engineering Manager  (8–12 years)
    ↓
Senior Manager       (12–16 years)
    ↓
Director             (16+ years)
```

### Timeline Notes

These timelines are minimums and medians — not guarantees. An engineer who reaches all proof points in 18 months should not wait for the clock. An engineer who has been in role for 36 months without meeting proof points should be in a structured development plan, not given a time-based promotion.

The timelines compress for exceptional performers and stretch for engineers who are growing steadily but not at pace. A manager who is not having this conversation explicitly with every engineer at every level is creating ambiguity that will eventually become an attrition or legal risk.

### What Triggers the Management Track Decision

The fork to management is not a reward for technical excellence. It is a role change that requires a different set of strengths. You should have this conversation with an engineer when you observe three or more of the following consistently:

- They are actively mentoring junior engineers without being asked, and the junior engineers are improving measurably
- They are asking about team health, not just incident metrics — "how is the team holding up" rather than "what was the MTTR"
- They are navigating ambiguous people situations (interpersonal friction, unclear ownership, scope disputes) with maturity rather than avoiding them
- They express frustration that the team's processes or structure are slowing things down — and they have ideas for how to fix the structure, not just the immediate problem
- They are comfortable being the person who says "I don't know, let's figure it out together" rather than needing to have the technical answer

If an engineer is technically exceptional but scores low on any of these dimensions, the management track is likely wrong for them. Routing a great IC into management because "there's no other path" is how you lose both a great IC and produce a mediocre manager.

### Lateral Moves Between Tracks

Lateral movement between IC and management tracks is supported and encouraged at the right transition points.

**Staff SRE → Engineering Manager**: A Staff SRE who decides to move into management enters at the L6 Engineering Manager level — not as a Team Lead. They bring organizational technical credibility that makes them effective immediately in the management role. The adjustment is in the skills they use daily, not in compensation or seniority.

**Engineering Manager → Senior SRE**: A manager who decides to return to IC work enters at the Senior SRE level. If they have kept their technical skills current, this transition is clean. If they have been in management for more than 3 years without hands-on technical work, they may need a 6-month re-entry period with explicit goals before resuming at full Senior SRE scope.

**Senior Manager → Principal SRE**: Rare, but possible. Requires an honest skills assessment. Senior managers who have maintained deep technical involvement through their management years can make this transition. Those who have moved fully into organizational leadership will find the reentry difficult and should have that conversation honestly before committing.

Lateral moves do not reset compensation. They may adjust it based on the market rate for the new role, but they are not treated as demotions unless the engineer is moving to a materially lower scope.

### The Management Track Is Not a Promotion

This framing must be explicit in how you talk about careers on your team. Becoming a manager is a role change — not a promotion from Senior SRE. A Senior SRE and a Team Lead are peers. A Staff SRE and an Engineering Manager are peers. A Principal SRE and a Director are peers.

If your culture treats management as the "real" advancement, you will lose your best ICs to management roles they do not want and are not suited for, and you will lose your best managers to imposter syndrome because they feel like they "gave up" their technical identity.

Make this explicit in every career conversation. Make it explicit in how you compensate. Make it explicit in how you staff key technical work.
