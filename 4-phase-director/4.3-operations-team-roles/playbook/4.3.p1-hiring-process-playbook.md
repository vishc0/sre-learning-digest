# Hiring Process Playbook — Operations Roles

> **Audience**: Director of Operations and domain managers conducting hiring for SRE, Platform Engineering, Problem Analyst, or Change Management roles.
> **Purpose**: End-to-end hiring process from job description finalization through Day 1 preparation. Follow the stages in order. Do not shortcut Stage 5 (behavioral interview) or Stage 6 (domain scenario) — these are where candidates who look strong on paper reveal gaps.

---

## Stage 1 — Job Description Finalization

**Start with the job-description-template.md** from the `templates/` directory. Do not write a JD from scratch — the template ensures consistency in format and forces you to make explicit decisions about what this role actually requires.

**Fill in each section with specifics:**

- **Title and level**: Agree on the level with your HR partner before posting. Under-leveling to fit a budget creates trust problems at offer time. If the work is Staff-level, post for Staff — do not post for Senior and plan to negotiate up.
- **Mission**: One sentence. What does this role exist to accomplish? Not "you'll join a team that cares about reliability" but "you own the reliability posture of our RabbitMQ and Kafka messaging infrastructure, which processes X transactions per day for 5 internal platform teams."
- **What you'll own**: Specific domains, systems, and responsibilities. If you're not sure what this person will own on Day 1, you're not ready to post the JD.
- **Day-to-day**: What does a normal week look like? On-call rotation frequency, standing meetings, primary work type (incident response, automation, infra management). If the role is 80% reactive, say so — candidates who discover this after they start will leave.
- **Must-have competencies**: 3–5 things that are non-negotiable. If a candidate lacks these, they don't advance regardless of other strengths. Be specific: not "experience with monitoring tools" but "has built or maintained Splunk dashboards for distributed service monitoring at scale" or "has operated a Kubernetes cluster in production with > 50 nodes."
- **Nice-to-have**: Things that would differentiate a strong candidate from a great one. These are tie-breakers, not requirements.

**Remove aspirational JD language that isn't currently true.** If your org has not yet implemented a blameless postmortem culture, do not put "you'll thrive in a blameless culture" in the JD. Candidates will hold you to it. If your PIRs still have names in the "who caused it" field, your culture is not blameless yet. Describe where you are, not where you want to be.

**Pre-posting reviews:**
1. HR partner review: level alignment, compensation band accuracy, legal compliance
2. One current team member review: ask them "does this description match what the person hired into this role would actually do?" Their feedback catches anything unrealistic or missing.

## Stage 2 — Sourcing

**Channels by role type:**

| Role | Primary Channel | Secondary Channel |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| Staff SRE / Platform Architect | Direct outreach, referrals | LinkedIn (low yield for senior) |
| Senior SRE (L3) | Referrals, LinkedIn | Internal job board |
| SRE L2 / Associate | LinkedIn, internal job board | Bootcamp partnerships |
| Problem Analyst / Change Manager | LinkedIn, referrals | Internal transfers |
| Director / Manager | Executive search, referrals | LinkedIn Premium |

**Referral pipeline target for Senior IC roles (Staff, Senior SRE):** 50% of pipeline from referrals. Referral candidates for operations roles are significantly higher-yield than job board applicants — they arrive with social proof, domain knowledge is easier to validate, and ramp time is typically shorter. Incentivize referrals if your org has a referral bonus program.

**Direct outreach for Principal and Staff roles:** Job boards are low-signal for senior candidates who are not actively looking. If you need a Staff SRE or Platform Architect, identify 15–20 candidates on LinkedIn, look for public indicators of the relevant skills (blog posts, conference talks, open source contributions in relevant domains), and reach out with a message that demonstrates you've read their background — not a templated "exciting opportunity" blast.

**Sourcing timeline target:** 15 business days from posting to a first slate of candidates for IC roles. 30 business days for Director or Manager roles. If you're past these timelines without a slate, review the JD (is it too restrictive?), the compensation band (is it below market?), and the channels (are you reaching the right audience?).

## Stage 3 — Resume Screen and Recruiter Phone Screen (30 minutes)

**Recruiter phone screen purpose:** Surface the basics — compensation alignment, availability, work authorization, location, and a rough background fit check. The recruiter is not assessing technical depth; that comes in Stage 4.

**Hiring manager resume review:** Before the recruiter calls the top candidates, the hiring manager reviews the resumes and flags must-haves that are missing. The recruiter then asks about those specific gaps directly: "The role requires experience operating Kubernetes in production — can you tell me about your direct experience with that?"

**Pass criteria from Stage 3:** At least 2 of 3 must-have competencies are either evident in the resume or confirmed in the phone screen. One missing must-have can be forgiven if the candidate's background is exceptionally strong in the others and the gap is learnable quickly. Two missing must-haves: do not advance.

**Stage 3 outputs:** A ranked shortlist of candidates for Stage 4. The recruiter provides a 3-line summary per candidate: background fit, compensation alignment, availability. Hiring manager reviews and confirms the list before Stage 4 is scheduled.

## Stage 4 — Technical Screen with Domain Manager (60 minutes)

**Format:**
- First 15 minutes: candidate background (their description, not your reading of the resume) and role overview (what the job actually is, what the team looks like, what the challenges are)
- Middle 30 minutes: domain-specific technical questions (pull from the question bank in ch04 — use 3–4 questions, not the full bank)
- Last 15 minutes: candidate questions — listen carefully; the questions a candidate asks reveal their priorities, their risk awareness, and how they think about work

**Technical assessment focus:**
- Depth, not breadth: one area of genuine expertise is worth more than surface knowledge of five areas
- Tool fluency: can they describe specific Splunk queries they've written, specific Kubernetes debugging steps they've taken, specific RabbitMQ or Kafka configuration decisions and why they made them?
- Failure literacy: have they experienced real system failures? Can they describe the failure, the investigation, and the resolution with specific technical detail?

**Pass criteria:** Demonstrates depth on 2 of 3 must-have competencies with specific examples. No red flags — red flags include inability to describe a real incident with any specificity, repeated pivoting away from technical detail to management context, or claims that directly contradict what's on the resume when probed.

**One note on tool names:** At T-Mobile scale, a candidate who has only worked with single-broker Kafka or a 5-node RabbitMQ cluster may not have the operational depth needed for a multi-cluster, high-throughput environment. Probe specifically: "What's the largest broker cluster you've operated? What were the operational challenges at that scale?" Scale matters for operations roles.

## Stage 5 — Behavioral Interview with Senior Team Member (60 minutes)

**Who runs this:** A Senior SRE or Staff Engineer from the team — not a manager, not the Director. Peer-level assessment catches interpersonal fit signals that manager-level interviewers often miss.

**Format:** STAR throughout — Situation, Task, Action, Result. If a candidate gives a vague answer, the interviewer follows up: "Can you be more specific about what you personally did?" and "What was the measurable outcome?"

**Questions to cover (choose 4–5):**
- "Tell me about the worst incident you were ever part of. What happened, what was your role, and what did you learn?"
- "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical direction and what you did about it."
- "Tell me about a time you had to deliver bad news — to a customer, a stakeholder, or a manager. How did you handle it?"
- "Tell me about a time you had to escalate. What was the situation, when did you decide to escalate, and how did it go?"
- "Tell me about a time your on-call shift went particularly badly. What contributed to it and what changed afterward?"

**What you're assessing:**
- Calm under pressure: does the candidate describe high-stakes situations with clarity and without panic or deflection?
- Communication: can they explain a technical situation to a non-technical person, and do they do it in the STAR response naturally?
- Collaboration: do they use "I" and "we" appropriately — owning their contribution without erasing the team?
- Escalation judgment: did they escalate too early, too late, or at the right time? And do they know which it was?

**Pass criteria:** At least 3 strong STAR responses with specific technical detail. "Strong" means the Situation is clear, the Action is specific and owned, and the Result is measurable. Vague answers ("we worked together to resolve it") with no specific ownership or outcome are not passing responses.

## Stage 6 — Domain Scenario Exercise (60–90 minutes)

**Purpose:** Assess judgment under ambiguity and structured communication in a controlled setting. This stage filters candidates who are technically knowledgeable but operationally unreliable — the engineer who knows the theory but freezes or goes solo at 2am.

**Live option (preferred for IC roles):**

Walk through an incident scenario in real time. The interviewer presents the scenario in phases:

- Phase 1: "You just got paged. Here's the PagerDuty alert. [Share alert text.] Walk me through your first 10 minutes."
- Phase 2 (after they've described initial triage): "You've checked the basic indicators and found [specific finding]. What are you doing next?"
- Phase 3 (after their next steps): "You've been working this for 25 minutes. Your manager hasn't been looped in. Your SLO is burning. What do you do?"

The interviewer is not looking for a perfect answer — they're looking for: clear prioritization, communication awareness (when to loop in stakeholders), hypothesis-driven triage, and recognition of when to escalate.

**Take-home option (preferred for Manager and Principal roles):**

Provide a structured case study — a sanitized PIR from a real incident with some details altered. Ask for a written response (max 2 pages) addressing:
1. What additional analysis would you do that isn't in this PIR?
2. What was the actual root cause vs. the symptoms described?
3. What are your top 3 recommended action items, and how would you prioritize them?
4. What would you tell the VP who's asking for a status update 2 hours after this incident?

**Evaluator:** Domain manager plus one senior IC from the team. They score independently using the rubric from ch04, then align.

**Pass criteria:** Demonstrates structured thinking in ambiguity. Communicates clearly at the right altitude (technical detail when relevant, stakeholder framing when relevant). Shows awareness of escalation timing and stakeholder communication — not just technical resolution.

## Stage 7 — Director Interview (45 minutes)

**This is not a technical re-screen.** The team has already assessed technical depth and operational judgment. The Director interview has a different purpose: strategic communication, values alignment, and a final fit assessment.

**What the Director is assessing:**
- Can this person explain a technical concept to a non-technical stakeholder clearly and without condescension?
- Do their values align with the team's operating principles — particularly around blameless culture, learning from failure, and sustainable operations?
- What motivates them, and does this role match those motivations?
- Would the Director trust this person to represent the team in a VP-level meeting?

**Questions the Director should always ask:**

1. "What did you learn from the worst incident you were ever part of?" — assesses learning orientation and ability to reflect without deflecting blame onto systems or other people

2. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical direction and what you did about it." — assesses whether the candidate engages constructively with disagreement or goes underground/passive

3. "What would your last manager say is the most important thing you need to grow in?" — tests self-awareness. A candidate who says "nothing, I'm pretty strong across the board" has low self-awareness. A candidate who answers specifically and doesn't get defensive when you follow up is demonstrating growth orientation.

**Pass criteria:** The Director can say "I would trust this person to represent the team in a VP-level briefing." This means they can communicate clearly, they don't panic under follow-up questions, they have self-awareness, and their values are compatible with the team's operating culture.

**If the Director has a strong negative reaction:** Surface it explicitly with the hiring panel before the debrief. "I had a concern about X" is a useful input. "I had a feeling" without substance is not — probe your own reaction before presenting it.

## Stage 8 — Offer Construction

**Level determination:** Based on the aggregate rubric scores from Stages 4, 5, 6, and 7. If the candidate performed at different levels across stages (e.g., strong technical, weak behavioral), discuss in the debrief and decide. Do not default to the average — decide which stage's assessment is most predictive for this specific role.

**Compensation anchoring:**

| Hire Quality | Band Anchor | When to Use |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Meets expectations | P50 | Solid candidate who fits the role |
| Strong hire | P75 | Strong candidate with clear strengths; worth investing in |
| Exceptional | P90 | Director or Staff level only; must justify to VP |

**Offer components:**
- Base salary (within approved band for the level)
- Annual bonus target (% of base, stated clearly)
- Equity / RSU grant (for permanent roles — state vesting schedule explicitly)
- Benefits note (do not over-sell; reference the standard benefits page)
- Start date

**Timeline:** Verbal offer within **2 business days** of the final interview completing. Written offer within **1 business day** of verbal acceptance. Delays in offers lose candidates — the operations talent market does not wait.

---

## Stage 9 — Offer Negotiation

Operations engineers with specialized skills (Kafka administration, Kubernetes platform engineering, TIBCO/RabbitMQ expertise at scale) have market leverage. Expect negotiation on most senior offers. This is not a problem — it's a signal that you've found someone worth competing for.

**What you can flex:**
- Base salary within band (up to P90 with Director approval)
- Signing bonus if your org has one available (one-time, not recurring — use for cases where the candidate needs to forfeit unvested equity at their current employer)
- Start date (within reason — 4-week notice periods are normal)

**What you should not flex:**
- **Level** — do not under-level a candidate to fit a tighter budget and plan to promote quickly. "We'll promote you in 6 months" is a promise that erodes trust when it doesn't happen. Level the candidate correctly or don't hire at this budget.
- **Title** — titles matter for external perception. If the market calls this role "Staff SRE" and you're calling it "Senior SRE II" to manage internal ladder consistency, that's a retention risk.

**Director approval required:** Any offer above P75 of band, any offer involving a signing bonus, and any counter-offer above the initial offer.

**If the candidate counter-offers:** Understand what they're optimizing for before responding. "What matters most to you in this offer?" often reveals that a candidate focused on base can be satisfied with a compensation mix shift. A candidate focused on level or title is telling you something different.

---

## Stage 10 — Background Check and Start Date

**Standard checks:** Criminal background, employment verification (last 3 employers), education verification if required by role (common for management roles).

**Timing:** Initiate background check immediately upon written offer acceptance. Do not delay the start date to wait for background check results unless a critical issue surfaces. Running the background check in parallel with the offer process is preferred where legally permissible.

**Start date preparation:**

Send a "what to expect on Day 1" email **3 business days before the start date**. Contents:
- Reporting location and time (or remote login details if fully remote)
- Who to ask for on arrival
- What access will be provisioned vs. what the new hire should expect to set up themselves
- A link to the universal first-week schedule (from ch07)
- One thing to know that isn't in any official document — the team Slack channel to join, the parking situation, whatever the practical thing is that everyone wishes they'd known on Day 1

Do not make this email formal. Write it the way a colleague would. The goal is to reduce Day 1 anxiety so the new hire's energy goes into orienting, not into logistical uncertainty.
