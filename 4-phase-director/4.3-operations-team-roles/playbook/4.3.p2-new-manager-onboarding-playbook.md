# New Manager Onboarding Playbook

> **Audience**: Director of Operations, onboarding a new domain manager (whether promoted from IC or hired externally).
> **Purpose**: A concrete week-by-week guide for getting a new manager to "trusted domain owner" in 90 days without abandoning them or micromanaging them. The Director's role in this process is active, not observational.

---

## Week 1 — Introductions and Context

The Director is responsible for setting up every introduction in Week 1. Do not assume the new manager will navigate this themselves. The org chart exists; the relationship map does not.

### Introductions the Director Sets Up

**Director's own 1:1 with the new manager (30 minutes, Day 1 or Day 2):**

The Director covers:
- The team charter: what this team exists to do, how it measures success, what it does not own
- The new manager's delegation charter (from ch06): here is your domain, here are the 4 metrics you own, here is what "healthy" looks like, here is what your first escalation trigger to me looks like
- Director's expectations in plain language — not "I expect excellence" but "I expect you to tell me bad news early, to make decisions in your domain without asking me, and to bring me data not narrative when you need my input"
- One thing about this team that doesn't appear in any document but matters for operating in it

**Peer manager introductions (all four together if possible, or individual 30-min sessions within the first 3 days):**

New manager meets: IPM-Mgr, CRM-Mgr, PRM, and ATL (or equivalent peers in your org structure). The Director makes the introduction but does not stay in the meeting — the new manager needs to build their own peer relationships, not inherit the Director's.

The Director's introduction sets context: "This is [Name]. They're taking over [Domain]. They'll be your primary contact for [specific interaction point — e.g., change requests that touch their domain, incident escalations in the messaging layer, on-call handoffs]."

**Cross-functional partner introductions:**

| Partner | Why They Matter | Introduction Method |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Product manager for their domain | Feature vs. reliability trade-off conversations, roadmap alignment | 30-min scheduled intro |
| Engineering leads for the services in their domain | Change coordination, deployment approval, incident bridge participation | 30-min scheduled intro |
| Security team contact | Vulnerability management in their domain, security incident response | Email introduction + standing meeting invitation |
| Business stakeholder | Understands the business impact of incidents in their domain | 20-min intro — Director attends this one |

**Introduction to each direct report (manager-led 1:1, 30 minutes each):**

The new manager runs these — the Director does not attend. If the new manager is uncertain what to ask, the Director coaches them beforehand: "These are listening meetings. Your questions are: What's going well? What's frustrating you? What would you change? Take notes. Don't commit to anything."

### Documents to Read in Week 1

The new manager should complete all of the following before the end of Week 1 — this is not optional reading, it's functional preparation:

| Document | What to Extract From It |
|----------|------------------------|
| Ch01–Ch06 of this framework | Structure, terminology, how the team operates |
| Their delegation charter (ch06) | The 4 metrics they own, the authority boundaries, the escalation triggers |
| Last 6 months of domain metrics | Incident trends, change success rate, SLO compliance, DORA metrics |
| Last 3 PIRs from their domain | Recurring failure patterns, action item completion rates, process gaps |
| Open JIRA epics and status | How much is in flight, what's overdue, what has no owner |
| Team roster and on-call schedule | Who's on rotation, rotation frequency, any known coverage gaps |

### Data to Review in Week 1

Pull these from your incident management tool and PagerDuty/VictorOps before the end of Week 1:

- **On-call load:** Pages per person per week for the last 90 days. Is the load distributed evenly? Who is carrying disproportionate volume?
- **Incident volume by severity, last 6 months:** Is the trend flat, increasing, or decreasing? Are P1/P2 counts stable or growing?
- **DORA metrics** (for CRM-Mgr/change-heavy domains): Deployment frequency, change failure rate, MTTR — where is the domain relative to target?
- **SLO compliance** (for PRM/reliability-heavy domains): Which services are meeting SLO, which are burning error budget?
- **Open problem records > 30 days old:** These are unresolved recurring incidents. How many are there? Do they have active owners?

## Weeks 2–4 — Shadow and Learn

### Shadow Protocol

The new manager attends the weekly ops review as an observer for weeks 2–3. They do not run the meeting. They do not introduce agenda items. They watch: who speaks, who defers, who challenges, how decisions get made, what topics are avoided. The dynamic of this meeting is more informative than its content.

In Week 4, the new manager runs the ops review for the first time (see Month 2 section).

The new manager should shadow their predecessor or the interim leader who ran the domain during the transition period. If there is no predecessor available (external hire into a newly created role), the Director or a peer manager shadows with them for 2 key meetings in Week 2.

### Second Round of Direct Report 1:1s

Week 2 or 3: repeat the 1:1 round with a deeper set of questions. First round was listening. This round asks: "What would you change if you could?" — more pointed, more likely to surface real frustrations.

Take notes again. Still don't commit to anything. The pattern across multiple people is the signal; one person's strong opinion is just one data point.

### Week 4 Deliverable to Director: "What I've Observed"

Format: 1 page (not a presentation, not a deck — a written brief). Contents:

1. **Top 3 domain risks** — specific, named, evidence-based. Not "reliability could be better" but "RabbitMQ broker in prod has a 4-hour MTTR for queue overflow incidents — the last 3 PIRs show the same root cause and the action items from PIR-1 were never completed."
2. **Top 3 domain strengths** — what's working, supported by data or observation
3. **One thing I don't know yet** — something the new manager needs help understanding: a system, a relationship, a historical decision. This signals self-awareness, not weakness.

Director reviews the brief within 2 business days. Director gives one piece of feedback: one observation they agree with, and one thing they want the manager to look deeper at. This is not a grade — it's a calibration.

## Month 2 — First Leadership Actions

### Making Deliberate Decisions

The new manager makes their first 3 decisions in Month 2. These should be small, selected deliberately — not the first big decision that arrives, but 3 low-risk decisions where the new manager can practice decision communication.

**Examples of good first decisions:**
- "I'm updating the on-call rotation schedule to balance load more evenly — here's what changes and why."
- "I'm moving our weekly domain review to Tuesday at 2pm — the Friday slot was conflicting with too many end-of-week obligations."
- "I'm setting a new standard for runbook updates — any runbook referenced in an incident must be reviewed and updated within 5 business days of the incident closing."

**Communication format for each decision:**

Post in the team channel: "Decision: [what]. Reason: [one sentence why]. Effective: [date]. Questions? [DM me or bring to ops review]." Consistent, brief, direct. No lengthy justification. No seeking approval after the fact. Communicate the decision as a decision, not a proposal.

### Running the First Domain Review

**Agenda for the first manager-run weekly ops review:**

| Item | Time | Owner |
|------|------|-------|
| SLO and incident metrics for the week | 5 min | Manager |
| Open incidents and active problem records | 5 min | Manager + IC leads |
| Change queue for next 2 weeks | 10 min | Manager |
| Open issues / blockers | 5 min | Team |
| One announcement or learning | 5 min | Rotating |

Keep it to 30 minutes. If the meeting runs long, identify what agenda item consistently over-runs and address that item separately — don't extend the meeting.

### 30-Day Check-In with Director

30 minutes. Not a status report — a calibration conversation.

Director asks: "What's going well? What's unclear? What do you need from me to be effective that you're not getting?"

Director provides explicit feedback on the Week 4 brief: "Here's what I agreed with. Here's what I want you to look deeper at. Here's one thing I'd add that you didn't mention."

New manager leaves the check-in with: one thing they're going to do differently based on Director feedback, and one open question they'll bring answers to by the end of Month 3.

### Presenting Domain Status to Director for the First Time

Format: data-led, not narrative-led.

| Section | What to Include |
|---------|----------------|
| Domain overview | Services owned, SLO targets, team size, on-call rotation |
| Current health | SLO compliance %, incident count (week and month trend), change success rate |
| Top 3 risks | Specific risks with supporting data |
| What I'm doing about each risk | One specific action per risk, with a timeline |

Director reviews and gives format feedback. The feedback from this first presentation shapes the standing format for the monthly domain metrics report going forward — establish it correctly now.

## Month 3 — Own the Domain

### 90-Day Deliverable to Director

This is a formal written deliverable — not a Slack message, not a verbal update. It should be in Confluence and linked in the ops monthly report.

**Contents:**

**Domain Assessment:**
- Services owned, their SLO targets, and current SLO compliance
- Incident trend (6-month chart if your tooling generates one, or a summary table)
- Change success rate trend
- On-call load trend

**Top 3 Risks with Mitigation Plans:**
Each risk is: named specifically, evidence-supported, and has a mitigation plan with an owner and a timeline. "We might have a RabbitMQ scaling problem" is not a risk entry. "RabbitMQ broker cluster has not been performance-tested at projected Q4 message volume — risk of queue overflow in October peak" is a risk entry. Mitigation: "Performance test scheduled for Aug 15 with ATL team; scaling plan to follow."

**Q1 Priority List:**
What gets worked on this quarter, in priority order, and why that priority order. Director should be able to read this and say "yes, that's the right order" or "I'd move X up — here's why."

**Personnel Brief:**
For each direct report: one sentence on their strength, one sentence on their primary development area. This is not a performance review — it's the manager's working model of the team. Director uses this to calibrate whether the manager is seeing the team accurately.

### Q1 Goal-Setting with Each Direct Report

Goals set in Month 3 cover the next quarter. For each direct report, set 2–3 specific, measurable goals. The format:

- **Goal**: [specific outcome]
- **Metric**: [how we know it's achieved]
- **Due**: [date]
- **Dependencies**: [what the engineer needs from you or from the org to achieve this]

Example: Goal: Reduce P2 MTTA in the messaging domain from 18 minutes to 10 minutes. Metric: 4-week rolling average MTTA pulled from PagerDuty. Due: End of Q1. Dependencies: Updated runbooks for top 5 messaging alerts (manager to facilitate), on-call runbook review session with full rotation.

### First Process Improvement Implementation

The process improvement identified in Month 2 is implemented in Month 3. Communicate to the team:
- What changed (specific, not vague)
- What they should do differently starting today
- Who to contact with questions or issues

Follow up 2 weeks after implementation: is it working? Are people using it? Did it create any unexpected problems?

### Director Check-In Schedule

The Director's check-in cadence with the new manager scales down as confidence builds. Stick to this schedule — reducing check-ins too fast leaves the manager unsupported; reducing them too slowly signals distrust.

| Period | Check-In Frequency | Format |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Week 1 | Daily | Slack check-in (5 minutes, async) |
| Weeks 2–4 | Every other day | Slack check-in (5–10 minutes, async) |
| Month 2 | Weekly 1:1 | 30 minutes, standing calendar |
| Month 3 | Weekly 1:1 + 90-day formal review | 30 min weekly + 60 min for the review |
| Month 4+ | Weekly 1:1 standing | 30 minutes; additional as needed |

**What "daily check-in" means in practice:** A Slack message, not a meeting. Director sends "how's it going — anything I should know?" New manager responds with the key update. If something needs synchronous discussion, they schedule a call. The daily cadence is about maintaining connection and catching problems early, not micromanaging.

### The "Trust Transfer" Test

At the end of Month 3, the Director should be able to answer "yes" to each of these:
- [ ] The manager understands what their domain owns and does not own
- [ ] The manager can communicate domain health in data without coaching
- [ ] The manager makes decisions in their domain without asking for approval
- [ ] The manager brings escalations with context and a recommendation, not just a problem
- [ ] The manager knows their team members and has documented growth plans for each
- [ ] The Director would be comfortable being out of contact for 2 weeks while this manager runs their domain

If any box is unchecked, that is a specific thing to address — not a general "needs more time." Name the gap. Address it with a specific action.
