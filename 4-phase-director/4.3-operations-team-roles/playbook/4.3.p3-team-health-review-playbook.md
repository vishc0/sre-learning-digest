# Team Health Review Playbook — Quarterly

> **Audience**: Director of Operations.
> **Purpose**: A repeatable, 3-hour quarterly process for measuring team health across all domains, identifying risks before they become attrition, and generating specific actions. The output is a 1-page health summary per domain and a prioritized action list for the next quarter.

---

## When to Run It

Run the quarterly health review on the **last week of each quarter** (Q1 = last week of March, Q2 = last week of June, etc.). Schedule 3 hours of dedicated Director time:
- 1 hour: review collected metrics and survey data
- 1 hour: skip-level 1:1s or domain manager 1:1s (30 min each, 2 conversations)
- 1 hour: synthesis and action planning

Output shared with: VP (your direct manager) and your HR business partner within 5 business days of the review.

---

## Pre-Work: Metrics Collection (1 Week Before Review)

Send each domain manager a data request 1 week before the review. Managers complete and return it within 3 business days.

### Per-Domain Data Request Template

| Metric | Source | Reporting Period |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| On-call load: pages per engineer per week (rolling 13 weeks) | PagerDuty / VictorOps export | Last quarter |
| Toil percentage: engineer survey result + manager estimate | Quarterly survey + ticket categorization | Last quarter |
| IC concentration: unique ICs for P2+ incidents | Incident tool (ServiceNow, JIRA) | Last quarter |
| New hire ramp time: days from start to first contribution | Manager tracking | Any new hire who started in the quarter |
| Time-in-level: each engineer's current level and months-at-level | HR system or manager record | Current as of review week |
| PTO coverage gaps: any gaps identified or at risk | Manager assessment | Current |
| Stretch assignments: who has one, what it is | Manager tracking | Current |

If a manager cannot produce one of these metrics, that is itself a signal — the metric isn't being tracked, which means the risk is invisible. Flag that gap in the review output.

---

## Pre-Work: Quarterly Anonymous Survey (2 Weeks Before Review)

Send to all team members 2 weeks before the quarterly review. Keep it anonymous. Use your org's survey tool (SurveyMonkey, Google Forms, Qualtrics — whatever is available). Keep it to 6 questions; longer surveys get lower response rates.

### Survey Questions

**Q1 — On-call sustainability (scale 1–5):**
"On a scale of 1 to 5, how sustainable is your current on-call load?"
(1 = unsustainable — I'm burning out; 5 = very sustainable — I could handle more without impact)

**Q2 — Toil estimate (multiple choice):**
"What percentage of your work week do you consider toil — repetitive, manual, low-value work that could be automated or eliminated?"
- Less than 25%
- 25–50%
- 50–75%
- More than 75%

**Q3 — Career visibility (yes/somewhat/no):**
"Do you feel like you know what career advancement looks like for you in this organization?"

**Q4 — Skill development opportunity (open text):**
"Is there a specific skill or area you want to develop that you're not currently getting opportunity to develop? (Optional — please be specific if you answer this.)"

**Q5 — Work visibility (yes/somewhat/no):**
"Do you feel like your work is visible to leadership — that the people above your manager know what you're contributing?"

**Q6 — One improvement (open text):**
"What is one thing that, if changed, would most improve your experience on this team? (Optional — be as specific as you want.)"

### Survey Response Targets

- **Green:** > 75% response rate
- **Amber:** 50–75% response rate (low response may indicate disengagement or survey fatigue — note it)
- **Red:** < 50% response rate (investigate before interpreting results — low response invalidates the data)

## During the Review: 1:1 Themes to Watch For

The quarterly review includes at least 2 skip-level 1:1s — the Director meeting directly with ICs (not managers) for 30 minutes. Select ICs based on: new hires (anyone in their first 6 months), engineers flagged as retention risks by their manager, and the engineer on each team who has been there longest.

### Patterns That Warrant Deeper Investigation

**Repeated mentions of the same engineer's name (positive or negative):**
If two different ICs independently mention the same colleague — "you should ask Sarah, she knows that system" or "we always have to wait on Dave to sign off" — that is a knowledge concentration or culture signal. Investigate which kind.

**"We always do it this way" without explanation:**
Process calcification. The person following the process doesn't know why it exists. This means the process will never improve and may not survive the departure of whoever designed it. Ask: "Does your manager know this is how it's done? Has anyone reviewed whether it still makes sense?"

**"I didn't know that was my job":**
The RACI or delegation charter is not landing. The engineer doesn't know their boundaries. This creates both operational gaps (things not getting done) and morale problems (getting blamed for things they didn't know were theirs). Surface to their manager immediately.

**No examples of learning or growth in the past quarter:**
A growth stall in progress. If an engineer cannot name one thing they learned, one skill they built, or one stretch assignment they worked on in the past 3 months, they are on the growth stall path. Risk: 6–12 months from an attrition conversation.

**"I cover for [person] whenever they're out":**
The coverage model is broken. The engineer you're talking to has become an undeclared single point of failure. They probably know it and resent it. The person they cover for may not know they're dependent. Fix the coverage model; don't just reassure the IC.

## Actions by Signal

These action thresholds are non-negotiable. When a signal crosses a threshold, the action is triggered. "We'll monitor it" is not an action.

---

### On-Call Load: Amber (5–10 pages/engineer/week)

**Threshold:** Rolling 4-week average between 5 and 10 pages per engineer per week in any domain.

**Action within 2 weeks:**
- Alert tuning sprint: identify the top 5 highest-volume alert sources in the domain. For each: what is the action rate (% of pages that require a human to do something)? If the action rate is < 50%, the threshold is too aggressive — tune it down or convert to informational.
- Target: 20% reduction in total page volume within the tuning sprint window.

**Action within 30 days:**
- Review rotation size. If the rotation has fewer than 5 engineers, the per-engineer load will spike whenever anyone is on PTO. Minimum viable rotation for sustainable load is 5 engineers. If you're below that, evaluate: add to rotation from adjacent team (temporary), pause new project work to address load, or escalate to VP for headcount.

---

### On-Call Load: Red (> 10 pages/engineer/week for > 2 consecutive weeks)

**Action: immediate (same week the threshold is crossed):**
- Add temporary backup coverage from an adjacent team. This is not optional. Engineers at > 10 pages/week for > 2 weeks are at burnout risk. The immediate action is to share the load, not to analyze why the load is high.
- Director is personally notified by the domain manager as soon as the 2-week threshold is crossed — not at the next weekly review, not via a dashboard — direct notification.

**Action within 1 week:**
- Director and domain manager review together: is this a tooling problem (alert configuration), a staffing problem (too few people in the rotation), or a system degradation problem (the service is genuinely failing more often)? The answer determines the intervention.

**Action within 30 days:**
- Open a problem record for each of the top 3 alert sources contributing to the load spike. Each problem record has an owner, a target resolution date, and is reviewed weekly by the domain manager until closed.

---

### Toil > 60% in Any Domain

**Threshold:** Manager-estimated or survey-reported toil percentage exceeds 60% for any domain.

**Immediate action (same week identified):**
- Stop assigning new reactive work to engineers already above 60% toil. This is a capacity constraint, not a morale preference. Engineers above 60% toil have no capacity for reliability investment. Assigning them more reactive work deepens the hole.

**Action within 30 days:**
- Director presents toil risk to VP. This framing matters: "The [domain] team is spending 65% of their time on toil. At this level, reliability improvement work has effectively stopped. This is a headcount and/or investment decision, not a time management problem. Here's what we need to change it." Do not let this stay as an SRE-level conversation — toil above 60% is an organizational risk that requires organizational resources to fix.

---

### Career Visibility Concern (> 30% of Survey Respondents Answer "No" to Q3)

**Threshold:** More than 30% of respondents say they do not know what career advancement looks like for them.

**Action within 2 weeks:**
- Director hosts a career ladder walk-through for the entire team (all-hands or domain-level). Walk through the levels, the criteria for each, and what advancement actually looks like in practice. Use real examples (anonymized). Engineers who don't know the ladder aren't failing to pay attention — the ladder hasn't been made visible to them.

**Action within 30 days:**
- Every manager reviews growth plans with each direct report. A growth plan that hasn't been discussed in > 3 months is a stale plan. Update it. Make the next milestone specific and dated.

**Action within 90 days:**
- At least 3 new stretch assignments created and filled across the team. A stretch assignment is a project or responsibility slightly above the engineer's current level — leading a PIR, owning a reliability project, designing monitoring for a new service. These must be real assignments with real deliverables, not token gestures.

---

### IC Concentration (2 Engineers Doing > 80% of P1/P2 ICs)

**Threshold:** In the trailing quarter, 2 or fewer engineers ran more than 80% of all P1/P2 incident commands.

**Immediate action:**
- The 2 concentrated engineers are moved to the bottom of the IC rotation. This is not punitive — it's protective. They need load relief, and the team needs to develop depth. Make this explicit when you communicate it: "You've been carrying too much of the IC load. We're restructuring the rotation to spread it — that's good for you and good for the team."

**Action within 30 days:**
- Identify 2 additional engineers to be promoted to P2 IC-capable status. Run a structured promotion process: mentored incident response (observer on 3 P2 incidents, IC with backup on 2 P2 incidents). The Senior SRE or Staff Engineer who currently holds IC concentration knowledge runs the mentored sessions.

**Action within 60 days:**
- Target: 5 engineers capable of IC'ing a P2 incident, 3 capable of IC'ing a P1. This is a hard capability target, not a preference. If you can't get there in 60 days with existing headcount, escalate — this is a team capability risk that requires a resourcing conversation.

## Quarterly Health Summary Format

Produce one 1-page summary per domain. Share with VP and HR business partner within 5 business days of the review. Store in Confluence.

```
Domain Health Summary — [Domain Name] — Q[X] [Year]

On-Call Load:       [Green / Amber / Red] — [X] pages/engineer/week avg (13-week rolling)
Toil Percentage:    [Green / Amber / Red] — [X]% (survey + manager estimate)
IC Concentration:   [Green / Amber / Red] — [X] engineers for [Y]% of P2+ ICs
New Hire Ramp:      [Green / Amber / N/A] — [X] days avg ([Y] new hires this quarter)
PTO Coverage:       [Green / Amber / Red] — [note any gaps]
Career Visibility:  [Green / Amber / Red] — [X]% of survey respondents answered "No"

Top Risk This Quarter:
[Specific, evidence-based statement of the biggest health risk in this domain.]

Action Assigned:
[Owner] will [specific action] by [date].

Manager Assessment:
[1–2 sentences on how the domain manager is performing — provided to HR partner only, not shared with the manager themselves.]
```

## The Director's Annual Pattern Recognition

At the end of Q4, review all four quarterly summaries side by side. You're looking for:

- **Persistent amber:** A metric that has been in amber for 3+ consecutive quarters. This is not a temporary fluctuation — it is a structural problem that requires a structural solution. Escalate to VP if you haven't already.
- **Degrading trends:** A metric moving from green → amber → red over 3 quarters. The trend is the warning; don't wait for red to act.
- **Survey Q6 themes:** Read all Q6 open-text responses across all 4 quarters. Cluster the themes. What comes up repeatedly? The recurring theme in Q6 is the thing that matters most to your team and that you have probably not yet fully addressed.
- **Action completion rate:** Of the actions generated in each quarterly review, what fraction were completed by the following review? If you're generating actions and not completing them, the review is producing noise, not improvement. Either reduce the number of actions (pick 1, not 5) or investigate why they're not completing (wrong owner, no capacity, no accountability).

The goal of the quarterly review is not a clean report. It is a team that is less burned out, more capable, and more engaged at the end of the year than it was at the beginning.
