# Chapter 7 — New Team Member Onboarding

> **Audience**: Director of Operations and domain managers.
> **Purpose**: A concrete, day-by-day onboarding framework for every role on the operations team. Use this as-is or adapt it to your org's tooling. The structure does not change; the tool names do.

---

## Section 1 — Universal First Week (All Roles)

Every person who joins the operations team — SRE, Platform Engineer, Problem Analyst, Change Manager, or Manager — follows the same first-week structure. Role-specific work begins in Week 2.

### Day 1: Access and First Contact

**Systems access provisioning — confirm all of the following are live before end of Day 1:**

| System | What to Confirm |
|--------|----------------|
| VPN | Can connect from home network without help desk intervention |
| JIRA | Can view and comment on tickets in their assigned project board |
| Confluence | Can view space; can create a draft page |
| ServiceNow | Can open and view incidents; can create a change request in draft |
| PagerDuty / VictorOps | Account active, mobile app installed, test page received and acknowledged |
| Splunk | Can log in, run a basic search (`index=prod sourcetype=*` returns results) |
| Datadog / Grafana | Can view dashboards for their assigned domain |
| Slack | Added to `#incidents`, `#changes`, `#sre-general`, team channel, and at minimum one domain-specific channel |
| GitHub / GitLab | Account active; read access to all repos in their domain, confirmed with a clone |
| Kubernetes (EKS) | `kubectl get pods -n <domain-namespace>` returns results; write access is NOT granted until Day 30+ |

**Laptop setup — before the end of Day 1, confirm:**
- `kubectl` installed and kubeconfig pointing to non-prod cluster
- `helm` installed
- AWS CLI installed and `aws sts get-caller-identity` returns a valid identity
- Preferred terminal configured (iTerm2, Windows Terminal, etc.)
- SSH key added to GitHub/GitLab

**15-minute intro with Director:**

The Director runs this — not the team lead, not HR. Keep it short. The purpose is to establish a direct relationship immediately, not to do a deep-dive orientation.

Director shares **three things they care most about**. Be specific. Not "I care about reliability" — say "I care about alert quality. If we're paging someone at 2am for something that auto-resolves, that's a failure of the team."

Director asks one question: **"What did you learn in your last role that you hope to use here?"**

Listen to the answer. Write it down. Reference it again in the 30-day check-in.

**Meet the immediate team:**

The team lead makes introductions — a 10-minute team standup or a brief Slack introduction in the team channel. No work is assigned on Day 1. No tickets are handed off. No "here's what's currently on fire." The new hire's job on Day 1 is to get access and feel oriented, not to contribute.

---

### Day 2: Context and Charter

**Read the following before Day 3:**
- The team charter (`ops-team-charter-template.md` in `reference/`) — this defines the team's mission, scope, and operating principles
- Chapters 1 through 3 of this framework at minimum (ch01 through ch03)
- The Director's Operating Manual if one exists — if it doesn't, the Director should have a 1-page "how I work" document; write one if you don't have it
- The domain map: where does this role fit, what does it own, who depends on it, what are the upstream and downstream dependencies

**Domain map questions the new hire should be able to answer by end of Day 2:**
1. Which services am I responsible for?
2. Who are the internal customers of those services (other teams, product lines, end users)?
3. Who are the external dependencies my services rely on (other teams, shared platforms, third-party systems)?
4. What is the escalation path if something in my domain breaks at 2am?

If a domain map document doesn't exist, this is the new hire's first documentation task — write it, and have a senior team member verify it by Day 30.

---

### Day 3: Incident History

**Shadow a live incident bridge call** — if a P1 or P2 is active, the new hire observes with mic muted, video on, and no speaking. Their job is to watch the IC run the bridge. After the call, the team lead debriefs: "What did you notice? What confused you? What would you have done differently?"

**If no active incident:** pull the last P1 bridge recording from Confluence or your incident management tool. Every P1 bridge should be recorded; if yours aren't, that is a process gap — flag it to your manager.

**Read the last 3 postmortems / PIRs (Post-Incident Reviews):**
- The new hire is looking for: what failed, what was the detection time, what was the response, what action items came out of it
- Look for recurring themes across all three: if the same system name, the same failure mode, or the same process gap shows up more than once, that's signal

**Day 3 task:** write down 3 questions that surfaced from reading the PIRs. These questions go to the buddy on Day 5, not to the manager. This is intentional — "dumb questions" to a manager have social cost; dumb questions to a buddy do not.

---

### Day 4: Environment Pipeline and Monitoring Architecture

**Review the environment pipeline:**
- What environments exist? In a mature T-Mobile-scale org, this is minimally: `dev`, `int` (integration), `staging`, `perf` (performance), `prod`
- How does code flow between environments? Who promotes it? What are the gate controls at each stage (automated test suite pass, manual approval, change ticket approval)?
- What can break at which environment and not affect production? What breaks silently?

**Understand the monitoring setup:**

| Tool | What to Understand |
|------|--------------------|
| Splunk | How is log ingestion structured? What indexes exist for your domain? Are there saved searches or dashboards for your services? What is the alerting model — threshold-based, anomaly-based, or manual correlation? |
| Datadog / Grafana | Which dashboards cover your services? Who owns them? What is the source of truth for SLO tracking? |
| PagerDuty / VictorOps | What is the routing model? Who gets paged for a `prod-critical` alert in your domain vs. a `prod-warning`? Is there a tiered escalation policy? |
| Kubernetes / EKS | What namespaces exist for your domain? What is the alerting on pod restarts, OOM kills, or deployment failures? |

**Review the service dependency map for your domain.** If one exists in Confluence, read it. If it doesn't exist, that's a Day 30 deliverable.

---

### Day 5: Buddy Meeting and 30-Day Plan

**Meet your assigned buddy.** The buddy is a senior team member — not the new hire's direct manager — assigned on Day 1. The buddy is available via Slack within 4 hours for any question, no matter how basic.

Day 5 agenda (30 minutes):
- New hire shares the 3 questions from Day 3 — buddy answers them or routes them to the right person
- Buddy introduces the new hire to 2–3 people they'll interact with regularly but haven't met yet
- Buddy shows the new hire "where things actually live" — the Confluence spaces that are actually maintained vs. the ones that are out of date, the Slack channels that are signal vs. noise, the runbooks that are trusted vs. the ones that are aspirational

**Complete your 30-day plan with your manager.** This is a 30-minute working session, not a document exchange. The 30-day plan has specific milestones (see Sections 2 and 3 below) — not vague commitments like "ramp up on the platform." Every milestone has a due date and a definition of done.

## Section 2 — New SRE (L2 / L3) — 30/60/90 Day Plan

### Days 8–30: Learn the Domain

**Shadow IC on all P2+ incidents in your domain.** You are never the IC during this phase. You are on the bridge with mic muted, taking notes. After every incident: write a 5-line summary of what happened and share it with the IC for feedback. This is not a performance artifact — it's a learning accelerator.

**Runbook review:** Complete a full read-through of every runbook in your assigned domain. For each runbook, flag:
- Steps that are unclear or ambiguous ("restart the service" — which service, which command, which cluster?)
- Steps that reference systems or tools that no longer exist
- Missing steps (the runbook says "verify the fix" but doesn't say what you're looking for)
- Runbooks for services that don't have one

Runbook gap list goes to your manager at the Day 30 milestone — you are not expected to fix all of them yet, just identify them.

**Set up personal Splunk saved searches for your top 5 services.** At minimum:
- Error rate query (`log_level=ERROR` scoped to your service namespace, last 15 minutes)
- Latency anomaly query (p99 latency from your service's access logs, compared against 7-day baseline)
- Deployment marker query (any `sourcetype=deploy` events in your domain namespace)

**Complete on-call onboarding exercises.** Your org should have a structured set of exercises — synthetic incidents, tabletop walkthroughs, or guided Splunk investigations — that train new engineers on on-call mechanics before they go live. If these don't exist, flag the gap to your manager. This is a risk.

**Map the top 10 services in your domain.** For each service, document:
- Owning team and primary point of contact
- Top 3 upstream dependencies (what does it call?)
- Top 3 downstream dependents (who calls it?)
- Current SLO (if defined)
- Who is on the on-call rotation for it

**Day 30 Milestone:** Present your domain map to your manager. A diagram with a dependency list is sufficient. The goal is to show you understand the terrain, not to demonstrate production mastery. Your manager gives feedback: anything missing, anything wrong, anything worth investigating deeper.

---

### Days 31–60: Own Small Pieces

**Serve as IC for P3 incidents with a Senior SRE as backup.** The Senior SRE is on the bridge and available but not speaking unless you ask. You run the incident. After each incident, debrief with the Senior SRE: what did you do well, what would you change?

**Write 2 new runbooks** for the highest-priority gaps you identified in your Day 8–30 runbook review. Each runbook follows the org's standard format and is reviewed by a senior team member before it's published.

**Contribute to one active problem investigation.** A problem record is an open investigation into a recurring incident or systemic failure. Own one hypothesis within the investigation: define what you're testing, run the analysis (Splunk, metrics, code review — whatever is appropriate), and report the finding to the problem record owner. Negative findings ("I tested X and it's not the cause because Y") are valuable — report them.

**Join the on-call rotation at the bottom of the queue.** For the first two weeks on rotation, you have a named backup — a senior engineer who you can escalate to within 15 minutes. The backup is not your primary IC support; you attempt the resolution first, then escalate if you're stuck.

**Day 60 Milestone:** IC two P3 incidents solo (backup engineer not engaged). Your manager reviews the incident logs and provides structured feedback on: escalation timing, communication clarity, resolution speed, and documentation quality.

---

### Days 61–90: Contribute Independently

**IC for P2 incidents independently.** A Senior SRE is available as backup but is not on the bridge unless you request them. You are responsible for bridge management, stakeholder updates, and resolution. The Senior SRE reviews your incident log afterward.

**Deliver a 20-minute lunch-and-learn** to the team. Topic is your choice — it should be something you learned between Day 1 and Day 60 that would have helped you when you started. A system deep-dive, a failure mode analysis, a Splunk query technique, a Kubernetes behavior you didn't know about. The point is to demonstrate that you can communicate technical concepts clearly to peers.

**Participate in on-call rotation without a backup.** You are a full rotation member. If you're stuck at 2am, you escalate through the standard escalation path — not to a named personal backup.

**Identify one toil item in your domain and propose a fix.** Document: what the toil is, how much time it costs (estimate hours per week across the team), what the fix would be (automation, process change, or elimination). Submit it as a JIRA epic or problem record. You don't build it yet — you propose it. Building it is a Q2 goal.

**Day 90 Milestone:** 90-day check-in with Director (30 minutes). At this check-in you should be able to answer: What do you own? What have you ICd? What's the biggest reliability risk in your domain? What do you want to work on next? If you can't answer those four questions, something in the onboarding plan was insufficient — tell your manager what was missing.

## Section 3 — New Manager (Promoted from IC or External Hire) — 30/60/90 Day Plan

### Days 8–30: Listen, Don't Lead

**Critical rule: do not change anything in the first 30 days.** You will observe things that are wrong. You will have opinions about what should be different. Write them down. Do not implement them. Your credibility as a manager depends on understanding before acting. A manager who changes things in week 2 is signaling that they don't trust the team's existing judgment — even when the change is right, the timing destroys trust.

**Hold a 1:1 with every direct report — first round.** This is not a performance conversation. The questions are:
- "What's going well right now?"
- "What's frustrating you?"
- "If you could change one thing about how this team operates, what would it be?"

Take notes. Do not commit to anything. Say "I'm listening and I'll come back to you on this after I've heard from everyone" if pressed. These conversations are inputs to your Day 30 deliverable, not a change request queue.

**Review last 6 months of domain metrics:** incident trends (count by severity, week-over-week), change success rate (how many changes caused incidents or rollbacks), SLO compliance (are the services meeting their targets?), and DORA metrics if tracked (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR). Identify the 3 biggest gaps — the areas where the data says the domain is underperforming relative to target.

**Shadow the weekly ops review.** Attend but do not run it. Observe who speaks, who defers, who challenges, who is silent. The social dynamics of this meeting will tell you more about the team's health than any document.

**Review all open JIRA epics and projects in your domain.** How much is in flight? What's overdue by more than 30 days? What has no owner? What was "in progress" six months ago and hasn't moved?

**Review on-call rotation health:** Pull PagerDuty/VictorOps data for the last 90 days. For each engineer on the rotation: how many pages per week (average and peak), what percentage of pages were during off-hours (outside 8am–6pm local), what was their average time to acknowledge. Flag anyone over 10 pages per week average. Flag anyone who hasn't been paged in 90 days (they may be excluded from rotation incorrectly or a coverage gap exists).

**Day 30 Deliverable to Director:** "What I've seen so far" — an informal 1-page brief. Format: top 3 domain strengths (what's working), top 3 gaps (where the data shows underperformance or risk), one thing I don't know yet and need help understanding. This is not a presentation — it's a conversation. Director reads it, provides one point of agreement and one point to investigate further.

---

### Days 31–60: Start Leading

**Run your first weekly domain review.** You set the agenda. You run the meeting. Standard agenda: domain metrics review (SLO, incident count, change success rate), open incidents and problem records, change queue for the next 2 weeks, team announcements. Keep it to 30 minutes; cut it off at 30 minutes even if items are incomplete (items that don't fit get a follow-up async).

**Make your first 3 intentional decisions.** Pick small ones deliberately — don't wait for a big decision to arrive. Examples: update the on-call rotation schedule, set the standing time for the weekly review, approve a runbook format change. For each decision: state what you decided, state why you decided it, communicate it to the team in writing (Slack or Confluence). Decision + reasoning in one message. No decision should be invisible.

**Hold your first quarterly 1:1 round — second pass.** This round has a different question set: "What does success look like for you in the next 6 months?" and "What's one skill you want to develop that you're not currently getting opportunity to work on?" The answers inform your growth plan conversations in Month 3.

**Present domain status to Director for the first time.** Use data, not narrative. The format: here's the domain, here's the current SLO compliance, here's the incident trend, here are the top 3 open risks, here's what I'm doing about each. Director gives feedback on the format and content — adjust based on it.

**Identify one process to improve.** Document the current state (what actually happens today, step by step), the proposed future state (what you want to happen), and the rationale (what problem this solves and what evidence supports it). Do not implement it yet. This becomes a Month 3 delivery.

**Day 60 Milestone:** Director gives explicit feedback on the domain status presentation. Director and manager agree on the format that will become the standing monthly deliverable going forward.

---

### Days 61–90: Own the Domain

**Deliver your 90-day domain assessment to Director.** Contents:
- Domain overview: services, SLOs, team roster, on-call rotation
- Current health: where the domain is meeting targets, where it isn't, supported by data
- Top 3 risks: specific, not abstract ("RabbitMQ broker cluster has a single-AZ deployment in prod — a zone failure takes down order processing" is a risk; "our messaging infrastructure could have reliability issues" is not)
- Priority order for next quarter: what gets addressed first and why
- One open question you want Director's input on

**Set Q1 goals with each direct report.** Goals are specific and measurable. "Improve reliability" is not a goal. "Reduce P2 incident count in the payments domain by 20% by end of Q1 through the alert tuning project" is a goal. Each goal has a clear definition of done and a check-in date.

**Implement the process improvement you identified in Days 31–60.** Communicate the change to the team: what changed, what they should do differently starting today, who to contact with questions.

**Deliver your first monthly domain metrics report in final format.** This becomes your standing monthly deliverable — same format, same metrics, every month. Consistency matters more than perfection; your VP-level audience needs to be able to compare month-over-month at a glance.

**Day 90 Review with Director:** The bar is: Director can say "I trust this person to run this domain without daily check-ins." That means the manager has demonstrated they understand the domain, can communicate its health in data, can make and communicate decisions, and have a clear view of what to do next quarter.

## Section 4 — Buddy Program

### Structure

Every new hire is assigned a buddy on Day 1. The buddy is a senior team member (Senior SRE, Staff Engineer, or experienced IC) — not the new hire's direct manager, not the Director. The buddy relationship runs for 90 days.

### Buddy Expectations

| Responsibility | Specifics |
|---------------|-----------|
| Weekly check-in | 30 minutes, standing calendar invite, buddy-led agenda |
| Async availability | Available via Slack for "dumb questions" within 4 business hours |
| Introductions | Makes at minimum 5 introductions to people outside the immediate team during the 90-day term |
| First artifact review | Reviews the new hire's first runbook or first IC incident log — gives written feedback |
| Orientation | Shows the new hire where things actually live vs. where they're documented — the trusted Confluence spaces, the active Slack channels, the runbooks that are real |

### What the Buddy Is Not

- The new hire's manager: do not direct their work, set their priorities, or assess their performance
- Their performance reviewer: buddy observations are not shared with the manager without the new hire's awareness
- Their on-call backup: the buddy is not in the on-call rotation as backup for the new hire
- A second manager: the buddy has no authority over the new hire's decisions

### Buddy Selection

The Director or domain manager selects the buddy based on:
1. Domain alignment — the buddy should know the systems the new hire will work on
2. Interpersonal fit — if you know from prior interactions that two people will clash, don't assign them
3. Current load — do not assign the most senior engineer on the team if they are already carrying > 8 pages/week or leading a major project

If no suitable buddy is available at hire time, delay assigning one for up to 5 business days rather than assigning the wrong person. A wrong buddy does more damage than a short delay.

### Buddy Offboarding

At Day 90, the buddy relationship formally ends. The Director or manager acknowledges the buddy's contribution — this is a significant investment of senior engineer time. Recognition can be as simple as a Slack shoutout in the team channel or a note in the engineer's performance documentation.
