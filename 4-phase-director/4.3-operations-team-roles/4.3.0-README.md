# Operations Team Roles — Framework Overview

**Audience**: Director of Operations and their management chain  
**Purpose**: Working reference for team design, role accountability, hiring, onboarding, and domain governance  
**Scope**: Mid-size enterprise operations teams (50–300 engineers, 200–2000 employees)

---

## What This Framework Covers

This framework gives a Director of Operations the structural scaffolding to build, staff, and run a high-functioning operations team from day one. It is not an academic overview — it is a working reference you open when you need to make a decision about your org.

The framework covers:

- **Team design**: org chart, span of control, scaling triggers, and where to start small
- **Role definitions**: mission, ownership, day-to-day activities, decision rights, escalation boundaries, and success/failure signals for every role on the team
- **Competency levels**: what "good" looks like at L3 through L7 across each discipline
- **RACI across domains**: who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed for every major operations domain
- **Hiring**: job descriptions, interview questions, hiring process, and evaluation criteria
- **Onboarding**: 30/60/90-day plans for new managers and new individual contributors
- **Team health**: recurring review cadence and health signals to watch

---

## Domains This Team Manages

Every role and team in this framework maps to one or more of these domains. Domain ownership is always named — no domain is "shared" without a designated primary owner.

| Domain | Brief Definition |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Incident Management** | Detection, triage, response coordination, and resolution of production outages and degradations |
| **Problem Management** | Root cause analysis, Known Error Database (KEDB), and permanent fix tracking post-incident |
| **Change & Release Management** | RFC governance, CAB process, Standard/Normal/Emergency change classification, and release pipeline governance |
| **Service Level Management** | SLO definition, SLA reporting, error budget tracking, and service health scorecards |
| **Availability & Continuity Management** | High availability design, DR runbooks, BCP ownership, and failover testing |
| **Environment Management** | Non-production environment provisioning, parity maintenance, and lifecycle management |
| **Performance Engineering** | Load testing, capacity planning, performance baselining, and bottleneck remediation |
| **Application Layer Taxonomy** | Tiering (Tier 0–3), criticality classification, dependency mapping, and service catalog ownership |
| **Architecture Principles & Governance** | ARB participation, architecture decision records (ADRs), standards enforcement, and technical debt tracking |
| **Application Portfolio & Transformation** | App inventory, health scores, EOL tracking, modernization roadmap, and decommission planning |

## How to Use This Framework

### New Director (first 90 days)

Read in this order:

1. **ch01** — Understand your org structure and what you're building toward
2. **ch02** — Learn every role's mission and decision rights before your first 1:1s
3. **ch03** — Map your current team to competency levels; identify gaps immediately

Then use **playbook/new-manager-onboarding-playbook.md** to structure your own 30/60/90.

### Hiring Manager (filling a specific role)

1. Go to **ch04** — hiring strategy and prioritization
2. Pull the relevant role definition from **ch02**
3. Use **templates/job-description-template.md** — fill in the blanks from the role definition
4. Pull interview questions from **reference/interview-question-bank.md**
5. Run the process per **playbook/hiring-process-playbook.md**

### New Team Member (joining the team)

1. Go to **ch07** — the onboarding guide is written for you
2. Ask your manager which domains you own from **ch02**
3. Understand the competency level expectations for your role from **ch03**
4. Ask for your delegation charter using **templates/delegation-charter-template.md**

### Quarterly Team Health Review

Run **playbook/team-health-review-playbook.md** every quarter. It takes 2 hours and surfaces burnout, coverage gaps, and skill atrophy before they become incidents.

## Core Principle

> **Accountability without clarity is theater.**

Every person on this team must be able to answer four questions about their role without hesitation:

1. What do I own? (domains and systems)
2. What can I decide without asking anyone? (decision rights)
3. What must I escalate? (escalation boundary)
4. How will I know if I'm doing it well? (metrics)

If anyone on your team cannot answer all four, the problem is structural — not a performance issue. Fix the structure first.

## A Note on Scale

The org model in this framework is calibrated for a mid-size enterprise: 50–300 engineers, 200–2000 employees, managing a platform of 50–300 services. Examples are grounded in enterprise middleware stacks — RabbitMQ, TIBCO, Kafka — and infrastructure patterns common in large-scale Kubernetes/EKS deployments with Splunk for observability.

If your org is smaller, ch01 includes explicit guidance on what to collapse and what to defer. If your org is larger, ch01 includes scaling triggers that tell you when headcount additions pay for themselves.
