# Chapter 01 — Team Structure and Org Design

**For**: Director of Operations  
**Purpose**: Understand what you are building, how each team is structured, and how to make smart sequencing decisions as you staff up

---

## The Org Chart

This is the target-state org for a mid-size enterprise operations team managing 50–300 services across a platform that includes enterprise middleware (RabbitMQ, TIBCO, Kafka), Kubernetes/EKS workloads, and Splunk-driven observability.

```
Director of Operations (L8)
│
├── Incident & Problem Management Manager (L6)
│   ├── Senior SRE — Incident Command (x2, L5)
│   ├── Problem Analyst (x1, L4)
│   └── On-Call Coordinator (x1, L3/L4)
│
├── Change & Release Management Manager (L6)
│   ├── Change Manager (x1, L4/L5)
│   ├── Release Engineer (x2, L4)
│   └── Environment Engineer (x1, L4)
│
├── Platform Reliability Manager (L6)
│   ├── SRE - Availability (x2, L4/L5)
│   ├── SRE - Performance Engineer (x1, L4/L5)
│   └── Service Level Analyst (x1, L3/L4)
│
└── Architecture & Transformation Lead (L7)
    ├── Platform Architect (x1, L7)
    ├── Transformation Engineer (x2, L5)
    └── Application Portfolio Analyst (x1, L3/L4)
```

**Direct reports to you**: 4 managers + 1 technical lead = 5  
**Total team headcount**: 17 (including you)  
**On-call rotation headcount**: minimum 5 (Senior SRE x2, SRE-Availability x2, SRE-Performance x1)

## Team 1: Incident & Problem Management

### What This Team Owns

This team owns the full lifecycle of production failure — from the moment an alert fires to the moment the root cause is permanently resolved.

**Domains**: Incident Management, Problem Management  
**Systems they live in daily**: Splunk (log search, alert triage), PagerDuty or equivalent on-call tooling, JIRA (problem tickets), the Known Error Database (KEDB), runbook libraries  
**Key interfaces**: every engineering team (consumers of incident response), Change & Release (post-incident change requests), Platform Reliability (SLO data during incidents)

**Specific responsibilities**:
- Own the SEV1/SEV2 incident response process end-to-end: detection → triage → incident command → resolution → post-mortem
- Maintain and improve the KEDB — every known production defect with a workaround must have an entry
- Facilitate post-incident reviews (PIRs) — the team does not write the PIR alone; they facilitate the engineering teams in writing it
- Own on-call rotation scheduling, runbook currency (no runbook older than 90 days without review), and escalation paths
- Drive root cause from suspected to confirmed — the Problem Analyst owns the investigation; the Senior SREs own the command

### Span of Control

The Incident Manager has 4 direct reports. This is appropriate because the team runs a 24/7 operational function. The manager's job is not to be on-call themselves — it is to run the system that makes on-call sustainable. A manager who is also on the rotation is a manager who cannot think clearly about the rotation.

**Minimum viable span**: 3 ICs (if you only have one Senior SRE and one Problem Analyst and one On-Call Coordinator, the manager still functions)  
**When span becomes a problem**: above 6 direct reports, an incident manager starts missing 1:1s and the quality of PIR facilitation drops

### Starting Small

If you are staffing this team from scratch with limited budget:

1. Hire the Incident Manager first — they set the culture of incident response before anyone else joins
2. Add one Senior SRE (Incident Command) — they become the first rotation anchor and runbook owner
3. Combine Problem Analyst and On-Call Coordinator into one role for the first 6–12 months (a strong L4 can hold both)
4. Add the second Senior SRE when on-call burden is above 3 pages/week per engineer averaged over a quarter

### Scaling Triggers

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| On-call burden exceeds 3 pages/week per engineer (90-day average) | Add Senior SRE or expand rotation eligibility to Platform Reliability |
| PIR backlog exceeds 10 unreviewed incidents | Hire or dedicate the Problem Analyst role immediately |
| More than 2 SEV1s per month are being RCA'd to "unknown" | The Problem Analyst is either missing or underwater — fix before adding more oncall capacity |
| Runbook coverage drops below 80% of Tier 1/2 services | Add On-Call Coordinator or assign runbook ownership as a formal OKR |

## Team 2: Change & Release Management

### What This Team Owns

This team owns every change that touches production. They are not a gatekeeper — they are the system that makes change safe and fast at the same time. The mistake directors make is letting this team become a rubber-stamp bureaucracy. Done right, they reduce MTTR by catching bad changes before they ship.

**Domains**: Change & Release Management, Environment Management  
**Systems they live in daily**: ServiceNow (or equivalent ITSM for RFC governance), Jenkins/GitHub Actions (pipeline observability), Kubernetes/EKS deployment dashboards, non-production environment tooling  
**Key interfaces**: every delivery team that ships to production, Architecture & Transformation (for major change reviews), Incident & Problem (post-incident emergency changes)

**Specific responsibilities**:
- Run the Change Advisory Board (CAB) — weekly meeting, all Normal changes reviewed and approved/rejected
- Maintain the Standard Change library — pre-approved change templates for routine operations (EKS node drain, RabbitMQ queue purge, Kafka partition reassignment, etc.)
- Own Emergency Change process — a defined fast-path that takes less than 30 minutes from request to approval for active SEV1s
- Own the release pipeline governance — they do not write the pipelines, but they approve the deployment model and set the standards
- Own non-production environments — parity checks against production config, environment reservation scheduling, and lifecycle management for dev/test/staging/perf environments

### Span of Control

The Change & Release Manager has 4 direct reports. This is lean for the scope. The manager earns their leverage by building reusable Standard Change templates — every new Standard Change reduces future CAB load.

**The ratio that matters**: Standard Changes vs. Normal Changes. A healthy org runs 70–80% Standard, 15–25% Normal, and under 5% Emergency. If you are above 20% Emergency, the Change Manager is in a reactive posture and cannot build the system.

### Starting Small

1. Hire the Change Manager (the individual contributor, not the manager) first if budget forces a choice — they can run CAB without a manager above them temporarily
2. The Release Engineers are the second hire — they integrate with delivery teams and start the Standard Change library
3. The Environment Engineer is often the last hire and the most painful gap: non-prod environments degrade silently until a major release fails in staging because the config was 3 months stale

### Scaling Triggers

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| CAB is reviewing more than 30 Normal changes per week | Standard Change library is too small — add Change Manager capacity or a dedicated Standard Change author |
| Release pipeline failures are above 5% | Add a second Release Engineer and give them a specific pipeline domain |
| Staging-to-production config drift is being discovered during incidents | The Environment Engineer role is either missing or not empowered — fix urgently |
| Emergency changes are being retroactively documented instead of pre-approved | The Emergency Change process is broken — Change Manager needs to redesign it with Incident Management |

## Team 3: Platform Reliability

### What This Team Owns

This team owns the long-horizon reliability of the platform. They are the difference between an operations team that fights fires and one that prevents them. The Incident team handles today's outage; this team makes sure next month's outage doesn't happen.

**Domains**: Availability & Continuity Management, Service Level Management, Performance Engineering  
**Systems they live in daily**: Splunk dashboards, Kubernetes/EKS cluster metrics (HPA, resource quotas, pod disruption budgets), load testing tools (k6, Gatling, or equivalent), SLO tracking platforms (Nobl9, Honeycomb, or internal), DR runbook repositories  
**Key interfaces**: Architecture & Transformation (capacity for new services), Incident & Problem (SLO data during incidents), business stakeholders (SLA reporting)

**Specific responsibilities**:
- Own SLO definitions for all Tier 0 and Tier 1 services — every service in those tiers must have an error budget
- Publish monthly SLA reports to business stakeholders — not engineers writing prose, but the Service Level Analyst producing a structured scorecard
- Own DR runbook authorship and testing — at minimum annual DR test per Tier 0 service; quarterly for any service with a new dependency
- Own load testing for every major release that touches a Tier 0/1 service — the Performance Engineer runs the test before the Change Manager approves the RFC
- Own availability architecture reviews — when an engineering team proposes a new service, this team reviews the HA design before it goes to ARB

### Span of Control

The Platform Reliability Manager has 4 direct reports. The two SRE-Availability roles and the SRE-Performance role will occasionally overlap with the Incident team's rotation — that is intentional. Cross-team on-call participation is a scaling mechanism, not a failure of org design.

**The skill gap to watch**: Availability and Performance are different muscles. An SRE who is excellent at DR drills often struggles with load testing design, and vice versa. Hire for both and keep them on different skills tracks.

### Starting Small

1. Start with one SRE-Availability — they anchor the DR program and SLO work
2. Add the Service Level Analyst second — you cannot report SLAs without someone whose full job is tracking the data
3. SRE-Performance is often the last hire and a common mistake is to leave it as a "nice to have." Do not. A production performance incident on a Kafka consumer group processing 50K messages/second without a performance baseline is a 6-hour outage. Hire this role before you need it.

### Scaling Triggers

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| SLO breach frequency is above 2 per month per Tier 0 service | Add SRE-Availability capacity or begin a formal availability review of the worst-offending service |
| DR tests are consistently finding gaps that take more than a sprint to fix | Add SRE-Availability and give them DR test ownership as their primary OKR |
| Performance regressions are being caught in production rather than pre-prod | Add or fully activate the SRE-Performance role; make load testing a hard gate in the release pipeline |
| SLA reporting takes more than 2 days to produce per month | The Service Level Analyst is either missing or the data pipeline is broken — fix the data first |

## Team 4: Architecture & Transformation

### What This Team Owns

This team owns the shape of the platform over the next 1–3 years. They do not build features. They do not run on-call. They ensure that the technical decisions made today do not become the operational nightmares of next year.

**Domains**: Architecture Principles & Governance, Application Portfolio & Transformation  
**Systems they live in daily**: architecture decision record (ADR) repositories, application inventory tooling, CMDB, JIRA (transformation project tracking), Confluence (architecture standards documentation)  
**Key interfaces**: every engineering team (standards and review), senior leadership (portfolio reporting and modernization roadmap), Platform Reliability (capacity planning for new services)

**Specific responsibilities**:
- Own the Architecture Review Board (ARB) process — the Platform Architect chairs, the Transformation Engineers prepare reviews, the Director attends as a stakeholder for major decisions
- Maintain the architecture principles library — document, enforce, and update standards for Kubernetes workload design, middleware topology (RabbitMQ cluster configuration, Kafka topic naming, TIBCO adapter patterns), and service communication
- Own the application inventory — every service in production has an entry: owner, tier, health score, age, dependencies, and EOL status
- Track and drive the modernization roadmap — identify services on EOL runtimes or legacy middleware, build the business case for migration, and own the project plan through completion
- Produce the quarterly application portfolio health report — a board-level artifact showing the state of the platform: healthy, degrading, at-risk

### Span of Control

The Architecture & Transformation Lead (L7) is a peer-level technical leader, not a manager in the traditional sense. They have 3 direct reports but their authority is influence-based, not hierarchical. They do their best work in ARB rooms and design reviews. If they are spending more than 20% of their time in operational tasks (on-call, incident response, pipeline work), something has gone wrong — they have been pulled in the wrong direction.

**The L7 distinction**: this role requires someone who can walk into a room of Staff Engineers and Principal Engineers and be taken seriously on technical merit. You cannot fill it with a good manager who is not deeply technical. It requires both.

### Starting Small

1. The Architecture & Transformation Lead is often the first hire in this quadrant and often the hardest to find. Accept that this role may take 3–6 months to fill correctly.
2. While the Lead is being hired, the Application Portfolio Analyst can be hired first — they build the inventory that the Lead will use to prioritize the modernization roadmap.
3. Transformation Engineers are the last hire — you need the Lead to know what to transform before you add execution capacity.

### Scaling Triggers

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| ARB review backlog exceeds 3 weeks | Add a second Platform Architect or delegate Standard Architecture decisions to a documented fast-path |
| Application inventory is more than 6 months out of date | The Portfolio Analyst is either missing or being used for the wrong work — protect their time |
| Modernization projects are consistently failing to complete | Add a Transformation Engineer and give them a single project with a named completion date |
| Architecture debt is being raised in incident PIRs at a rate of more than 2 per quarter | Escalate the modernization roadmap to a Director-level priority; the platform is degrading faster than the team can fix it |

## Team Design Principles

### 1. Domain Ownership: One Named Primary

Every domain in the framework has one named manager as primary owner. "Shared ownership" is not a management pattern — it is a blame-diffusion pattern. When something goes wrong in Problem Management, one person is accountable: the Incident & Problem Management Manager. Consultation is fine; shared accountability is not.

When two teams have overlapping responsibility for a domain (e.g., both Incident Management and Change Management touch a post-incident Emergency Change), define the handoff explicitly: Incident Management owns the request, Change Management owns the approval.

### 2. On-Call Rotation: Minimum Five Engineers

The minimum viable on-call rotation has five engineers. Below five, the math does not work: a five-person rotation gives each engineer one week on-call every five weeks. At four engineers, it is every four weeks. At three, it is every three weeks — which is the threshold where engineers start burning out within six months and leaving within twelve.

Your target on-call rotation pulls from: Senior SRE (x2), SRE-Availability (x2), SRE-Performance (x1). That is exactly five. If any of those roles are vacant, you have a rotation problem before you have a headcount problem. Escalate to hiring before the rotation degrades.

If a Kubernetes/EKS node pool failure or a RabbitMQ split-brain event at 2am hits and your best on-call engineer is on their third consecutive week of pages, your incident response quality collapses. This is a structural failure, not a people failure.

### 3. Bus Factor: No Single Keeper of Critical Knowledge

No single person on this team should be the only person who can do any of the following:
- Log into the Splunk admin console and modify extraction configs
- Perform a RabbitMQ cluster node replacement
- Run the DR failover script for a Tier 0 service
- Chair the CAB meeting
- Access the production KEDB and add a workaround entry

Identify your bus-factor-1 risks in your first 60 days. For each one, assign a backup who must demonstrate the capability within 90 days. Document the capability in a runbook. Review quarterly.

### 4. Career Ladder Visibility

The org structure must show a junior engineer where they can be in five years. This is a retention tool, not an HR formality. A concrete five-year path in this org:

- **Year 1–2**: On-Call Coordinator (L3/L4) — learns incident response mechanics, runbook authorship, rotation management
- **Year 2–3**: Problem Analyst (L4) — learns RCA methodology, KEDB management, PIR facilitation
- **Year 3–4**: Senior SRE (L5) — leads incident command on SEV1/2, mentors junior engineers on-call
- **Year 4–5**: Platform Reliability Manager (L6) — takes a manager track, or laterals to Platform Architect (L7) on the technical track

The org chart is the career map. If a person cannot see their path from where they are to where they want to be within the org, you will lose them to a team where the path is visible.

## Where to Start: First 90 Days as a New Director

You are not building the full org from scratch on day one. You are building it in phases. Here is the hiring sequence that gives you the highest return on the first six months of headcount:

**Phase 1 — Months 1–2: Foundation**
1. Incident & Problem Management Manager — sets the incident culture before any IC joins
2. Change & Release Management Manager — starts the Standard Change library before the team grows
3. Platform Reliability Manager — establishes SLO baselines before you have performance data gaps

**Phase 2 — Months 2–4: Operational Core**
4. Senior SRE (first) — first on-call anchor; mentor and runbook author
5. Change Manager — first CAB operator; starts reducing Emergency Change volume
6. SRE-Availability (first) — first DR program owner; begins Tier 0 coverage

**Phase 3 — Months 4–6: Specialization and Scale**
7. Architecture & Transformation Lead — starts ARB, begins application inventory audit
8. Senior SRE (second) — rotation is now viable at 5 with Phase 3 completions
9. Problem Analyst — PIR backlog should be visible by now; dedicate a person to it
10. Service Level Analyst — start producing SLA reports before the first business stakeholder asks

**Phase 4 — Months 6–12: Full Staffing**
Fill remaining roles based on which domains are showing the highest operational strain.

Do not hire all managers before any ICs. A manager without a team spends their time in meetings instead of building systems.

## Reporting Rhythm

The operations team runs on a layered cadence. Each meeting has a fixed purpose — do not let them collapse into one another.

### Daily

| Meeting | Participants | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|----------|---------|
| Ops Standup | All ICs + Managers | 15 min | Active incidents, today's changes, blockers |
| Incident Handoff | On-call engineers (outgoing + incoming) | 10 min | State of play for any open incidents or elevated alerts |

### Weekly

| Meeting | Participants | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|----------|---------|
| Change Advisory Board (CAB) | Change Manager, Release Engineers, representatives from each delivery team, Director (optional) | 60 min | Review and approve/reject all Normal change requests for the coming week |
| Operations Review | Director + 4 managers + Architecture Lead | 45 min | SLO status, change volume, incident count, problem backlog, open risks |
| 1:1s | Director with each direct report | 30 min each | Career, blockers, team health — not status updates |

### Monthly

| Meeting | Participants | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|----------|---------|
| SLA Report Review | Director + Platform Reliability Manager + Service Level Analyst + business stakeholders | 60 min | Published SLA data reviewed and signed off by business |
| Application Portfolio Review | Director + Architecture Lead + Portfolio Analyst | 45 min | Health scores, EOL risks, modernization project status |
| Team Health Review | Director (solo) | 90 min | Run the team health playbook; no managers present for initial assessment |

### Quarterly

| Meeting | Participants | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|----------|---------|
| DR Test | Platform Reliability Manager + SRE-Availability + on-call team | Half day | Execute at least one Tier 0 DR test; produce a findings report |
| Competency Review | Director + each manager | 30 min per manager | Assess IC competency levels; identify promotions, PIPs, and skill gaps |
| ARB Strategy Review | Architecture Lead + Platform Architect + Director | 60 min | Review architecture principles for currency; update ADRs; reprioritize modernization |

## T-Mobile Scale — Grounding Examples

The design decisions in this chapter are calibrated for the complexity of an enterprise platform comparable to T-Mobile's operations environment:

- **Middleware topology**: A platform running both RabbitMQ (async event processing between microservices) and TIBCO (legacy SOA integration layer still active for core billing/order flows) and Kafka (streaming for customer data events) represents three different failure modes, three different runbook libraries, and three different on-call skill profiles. The Incident team must be competent in all three.
- **Kubernetes/EKS**: A Kubernetes cluster running 150+ microservices across three availability zones introduces HPA misconfiguration, node pressure, and pod eviction as common incident patterns that require both Incident Management expertise and Platform Reliability Architecture knowledge. The SRE-Availability role exists specifically because these incidents are architectural, not just operational.
- **Splunk**: At enterprise scale, Splunk is the authoritative observability layer. The Senior SRE's competency in SPL (Splunk Processing Language) is not optional — it is the primary tool for incident triage. A Senior SRE who cannot write a search to pivot from an alert threshold breach to the relevant application log within five minutes of being paged is not yet Senior SRE qualified.

When you are evaluating candidates for this team, ground your interview questions in these platforms. Generic SRE interview responses that do not reference actual platform complexity are a yellow flag at L4 and a red flag at L5.
