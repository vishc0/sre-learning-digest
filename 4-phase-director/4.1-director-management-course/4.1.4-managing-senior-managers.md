# Module 04: The Middle Layer
## Managing People Who Manage People Who Manage People

---

## The Hardest Transition

The move from IC to manager is hard. The move from managing ICs to managing managers who manage managers is harder — and most people are not prepared for it because no one explains what changes.

When you managed ICs, you had direct visibility. You could read the code. You could sit in the ticket review. You could pair on the problem. Feedback was immediate and specific.

When you manage Senior Managers, that visibility disappears. The feedback loops are longer. The signals are indirect. The standard tools — code review, pairing, direct observation — no longer apply. You are now managing through other people, and the quality of your judgment about those people determines the quality of everything below them.

Most new Directors compensate by staying too close to the work — reviewing tickets, joining incident bridges, answering questions that should go to the Senior Manager. This feels productive. It is not. It undermines the Senior Managers, creates bottlenecks at your level, and prevents you from doing the actual work of your job.

This module is about how to manage the Senior Manager layer effectively: setting up the relationship correctly, holding accountability without micromanaging, building capability, and running your Senior Managers as a unit rather than as independent silos.

---

## Section 1: The Director–Senior Manager Relationship

Your Senior Managers are your amplifiers. Every decision you make, every priority you set, every expectation you communicate — it all flows through them to the org below. If the relationship is not working, nothing else works.

**What the Senior Manager needs from you:**

**Clear authority.** The most common Senior Manager complaint about Directors is ambiguity about what decisions they own. Without explicit clarity, Senior Managers either over-escalate (constantly asking for your approval on things they could decide themselves, clogging your calendar) or under-escalate (making calls that actually needed your input, and you find out after). Both are expensive.

Have the explicit conversation: "Here is what you decide on your own. Here is what you bring to me before acting. Here is what I need to know about even if you have it handled." Do this in the first 30 days. Revisit it when scope changes.

**Air cover.** Senior Managers should not be spending their energy absorbing organizational friction from above — that is your job. When Finance asks why headcount is behind plan, you answer that. When a peer VP has a complaint about your org, you handle that. The Senior Manager's job is to manage their domain. Yours is to protect the space in which they can do that.

**Development.** Quarterly conversations about where they are going, not just what they are doing. Most Senior Managers at this level are ambitious. If they cannot see a path from where they are to where they want to be — within your org or beyond it — you are a retention risk.

**Feedback.** Specific, timely, behavior-based. Not "you're doing great" at review time. Not vague discomfort murmured in a 1:1. Specific: "In the cross-team review on Tuesday, you shut down the conversation about the monitoring gap before we had a resolution. I need you to hold that space open longer even when it's uncomfortable." That is usable feedback.

**What you need from your Senior Managers:**

**Weekly signal that is actually honest.** Not sanitized status. Not everything described as "on track." What is actually going on, including the things that are uncomfortable to surface. A Senior Manager who only tells you good news is a liability — you will be blindsided.

**Real escalations.** Things they genuinely cannot resolve at their level, or decisions that require your authority or visibility. Not escalations that are actually avoidance — things they don't want to own or conversations they don't want to have.

**Visibility into their team before you need it.** "I think we have a retention risk on [engineer]" should come from the Senior Manager before it comes from an exit interview. Early warning, not post-mortem reporting.

## Section 2: The 1:1 With a Senior Manager

30–45 minutes, weekly. Not a status report — status comes in the ops review. This is a management conversation about the Senior Manager's work as a manager, not a review of their team's operational metrics.

**Structure:**

**First 5 minutes — let them set the agenda.**
"What's the most important thing we should talk about today?"

If the answer is always "nothing" or "everything is fine," that is data. Either they don't trust you enough to surface real problems, they are conflict-averse, or they genuinely don't have visibility into their own domain's issues. All three are worth probing. "Nothing is fine" is the answer from a Senior Manager who is performing at a very high level in a very stable environment. That is rare.

**Middle 20 minutes — focused conversation on what they raised.**
Your job here is to ask questions, not give answers.

- "What have you tried?"
- "What's stopping that approach?"
- "What would you do if I weren't available to consult on this?"
- "What does the manager/engineer involved think?"

You are coaching, not solving. If you solve the problem for them in the 1:1, you have taught them to bring problems to you instead of developing their own judgment. The Senior Manager should leave the 1:1 more capable of handling this situation, not dependent on your answer.

**Last 10 minutes — your agenda.**
What do you need from them this week? What organizational context should they have that they don't? Is there anything coming from above that affects their planning?

**What to listen for:**

**Escalations in disguise.** "I'm just giving you visibility that X is a problem" often means "I want you to solve this." Clarify: "Are you looking for me to act on this, or are you giving me visibility so I can support you while you handle it?" The answer tells you whether they are developing their agency or offloading ownership.

**Protection-seeking vs. real lateral problems.** "The other team is being difficult about the environment deployment window" might be a real cross-team conflict requiring your involvement. Or it might be a coaching opportunity — the Senior Manager needs to develop the relationships and negotiation skills to resolve this themselves. Ask which it is before assuming it needs you.

**How they talk about their direct reports.** "Everyone is stretched" tells you very little. "Two of my managers are at risk of burning out because the on-call rotation hasn't been rebalanced since we onboarded the new service in Q2" tells you they have visibility and a handle on what's driving it. Probe until you get specifics.

**The 1:1 mistake most Directors make:**
Turning it into a status update, or filling the time with your own priorities and action items. This converts the 1:1 into a reporting session. The Senior Manager leaves feeling like a reporting unit, not a leader. The test: is the Senior Manager doing most of the talking? Are they leaving with clarity about their own next steps? If not, you are using the time wrong.

## Section 3: Holding Senior Managers Accountable

Accountability at this level is different from holding an IC accountable. You cannot review their work directly. What you can do is define outcomes clearly, review those outcomes regularly, and have honest conversations when the outcomes are not being met.

**Outcome accountability:**

Every Senior Manager should own a set of outcomes that you review monthly — not as a performance review, but as a management conversation.

Examples of Senior Manager-owned outcomes in an SRE/Operations org:
- MTTR for incidents in their domain
- Problem backlog aging (unresolved known issues)
- Change failure rate for their services
- On-call load (pages per engineer per week)
- Team retention rate
- New hire ramp time to full contribution

When an outcome is below target: "MTTR went up this month — walk me through what drove that and what the plan is." Not "why did this happen" as an accusation. As a genuine request for diagnosis.

Distinguish between outcomes they control and outcomes that were genuinely outside their control. A spike in on-call volume because a vendor pushed a bad firmware update is not the same as a spike caused by unreviewed changes to a service they own. Don't penalize the former — but probe both, because understanding root cause is part of the job.

**Behavior accountability:**

Outcomes tell you what happened. Behavior accountability is about how the Senior Manager is doing their job — and this requires more judgment.

- Are they developing their own managers? (Their managers' growth and retention is a Senior Manager responsibility)
- Are they building effective lateral relationships? (Do peer teams trust them and work with them effectively?)
- Are they communicating up accurately? (Are you getting real signal or sanitized signal?)
- Are they making decisions within their authority? (Or escalating things that are actually theirs to decide?)

These do not have clean metrics. You develop judgment on these through the 1:1, through skip-levels, through feedback from peer Directors, and through observing how the Senior Manager shows up in group settings.

**The accountability conversation that stays productive:**

Lead with data, not opinion: "MTTR has been above target for 8 consecutive weeks."

Separate diagnosis from judgment: "Walk me through what you think is driving it."

Identify the type of problem: Is this a **skill gap** (they don't know how to improve MTTR)? A **will gap** (they're not prioritizing it)? Or a **situation gap** (they don't have the resources, authority, or information to fix it)?

End with clarity: "Here's what I need from you by [specific date]" — not "let's see how it goes." Ambiguous accountability is not accountability.

**The timing failure most Directors make:**

Waiting. A Senior Manager who has been underperforming for 6 months without a direct conversation interprets that silence as acceptable performance. By the time you try to have the hard conversation at month 9, they are blindsided and defensive, and you have destroyed the trust needed for the feedback to land. Have the conversation at month 2 when the pattern is emerging, not after it has calcified.

## Section 4: The Skip-Level 1:1

As a Director, you should periodically meet with the managers and occasionally with ICs who are one or two levels below your direct reports. This is not to bypass your Senior Managers. It is to maintain an independent pulse check on org health.

**Why skip-levels are necessary:**

Your Senior Managers will — not out of malice, but out of human nature — filter and shape the signal they send you. They protect their teams, soften difficult news, or may not have full visibility into what their managers are experiencing. Skip-levels give you an unmediated view.

What skip-levels reveal that Senior Manager 1:1s do not:
- Whether the Senior Manager's communication to their team is accurate and aligned with your direction
- Whether there are management issues the Senior Manager is not surfacing (or not seeing)
- Retention risk signals before they surface as exit conversations
- Whether the Senior Manager is developing their managers or just directing them

**How to run them without undermining your Senior Managers:**

Tell the Senior Manager in advance: "I want to do skip-levels with your managers this quarter to get a broader view of the org. I'll share my themes with you afterward." No surprises. No secrecy.

In the skip-level conversation: listen, do not direct. You are gathering signal, not issuing instructions. If a manager surfaces a problem they think you can fix, your response is: "Have you raised this with [Senior Manager]? What happened?" You are not there to solve their problems — you are there to understand whether the management layer above them is working.

After the skip-level: share themes (not individual names) with the Senior Manager. "I heard from a few people that on-call load is feeling unsustainable — how are you thinking about that?" This creates a feedback loop without burning the source.

**Frequency:** Quarterly at minimum. Monthly if you have reason to believe a domain is stressed — retention risk, performance concerns about the Senior Manager, or a recent significant incident.

## Section 5: When a Senior Manager Is Struggling

Signs a Senior Manager is not performing at the level the role requires:

- Domain metrics are consistently below target with no improving trend and no credible plan
- Team has elevated attrition or morale issues they are not surfacing or addressing
- They escalate decisions that are within their authority — repeatedly
- Peer teams consistently experience friction working with their team
- In 1:1s, they describe symptoms but don't come with diagnoses or plans
- You are being pulled into their operational details regularly — for decisions and conversations they should be handling

**The conversation you must have early:**

Do not wait for a review cycle. The moment you see a pattern — not a single bad week, but a pattern — have the conversation.

Frame: "I'm seeing [specific observable behavior or outcome]. It's concerning me. Here's why. I want to understand what's going on."

Diagnosis first: Is this a **skill gap** (they don't know how to do what the role requires), a **will gap** (they are not motivated or committed), or a **situation gap** (they don't have what they need to succeed)?

| Gap Type | What It Looks Like | Response |
|----------|--------------------|---------|
| Skill gap | They want to do it but don't know how | Coaching, specific training, pair-solving with you on 1–2 real examples |
| Will gap | They know how but aren't doing it | Harder — explore root cause first (burnout? misalignment? personal situation?). Be honest about stakes. |
| Situation gap | They can't do it because of missing resources, authority, or clarity | Fix the situation. Clear the obstacles. Adjust scope. |

Will gaps are the hardest. A Senior Manager who has lost motivation or conviction is not going to be fixed by better coaching. Have the honest conversation: "I want to make sure you're in the right role. Are you?" Sometimes the answer surprises you. Sometimes it confirms what you feared. Either way, clarity serves both of you.

**If improvement is not happening:**

If you have had the direct conversation, identified the gap, provided specific support, set a clear timeline, and the trajectory is not improving — the next step is an honest conversation about fit. This should not be a surprise to the Senior Manager by the time it happens. Every step prior should have made the stakes clear.

Performance management at this level is the Director's responsibility, not HR's. HR supports the process. You own the relationship and the conversation.

## Section 6: Building Senior Manager Capability

Your Senior Managers are only as capable as you develop them to be. The most effective Directors invest in this continuously, not just at review time.

**Stretch assignments:**

Give Senior Managers work outside their domain. The Senior Manager who owns incident management can lead the vendor renegotiation. The Senior Manager who owns platform reliability can lead the cross-team on-call redesign. This builds:
- Cross-functional judgment they will need as they advance
- Relationships with peers they wouldn't otherwise develop
- Confidence in operating beyond their domain

Stretch assignments also give you data about their ceiling — how they handle ambiguity, how they build influence without authority, how they perform under conditions that are genuinely new.

**Executive visibility:**

Bring Senior Managers to your quarterly business reviews. Have them present a section — not as a reporting exercise, but as a development one. Presenting to VP-level and above is a skill that requires reps. Give them the reps in a setting where you can debrief afterward.

The debrief: "How did that feel? What would you do differently? What did you notice about how [VP name] engaged with the content?" This is the learning. The presentation is the practice.

**Debrief every significant event:**

After a major incident, a vendor failure, a difficult org change — sit with the Senior Manager and debrief as managers, not as incident commanders.

"Walk me through how you managed the team through that. What decisions did you make? What were you uncertain about? What would you do differently?"

This is informal coaching that compounds. A Senior Manager who has debriefed 20 significant events with you has developed pattern recognition that no training course provides.

**Peer learning:**

If two Senior Managers are facing the same challenge — say, unsustainable on-call load — do not solve it separately for each of them. Put them in a room and ask them to solve it together. You get two benefits: a better solution (they have more context than you do about their specific constraints) and a working relationship between them that will generate future collaboration.

**The Senior Manager development conversation:**

Quarterly. Not a formal HR document — a direct conversation.

"Here's where I think you are. Here's where I want to see you in 12 months. Here are the specific things that would close that gap."

Three elements: current state (honest), target state (specific), and the path (actionable). This conversation takes 30 minutes. Do not skip it.

## Section 7: Running the Senior Manager Team as a Unit

Your Senior Managers are not independent functional heads. They are a leadership team. The difference is significant: independent functional heads optimize for their own domain; a leadership team optimizes for the org.

Most Director-level orgs do not have a real leadership team at the Senior Manager level — they have a collection of people who happen to report to the same Director. Building a real team requires deliberate investment.

**Weekly ops review (all Senior Managers together, 50 minutes):**

Structure this as a shared visibility session, not a reporting session.

- Each Senior Manager has 8–10 minutes: domain health, key metrics, what they need from other domains this week.
- 15 minutes for cross-domain issues: problems that require two domains to solve together.
- 5 minutes: Director context — what's coming from above, organizational changes, priorities for the week.

What this session creates:
- Every Senior Manager knows what is happening in every domain — no information silos
- Cross-domain problem-solving happens in the room, not in separate bilateral conversations with you
- Collective accountability: they can see each other's metrics, and peer accountability is real

What it requires from you: not running the session as a Director briefing. Run it as a team problem-solving session. If you are talking more than 20% of the time, the format is wrong.

**Quarterly offsite (2–4 hours, not on operations):**

If budget allows, take the Senior Manager team off-site quarterly for a working session that is not about incident metrics and deployment schedules. The agenda: team health, cross-domain friction, 12-month strategy, and what they need from each other.

This session builds the trust that makes the weekly ops review actually work. People who have had an honest conversation about what is not working across domain lines are better equipped to work across those lines when the pressure is on.

**The leadership team failure mode:**

Senior Managers who optimize for their domain at the expense of the org. This surfaces as: "that's not my problem" when their environment failures are driving incident volume in another team's domain. Or credit-claiming for successes that required cross-domain work. Or withholding information from peers that would help them.

You see this failure mode early in how Senior Managers talk about each other in 1:1s. "The platform team isn't being helpful" — probe it. Is this a real collaboration failure? A misunderstanding? Or the Senior Manager describing a peer team as a blocker because that is easier than solving the problem together?

The fix: make cross-domain collaboration visible and valued. When a Senior Manager solves a problem across domain lines — call it out explicitly. When two Senior Managers disagree in the ops review and work it out in the room — that is a team. When they take it to separate 1:1s with you — it is not.

## What Good Looks Like

**At the Senior Manager level:**
- Making domain decisions confidently without waiting for your approval
- Surfacing real escalations when they genuinely need your authority or input
- Developing their own managers — not just directing them
- Operating as a leadership team rather than parallel functional silos

**At your level:**
- Your 1:1s are management conversations, not status reports
- You have an honest view of every domain through a combination of Senior Manager signal and skip-level pulse checks
- You are not regularly pulled into operational decisions that belong to the Senior Manager layer
- Your Senior Managers are growing — individually and as a team

**What failure looks like:**
- You are the de facto decision-maker for your Senior Managers' domains
- Senior Managers blame each other for cross-domain problems rather than solving them
- Skip-levels surface things your Senior Managers were not telling you
- You are the bottleneck for your own org's execution

The Director's job is not to be the best manager in the room. It is to build a layer of Senior Managers who are better managers of their domains than you could ever be from the outside. Everything in this module is in service of that outcome.
