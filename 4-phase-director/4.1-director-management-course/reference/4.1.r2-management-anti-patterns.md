# Management Anti-Patterns

**Audience**: Director of Operations  
**Purpose**: The most common Director-level management failures — what each looks like, why smart people fall into it, and how to course-correct  
**When to read**: First 90 days. Then quarterly, as a self-audit.

---

## How to Use This Document

Read each pattern, then ask: "Do I do this?" The honest answer matters more than the comfortable one. Most Directors have two or three of these running in their org at any given time. The goal is not to eliminate them all at once — it is to name them, own them, and work on the highest-impact ones first.

The patterns are listed in roughly the order of organizational damage they cause.

---

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Brilliant Jerk Promotion

**What it looks like:**
You promote your best technical Senior SRE to Senior Manager because they are brilliant — they solve problems no one else can, they know the platform cold, and they have strong opinions about how things should be done. Six months later, you have a technically excellent manager who runs their team through fear, dismisses teammates who challenge them, and is quietly hollowing out the team's confidence and retention.

**Why it happens:**
Technical excellence is visible. It produces results you can point to: incidents resolved, platforms stabilized, tickets closed. Management capability is invisible until it is already broken. The indicators of future management success — seeking input before deciding, coaching rather than solving, tolerating disagreement without shutting it down — are rarely on the checklist when the promotion decision is made.

The Director makes the promotion because it solves their problem (they need a manager) and rewards the person they rely on most. It often simultaneously destroys the team below.

**How to avoid it:**
Before promoting to a management role, assess these specific behaviors — not as hypotheticals, but based on observed evidence:
- Do they seek input from peers and junior engineers before making technical decisions? Or do they tell?
- Do others seek their advice proactively? Or do they approach them only when required?
- When they have context another person lacks, do they teach or do they correct?
- When their idea is challenged, can they change their position? Or do they make the challenger regret it?
- Are they managing their own stress in a way that is not visible to their team? Or do they leak it?

If the answers are mostly "tell / correct / make challengers regret it / leak stress" — you have a brilliant technical contributor, not a manager. Promote them to a Staff or Principal role, not a management role. Brilliant jerks in IC roles are sometimes manageable. Brilliant jerks in management roles are org-level problems that take years to clean up.

**How to course-correct if you've already made this promotion:**
- Name the behaviors you are observing. Specific, behavioral, factual.
- Set clear expectations: "A manager who produces fear in their team is not a manager who is succeeding — regardless of their technical output."
- Give 90 days with coaching and clear behavioral targets.
- If the behavior does not change, the manager needs to move to an IC role or exit. This is the hardest decision a Director makes. It is almost always the right one.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Inbox Manager

**What it looks like:**
The Director's entire day is reactive. Slack has 400 unread messages. Email is a triage exercise. The calendar is a series of escalation bridges, status calls, and meetings that accumulated because no one else could handle them. Strategic work — roadmap building, vendor relationship management, team development — never happens because tactical demands always fill the space. By 6pm, the Director has been "busy" all day and accomplished nothing that matters in 90 days.

**Why it happens:**
Reactive work has the property of feeling urgent and important at the same time. It arrives at high volume and produces clear feedback: if you respond to an escalation, it goes away. Strategic work has no clear signal, no immediate feedback loop, and the consequences of not doing it are weeks away. Brains naturally prefer the task that gives immediate relief.

The structural driver: the Director was promoted out of a Senior Manager role where responsiveness was the primary leadership behavior. No one told them the job had changed.

**How to fix it:**
The fix is structural, not motivational. Willpower is not enough.

Step 1 — Audit your week. For one full week, track every 30-minute block: was it reactive (responding to something) or generative (producing something that did not exist before)? Most Directors find 80–90% reactive. This is not normal — it is a dysfunction.

Step 2 — Block generative time first. Book two 90-minute blocks per week (minimum) on your calendar for strategic work. Name them. Treat them as committed appointments. The first thing that should be cancelled when the week gets busy is not these blocks — it is everything else.

Step 3 — Reroute first-pass escalations. If a Slack message from a Senior Manager reaches you and it could have been resolved by that Senior Manager, ask: "What would you recommend?" Then require them to implement their own recommendation. This takes longer in the short run. It builds a self-sufficient team in the long run.

Step 4 — Define your response time expectations. "I respond to Slack in < 4 hours during business hours. For P1 situations, page me." This sets expectations and removes the implicit expectation that you are always reachable in real time.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Invisible Manager

**What it looks like:**
The Director's calendar is full of meetings. Metrics get reviewed. Operational reviews happen on schedule. But the Senior Managers do not feel managed. 1:1s are the first thing cancelled when the calendar gets full. Feedback — both recognition and redirection — is rare or absent. Development conversations have never happened. Senior Managers feel technically capable and personally unseen.

The result: the Director is surprised when a Senior Manager gives notice, when a team is quietly dysfunctional, or when a performance issue has metastasized into a morale crisis.

**Why it happens:**
Meetings feel like management. They produce activity, they have agendas, they generate action items. 1:1s feel less productive — the output is harder to measure, the discomfort is higher (especially feedback conversations), and the benefit is invisible until it has compounded over months.

The Director tells themselves they are managing through the ops review and the metrics cadence. They are managing the work. They are not managing the people.

**How to fix it:**
Protect 1:1 time with the same priority you give to executive meetings. A cancelled 1:1 sends a message. The message is: "My time is more important than your development." Senior Managers hear it even when they say they understand.

For every Senior Manager, maintain a simple running document (can be a shared note):
- What are they working on this month that is stretching them?
- What feedback have I given them recently?
- When did I last have a development conversation (not a work update)?
- What do I know about their career aspirations and how am I actively supporting them?

If any of these answers is "I don't know" or "not recently" — you are invisible.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Information Hoarder

**What it looks like:**
The Director has context from executive conversations that their Senior Managers do not have. Organizational changes are coming that the Director knows about but hasn't communicated. Strategic direction has shifted but the teams are still executing against the old plan. The Director knows why a budget decision was made — a decision that affects every headcount ask — but hasn't told anyone.

The consequence: Senior Managers are making resource, priority, and hiring decisions based on incomplete information. Some of those decisions will be wrong. When they discover the information they were missing, they will feel disrespected — and they will be right.

**Why it happens:**
Directors are told things in confidence legitimately — executive reorganizations, acquisition discussions, personnel decisions, financial targets that have not been communicated. This is appropriate.

The error is over-generalizing what must be kept confidential. Most of what Directors hear in executive meetings is not confidential — it is just contextual information that hasn't been formally communicated yet. The Director defaults to holding it because they are not sure what they can say.

**How to fix it:**
Default to sharing, not holding. Apply a simple filter to every piece of information:
- Is this explicitly told to me in confidence? (Rare)
- Is sharing this likely to cause harm? (Even rarer)
- If neither: share it.

When you cannot share the detail, share the direction: "I can't share specifics yet, but the direction is X. You should plan for Y." This is almost always better than silence.

A team that has context makes better decisions. A team that is information-starved makes local optimizations that contradict organizational direction — and then feels betrayed when that direction is revealed.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Consensus Seeker

**What it looks like:**
The Director calls meetings, seeks input, facilitates discussion, and builds alignment — and makes very few decisions. Team members feel heard in the process but see little action. Decisions that should take a week take two months. The team starts working around the Director rather than through them, because they know the Director will want to discuss it further.

When a decision does get made, it is hedged: "Based on what I'm hearing from the team..." rather than "Here is what we are going to do and why." The team has no clear signal about what the Director actually thinks.

**Why it happens:**
Many Directors came from technical leadership cultures where consensus-building was a virtue and top-down decisions were considered dysfunctional. That instinct is correct in certain domains (technology choice, architectural direction) and incorrect in others (organizational structure, priority calls, resource allocation, personnel decisions).

The over-corrected version of "collaborative leader" becomes "no one leads." The team fills the vacuum — often with the most aggressive voice, not the most correct one.

**How to fix it:**
Distinguish between decisions that benefit from consensus and decisions that require a Director call.

Decisions that benefit from consensus: standards, process design, tooling selection for the team, working agreements. These decisions will be implemented by the people in the room — their buy-in is load-bearing.

Decisions that require a Director call: resource allocation, priority trade-offs, personnel, vendor relationships, risk acceptance, cross-functional commitments. These decisions require authority and clarity. Seeking consensus on them does not produce better decisions — it produces slower ones, and it confuses the team about who is accountable.

The pattern to practice: "I've heard your input. Here is my decision. Here is why. Here is what I need from you to execute it." Say it. Move on.

## Anti-Pattern 6: The Metric Theater

**What it looks like:**
The team has beautiful dashboards. There are charts for everything. Weekly ops reviews have a metrics section. Reports are generated, sent, and archived. The metrics are technically accurate and visually impressive.

And nothing changes because of them.

Metrics go red. Ops review notes it. Next week the metric is still red. It is noted again. Someone asks "what is the plan?" A plan is mentioned in vague terms. The metric is still red next week. It becomes part of the background — a number that is red, has been red, and will be red. The team has normalized it.

**Why it happens:**
Metrics are easier to create than to act on. Building a dashboard is a one-time effort that produces visible output. Acting on what a metric reveals often requires difficult conversations, resource trade-offs, or process changes. Those are harder and more uncomfortable.

The Director approves more dashboards because dashboards feel like progress. They are not progress — they are visibility. Progress is what happens because of the visibility.

**How to fix it:**
For every metric in your operational review, define in writing:
- **What does "red" mean?** (The specific threshold)
- **Who owns the action when this goes red?** (A named person, not a team)
- **What is the action?** (Specific, within 48 hours)
- **What would make this metric close?** (What change makes it go back to green?)

If you cannot answer all four questions for a metric, do not put it in your ops review. It is decorative.

Apply this to your existing metric set. You will likely find that 40–60% of your current metrics have no defined action. Remove them until you have defined the action, or until you are ready to define the action.

## Anti-Pattern 7: The Over-delegator

**What it looks like:**
The Director delegates everything — decisions, vendor relationships, executive presentations, hiring, and budget management — down to Senior Managers. When asked about a domain, the Director says "talk to [Senior Manager]." When a decision needs to be made, the Director says "you have the authority to decide that." When a vendor escalates, the Director says "my Senior Manager handles that relationship."

Senior Managers feel unsupported and unseen. Decisions fall through the cracks because no one is certain who has final authority. Peers and executives lose confidence in the Director because they cannot get a direct answer from them.

**Why it happens:**
Over-delegation is the over-correction from micromanagement. The Director, having been told to "trust their team" and "delegate more," has removed themselves entirely. They have confused abdication with empowerment.

True delegation means: clear ownership transferred, outcome accountability maintained, Director still reachable when the Senior Manager needs support or when the decision requires Director authority.

**How to fix it:**
Map every domain and decision type to one of three categories:
- **Fully delegated**: Senior Manager decides and executes. Director reviews outcome. No Director involvement in the decision.
- **Senior Manager recommends, Director decides**: Senior Manager does the analysis and brings a recommendation. Director makes the final call.
- **Director owns**: Senior Managers inform. Director decides and is accountable externally.

Be explicit with each Senior Manager about which category applies to which decisions. The goal is not to reclaim authority you delegated — it is to be clear about the authority structure so neither of you is surprised.

Check in on delegated work through outcomes, not process. "How did the vendor negotiation land?" is a check-in. "Walk me through every step of the vendor negotiation" is micromanagement.

## Self-Audit Checklist

Use this quarterly. Be honest.

| Anti-pattern | Honest self-assessment | Evidence (specific behavior you've observed in yourself) |
|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| Brilliant Jerk Promotion | I assess management potential separately from technical capability before promoting | |
| Inbox Manager | I have protected blocks of generative time that I actually protect | |
| Invisible Manager | I have given meaningful feedback — both positive and redirectional — to each Senior Manager this quarter | |
| Information Hoarder | My Senior Managers have the context they need to make good decisions in their domains | |
| Consensus Seeker | I make clear decisions and communicate them with my reasoning, without waiting for consensus | |
| Metric Theater | Every metric in my ops review has a defined action when it goes red, and I act on it | |
| Over-delegator | I know the status of every major initiative in my org and can speak to it without reading notes | |

For any row where your honest assessment is "no" or "sometimes": name one specific change you will make in the next 30 days. Not a resolution — a specific, observable behavior change.
