# Management Reading List

**Audience**: Director of Operations — stepping in, scaling up  
**Purpose**: Curated reading, organized by topic and timing, with context on what each book actually teaches and when it is most useful  
**Cadence**: One book per quarter. Journal three things per book that apply to your current situation. Discuss one of those three with a peer Director or mentor.

---

## How to Use This List

Do not read these in order. Read them in response to what you are dealing with right now.

Starting a new role? Read *The Manager's Path* first.  
Struggling with a budget conversation? Read *The Pyramid Principle* before your next executive presentation.  
About to enter a vendor contract negotiation? Read *Never Split the Difference* first.  
Watching a Senior Manager fail to give feedback? Read *Radical Candor* and hand them a copy.

The books in the Foundational section have no prerequisites and maximum signal density. Start there.

## Foundational — Read in the First 90 Days

### *The Manager's Path* — Camille Fournier

**What it teaches:**
The full engineering management ladder, from Tech Lead through VP of Engineering — each role described from the inside. What the job actually looks like day to day. What mistakes people at each level make. What you owe your team at each level. The chapter on Engineering Director and the VP chapters are the ones most directly applicable to your role.

**Why it matters for you:**
Fournier wrote this for people who came up through engineering operations. She describes, with precision, the traps that technical people fall into when they take Director and VP roles — and how to avoid them. She is also clear that the skills that made you successful as a Senior Manager will actively work against you as a Director if you do not consciously change them.

**When to read:** Week 2 of your new role. Read the Director chapter immediately. Return to the VP chapter in Month 3 when you are building your leadership strategy.

**Key passage to find:** The section on "getting comfortable with being uncomfortable with information asymmetry" — the idea that the higher you go, the less complete information you will have when you must decide.

---

### *High Output Management* — Andy Grove

**What it teaches:**
Written by Intel's CEO in 1983. Still the best single-volume description of what management actually is and how to measure it. The core concept: managerial output is not what you produce directly — it is the output of your organization plus the output of the organizations you influence. Everything else follows from that.

The "one-on-ones" chapter alone is worth the read. Grove's argument that a Senior Manager who is surprised by a problem has failed, because the 1:1 exists to surface problems before they become surprises, is as accurate today as it was at Intel.

**Why it matters for you:**
Grove's lens is production-line management adapted for knowledge work. As a Director with an operations function, you are running a production system — incidents are production failures, reliability is quality, on-call is the maintenance floor. His frameworks map directly.

**When to read:** Month 1. Read it in parallel with your operational observation, not after. You will immediately see your org through his frameworks.

**Key concept to internalize:** "Managerial leverage" — the idea that some activities produce 10x the output of other activities, and that the Director's job is to identify and spend time on high-leverage activities. The anti-pattern this resolves: the Inbox Manager (see Anti-Patterns document).

---

### *Turn the Ship Around!* — L. David Marquet

**What it teaches:**
Marquet took command of the USS Santa Fe, the lowest-performing submarine in the US Navy fleet, and turned it into the highest-performing in less than a year — without replacing a single crew member. The mechanism: moving from leader-follower (the leader decides, everyone else executes) to leader-leader (every crew member given the authority and competence to make decisions).

His practical mechanism: changing language from "Request permission to..." to "I intend to..." This single shift forces the person speaking to own the decision, and it forces the commander to give authority rather than collect it.

**Why it matters for you:**
Operations organizations that are purely reactive run on leader-follower. On-call engineers wait to be told what to do. Senior Managers escalate to the Director for decisions that should be made at their level. The Director becomes a bottleneck during incidents.

Marquet's framework describes exactly how to build the opposite — a team that makes decisions at the right level because they have the competence and the authority.

**When to read:** End of Month 1, as you are establishing your operating rhythm. The framework shapes how you run incidents and how you design the delegation structure with your Senior Managers.

**Key concept to internalize:** "Don't move information to authority — move authority to information." The person closest to the incident knows the most. Design your org so that person has the authority to act without escalating.

## Financial and Strategic Management

### *The Effective Executive* — Peter Drucker

**What it teaches:**
Drucker's core argument: effectiveness is a discipline that can be practiced, not a talent that some people have and others don't. The five practices of effective executives: time management, contribution focus, building on strengths, setting the right priorities, making effective decisions.

The chapter on "What Can I Contribute?" is the most important for a new Director. It reframes the question from "what am I good at?" to "what does this organization need from me, and how do I make sure I am producing that?"

**Why it matters for you:**
Most Directors spend their time on the wrong things — things they are good at, things they enjoy, things that feel urgent. Drucker gives you the diagnostic and the discipline to spend your time on the things that actually produce results at Director scale.

**When to read:** Month 2, after you have completed your observation phase. Read it when you are deciding how to structure your months 3 onward.

**Key concept:** The "contribution focus" chapter — particularly the distinction between efficiency (doing things right) and effectiveness (doing the right things). Directors who optimize for efficiency while working on the wrong problems produce excellent results on things that don't matter.

---

### *Measure What Matters* — John Doerr

**What it teaches:**
The OKR (Objectives and Key Results) framework, via case studies from Intel, Google, Bono's foundation, and others. How to write objectives that are directional and inspiring. How to write key results that are binary, measurable, and achievable within a quarter. How to cascade OKRs from the CEO to the team level without turning them into a bureaucratic exercise.

**Why it matters for you:**
Your VP likely uses OKRs or a similar framework. Your Director-level objectives need to connect to organizational OKRs, and your Senior Managers' key results need to connect to yours. If you cannot trace the line from a Senior Manager's weekly work to a company-level outcome, the alignment is broken somewhere.

**When to read:** Before your first full planning cycle — the quarter before your first OKR-setting conversation with Senior Managers.

**Key practical skill:** Writing the distinction between objectives and key results. Doerr is clear: an objective is a direction (aspirational, qualitative). A key result is a measurement of progress toward that direction (binary, time-bound, graded). Most people write objectives that are actually key results, and key results that are actually tasks.

## People and Culture

### *Radical Candor* — Kim Scott

**What it teaches:**
A feedback framework built on two dimensions: caring personally (do you actually care about this person as a human?) and challenging directly (are you willing to say the uncomfortable thing?). The failure modes: ruinous empathy (you care but you won't challenge), obnoxious aggression (you challenge without caring), and manipulative insincerity (you do neither).

Most managers default to ruinous empathy. They care about their people and therefore do not tell them the things that would actually help them. This is not kindness — it is a failure to provide the information the person needs to grow.

**Why it matters for you:**
At Director level, you are giving feedback to Senior Managers who are giving feedback to their teams. Your feedback standards cascade. If you accept vague, hedged, late feedback from Senior Managers, you have implicitly set that standard for the entire org.

**When to read:** Month 1. Then use the framework in every 1:1 from Month 2 onward. The framework is most useful when you have a specific feedback conversation coming up — re-read the relevant chapter before you walk in.

**Key question to internalize:** "Am I being candid because I care, or am I being silent because I care?" If the answer is "I'm being silent because I care" — that is ruinous empathy. You are not helping them.

---

### *An Elegant Puzzle* — Will Larson

**What it teaches:**
Engineering management at scale — when you are managing managers, managing across multiple teams, managing through ambiguity and growth. Larson covers systems thinking applied to organizations: how to debug an organization like you would debug a system, how to think about the "debt" that accumulates in organizational structures, how to navigate the specific tensions of a Director-level technical leadership role.

**Why it matters for you:**
Larson wrote this for people in your exact situation — technical leaders who are now leading leaders. His treatment of organizational debt (the patterns, shortcuts, and compromises that have built up in a team over time) directly maps to what you will find in your first 90-day assessment.

**When to read:** Month 2, after you have done your listening tour and have a picture of what you inherited. Larson's frameworks will help you name what you are seeing.

**Key concept:** "Succession planning" — Larson argues that the most important thing a Director can do is ensure that each Senior Manager could step into a larger role if needed. This discipline shapes your 1:1s, your delegation, and your development conversations.

---

### *The Culture Code* — Daniel Coyle

**What it teaches:**
Coyle studied the highest-performing teams in the world — from Navy SEAL teams to Pixar to the San Antonio Spurs — and identified three skills that distinguish their culture: build safety (psychological safety to take risks), share vulnerability (leaders go first in admitting uncertainty and weakness), and establish purpose (shared narrative about why the work matters).

**Why it matters for you:**
Culture is not a set of values on a poster. It is the sum of what behaviors are rewarded and punished in the organization. As a new Director, you will inherit a culture. Whether you shape it intentionally or let it continue as-is is a choice.

**When to read:** Month 3, when you are setting direction. Coyle gives you the vocabulary and the practices to build the culture you want, rather than just living with the one you inherited.

**Key insight:** The concept of "belonging cues" — the small behaviors that signal to people whether they are safe to contribute, challenge, and be honest. These cues are set by the senior leader in the room, whether they intend to set them or not. What you do when someone challenges you in a meeting is a belonging cue. What you do when someone makes a mistake is a belonging cue.

## Vendor and Partnership Management

### *Never Split the Difference* — Chris Voss

**What it teaches:**
Voss was the FBI's lead international hostage negotiator. His techniques — tactical empathy, calibrated questions, mirroring, "accusation audits" — are direct applications of behavioral psychology to high-stakes negotiation. The title is his summary of why compromise is a negotiating failure: when both sides split the difference, both sides lose something they needed.

**Why it matters for you:**
Vendor contract negotiations. Peer Director conflicts. Budget conversations with a VP who is about to cut your request. Difficult performance conversations. Every one of these is a negotiation, and most Directors approach them without a framework.

Voss's "calibrated question" technique — "How am I supposed to...?" or "What makes this work for you?" — is one of the most useful tools in a Director's repertoire for getting more information from a resistant counterpart without triggering defensiveness.

**When to read:** Before your first vendor contract renewal or renegotiation. Then keep it within reach — it is a reference book, not a one-time read.

**Key technique:** The "accusation audit" — before a difficult conversation, list all the negative things the counterpart might think or feel about you or your position. Then address them out loud at the start of the conversation. "I know this request looks like I'm asking you to absorb all the risk..." This technique disarms defensiveness before it develops.

---

### *Getting to Yes* — Roger Fisher and William Ury

**What it teaches:**
The principled negotiation framework from the Harvard Negotiation Project. Four principles: separate the people from the problem; focus on interests not positions; invent options for mutual gain; use objective criteria. The BATNA concept (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) — knowing your walk-away option is essential to negotiating without capitulating.

**Why it matters for you:**
This is the counterpart to *Never Split the Difference*. Voss gives you the behavioral and psychological tools for high-stakes adversarial situations. Fisher and Ury give you the framework for collaborative problem-solving situations — which is what most vendor negotiations and internal budget conversations actually are.

**When to read:** In parallel with *Never Split the Difference*, or directly after. They complement each other.

**Key concept:** BATNA. Before every significant negotiation — vendor contract, budget discussion, peer conflict — answer: "What is my best alternative if we do not reach agreement?" If you do not know your BATNA, you cannot negotiate. You can only accept or refuse.

## Executive Communication

### *The Pyramid Principle* — Barbara Minto

**What it teaches:**
How to structure any communication for an executive audience — written or verbal. The pyramid structure: lead with the conclusion (the single most important thing you want the audience to take away), support it with three or fewer key arguments, support each argument with data and evidence. Never bury the conclusion in the middle or at the end.

Used by McKinsey & Company for 50 years. Still the definitive reference for structured executive communication.

**Why it matters for you:**
Every executive presentation you give, every email you send to your VP, every budget defense you run is more effective when structured this way. The technique is learnable. It requires practice, not talent.

**When to read:** Month 1. Then apply it to your first VP status email. The habit builds with use.

**Key diagnostic:** Can you state your message in one sentence? If not, you have not organized your thinking yet. Minto calls this the "governing thought" — the single sentence that your entire communication supports. Write it before you write anything else.

---

### *Crucial Conversations* — Patterson, Grenny, McMillan, Switzler

**What it teaches:**
What happens physiologically and psychologically when a conversation becomes high-stakes — and how to stay in dialogue rather than falling into silence or violence (the two failure modes when safety breaks down). The STATE framework: Share your facts, Tell your story, Ask for the other's path, Talk tentatively, Encourage testing.

**Why it matters for you:**
The difficult conversations in the Difficult Conversation Playbook become easier with this framework. Crucial Conversations gives you the underlying model — why people get defensive, how to create safety without sacrificing candor, how to distinguish facts from stories.

**When to read:** Before your first difficult performance conversation with a Senior Manager. Also useful as a pre-read for any vendor escalation or peer conflict conversation.

**Key insight:** "The meaning is not in your words — it is in the story the other person tells themselves about your words." This is why specificity in difficult conversations matters so much. Vague feedback triggers the other person's interpretation. Specific behavioral facts constrain their interpretation to what actually happened.

## Ongoing — One Book Per Quarter

Assign yourself one book from the list above each quarter. The rotation matters — you will get more from *Radical Candor* in month 6 than in month 1 because you will have context for it.

### Suggested first-year sequence:

| Quarter | Book | Why this one now |
|---------|------|-----------------|
| Q1 | *The Manager's Path* + *High Output Management* | Foundation for how the role works |
| Q2 | *Radical Candor* + *An Elegant Puzzle* | You have enough context to start developing people; you can see the org patterns now |
| Q3 | *Turn the Ship Around!* + *The Pyramid Principle* | Building the team's decision authority; presenting your first formal roadmap |
| Q4 | *Never Split the Difference* + *Measure What Matters* | Budget season + OKR-setting season |

### Journaling practice (3 things per book):
After finishing each book, write:
1. The one idea that most directly applies to something I am dealing with right now
2. The one behavior I am going to change as a result of this book, starting this week
3. The one idea I want to share with a Senior Manager and why

Do not skip the journaling. Reading without reflection produces familiarity with frameworks, not application of them.
