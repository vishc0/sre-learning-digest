# Executive Presentation Playbook

**Audience**: Director preparing to present to VP, SVP, or C-suite  
**Purpose**: Prepare and deliver presentations that get decisions made, not meetings scheduled

---

## The Governing Principle

Executives make decisions all day. They are pattern-matching for signal in a high-noise environment. Your job is to give them the signal immediately, then provide evidence for the pattern they already sense is there.

Most operational presentations fail because they are structured for the presenter's comfort, not the executive's decision. They begin with background, build to the point, and deliver the ask at the end — after the executive has already made up their mind based on the first two slides.

Reverse the structure. Lead with the headline. Justify it with data. End with the specific ask.

---

## Preparation (1 week before)

### Know your audience

Before writing a single slide, answer these questions in writing:

**Who is in the room?**
- Name and title of each attendee
- What does each person care about most? (Uptime? Cost? Headcount? Compliance? Velocity?)
- Has any attendee had a recent negative experience with your org? You need to address it.
- Is any attendee a skeptic of your proposal? Identify them before you walk in.

**What decision do they need to make?**
A meeting without a decision is a briefing. Briefings are low-value. Know exactly what you need:
- Approval (binary — yes/no)
- Prioritization (choose between competing options)
- Awareness with no action required (the rarest valid reason to present to executives)
- Resource allocation (headcount, budget, tooling)

If you cannot state the decision in one sentence, you are not ready to present.

### Define your single ask

One presentation, one ask. If you have three things you need, pick the most important one and bring the others as context. Executives who walk out of a meeting with a list of commitments do not honor any of them. Executives who walk out with one commitment do.

Write your ask before you write your slides: "I am asking [audience] to [specific decision/approval/action] by [date]."

If this sentence is hard to write, your thinking is not clear enough. Keep writing until it is one clean sentence.

### Gather the right data

You do not need all the data. You need the data that supports your narrative and addresses the most likely objections.

Rule: every data point on your slides should directly support your ask or directly counter a known objection. If a number does not do one of those two things, cut it.

Common mistake: presenting data you collected to understand the situation, rather than data that helps the executive make the decision. Those are different data sets.

### Write the headline

The headline is the single sentence that your audience should remember when they walk out. It is your conclusion, stated plainly.

Examples:
- "Tier 1 SLA performance is at risk. I need approval to replace the aging monitoring platform this quarter."
- "We had our best incident response quarter in two years. Our investment in problem management is working."
- "Our engineering team is at on-call saturation. I need two additional SREs by Q3 or we accept elevated incident risk."

If you cannot write the headline in one sentence, you do not know your message. Do not start building slides until you can write it.

## Slide Structure (8 slides maximum for a 30-minute slot)

**Rule**: If you have more than 8 slides for a 30-minute meeting, you are not editing. Cut until you are under the limit.

### Slide 1: Operational Health Dashboard

This is your opening and your headline.

Content:
- Service health by domain (Green / Amber / Red, with brief status note per domain)
- Your headline at the top in large text — not a title, a statement
- Week/month/quarter timeframe clearly labeled

The executive looks at this slide and immediately knows: are we healthy or not? What is this meeting actually about?

### Slides 2–5: Substantive Content (varies by purpose)

**For a quarterly operational review:**

Slide 2: Quarter highlights — 3 things that went well, each with supporting data  
Slide 3: DORA metrics trend — 4 charts (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR) — show trend line, not just current value  
Slide 4: Incidents and SLA performance — P1/P2 volume, MTTR actuals vs. target, SLA compliance %, any credits issued  
Slide 5: Investment summary — budget vs. actuals, ROI on key investments made this quarter

**For a budget request:**

Slide 2: Current state — what you have, what it is producing (capacity, output, utilization)  
Slide 3: The problem — what is not possible with current budget, with the cost of not solving it  
Slide 4: The request — specific ask with ROI framing or risk framing  
Slide 5: Risk of not approving — specific, named risks with likelihood and impact

**For a risk escalation:**

Slide 2: The risk — specific, named, with current state and trajectory  
Slide 3: What has been done — existing mitigations and why they are insufficient  
Slide 4: Options considered — 2–3 options with trade-offs  
Slide 5: Recommended option — your recommendation, with rationale and cost

### Slide 6: Top Risks with Mitigation Status

Always include. Executives need to know what you are watching.

Format per risk:
- Risk name (one line)
- Likelihood / Impact (H/M/L)
- Current mitigation (one line)
- Status: On track / Needs attention / Escalation required

No more than 5 risks on this slide. If you have more than 5, you have not prioritized.

### Slide 7: Next Quarter Priorities

3–5 priorities for next quarter. Each priority should map to a company or org OKR — say which one. If a priority does not map to an OKR, either the priority is wrong or the OKR is wrong. Resolve that before you present it.

### Slide 8: What I Need from You

This is the most important slide. It should be the sharpest slide in the deck.

Format:
- **Decision**: [What you are asking for, in one sentence]
- **Options**: [If applicable — the 2–3 options you considered]
- **Recommendation**: [Your recommendation, if you have one]
- **Decision deadline**: [When you need an answer to keep on track]
- **If approved**: [What happens next]
- **If not approved**: [What the consequence is — say it plainly]

Do not leave slide 8 vague. "Your support and guidance going forward" is not an ask. "Approval to proceed with the monitoring platform replacement, with a budget authorization of $[X], with decision needed by [date] to hit the Q3 implementation window" is an ask.

## Delivery

### Lead with the headline, not the background

When you open, say the headline out loud before advancing past slide 1. "I want to say upfront: Tier 1 SLA compliance is at risk, and I am going to show you the data and ask for a specific decision today."

Executives who know where you are going will listen differently. Executives who do not know where you are going are spending cognitive resources trying to figure it out — while you are presenting.

### Spend time in the right ratio

Most presenters spend 70% of their time on "what happened" and 30% on "what's next." Executives want the inverse. They know what happened — they were in the review last week. They want to know what you are going to do about it.

**Target ratio**: 1/3 of your time on performance data; 2/3 on priorities, decisions, and next quarter.

### Slide 8 is the only slide that cannot be cut

If a meeting goes long and you have 5 minutes left, skip slides. Do not skip slide 8. The ask is why you are in the room. Getting to the end without making the ask is a wasted meeting.

Know your ask cold. If you have to read it off the slide, you are not confident enough in it.

### If you don't have the answer

When asked a question you cannot answer:

"I don't have that data with me. I'll follow up with [specific data point] by [specific date]."

Never make up an answer. Executives remember the one thing you got wrong far longer than the ten things you got right. A single fabricated or uncertain number destroys your credibility in the room.

Never say "I think it's around..." unless you immediately follow it with "but I'll confirm the exact number."

### Read the room

Watch for:
- Executives who stop taking notes — you've lost them; pick up pace or move forward
- A question that pulls the conversation sideways — answer briefly, anchor back to your message: "That's worth tracking down — I'll bring it to the follow-up. On the main question..."
- A skeptic who goes quiet — they are building an objection. Call it: "I notice we haven't heard from [name]. Is there a concern about this approach that I should address?"
- An executive who starts answering your question before you finish asking it — they are decided; stop presenting and lock in the commitment

## Post-Presentation (within 24 hours)

Send a written follow-up to all attendees:

```
Follow-up: [Meeting name] — [Date]

Thank you for the time today. Summary of what was discussed and decided:

Decisions made:
- [Decision 1 — owner — action — deadline]
- [Decision 2 — owner — action — deadline]

Open items (to be resolved before [date]):
- [Item — owner — how it will be resolved]

Actions I committed to:
- [Action — my deadline]

Next review: [date and format]
```

This serves two purposes: it creates a written record of commitments, and it gives the executive the opportunity to correct the record if they believe something was misrepresented. If no correction comes within 48 hours, the commitments are confirmed.

## Common Presentation Errors

| Error | What it looks like | What to do instead |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Building from background to conclusion | Slides 1–5 are context; the ask is on slide 7 | Put the ask on slide 1 and let the data support it |
| Too many slides | 20+ slides for a 30-minute meeting | One slide per 3–4 minutes maximum |
| Data dumps | Tables with 12 columns, dashboards screenshot from monitoring | One insight per slide, one chart per insight |
| Missing the ask | Ending with "questions?" and no specific decision request | Slide 8 exists for a reason; use it |
| Reading slides aloud | Reading bullet points verbatim | Your slides are prompts, not a script |
| Burying the risk | Risk is mentioned as a footnote or in the appendix | Risks go on slide 6, named and sized |
| Hedging the recommendation | "Option A or B both have merit and it depends..." | Executives want your opinion; give it and justify it |
| Not following up in writing | Verbal decisions that fade within a week | 24-hour written summary, every time |
