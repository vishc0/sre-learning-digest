# Vendor / Partner QBR Playbook

**Audience**: Director of Operations  
**Format**: 90-minute in-person or video meeting, quarterly cadence  
**Purpose**: Hold vendors accountable to commitments, resolve service gaps with specificity, and align on the next quarter before it starts rather than explaining the last quarter after it ends.

---

## The Governing Principle

A QBR is not a vendor presentation. It is a structured accountability session. The vendor shows up with a polished deck about their roadmap. Your job is to show up with specific data about their performance and a clear list of what needs to change. If you leave without written commitments, you held a relationship meeting, not a QBR.

The ratio that matters: spend 40% of the meeting on performance (what happened), 60% on the next quarter (what will happen). Most QBRs do the reverse.

---

## Pre-Meeting (1 week before)

### Confirm the agenda with the vendor

Send the agenda in writing at least 5 business days before the meeting. Format:

```
Agenda — [Vendor Name] QBR — [Date]

1. Last quarter's commitments review — both parties (10 min)
2. Your performance data — our view (20 min)
3. Vendor review — their view and roadmap (20 min)
4. Gap discussion — items where expectations are not met (20 min)
5. Next quarter commitments — documented (15 min)
6. Strategic alignment (10 min)
7. Close and next steps (5 min)

Please come prepared to respond to our performance data. We will share our data package 48 hours before the meeting.
```

Do not surprise the vendor with your performance data. Send it in advance. A vendor who comes prepared to respond is more useful than a vendor scrambling to defend numbers they have never seen.

### Collect data from the internal owner

The Senior Manager (or technical owner) responsible for this vendor should provide:
- SLA actual vs. contracted for the quarter (each SLA metric, each month)
- Utilization data (are you using what you're paying for?)
- Ticket data: how many support tickets opened, P1/P2/P3 breakdown, average resolution time
- Any specific incidents where the vendor's platform or service contributed to a production issue
- Satisfaction rating from the engineering team that uses this service (scale 1–5; even an informal poll is better than nothing)
- Any issues that have been raised with the vendor in the quarter and not resolved

**Critical**: the internal owner attends the QBR. They have context you do not. They also need to own the commitments that come out of the meeting.

### Brief your Senior Manager on the objectives

Before the QBR, 15-minute conversation with the Senior Manager:
- "What do you want resolved in this meeting that hasn't been resolved through normal channels?"
- "What should not be on the table in this meeting — what's sensitive or in negotiation separately?"
- "What's the one thing, if the vendor commits to it today, that makes this meeting a success?"

Make sure you and the Senior Manager are aligned on the signals you're sending. The vendor will read inconsistency between Director and Senior Manager and use it.

### Review last quarter's commitments

Pull the commitment list from the last QBR (or the last written meeting summary). For each commitment:
- Party responsible (vendor or your org)
- Was it delivered? If not, what happened?

If you do not have written commitments from the last QBR, you cannot hold anyone accountable. That changes today.

## Meeting Structure

### Opening (5 min)

State the agenda and time allocation out loud. Do not assume everyone read the pre-read.

"Welcome. We have 90 minutes. Here is how we are using it: [walk through agenda briefly]. Before we get into performance data, let's start with a review of last quarter's commitments from both sides. [Name from vendor], can you start by sharing how you tracked against your commitments from last quarter?"

Starting with commitments review — before your data — establishes the tone: this is a two-way accountability session, not a vendor briefing.

### Your Performance Review (20 min)

Present your data. Be specific. General dissatisfaction is not a QBR agenda item.

**SLA performance table:**
For each contracted SLA metric, show:
- Contracted target
- Actual performance each month of the quarter
- Quarter average
- Status: met / missed / borderline

**Incident attribution:**
If any production incidents had vendor contribution as a factor, name them:
- Date and duration
- What failed
- Impact to your operations (customer impact, engineer hours spent, SLA credits owed)
- Was a root cause analysis provided? Was it complete?

**Specific example format:**
"In March, your platform was unavailable for 4 hours between 2:00–6:00am EST on the 14th. Our on-call team spent 6 hours on recovery. Three T2 services were degraded during that window. We incurred a P2 incident and had to notify two enterprise customers. We received a root cause document 8 days later. We expected it in 48 hours."

Do not soften this data. Vague dissatisfaction produces vague commitments. Specific incidents produce specific accountability.

After presenting: "Are you seeing this data the same way from your side? If your numbers are different, we need to reconcile that before we can discuss commitments."

### Vendor Review (20 min)

Turn the floor over. Let them present their view of the relationship and their roadmap.

Your posture during this section: ask questions, do not defend. You are in information-gathering mode.

Questions to ask during vendor review:
- "What issues on your side affected your ability to deliver this quarter?"
- "What is on your roadmap in the next 6 months that will affect our environment?"
- "Are there things we are doing that make your service harder to deliver? I want to know."
- "What does a great customer look like to you, and how do we compare?"

Do not use this section to re-litigate the performance data. That is for the gap discussion.

### Gap Discussion (20 min)

This is the highest-value section of the QBR. Do not let it run short because the previous sections ran long.

For each gap between contracted expectation and actual delivery:

1. State the gap factually: "Your MTTR on P1 tickets is averaging 6 hours. Our contract specifies 2 hours."
2. Ask for their explanation: "Help me understand what's driving that."
3. Listen. The answer matters — it tells you whether this is a capacity problem, a process problem, or a prioritization problem.
4. State the requirement: "We need MTTR under 2 hours on P1s. What do you need to make that consistent?"
5. Get a specific commitment: "We'll improve" is not a commitment. "We commit to <2 hour MTTR on 95% of P1 tickets in Q[N], and we will assign [named engineer] as your dedicated support resource" is a commitment.

**The vague commitment trap**: Vendors will offer "we'll prioritize your account" or "we're working on improvements." Push back every time.

"I need something more specific. What exactly are you committing to, what is the measurable success criteria, and by what date?"

If the vendor cannot answer these three questions, the commitment goes in the issues column, not the commitments column.

### Next Quarter Commitments (15 min)

Document commitments from both parties before leaving the room. Do not let this be done post-meeting — commitments fade and get diluted.

Format for each commitment:

| Party | Commitment | Success criteria | Date |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|------|
| Vendor | Assign dedicated support engineer to our account | Named engineer confirmed by email | [Date +7 days] |
| Vendor | Deliver P1 MTTR under 2 hours | 95% compliance for Q[N] | End of Q[N] |
| Your org | Provide 5-day advance notice for major change windows | Change advisory email sent to vendor weekly | Starting [date] |

Both parties should have commitments on this list. A QBR where only the vendor has commitments creates a compliance-and-accountability dynamic that vendors resent and resist. A QBR where both parties have commitments creates a partnership dynamic.

### Strategic Alignment (10 min)

This section is forward-looking. The questions:
- "We are planning to [major initiative — platform migration, new product launch, significant volume growth] in the next 12 months. How does that affect how you serve us?"
- "What is on your roadmap in the next 2–4 quarters that is most relevant to our needs?"
- "Are there ways we are using your product that limit the value you can provide? What should we be doing differently?"
- "We have a contract renewal in [N months]. I want to flag that now so we have time to evaluate the relationship properly rather than rushing the decision."

If the contract is coming up for renewal, say so explicitly. The vendor's behavior in the next quarter will be shaped by whether they think renewal is at risk.

### Close (5 min)

Verbally summarize:
- Key commitments from both sides (reference the commitment table)
- Next QBR date
- Confirm escalation contacts: "If we have a P1 issue at 2am, who on your side can I call?" Get a named person, not a queue.
- "We'll send a written summary of today's commitments within 24 hours. We ask that you confirm receipt and accuracy within 48 hours."

## Post-Meeting (within 24 hours)

Send a written summary to all attendees:

```
QBR Summary — [Vendor Name] — [Date]

Attendees: [list]

Last Quarter's Commitments — Closed:
[List items that were confirmed met]

Last Quarter's Commitments — Not Met:
[List items that were not met, with explanation if provided]

Next Quarter Commitments:
[The full commitment table from the meeting]

Open Issues (not yet resolved):
[Any gaps that did not reach commitment status — track these for next QBR]

Next QBR date: [date]
```

Request written confirmation from the vendor within 48 hours. "Confirmed" from a vendor VP is more durable than a verbal agreement.

If the vendor does not confirm within 48 hours, follow up once. If still no response, escalate to your vendor contact's manager.

---

## When a QBR Escalates

Signs that a QBR has become a performance intervention, not a routine review:
- SLA breaches with customer credit implications
- Repeated unresolved commitments from prior QBRs
- Incident attribution that caused significant damage
- A pattern of commitments made but not kept

In these cases:
- Bring your VP or a Senior Leader. The vendor needs to feel the weight of the relationship.
- Request vendor VP or Executive Sponsor attendance.
- Come with a specific improvement plan you are asking them to sign off on.
- Set a clear consequence: "If we do not see [specific improvement] by [date], we will be [reviewing contract options / initiating RFP process / escalating to your executive team]."

State the consequence once, clearly, and do not soften it. Vendors respond to consequences they believe are real.

## QBR Readiness Checklist

**1 week before:**
- [ ] Agenda sent to vendor with data package timing noted
- [ ] Internal data collected from Senior Manager / technical owner
- [ ] Last QBR commitment list pulled and reviewed
- [ ] Senior Manager briefed on objectives
- [ ] Attendees confirmed on both sides

**48 hours before:**
- [ ] Performance data package sent to vendor

**Day of:**
- [ ] Commitment table template ready to fill in real-time
- [ ] Note-taker designated (not the Director — you are running the meeting)

**Within 24 hours after:**
- [ ] Written summary sent
- [ ] Confirmation requested from vendor

**Ongoing:**
- [ ] All commitments tracked in Vendor Contract Register
- [ ] Next QBR date on calendar for both parties
