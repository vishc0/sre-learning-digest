# Difficult Conversation Playbook

**Audience**: Director of Operations  
**Purpose**: Step-by-step guide for the four most common high-stakes conversations Directors face  
**Governing principle**: Prepare more than you think you need to. The quality of a difficult conversation is determined before the conversation starts, not during it.

---

## Before Any Difficult Conversation

Three rules that apply to every conversation in this playbook:

**Rule 1: Specific over general.**
"Your incident reports are consistently late" is a judgment. "The last four incident reports for P2 events were submitted more than 5 days after incident close — the SLA is 48 hours" is a fact. Facts are not arguable. Judgments invite defensiveness and derailment.

**Rule 2: Impact over feeling.**
"It's frustrating when..." is your experience. "When incident reports are late, the problem management team cannot open a problem ticket within the SLA window, which means we're accruing risk on unmitigated known errors" is impact. Impact focuses the conversation on the operational reality, not the interpersonal dynamic.

**Rule 3: Expectation over hope.**
"I'd love to see improvement" is hope. "I need incident reports submitted within 48 hours of incident close, for every P2 or higher, starting with the next incident" is an expectation. Expectations are measurable. Hope is not.

## Conversation 1: Senior Manager Performance Below Expectations

### When to have this conversation

As soon as the pattern is clear — not at a performance review cycle. Performance reviews should confirm what has already been discussed, not reveal it for the first time.

"Clear pattern" means:
- Two or more specific instances of the same failure, OR
- One significant failure with documented impact, OR
- A sustained trend visible in operational data

Do not wait for a third strike. By the time you have three examples, the Senior Manager has been underperforming long enough that your inaction has become part of the problem.

### Preparation

**Gather before the conversation:**
- Three specific observable examples. Not general impressions. Observable means you can describe what happened, when it happened, and what was affected.
- The operational impact of each example (not your frustration — the actual consequence to the team, metrics, or service)
- The specific behavioral change you need (not "be more accountable" — "I need incident reviews complete within 5 days of closure, presented in the ops review format, for every P2 and above")
- Your answer to "what do they need from me?" — coaching, resources, clarity, time?

**Know what you will do if this conversation does not produce change.** If you do not know, the Senior Manager will sense it, and the conversation loses its weight. The consequence does not have to be termination — it can be a formal PIP, a role change, a scope reduction. But you need to know what it is.

### Structure

**Step 1 — Open (set the tone):**
"I want to have a direct conversation with you about something I'm observing. I'm bringing this to you now, early, because I want to address it before it becomes a bigger issue. This is not a formal review — it's me doing my job as your manager."

Do not open with compliments or softening. It dilutes the message and signals that what follows is negotiable.

**Step 2 — Observation (facts only):**
"Over the past [timeframe], I've observed [specific example 1], [specific example 2], and [specific example 3]."

Name the behavior or outcome. Do not interpret. Do not say "you seem disengaged" — that is an interpretation. Say "the last three incident reviews were not prepared before the ops review, and the meeting ran without the data we need to make problem management decisions."

**Step 3 — Impact:**
"The effect of this on the org is [specific impact]."

Operational impact, not personal feelings. "The problem management backlog has grown because we are not opening problems on the right timeline" is more useful than "it makes the team feel unsupported."

**Step 4 — Inquiry (listen):**
"Help me understand what's happening."

Then stop talking.

This is the most important step that managers skip. The answer tells you whether this is a skill gap, a will gap, or a situational factor you were not aware of. A Senior Manager who is dealing with a personal crisis needs different support than one who has decided your process is not worth their time. You cannot know which you are dealing with until you ask and listen.

Do not interrupt. Do not offer solutions during this step. Listen.

**Step 5 — Expectation (clear and specific):**
"Here is what I need from you, starting [date]: [specific, behavioral, time-bounded expectation]."

Example: "Starting next Monday, I need incident reviews for all P2 and above submitted via [system] within 48 hours of incident close, and presented in the weekly ops review using the standard template. That's the expectation."

**Step 6 — Support:**
"What do you need from me to make this happen?"

Listen. If they identify a real barrier (unclear process, tooling gap, team capacity), address it. If they identify no barrier, you have confirmed this is a will issue. Note that.

**Step 7 — Check-in:**
"We will check in on this in 30 days. I'm going to track [specific metric]. That's what success looks like."

**Step 8 — Close:**
"I want you to succeed in this role. That's why I'm having this conversation now."

Say it only if you mean it.

### After the conversation

Send a written summary to the Senior Manager within 24 hours:

```
Following up on our conversation today.

We discussed: [2-3 sentence factual summary of what was observed and the impact]

What I need from you: [The specific expectation, verbatim]

Check-in: We will review progress on [date].

If you have questions or want to discuss further, I'm available.
```

This document is not a punitive act. It is a shared record. If the situation escalates, this document is evidence that the conversation happened and was documented. If the situation improves, it becomes a record of a manager who handled things early and directly.

Keep a copy. Share with HR if the situation escalates.

### If the conversation does not produce change

After the check-in date, if expectations are still not being met:
- Escalate to HR
- Initiate a formal performance improvement plan (PIP)
- The PIP formalizes what you have already communicated — it should not be new information to the Senior Manager

A PIP that is surprising to the recipient means the performance conversations came too late or were not clear enough.

## Conversation 2: Vendor Not Meeting Expectations

### When to have this conversation

After the second breach of a material SLA or after a significant project delivery failure. Do not have this conversation after the first breach — you will lose credibility and the vendor will treat it as routine dissatisfaction. Do not have it after the fifth — you will lose leverage because you tolerated it.

### Preparation

- SLA breach data: dates, metrics, magnitude of each breach
- Operational impact: what did each breach cost in engineer hours, customer impact, SLA credits?
- Prior communications: have you raised this through normal channels? What was promised? Was it delivered?
- Your escalation option: are you prepared to actually put the contract at risk? If not, the conversation will be read as a complaint, not a consequence.

### Structure

**Step 1 — Factual opening:**
"I want to have a direct conversation about SLA performance. We have had [N] breaches in the last [timeframe]. I want to address this directly."

No preamble. No relationship softening. This is a business conversation about a breach of contract.

**Step 2 — Impact statement:**
"These breaches have caused [specific operational impact] for our team and our customers. In [month], [specific incident] cost [X hours / Y customers affected / Z in SLA credits]."

**Step 3 — Resolution expectation:**
"I need two things from you. First, a root cause analysis for each breach, within 10 business days. Second, a written improvement plan with measurable targets and timelines."

**Step 4 — Consequence framing:**
"If we do not see [specific improvement — name the metric and the target] by [date], we will be reviewing our contract options. I want to be direct about that so there is no ambiguity about where we stand."

Say this once. Clearly. Do not repeat it, and do not soften it with qualifiers. Repeated or hedged consequences are not believed.

**Step 5 — Escalation request:**
"I would like a VP-level owner assigned to our account going forward. Our current engagement is not producing the results we need."

This signals that you are elevating the relationship, not just venting to the account team. The account team will immediately escalate internally when they hear this.

### After the conversation

Send written confirmation of the requests within 24 hours. Set a calendar reminder for the RCA deadline. Track delivery. If the RCA arrives late or is inadequate, note it — it is data about the vendor's commitment to the relationship.

## Conversation 3: Peer Director Not Cooperating

### When to have this conversation

After a pattern of cross-functional failures that have been raised at the operational level and not resolved. Not after the first issue — operational teams sort many issues without Director involvement. After the third recurrence of the same category of problem with no improvement, the issue has escalated above the operational level.

### Preparation

- Document the specific incidents: dates, what failed, what was raised, what was promised, what happened
- Understand your peer's perspective: is this a priority gap, a resource gap, or a process gap? Or is it deliberate? (The latter is rare and usually has a political driver you should understand before the conversation.)
- Know your ask: what specific process change or commitment would resolve this?
- Consider whether to alert your VP before having this conversation. For most operational issues, no — handle it peer-to-peer first. For politically sensitive issues, yes — your VP should not be surprised by an escalation.

### Structure

**Step 1 — Direct opening:**
"I want to address something directly with you, peer-to-peer, before it goes anywhere else."

This signals respect (you are not going over their head) and urgency (this cannot continue).

**Step 2 — Specific:**
"The last three change windows where your team had a deployment, we had incidents that were traced back to those changes. We have raised this twice at the operational level — in the ops review on [date] and in the CAB process on [date]. The pattern has not changed."

Do not say "your team always does X" or "you never prioritize us." Names, dates, specifics.

**Step 3 — Propose a joint solution:**
"I don't think this is a problem either of us can solve separately. I'd like us to design a joint pre-deployment review process. I'll bring [my senior manager responsible for this domain]. Can you bring [your equivalent]? I'd rather solve this in a room than through emails."

This is the key move. You are offering collaboration, not blame. Peer Directors who feel accused defend their team. Peer Directors who are invited to solve a shared problem usually engage.

**Step 4 — Set a timeline:**
"Can we put 60 minutes on the calendar in the next two weeks? I want to move on this before the next deployment window."

**If the joint process does not resolve it:**

After a sincere attempt at joint resolution — you had the meeting, you designed a process, the process was not followed — escalate to VP level.

The escalation framing is critical: "We have an issue that is above the level at which [peer] and I can resolve it. We have tried [describe what was tried]. I would like the two of us to bring this to our shared VP so they can make a call on the priority and resource question. I am not escalating this as a complaint against [peer] — I am escalating it because it requires a decision at a level above us."

Ask your peer if they will co-escalate. A joint escalation is dramatically more effective than a unilateral one. It signals that the problem is real and that both parties have tried. A unilateral escalation looks like a complaint.

## Conversation 4: Team Member Who Should Exit the Organization

### Overview

This is the hardest category. At the Director level, the role is rarely to have this conversation directly — it is to ensure the Senior Manager has it, and to support them when they do.

Your responsibilities:
- Ensure the Senior Manager is not delaying the inevitable out of discomfort
- Ensure the process is followed correctly (HR involvement, documentation)
- Support the Senior Manager before and after
- Communicate to the broader team after the departure in a way that maintains trust

### Director pre-work

**Ensure the documentation exists.** Before any exit conversation:
- Performance history: documented 1:1 conversations that addressed the issue
- Coaching history: at minimum, one PIP with specific expectations and results
- HR is involved: no exit should happen without HR in the loop, both for the employee's protection and yours

If the Senior Manager comes to you saying "I want to let [name] go" and there is no documentation, the answer is: "We cannot do that yet. Here is what we need to document first. I will help you build it."

**Know the process and terms.** Before the conversation happens:
- Severance terms, if applicable
- Final paycheck timing and method
- Benefit continuation details
- HR's role on the day of

**Prepare the Senior Manager.** The exit conversation itself is led by the Senior Manager, with HR present. Coach them on:
- What to say: "We are ending your employment with [company], effective [date]. This decision is final."
- What not to say: do not give a long explanation, do not apologize repeatedly, do not leave room for negotiation if the decision is made
- What to expect: the range of reactions (silence, anger, tears, negotiation attempt) and how to respond to each
- The physical logistics: who else is in the room, what paperwork is ready, how the person leaves the building

### The exit conversation itself

Director is typically not in the room for the exit conversation. This is between the Senior Manager, the employee, and HR.

Exception: if the employee is a direct report of the Director (not a Senior Manager's report), the Director conducts the conversation with HR present.

Structure of the exit conversation (brief, factual, final):
1. "I need to tell you that we are ending your employment with [company], effective [date]."
2. Allow the person to respond.
3. "I know this is difficult. [HR representative] will walk you through the logistics and your separation agreement."
4. Do not engage in lengthy explanation or justification after this point. The time for feedback was during the performance process.

### Communicating to the team after

The Director communicates to the broader team — or coaches the Senior Manager to do so — using a brief, factual statement:

"I want to let you know that [name] has left the organization. I know change can be unsettling. I'm not able to share details. If you have questions or concerns, talk to your manager or reach out to me directly. I'm here."

Do not:
- Explain why the person left (even if you are tempted to protect the org's reputation)
- Ask the team to not speculate (this guarantees they will)
- Make it a big deal (this amplifies anxiety)

The team will take their emotional cue from you. If you treat it as a difficult but normal part of organizational life, they will do the same. If you treat it as a crisis, they will panic.

### Supporting the Senior Manager after

Within 24–48 hours of the exit:
- Check in directly: "How are you doing? That kind of conversation takes something out of you even when it's the right call."
- Acknowledge the difficulty: "You did the right thing. I know it wasn't easy."
- Debrief on anything that went unexpectedly: "Was there anything that came up that you weren't prepared for?"

Senior Managers who execute difficult personnel decisions without Director support become reluctant to make them. The support you give after shapes their willingness to act early next time.

## Conversation Preparation Checklist

Use before any of the four conversations:

- [ ] Do I have specific, observable facts — not general impressions?
- [ ] Can I state the operational impact — not just my reaction?
- [ ] Can I state the specific expectation or resolution I need?
- [ ] Do I know what happens if this conversation does not produce change?
- [ ] Have I allowed time to listen — not just to deliver the message?
- [ ] Is HR aware if this conversation is about employment status?
- [ ] Do I have time blocked for a written follow-up within 24 hours?
- [ ] Have I told my VP this conversation is happening (if politically material)?
