# New Director — First 90 Days Playbook

**Audience**: Incoming Director of Operations, 20–100 person org  
**Purpose**: Week-by-week guide to observing before acting, building credibility before changing anything, and landing your first 90 days with authority

---

## The Governing Principle

Your first 90 days are not about proving you can lead. They are about learning what you inherited. Every decision you make before you understand the system is a guess. Guesses from Directors are expensive.

The three-phase model:
- **Days 1–30 (Listen)**: Observe, read, ask, take notes. No structural changes.
- **Days 31–60 (Stabilize)**: Establish rhythm, surface risks, signal your style.
- **Days 61–90 (Set Direction)**: Build the 12-month roadmap, set objectives, launch first quick win.

---

## Phase 1 — Listen (Days 1–30)

### Week 1: Orientation

**What to read:**
- Team charter and org chart (who reports to whom, how far down)
- Domain framework documents (runbooks, architecture diagrams, service catalog)
- Last 6 months of key operational metrics (SLO compliance, incident volume, DORA metrics if available)
- Last 3 post-incident reviews (PIRs) — read for themes, not just facts
- Any open audit findings or compliance items
- The last VP-level operational review deck

**Who to meet (scheduled 1:1s, 45 min each):**
- Every direct report (Senior Managers) — one per day if possible
- Your VP — set expectations for your first 90-day plan and preferred communication cadence
- Key peers: Engineering Director, Product Director, Security Director

**What NOT to do this week:**
- Do not make any structural changes to the team
- Do not promise anything to anyone about how things will change
- Do not publicly criticize anything you observe — surface it privately to your VP if urgent
- Do not skip the reading. The data tells you what the people won't.

**Week 1 1:1 agenda with each Senior Manager (listen mode only):**
1. Tell me about your domain — what are you responsible for?
2. What's working well that you want to protect?
3. What's broken that you've been trying to fix?
4. What do you wish your last Director had done differently?
5. What do you need from me to do your job well?

Take notes. Do not respond with solutions yet.

---

### Week 2: Shadow Operations

**Attend (observer role only, do not run anything):**
- At least one incident bridge call — full duration, note communication patterns, who leads, who goes silent
- CAB (Change Advisory Board) meeting — observe what gets approved, what gets rejected, the decision quality
- Problem review meeting — are problems being driven to root cause or recycled?
- KEDB (Known Error Database) review if one exists — age of open known errors tells you a lot about problem management discipline

**What to note in each session:**
- Who is leading? Is the right person leading?
- Is the conversation structured or reactive?
- Are decisions being made or deferred?
- What is the quality of the data being used to make decisions?
- What tensions exist between teams?

**End of Week 2 task:** Write one page of raw observations. No conclusions yet. Facts and patterns only. Keep this private.

---

### Week 3: Skip-Level Listening Tour

**Who:** All managers (and senior engineers if no managers) who report to your Senior Managers. These are the people two levels below you.

**Format:** 30-minute 1:1s. Listen only. No promises. No agenda other than these questions:
1. What's the most frustrating part of how we operate today?
2. What do you wish leadership understood about the day-to-day?
3. What's one thing that, if fixed, would make the biggest difference for your team?
4. Who in the organization do you go to when you need something done? (This reveals informal power structure.)

**What to look for across conversations:**
- Themes that repeat across multiple people = signal
- Things only one person mentions = that person's lens, not a systemic issue
- What people do NOT mention (sometimes the absence is the data)

**End of Week 3 task:** Cluster your themes. You should have 3–5 operational or cultural patterns emerging. Write them down. You will validate these with Senior Managers in Month 2.

---

### Week 4: Data Review and Assessment

**Pull and review:**
- 6 months of incident volume by severity (P1/P2/P3/P4)
- 6 months of SLO compliance by service tier
- DORA metrics if tracked: deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR
- On-call page volume per engineer per night
- Problem backlog age and closure rate
- Budget: actuals vs. plan this year

**Produce (private document, not for circulation yet):**

**"State of the Org" — First Draft (private)**
- What is this org responsible for?
- What is working well (with data to support it)?
- What is not working (with data to support it)?
- What is the team's current capacity and capability?
- What are the 3 biggest risks I see?
- What are the 3 biggest opportunities?

This is your internal compass. It is not for VP presentation yet. It will evolve.

**End of Week 4:** Share the "State of the Org" draft with your VP for a gut-check. "Does this match what you see? Am I missing anything?" This is how you build trust with your VP early.

## Phase 2 — Stabilize (Days 31–60)

### Establish Operating Rhythm

By Day 35, you should be running these recurring meetings in your format:

| Meeting | Cadence | Attendees | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|---------|-----------|----------|---------|
| Weekly Ops Review | Weekly | All Senior Managers | 60 min | Metrics, risks, decisions |
| 1:1 with each Senior Manager | Weekly | Director + SM | 30 min | Status, coaching, unblocking |
| Incident review | Weekly (or after each P1) | SM + relevant leads | 45 min | Pattern identification |
| Director staff meeting | Bi-weekly | Senior Managers | 60 min | Cross-domain coordination |
| VP status | Weekly | Director → VP | Async (written) | Keep VP informed |

**For the Weekly Ops Review:** Lead with metrics, not anecdotes. Use the standard metrics format (see `reference/director-metrics-quick-reference.md`). The meeting agenda is fixed — do not let it drift.

### Make Small Decisions That Signal Your Style

Between Days 30 and 60, you will face small decisions. Make them. Decisively. This is how the team learns what you stand for.

Examples:
- A P2 incident drags past MTTR target → you join the bridge. Signal: I hold the line on MTTR.
- A change gets approved by CAB that you think is risky → you call it out. Signal: I have technical standards.
- A Senior Manager cancels a 1:1 repeatedly → you reschedule and hold it. Signal: 1:1s are not optional.

Avoid making big structural decisions in this phase. Small, visible, consistent decisions build more credibility than large reorganizations.

### Identify Top 3 Operational Risks

By Day 45, you should have enough data to name your top 3 operational risks. Not general risks — specific, named risks with owners and current mitigation status.

Format per risk:
- **Risk**: [Specific thing that could go wrong]
- **Current state**: [What is happening right now that makes this a risk]
- **Likelihood**: High / Medium / Low
- **Impact**: High / Medium / Low
- **Current mitigation**: [What is already being done]
- **Gap**: [What is not being done that should be]
- **Proposed owner**: [Who should own driving this down]

Present these to your VP by Day 50. Do not wait for the 90-day review.

### Build Peer Relationships

By Day 60, you should have had at least one substantive working conversation (not a corridor chat) with:
- Engineering VP or Director
- Product VP or Director
- Security Director
- Finance business partner

The goal is not to be liked. The goal is to understand their priorities, their pain points with your org, and their view of what "good operations" means to them. These conversations will shape your roadmap.

## Phase 3 — Set Direction (Days 61–90)

### Build the 12-Month Operational Roadmap

This is your first major deliverable. It should cover:

1. **Reliability foundation**: What SLO targets are you committing to? What investments are required?
2. **Operational efficiency**: Toil reduction, automation, tooling improvements
3. **Transformation support**: What platform or product work does your org need to enable?
4. **People and capability**: What roles are being hired? What development is planned?
5. **Vendor and tooling**: What contracts are up? What tools need replacement or extension?

**Process (do not skip):**
1. Draft the roadmap yourself, based on your first 60 days of data
2. Share with each Senior Manager for their domain input and corrections
3. Revise with their input
4. Present to Senior Managers as a group — get their alignment and their names on it
5. Present to VP

The Senior Managers must feel they contributed before it goes to the VP. If they see it for the first time at the VP presentation, you have made a political error that will take months to recover from.

### Set Q Objectives with Each Senior Manager

By Day 80, each Senior Manager should have 3–5 Q objectives. Format:
- **Objective**: [Qualitative outcome — what is the direction?]
- **Key result 1**: [Measurable, binary, achievable in the quarter]
- **Key result 2**: [Measurable, binary, achievable in the quarter]
- **Owner**: [Senior Manager name]
- **How I support**: [What you will do to remove blockers]

These should connect to your 12-month roadmap. If a Senior Manager's objectives don't connect to the roadmap, the roadmap is wrong or the objectives are wrong.

### Identify and Launch First Quick Win

A quick win is a visible operational improvement that:
- Can be completed within the first quarter
- Is measurable (before/after data)
- Is meaningful to the team or to your VP

Examples:
- Reduce on-call page volume by 30% through automated noise reduction
- Close the 5 oldest P1 problems in the problem backlog
- Reduce change failure rate from 12% to <5% with improved pre-change checklists
- Automate a manual process that consumes >4 hours per week of engineering time

The quick win signals that you are operational, not just strategic. Pick something the team can execute without major new budget. Execute it. Announce the result with data.

### Senior Manager Development Conversations

By Day 90, have had a dedicated development conversation with each Senior Manager. This is separate from the 1:1.

Structure:
1. "Tell me about where you want to be in 3 years."
2. "What skills are you developing right now?"
3. "What do you want to work on that you're not currently working on?"
4. "Where do I see your strengths? Where do I see opportunity?" (Share your honest assessment.)
5. "Here's what I'm going to prioritize in supporting your development."

Document this conversation and reference it in future 1:1s. Senior Managers who feel their development is being actively managed by their Director are retained. Senior Managers who feel invisible leave — and they usually leave at the worst possible time.

## 90-Day Deliverable Checklist

### 30-Day Deliverable
- [ ] Private "state of the org" assessment written and shared with VP for gut-check
- [ ] 1:1 cadence established with all direct reports
- [ ] Skip-level listening tour complete
- [ ] CAB, incident bridge, and problem review attended

### 60-Day Deliverable
- [ ] Operating rhythm fully running (ops review, 1:1s, incident review)
- [ ] Top 3 operational risks identified, named, with owners, presented to VP
- [ ] Peer relationships initiated with Engineering, Product, Security counterparts
- [ ] Budget and vendor register reviewed

### 90-Day Deliverable
- [ ] 12-month operational roadmap presented to VP (after Senior Manager alignment)
- [ ] Q objectives set with each Senior Manager
- [ ] First quick win identified and in flight (ideally, already showing results)
- [ ] Senior Manager development conversations complete

## Common Errors in the First 90 Days

| Error | What it looks like | Why it's wrong |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| Moving too fast | Reorganizing teams or changing processes in week 2 | You don't know what you're changing yet. You will break things you don't know are load-bearing. |
| Making promises you can't keep | "I'll get you more headcount" before budget cycle | You train the team to distrust your commitments before you've made any. |
| Skipping Senior Manager alignment | Going directly to VP with your roadmap | Senior Managers will feel bypassed. They will be right. |
| Staying in meetings all day | Your calendar is full but you're not making decisions | Meetings are not management. Protect thinking time from week 1. |
| Trying to prove technical depth | Joining incident bridges to solve the technical problem yourself | Your job is to ensure the team can solve it, not to solve it. |
| Ignoring the data | Running on instinct and observation without pulling the numbers | Intuition is a starting hypothesis. Data confirms or refutes it. |
