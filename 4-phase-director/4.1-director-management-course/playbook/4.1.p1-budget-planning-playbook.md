# Budget Planning Playbook

**Audience**: Director of Operations  
**Cadence**: Annual. Timeline below assumes a January 1 fiscal year start. Adjust offsets for your org's fiscal calendar.  
**Purpose**: Walk through the full annual budget cycle — inventory, build, submit, defend, distribute — and manage in-year to plan.

---

## The Governing Principle

Budget planning is not a finance exercise. It is a prioritization exercise. Every dollar you request is a bet on what matters most. Every dollar you don't defend is a capability you're choosing not to have. Your VP will approve or cut based on the quality of your framing, not the size of your spreadsheet.

Know your number before you walk in the room. Know your cut scenario before they ask for it. Know your risk narrative before they say no.

| Phase | When (relative to Jan 1) | Output |

## Phase 1 — Inventory (Month -4)

**Purpose**: Understand exactly what you are spending today before you build next year's request.

### Step 1: Pull current-year actuals from Finance

Request from your Finance business partner:
- Year-to-date actuals by cost category (headcount, tooling, vendor services, training, travel)
- Full-year forecast (actuals + committed spend for remainder of year)
- Comparison to approved budget: where are you over? Where are you under?
- Any one-time spend this year that Finance has flagged (severance, unplanned tooling, emergency contracts)

Do not estimate. Pull the actual numbers. If Finance cannot provide line-level data, escalate. You cannot build a credible budget on approximations.

### Step 2: Senior Manager domain spend audit

Ask each Senior Manager to provide:
- Their domain spend this year vs. plan (if they have visibility to it)
- Their headcount actuals (filled seats vs. approved headcount)
- Their top 3 needs for next year — with business justification, not wish list framing
- Any vendor or tooling contracts they depend on that are renewing

**Warning**: Senior Managers will submit maximalist lists. That is appropriate at this stage. Your job in Phase 2 is to prioritize and cut, not theirs.

### Step 3: Vendor contract audit

Pull every active vendor contract in your domain. For each one:
- What is the annual value?
- When does it renew?
- What is the auto-renew notice period (typically 30–90 days)?
- Is it being used? (Ask the technical owner, not the vendor.)
- Is the SLA being met?
- Is the price going up next year? By how much?

Use the Vendor Contract Register template to track this. Every contract with a decision deadline in the next 18 months needs a decision by end of Phase 3.

**High-risk contracts to flag immediately**: any contract with a notice period where the deadline falls inside your budget season. If you miss the window, you auto-renew at full price whether you wanted to or not.

### Step 4: Identify recurring unplanned spend

List any spend item this year that was unplanned but will recur:
- Emergency licensing purchased mid-year
- Consulting or professional services that started mid-year and will continue
- Headcount replacements that came in late and now carry full cost next year

These must be baselined into next year's budget. Finance will not assume they're real unless you include them.

**Phase 1 output**: A complete inventory document. One table for headcount (current, attrition, new), one table for tooling (vendor, cost, renewal date, status), one table for known gaps.

## Phase 2 — Build (Month -3)

**Purpose**: Consolidate inputs into a defensible budget request with three scenarios.

### Step 1: Consolidate Senior Manager inputs

Take every SM request from Phase 1 and categorize:
- **Must have**: Required to maintain current service levels or compliance
- **Should have**: Would meaningfully reduce risk or improve efficiency, with measurable ROI
- **Nice to have**: Beneficial but deferrable

This is your first cut. You are not removing items yet — you are categorizing them so you know where the cuts will come from if needed.

### Step 2: Build the headcount plan

Format:

| Role | Current FTE | Planned Attrition | Replacement (Y/N) | New Role (Y/N) | Justification |
|------|------------|------------------|-------------------|---------------|---------------|
| Senior SRE | 6 | 1 (Q2) | Yes | No | Maintain on-call capacity |
| Platform Engineer | 4 | 0 | No | No | — |
| Engineering Manager | 1 | 0 | No | Yes | Scale to second team | New team launch in Q3 |

**For each new role**: justify with either a risk (what breaks without this role?) or an ROI (what does this role enable?). "We're growing" is not a justification.

**For attrition replacements**: document why you're backfilling vs. absorbing. "We are at minimum viable staffing" is a justification. "We have capacity" means you should not replace.

### Step 3: Build the tooling budget

| Tool | Current Annual Cost | Renewal Date | New Cost (if known) | Status | Decision |
|------|--------------------|--------------|--------------------|--------|---------|
| [Tool name] | $[X] | [Date] | $[X] or TBD | Renew / Renegotiate / Exit | [Owner] |

Separate into:
- **Renewals** (existing tools you are keeping)
- **New investments** (tools you are adding)
- **Exits** (tools you are removing — note the savings)

For new investments: ROI framing required. "This tool eliminates 8 hours/week of manual work across the team" is defensible. "Everyone uses it now" is not.

### Step 4: Build the transformation investment budget

Any project or initiative requiring dedicated budget (not covered by headcount run-rate):
- Consulting or professional services
- Platform migration costs
- Training programs (certifications, external)
- Pilot programs or POCs requiring vendor spend

For each item: scope, cost, expected outcome, timeline. If you cannot state the expected outcome in measurable terms, defer it to Phase 1 next year.

### Step 5: Total and pressure-test the request

Add up:
- Headcount total (current run-rate + attrition replacement + new roles)
- Tooling total (renewals + new investments - exits)
- Transformation investments total
- **Grand total**

Now ask yourself: is this within the realistic approval range for your organization? If it is more than 15–20% above last year's approved budget without a major strategic shift, you will be asked to cut. Do not wait to be asked.

### Step 6: Build the cut scenario before they ask

If asked to reduce by 15%, what goes?

Build this table explicitly:

| Item cut | Savings | Risk if cut | Risk level |
|---------|--------|-------------|-----------|
| [Role X] | $[X] | [Specific operational consequence] | H/M/L |
| [Tool Y] | $[X] | [Specific capability lost] | H/M/L |

The cut scenario is not a negotiating tactic. It is intellectual honesty. You are telling your VP: "Here is the trade-off you're making when you cut this." This framing puts the decision where it belongs — with the person who has the authority to accept the risk.

**Phase 2 output**: Three-table budget (headcount, tooling, transformation) + cut scenario table.

## Phase 3 — Submit and Defend (Month -2)

**Purpose**: Submit a written budget with narrative, and defend it in front of Finance or VP.

### Written submission format

**Section 1: Executive summary** (one paragraph)
What you're requesting, the total amount, and the one-sentence rationale: "This budget maintains Tier 1 SLA commitments, funds two roles required to support the Q3 platform migration, and invests in observability tooling that reduces MTTR by an estimated 20%."

**Section 2: Headcount table** (from Phase 2)

**Section 3: Tooling table** (from Phase 2)

**Section 4: Transformation investments** (from Phase 2)

**Section 5: Total and comparison to prior year**
Show: last year approved, this year actuals, next year requested, % change. Explain any variance >10%.

**Section 6: Risk of not approving**
For every item above "should have" threshold: what is the operational risk if this is not funded? Be specific.

### How to defend it in the room

**The "why do you need X?" question:**
Answer in one of two ways:
- ROI framing: "This [tool/role] will [save X hours/reduce X incidents/enable Y] which translates to approximately $[X] in value or risk reduction."
- Risk framing: "Without this, we are exposed to [specific operational risk] at [likelihood and impact]. The cost of a single [incident/breach/SLA miss] at this level is $[X]."

Do not say "everyone needs it" or "it's industry standard." Those are not arguments.

**The "what if you only get 80%?" question:**
Pull your cut scenario table. Show it. "If we receive 80%, I would recommend cutting [items] because they have the lowest impact relative to cost. The resulting risks are [specific list]. I would recommend accepting [low-risk cuts] and flagging [high-risk cuts] to you as accepted organizational risk."

Do not say "we'll make it work." That is not an answer.

**Phase 3 output**: Written budget submission document. Verbal defense of it in Finance/VP review.

## Phase 4 — Approved and Distributed (Month -1)

**Purpose**: Communicate the outcome clearly and distribute spending authority.

### Communicate to Senior Managers

Within one week of approval:
- What was approved (line by line, not summary)
- What was not approved (name it — do not let Senior Managers discover it later)
- Why items were cut (if known — "Finance did not approve new headcount in H1" is better than silence)
- What gaps exist as a result, and how you are handling them

If something a Senior Manager requested was cut, tell them directly before the general communication. Surprises in group settings erode trust.

### Flag gaps that affect delivery

If an approved budget creates a delivery gap:
- Name the gap in writing to your VP: "The Q3 platform migration requires [resource] that was not approved. Current plan is [workaround]. This carries [specific risk]. I want you to be aware."
- Do not silently absorb gaps. Surface them with your recommended mitigation.

### Distribute budget authority

Define, in writing, what each Senior Manager can approve without Director sign-off:

| Spend category | Senior Manager can approve up to | Director approval required above |
|----------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Training / conference | $[X] per person | Above $[X] or team-wide programs |
| Software licensing | $[X] annual | Above $[X] |
| Vendor statement of work | $[X] | Above $[X] |
| Contract changes | None | Always Director |
| Headcount additions | None | Always Director + Finance |

This table should be shared with Senior Managers and your Finance BP. Undocumented approval authority creates approval chaos mid-year.

**Phase 4 output**: Written communication to Senior Managers. Documented approval authority matrix. Budget loaded into Finance system by your Finance BP.

## In-Year Budget Management

### Monthly

- Pull actuals vs. plan from Finance (do not wait to be sent it — request it by the 10th of each month)
- For any variance >5% in a category: write a one-paragraph explanation
- Categories consistently underspending: flag for reforecast — unspent budget is not a win if it signals capability gaps
- Categories consistently overspending: identify the driver and either address it or reforecast

### Quarterly Reforecast

At end of each quarter:
- Update full-year forecast based on actuals to date
- Identify any items that will not be spent (opportunities to reallocate)
- Identify any items where spend will exceed plan (escalate to VP if material)
- Present reforecast in your quarterly VP review

### Mid-Year Unplanned Requests

When a Senior Manager brings you an unplanned budget request:
- What is the specific need?
- Is there an existing budget line it can come from?
- If not: what is the ROI or risk of not doing it?
- Can it wait until next budget cycle?

If you need to escalate to VP for unplanned spend, format it as: "I have an unplanned need for $[X] to [solve specific problem]. This was not in the budget because [reason]. The risk of not doing it is [specific]. I am requesting approval to reallocate from [source] or request an exception."

### Common In-Year Errors

| Error | Consequence |
|-------|------------|
| Losing track of committed vs. actual spend | Finance surprises at year-end |
| Letting Senior Managers spend outside their authority | Governance problems; audit risk |
| Not flagging gaps when they occur | VP is surprised; you lose credibility |
| Treating underspend as success | Missed capability investments; budget cut next year ("you didn't need it") |
| Approving unplanned spend without Finance visibility | Year-end overrun; relationship damage with Finance |
