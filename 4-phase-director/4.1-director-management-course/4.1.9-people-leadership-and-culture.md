# Module 09 — People Leadership and Culture

**Leading People at Scale: Culture, Performance, Development, and the Hard Conversations**

---

## Section 1: What Leadership Looks Like at Director Scale

At IC and Manager level, leadership is personal — you lead the people you work with daily. At Director scale, you lead through systems: the culture you set, the standards you enforce, the behaviors you reward and tolerate, the development you enable. Most of the 50–100 people in your org will not have regular direct contact with you — they experience your leadership through how their managers behave and what the org's systems reward.

This means: the culture you articulate only matters if your Senior Managers embody it in how they manage their teams. Your words are amplified by their behavior. If you say "we value proactive communication" but your Senior Managers punish engineers for flagging bad news, your culture is the Senior Managers' behavior, not your words.

## Section 2: Defining and Maintaining Culture

Operations culture has specific tensions that other functions don't.

### Tension 1: Heroics vs. Systems

Operations rewards firefighters — the engineer who saves the day at 2am. But organizations that optimize for firefighting create more fires. The Director's job is to explicitly reward the engineers who reduce the need for firefighting (problem resolution, automation, reliability engineering) at least as visibly as the engineers who fight fires.

Practical mechanism: in your monthly ops report and QBR, name both types of wins — "this week [engineer] resolved the payment service cold start problem that was causing 35% of our P3 incidents. We estimate this prevents 8 incidents per quarter."

### Tension 2: Speed vs. Rigor

Product teams want fast; operations teams want safe. The Director navigates this daily — in the change governance cadence, in the environment promotion gates, in the incident resolution vs. true-fix decision.

Your stance: non-negotiable on Tier 1 safety gates; flexible on speed for Tier 3 systems. Document this explicitly. "We do not take shortcuts on Tier 1 production changes. We accept managed risk on Tier 3 changes to enable development velocity." This is a cultural statement and a policy position.

### Tension 3: Blame vs. Learning

The blameless postmortem culture is essential for operational improvement. But "blameless" is often misunderstood as "no accountability." These are not the same thing.

**Blameless** means: the system is the focus, not the person. "Why did the system allow this configuration change to reach production without validation?" not "why did John make this configuration change?"

**Accountability** means: people are responsible for their roles. If a Senior Manager repeatedly fails to close P1 problems, that is a management accountability issue. Blameless culture does not protect managers from accountability for systemic failures in their domain.

### Defining Your Culture Explicitly

Write 5–8 operating principles for your org. Not values (integrity, excellence — every org says this). Principles: specific, behavioral, distinguishing.

Examples:
- "We escalate early. The cost of a false alarm is zero. The cost of a delayed escalation is an SLA breach."
- "We fix the system, not the symptom. Every workaround has a problem record. Every problem record has a due date."
- "We own the outcome of our domain, not just the tasks within it. If something in our domain is broken and we know it, we own it even if someone else caused it."
- "We make our work visible. Unreported progress doesn't exist. The metrics we track are the metrics we manage to."

Post these, reference them in 1:1s, use them to frame performance conversations. Culture is the operating principles you enforce consistently, not the posters on the wall.

## Section 3: Performance Management at Director Scale

You directly manage the performance of your Senior Managers. The rest of the org is managed through the layers below you.

### What "Good Performance" Looks Like for a Senior Manager

- Domain metrics are at or improving toward target, with a credible explanation for any misses
- Their team has low attrition and reasonable on-call load
- Peer teams describe working with their team as productive
- They develop their direct reports — managers under them are getting better, ICs under them are growing
- They surface real problems early, not just after they've become crises
- They make decisions within their authority without needing Director involvement for routine matters

### The Performance Conversation Model (for Senior Managers)

**Quarterly (informal, in 1:1):**
"Here's what I'm seeing this quarter. [Specific observations, both positive and developmental]. Where do you think you are? What's the thing you're working on?"

**Annual (formal review):**
"Here's my assessment of your year against the objectives we set. [Rating/level conversation]. Here's what I see as your trajectory. Here's what we're committing to for the next year."

**When to have the difficult performance conversation:**
- Domain metrics consistently below target with no improvement plan
- Team health indicators declining
- Peer relationship failures
- Decisions escalated to you that are within their authority

**The conversation structure:**
1. **Observation** (specific, behavioral): "Over the past 6 weeks, I've seen [X] happen [N] times."
2. **Impact**: "The effect of this is [Y]."
3. **Expectation**: "What I need is [Z] by [date]."
4. **Support**: "What do you need from me to get there?"
5. **Follow-up**: "We'll review this in [30 days]."

Document the conversation. Not a formal PIP document necessarily, but a written summary shared with the Senior Manager within 24 hours.

## Section 4: Developing Your Direct Reports

The three development conversations every Senior Manager should have with you annually:

### 1. The Strength Conversation

"Here's what I see as your superpower in this role. Here's how I think you can deploy it even more."

People don't develop by only working on weaknesses — they develop by deploying strengths in bigger contexts.

### 2. The Growth Edge Conversation

"Here's the thing that will limit your ceiling if you don't develop it. Here's why I think that. Here's what I'd suggest."

Honest, specific, forward-looking.

### 3. The Aspiration Conversation

"Where do you want to go? What does your 3-year vision look like? What role do you want to be in?"

This is the most important conversation most managers never have. If you don't know where your Senior Manager wants to go, you can't help them get there — and they will eventually find an org that will.

## Section 5: The Retention Problem — What Actually Keeps People

People stay for three reasons:
1. **Growth**: they feel like they're getting better and going somewhere
2. **Relationships**: their team and their manager are people they respect and enjoy working with
3. **Meaning**: they believe the work matters

People leave for three reasons:
1. **Stagnation**: same job, same skills, no trajectory
2. **Management**: their immediate manager is not investing in them, not advocating for them, or actively making their life harder
3. **Overload**: on-call load, toil, and the feeling of running on a treadmill

Salary is a threshold — not the driver. Pay people fairly and competitively. Below market, they'll leave for money. At market, salary is not the reason they stay or go.

### Director's Retention Levers

- **Visible career paths**: the ladder is explicit and your Senior Managers are actively discussing it with their teams
- **Stretch assignments**: engineers and managers get opportunities outside their comfort zone
- **Toil management**: you track toil, you invest in reducing it, you protect engineering time for meaningful work
- **Recognition**: operational excellence is recognized, not just taken for granted
- **Honest conversations**: people know where they stand — no surprises in reviews

The skip-level as a retention tool: quarterly skip-level 1:1s with your Senior Managers' reports give you early warning of attrition risk before an exit conversation happens.

## Section 6: The Director's Personal Leadership Practice

How you show up as a leader:

### Consistency

Your team needs to know what they're going to get from you. Not predictability in a boring way — consistency in your principles, your communication standards, your expectations. Directors who are emotionally unpredictable make their teams anxious and careful rather than engaged.

### Presence

You are not the most important person in any meeting you attend. Your job in most meetings is to listen, observe, and occasionally steer — not to be the smartest person in the room. Senior leaders who talk too much in meetings crowd out the information they need and signal to their teams that they value their own voice over collective intelligence.

### Self-Awareness

You have a significant status effect on every conversation in your org. When you express a preference, people hear it as a directive. When you ask a question, people think you already know the answer. Use this effect deliberately — and when you want genuine input, be explicit: "I don't have a view on this yet. I want your actual opinion."

### Learning

The Director who stops learning becomes the Director who becomes obsolete. Your domain knowledge will drift unless you maintain it. Your management skills require as much deliberate development as your technical skills once did. Find one management book per quarter, one peer Director outside your org to learn from, one mistake per quarter to genuinely reflect on.
