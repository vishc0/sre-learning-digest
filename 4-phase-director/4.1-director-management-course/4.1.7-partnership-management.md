# Module 07 — Strategic Partnership Management

**Audience**: Director / VP of Operations, managing a 20–100 person org in enterprise telecom.
**Length**: ~3000 words
**Prerequisite**: Module 03 (Budgeting), Module 05 (Vendor Management)

---

## The Vendor vs. Partner Distinction

A vendor sells you something. A partner co-creates value with you.

That sounds abstract, so make it concrete: a vendor who sells you Splunk licenses and a partner who helps you build a Splunk-based observability practice are two completely different relationships. The first is transactional — you pay, they deliver. The second is collaborative — both parties are invested in an outcome.

Why does this distinction matter operationally? Because the governance model is completely different.

| Dimension | Vendor Relationship | Partnership |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------|
| Contract type | Purchase order / license | SOW, MSA + partnership agreement |
| Success metric | "Did we receive what we paid for?" | "Did we achieve the shared outcome?" |
| Review cadence | Invoice approval | Quarterly business review |
| Accountability | Delivery of artifact | Operational outcome |
| Exit model | Cancel subscription | Transition plan + knowledge transfer |

A Director who treats all partners like vendors will under-extract value from partnerships. A Director who treats all vendors like partners will over-invest governance in low-stakes relationships. Know which is which.

## Section 1: Types of Partnerships an Operations Director Manages

### System Integrator (SI) Partners

**Examples**: Accenture, Infosys, Wipro, Cognizant, HCL, Unison — large delivery organizations that implement, integrate, and modernize complex technology programs.

**What they bring**:
- Delivery capacity you don't have internally (a team of 20 for an 18-month program)
- Methodology and playbooks for common patterns (cloud migration, SAP implementation, ITSM rollout)
- Specialized skill sets: Salesforce, SAP, Kubernetes, specific cloud platforms
- Offshore leverage: cost arbitrage for high-volume or repeatable work

**What they don't bring**:
- Knowledge of your specific environment — they are building that from scratch on day one
- Long-term ownership mentality — the project ends, they leave
- Accountability for operational outcomes after delivery — once they hand off, their obligation typically ends

**Where they fail**: SI engagements most commonly fail on knowledge transfer. The project delivers the capability, the SI leaves, and six months later you have a Tier 1 incident on the system and nobody internal knows how to operate it.

**Governance model**: Statement of Work (SOW), milestone-based delivery, formal acceptance criteria, knowledge transfer obligation.

---

### Independent Software Vendors (ISVs) / Product Partners

**Examples**: Splunk, ServiceNow, MuleSoft, Confluent (managed Kafka), Dynatrace, PagerDuty — companies whose software products are embedded in your operations.

The distinction from a pure vendor: an ISV partner relationship is deeper than licensing. It typically includes professional services, joint engineering touchpoints, beta program access, and mutual investment in the relationship's success.

**What the ISV partnership offers beyond licensing**:
- Co-development of connectors or integrations tailored to your environment
- Early access to beta features (you influence the product before it ships)
- Joint customer reference opportunities (leverage for negotiating)
- Dedicated support channels with faster SLAs than standard enterprise support
- Roadmap influence — your feedback is formally considered

**What you offer the ISV**:
- A reference customer and case study
- Product feedback that improves their offering
- Introductions to other potential customers in your network

**Governance model**: License agreement plus formal partnership agreement. Quarterly business reviews. Structured product feedback loop.

---

### Managed Service Providers (MSPs)

**Examples**: Companies that operate your NOC, SOC, DR services, or cloud managed services on your behalf.

This is the highest-stakes partnership category because the MSP's operational performance is your operational performance. If the MSP's NOC misses a P1 alert at 2am, your SLA is breached — not theirs.

**What distinguishes MSPs from vendors**:
- They operate on your behalf, with your obligations
- They hold institutional knowledge about your environment that may not exist anywhere else
- Their people become extensions of your team — or a substitute for it

**Risk profile**: Operational knowledge concentration. If the MSP holds all the runbooks and your internal team has atrophied, you are operationally dependent on a commercial relationship. If they raise prices, change key staff, or go out of business, you are exposed.

**Governance model**: Detailed SLA with pass-through language, monthly operational review, escalation path to MSP leadership, staff continuity requirements.

---

### Strategic Technology Alliances

**Examples**: AWS Premier Partner, Google Cloud Partner, Microsoft Partner Network — corporate-level designations that create access and benefits at scale.

These are often managed at a company level, but the Director's role is to ensure your teams are actually consuming the benefits:
- Training credits (are your engineers using them?)
- Technical account manager escalation path (is it activated when you need it?)
- Co-sell or co-marketing opportunities (are they being used strategically?)

**Director's obligation**: Quarterly check — what benefits are available under this alliance, which are being consumed, which are not. If a benefit isn't being used, either it has no value to your org (drop it) or nobody knows about it (communicate it).

---

### Professional Services Partners

Boutique consulting firms, specialized advisory, staffing partners. Smaller scale than SI; more focused scope.

**Governance**: SOW-based, project-scoped, deliverable acceptance. Simpler governance model than a full SI engagement because the scope is narrower.

## Section 2: Managing SI Partners

SI partnerships for major transformation programs (cloud migration, platform modernization, ITSM implementation) are complex, multi-month, high-stakes engagements. Managing them well is a specific discipline.

### The SOW as Your Primary Protection

The Statement of Work defines the relationship. A weak SOW creates room for scope creep, invoice disputes, and delivered-but-unusable outcomes. A strong SOW closes those gaps in advance.

**What every strong SI SOW must contain**:

| SOW Element | Weak Version | Strong Version |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Scope | "Cloud migration support" | "Migration of 23 services to AWS EKS with IaC in Terraform, 100% passing unit and integration tests, and runbooks for each service in your standard format" |
| Deliverables | "Knowledge transfer" | "Runbooks for all 23 services, accepted by SRE lead by [date], covering: alert description, diagnostic steps, resolution steps, escalation path, validated in tabletop" |
| Acceptance criteria | "Delivered" | "Reviewed and formally accepted by [named role] using [defined acceptance checklist]" |
| Milestones | "Upon completion" | "Payment tied to: design complete (20%), implementation complete (40%), testing complete (20%), knowledge transfer accepted (20%)" |
| Resource continuity | Not mentioned | "Key personnel listed in Exhibit A. Substitutions require 30-day advance notice and Director approval" |
| Knowledge transfer | Implied | "Project is not closed until internal team passes tabletop on each service without SI assistance" |

**The payment milestone principle**: tie every major payment to a deliverable accepted by your team. "We're almost there" is not an accepted deliverable.

### Governing the In-Flight Engagement

**Weekly**: project status review. Not a ceremony — hold them accountable to last week's commitments. Scope, schedule, risks, blockers.

**Monthly**: milestone review + payment release + risk assessment. If the milestone isn't met, the payment doesn't release. This concentrates their attention.

**Director's personal involvement**: you don't need to run the weekly. But you should be visible enough that the SI's leadership knows there is Director attention on this engagement. That visibility changes behavior.

### The Knowledge Transfer Problem

The most common failure mode in SI partnerships: the project completes, the SI leaves, and you have a production incident three months later that your team cannot resolve because the SI held all the knowledge.

**Prevention mechanisms**:

1. **Shadow staffing from day one**: your engineers shadow the SI team during implementation — not just for a handoff week at the end. If a new service is being built, your SRE who will own it is in the room while it's being built.

2. **Documentation as you go**: require runbooks for each component before the next component starts. Not a documentation sprint at the end of the project — documentation is a parallel workstream throughout.

3. **Tabletop before sign-off**: the acceptance test for knowledge transfer is a tabletop exercise. The SI is not in the room. Your team encounters a simulated production incident on the delivered system. Can they resolve it without calling the SI?

4. **Make it a payment milestone**: the final payment does not release until the knowledge transfer milestone is formally accepted by your operations lead.

## Section 3: Managing ISV / Product Partners

The ISV partnership is a two-way relationship. You are both a customer and a participant in their ecosystem.

### What a Good ISV Partnership Delivers

**Roadmap influence**: you provide structured product feedback, and the ISV demonstrates in their roadmap that it was considered. This is the primary value beyond the license.

**Technical alignment**: their roadmap direction matches your platform direction. If you are Kafka-first and the ISV is building deep integration with a competing platform and deprioritizing Kafka, the relationship has a horizon — plan accordingly.

**Support tier**: formal ISV partnerships typically include named enterprise support contacts, faster escalation SLAs, and early access to critical bug fixes before they go to the general release.

### Managing the ISV Relationship

**Quarterly Business Review (QBR)** — their attendance is mandatory, not optional:
1. Your usage and outcomes: data on how you're using the product, what outcomes you're getting, where the pain is
2. Their roadmap: what's shipping next two quarters, how does it affect you
3. Your product feedback: structured list of requests, bugs, usability issues — formal, not ad hoc
4. Next quarter's joint activities: commitments both sides are making

**Product feedback loop**: maintain a running log of product requests, bugs, and usability issues. Share it formally at each QBR. This is your leverage for roadmap influence — if you have 12 documented requests and they've addressed 3, you have a data-driven conversation.

**Version management**: establish your upgrade policy. How many major versions behind current release are you permitted to be? More than 2 major versions is a risk: support coverage narrows, security patches may not backport, known vulnerabilities accumulate.

## Section 4: Managing MSPs

Because MSP performance is your SLA performance, the governance model must be tighter here than for any other partnership type.

### Contract Requirements

| Requirement | What it means |
|-------------|---------------|
| Pass-through SLA language | If the MSP's MTTA target is 15 minutes and they miss it, your customer-facing SLA is also breached. Both sets of obligations must align. |
| Runbook ownership | Define who owns runbook updates and the process for keeping them current as your environment changes. |
| Escalation path | At what point does the MSP hand off to your internal team? What information must they include in the handoff? |
| Staff continuity | Key personnel on your account cannot be rotated without advance notice and your approval. |
| SLA credit mechanism | When the MSP misses SLA, what is the financial remedy? Credits must be automatic, not negotiated after the fact. |

### Monthly MSP Operational Review

| Metric | Why it matters |
|--------|----------------|
| MTTA actual vs. SLA | Primary performance indicator |
| Escalation rate (MSP → internal team) | High rate = MSP lacks runbook coverage or capability |
| Detection rate (MSP-detected vs. customer-reported) | Measures monitoring quality, not just response quality |
| SLA credits owed | Financial accountability |
| Quarter-over-quarter trend | Is performance improving, static, or declining? |

### The MSP Independence Test

Ask this question quarterly: "If the MSP went out of business tonight, how long before my team could perform their function independently?"

If the answer is more than 30 days, you have a dangerous dependency. The mitigation is not to fire the MSP — it is to build internal capability in parallel, so you are never fully dependent. At minimum, your team should be able to perform the MSP's function at degraded capacity.

## Section 5: Conducting a Partner QBR

A Quarterly Business Review with a strategic partner is a governance requirement, not a relationship maintenance exercise. Both parties should leave with data, clarity, and specific commitments.

### QBR Agenda (90 minutes)

| Block | Owner | Duration | Content |
|-------|-------|----------|---------|
| Operations review | Your team | 20 min | Usage data, SLA performance, incidents involving the partner, your satisfaction assessment (1–5, with rationale) |
| Partner review | Partner | 20 min | Their view of relationship health, what they've delivered, their operational issues, product/service roadmap |
| Gaps and issues | Joint | 20 min | Items where expectations aren't being met — specific, data-backed, proposed resolution and owner |
| Next quarter commitments | Joint | 20 min | Specific, measurable commitments from each party — carried forward to the next QBR's opening review |
| Strategic alignment | Joint | 10 min | Are we still heading the same direction? Is this partnership still serving both parties? |

### What Makes a QBR Useful vs. a Waste of Time

**Useful**:
- Both parties come with data, not anecdote
- Issues are raised directly and resolved, not avoided to preserve harmony
- Prior commitments are reviewed before new ones are made
- Both parties leave knowing what success looks like next quarter

**Waste of time**:
- Slide deck from the partner, no data from your side
- Issues exist but aren't raised because the relationship "feels good"
- No one takes notes on commitments
- No one reviews what was committed at the last QBR

The relationship preservation instinct — avoiding hard topics to keep the partner happy — is the enemy of useful QBRs. Partnerships that can't survive honest performance conversations are not partnerships.

## Section 6: Partnership Governance Structure

For each active strategic partnership, assign three roles:

| Role | Level | Responsibility |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Executive Sponsor | Director or above | Relationship owner. Attends QBRs. Makes strategic decisions about partnership direction. |
| Technical Owner | Senior Manager or Senior Engineer | Day-to-day relationship management. Technical coordination. Operational issue escalation. |
| Contract Owner | Director or Finance | Tracks contract terms, renewal dates, SOW changes, payment milestones. |

### Partnership Health Scorecard (Reviewed Quarterly)

| Partnership | Deliverables on Track | SLA Compliance | Satisfaction (1–5) | Renewal Recommendation |
|------------|----------------------|---------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| SI Partner A | Yes / No | X% | N | Renew / Renegotiate / Exit |
| ISV Partner B | Yes / No | X% | N | Renew / Renegotiate / Exit |
| MSP Partner C | Yes / No | X% | N | Renew / Renegotiate / Exit |

This scorecard goes to your VP quarterly. It creates visibility and creates a clear paper trail if you need to make an exit decision.

## Section 7: When and How to Exit a Partnership

### Signals a Partnership Is Not Working

- Deliverables are consistently late or below acceptance criteria — not once, as a pattern
- Key personnel rotate off your engagement and replacements lack context, without improvement
- SLA breaches occur without credible root cause analysis or improvement plans
- The partner's energy is concentrated on selling you the next engagement, not delivering the current one
- Their product or service roadmap has materially diverged from your direction

### Exit Process

**Step 1 — Contract review**: what are the exit terms? Notice period? What are the final deliverable obligations? Who owns what after exit?

**Step 2 — Knowledge capture before announcing**: before the exit conversation happens, ensure all documentation, credentials, access credentials, and institutional knowledge are extracted and owned internally. Once an exit is signaled, partner cooperation may decrease.

**Step 3 — Overlap period**: if possible, structure an overlap between the exiting partner and the replacement. A 30–60 day handoff period dramatically reduces knowledge loss.

**Step 4 — Post-exit documentation**: write a brief post-mortem on why the partnership failed. This informs future partner selection criteria and prevents repeating the same mistake with the next partner.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Partners are not vendors. The governance model must match the relationship type.
2. For SI partners: the SOW is your protection. Knowledge transfer is a payment milestone, not a courtesy.
3. For ISV partners: the primary value beyond licensing is roadmap influence. You only get it if you show up with data and formal feedback.
4. For MSPs: their SLA failure is your SLA failure. Build the MSP independence test into your quarterly governance.
5. The QBR is a governance tool, not a relationship ceremony. Both parties leave with specific commitments.
6. Exit partnerships based on data and trends, not single incidents. But when the pattern is clear, exit cleanly.
