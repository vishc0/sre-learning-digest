# Module 08 — Governance, Risk, and Compliance

**Audience**: Director / VP of Operations, managing a 20–100 person org in enterprise telecom.
**Length**: ~2500 words
**Prerequisite**: Module 04 (Incident Command), Module 05 (Vendor Management)

---

## Operating Within the Rules

A Director of Operations inherits a governance framework they didn't design. How you relate to that framework determines your credibility with the organization.

Directors who fight governance — treating audits as adversarial, treating policy as bureaucracy, treating compliance as something that happens to them — consistently lose. They create friction, accumulate risk, and lose the trust of finance, legal, and internal audit.

Directors who use governance as a management tool — designing controls that actually work, using the risk register as a leadership instrument, approaching audits as a verification of work already done — build credibility and political capital that compounds over time.

This module teaches the second posture.

---

## Section 1: The Governance Hierarchy

Your organization operates within multiple layers of governance. Knowing which layer you own and which you inherit shapes everything about how you respond to policy questions.

```
Company Policy
(set by Board / Legal / Compliance — you implement, you do not waive)
    ↓
Enterprise Standards
(set by CTO / Architecture / Security — you align to, you may influence)
    ↓
Domain Policies
(set by you — within the constraints above)
    ↓
Procedures and Playbooks
(set by your Senior Managers — operationalize your policies)
    ↓
Guidelines and Runbooks
(set by your teams — operationalize the procedures)
```

**Your authority** sits at the Domain Policy layer. You define how your organization implements the enterprise standards and company policies above you. You do not get to waive a company policy — but you do get to define the most operationally practical way to meet it.

**Where Directors get into trouble**: confusing "I don't like this policy" with "this policy doesn't apply to my org." Company policy applies. The Director's job is to design a compliant implementation, not to argue whether compliance is required.

**Where Directors create the most value**: operations teams own significant portions of enterprise governance — change governance, incident governance, environment governance, access governance. How well the Director designs and operates these controls determines the quality of the organization's overall compliance posture.

## Section 2: Operational Risk Management

Your organization owns operational risk — the probability that process, system, or people failures cause business disruption. Managing that risk is your responsibility, not the risk team's. The risk team provides the framework; you provide the substance.

### The Director's Risk Register

A risk register is a living document of the top 10–15 risks your organization is actively managing. It is not a set-and-forget artifact — it is a leadership instrument.

**Risk register structure**:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Risk description | Plain-language statement of what could go wrong |
| Likelihood | H / M / L — probability of occurrence in the next 12 months |
| Impact | H / M / L — business impact if the risk materializes |
| Current mitigation | What you are actively doing to reduce likelihood or impact |
| Mitigation effectiveness | H (eliminates) / M (reduces severity) / L (reduces likelihood only) |
| Residual risk | What remains after mitigation — is it acceptable? |
| Owner | Which Senior Manager is accountable for this risk |
| Review date | When this risk entry was last reviewed |

**Cadence**: you review the full register monthly. Your VP reviews the top 5 risks quarterly. This ensures risks don't age out of relevance and accountability stays clear.

**Why the risk register matters in audits**: a well-maintained risk register is evidence that you are managing risk proactively, not reactively. An auditor who asks "how do you manage operational risk?" receives a document with dates, owners, and mitigations — not a verbal description of how good your team is.

### Common Operational Risks for an Operations Director

| Risk | Typical Trigger | Mitigation Approach |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Tier 1 SLA breach | Capacity, cascading dependency failure | Capacity reviews, DR testing, dependency mapping |
| Key person dependency | Single engineer who knows a critical system | Cross-training mandate, runbook requirement for all Tier 1 systems |
| Vendor / MSP failure | Single-source provider outage or insolvency | MSP independence test, secondary option maintained |
| On-call coverage gap | Attrition, insufficient headcount for 24x7 | Pager load monitoring, hiring trigger thresholds |
| EOL / obsolete technology | Running software past vendor support date | EOL register, remediation roadmap with dates |
| High change failure rate | Weak testing, insufficient change process | Change failure rate metric, CAB quality reviews |
| Environment parity failure | Prod/non-prod divergence | IaC enforcement, parity testing in pipeline |

## Section 3: Compliance Obligations for an Operations Team

In enterprise telecom, your operations org touches multiple compliance frameworks. You do not need to be a compliance expert — but you need to know which frameworks apply to your domain, what evidence they require, and who on your team is responsible for producing it.

### Compliance Frameworks and Operations' Role

| Framework | What It Requires from Operations | Director's Obligation |
|-----------|----------------------------------|----------------------|
| **SOC 2 Type II** | Evidence that operational controls (change management, access management, incident response) are designed and operating consistently | Ensure controls are designed correctly. Ensure evidence is generated through normal operations, not reconstructed for audits. |
| **PCI DSS** | Change management controls specifically for systems in cardholder data scope. Separate approval chains, stronger access controls. | Maintain clear scope boundary. Separate change governance for PCI systems. Produce change records on demand. |
| **FCC / CPNI** (telecom-specific) | Outage reporting obligations — FCC Form 480 for major outages within 30 minutes. Customer data handling requirements. | Ensure IC process includes FCC notification trigger. Know the threshold. Do not leave this to legal to catch. |
| **ISO 27001** | ISMS alignment — risk management, change management, incident management processes map to defined controls | Map operational processes to ISO control domains. Provide evidence at certification/surveillance audits. |
| **Internal audit** | Annual or biennial review of operational controls. Self-assessment readiness. | Maintain control evidence throughout the year. Conduct self-assessment 30 days before scheduled audit. |

### The Audit-Readiness Test

Run this test quarterly: "If an auditor walked in today and asked for evidence that our change management process, incident management process, and access management process are operating as documented — could we produce it within 24 hours?"

If yes: you are audit-ready. The audit is a verification of work already done.

If no: you have a control gap. A control gap found by your internal team is a finding you manage. A control gap found by an external auditor is a finding that goes to your VP and the audit committee.

### Evidence Types Auditors Request

| Control Area | What Auditors Ask For |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Change management | CAB minutes, change approval chain in ITSM, change records with approval timestamps |
| Incident management | Incident records with severity, timeline, impact, resolution time (from ServiceNow or equivalent) |
| Access management | Access review records — who has access to what, reviewed by whom, on what date, what was removed |
| Patch management | Patch records showing what was patched, when, and evidence of testing before production deployment |
| Training completion | Annual security awareness completion by percentage and name |

**The audit evidence discipline**: audit evidence should be a byproduct of operations running correctly, not a project that happens when an audit is scheduled. If your Change Manager is logging changes correctly in ServiceNow throughout the year, evidence collection is a report. If they're not, you are reconstructing history — which is both painful and suspicious to auditors.

## Section 4: Your Role in Security Governance

You are not the CISO. But operations and security intersect constantly, and the Director must own that intersection.

### Where Operations Touches Security

**Change management for security patches**: security patches must flow through your change governance, but with an expedited path. A critical vulnerability (CVSS 9+) cannot wait for the next CAB meeting. Your Emergency Change process must accommodate security patches with reduced cycle time and appropriate approvals.

**Access management**: your teams have privileged access to production systems — root, kubectl cluster-admin, database admin credentials. This access must be:
- Inventoried (who has what access, where is it documented)
- Reviewed quarterly at minimum (is this person still in this role? do they still need this access?)
- Governed by least-privilege principle (they have what they need, not everything that's convenient)

**Incident response coordination**: when a security incident is also an operational incident — ransomware, DDoS, data exfiltration — two incident commanders may be running simultaneously. You own the operational response (keep services running, manage the customer impact). Security owns the investigation. Define in advance who the handoff is between, what information moves at the handoff, and when the operational IC defers to security vs. continues independently.

**Vulnerability management**: security will surface vulnerabilities in your domain. You need a defined process:
1. Receive vulnerability (from security team or tool)
2. Assess impact and exploitability in your environment
3. Assign remediation owner and SLA (Critical: 24h, High: 7d, Medium: 30d, Low: 90d)
4. Track to closure and report status to security monthly

### The Quarterly Security Posture Conversation

Meet with your CISO or security team lead quarterly. Agenda:

| Topic | What You're Discussing |
|-------|----------------------|
| Open vulnerabilities | What's in your domain, what's overdue, what's blocked |
| Access review status | Completed on schedule? Exceptions found and remediated? |
| Security incidents this quarter | Any that touched your systems? Lessons that change your controls? |
| Upcoming audit scope | What's being assessed? What evidence do you need to prepare? |
| Policy changes | Any enterprise security policy changes that require your domain policies to update? |

## Section 5: The Director and Policy

The governance hierarchy distinguishes between policies you implement and policies you create. Know which is which.

### Policies You Typically Own

- **Incident severity and declaration policy**: what defines a P1 vs. P2 vs. P3. Who has authority to declare at each level.
- **Change management policy**: what goes to CAB vs. standard change vs. emergency change. Approval chains for each.
- **Environment access policy**: who can access which environment (dev / QA / staging / prod), with what approval, logged how.
- **On-call policy**: rotation requirements, pager load targets, escalation paths, minimum staffing thresholds.
- **Toil budget policy**: what percentage of engineering capacity is acceptable for undifferentiated operational work before you invest in automation or staffing.

### Writing an Operational Policy

A policy is not a procedure. A policy states WHAT must happen. A procedure states HOW.

**Policy structure**:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Purpose | Why does this policy exist? What risk does it mitigate or what obligation does it fulfill? |
| Scope | Who does this apply to? Which systems, environments, or roles? |
| Policy statements | Numbered, specific, verifiable statements of what must be true. "All changes to production systems must be approved by a Change Approver before implementation." |
| Exceptions process | How does someone request an exception? Who approves it? How is it documented? |
| Owner | Which role is responsible for this policy, reviews it annually, and enforces it? |
| Review cycle | Policies must be reviewed at minimum annually. Define the trigger for off-cycle review (major incident, audit finding, organizational change). |

### The Exception Process Is Not Optional

Every policy needs a documented exception process. Without one, two things happen:
1. Teams apply the policy inflexibly when circumstances genuinely require flexibility
2. Teams quietly ignore the policy and don't tell you

An explicit exception process creates transparency. It surfaces real-world pressure points that indicate the policy needs refinement. It maintains the policy's integrity because exceptions are documented and owned — not hidden.

**Exception process minimum elements**:
- Who submits the exception request (and in what format)
- Who approves it (and at what level for what duration)
- What conditions must be met for exception approval
- How long the exception is valid (no permanent exceptions)
- How the exception is tracked and reviewed

## Section 6: Avoiding the Governance Bottleneck

The most common governance failure for new Directors: they position themselves as the approval for every governance decision. The result is a bottleneck that slows the organization, creates a single point of failure, and communicates to senior leadership that the Director cannot delegate.

### The Design Goal

Governance that works without Director involvement for routine decisions.

The Director's role in governance is to design the system, not to run it. You set the policies. You train the Senior Managers who operate them. You audit the outcomes. You intervene when the system is failing.

### Achieving Appropriate Delegation

**Define "standard" clearly enough** that your Senior Managers can classify a change or incident without asking you. If the classification criteria are ambiguous, every edge case comes to you. Write the criteria with enough specificity to eliminate 90% of classification questions.

**Delegate CAB authority** to the Change Manager for Standard and Normal changes. The CAB chair should be your Change Manager, not you. You attend the CAB occasionally — for visibility, for culture-setting — not as the approver.

**Delegate incident declaration authority** to ICs for P2 and below. Your Senior Managers should be authorizing P2 declarations without calling you. You are notified, not consulted.

**Reserve your direct involvement** for:
- Policy exceptions (Director-level, by definition)
- P1 / Tier 1 incidents (Executive Sponsor role, not IC)
- Emergency changes affecting Tier 1 systems
- Audit findings that require Director-level commitment to remediate

### The Governance Test

Run this test on yourself: "If I were on vacation for two weeks with no connectivity, what decisions would stop or be delayed because they need my approval?"

The answer should be: very few — and those few should be genuinely Director-level decisions (policy exceptions, P1 incident command decisions, vendor contract changes above a dollar threshold).

If the list is long, you have a delegation problem. Fix it by refining your policies and procedure clarity, not by being more available. The vacation test is a delegation quality check, not a staffing problem.

## Section 7: Governance as a Leadership Instrument

The most senior Directors don't experience governance as a constraint — they use it as a tool.

**Change governance tells you the health of your delivery pipeline**: change frequency, change failure rate, and CAB backlog are real-time indicators of engineering quality and organizational tempo. If you're reading CAB metrics monthly, you don't need to ask how delivery is going — the data tells you.

**The risk register tells you where your Senior Managers are losing sleep**: if a risk has been at High likelihood for three quarters with no mitigation progress, the owner needs coaching, resources, or escalation — not another review cycle.

**The incident record tells you where your architecture is fragile**: patterns in incident causation (same system, same failure mode, same hour of the week) surface before your monitoring does. Review incident trends monthly, not just individual postmortems.

**Audit preparation surfaces control gaps before auditors do**: the quarterly audit-readiness test is not for auditors — it is for you. Every gap you find and fix is a risk you've reduced. Every gap an auditor finds is a gap in your management posture.

Governance is not paperwork. It is the operating system of your organization. Design it deliberately, maintain it actively, and use the signal it generates to lead.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. The governance hierarchy defines your authority: you set domain policies within enterprise standards. You implement company policy — you do not waive it.
2. The risk register is a leadership instrument, not a compliance artifact. Review it monthly; use it to manage your Senior Managers' accountability.
3. Compliance obligations in telecom operations span SOC 2, PCI, FCC reporting, and internal audit. Know which frameworks apply to your domain and what evidence each requires.
4. Audit-readiness is an operational discipline, not a pre-audit project. Evidence should be a byproduct of operations running correctly.
5. Security governance is your co-responsibility. Own the intersection: change management for patches, access reviews, incident coordination, vulnerability remediation SLAs.
6. Design governance to work without you for routine decisions. Reserve your involvement for Director-level calls. Run the vacation test quarterly.
7. Governance generates management signal. Read the signal.
