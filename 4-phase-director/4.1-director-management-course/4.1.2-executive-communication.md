# Module 02 — Executive Communication

**Estimated time**: 2–3 hours  
**Apply for**: 1 week before moving to Module 03  
**Core question**: How do you build credibility with the VP and executives above you?

---

## Reporting Up, Building Trust, and Managing the Relationship with Your VP

The Director sits between the executive layer and the operational layer. Your org produces facts; executives need signals. Translating between these two layers — accurately, concisely, and credibly — is one of the most underestimated skills at this level, and one of the most visible.

Every communication you send upward either builds or erodes executive confidence in your org. There is no neutral.

---

## Section 1: What Executives Actually Need from You

Executives — VP, SVP, CTO, CEO — have a different information problem than you do. They have too much information, not too little. They receive signals from customers, peers, board members, and dozens of operational teams simultaneously. Your job is not to give them more information. Your job is to give them the right signal, pre-processed, so they can make decisions or direct resources.

What executives need from you:

**No surprises.** They hear about problems from you before they hear about them from anyone else — customers, peers, audit, the board. The moment an executive learns something about your org from an external source before you told them, your credibility takes a hit that takes months to recover.

**Clean signal.** "We had 3 P1 incidents this month, MTTR is improving 15% month over month, and here is the one risk I am actively managing." That is a clean signal. Forty-seven slides is not a signal — it is data transfer, and it makes the executive do your job of synthesizing it.

**Decision-ready framing.** When you need something from them — budget, air cover, a decision — you arrive with a recommendation and the context needed to approve it. Not a problem for them to solve. Not a request for their opinion on a question you should have answered. A recommendation with supporting rationale, sized to fit their available context.

**Confidence in the org.** Executives need to believe the operation is in capable hands. This confidence is not built by the absence of incidents — incidents happen in every org. It is built by the quality of your response to incidents, the accuracy of your forecasts, and the consistency between what you say will happen and what actually happens.

What executives do not need:
- Detailed operational rundowns they cannot act on
- Problems without proposed solutions
- Defensive explanations for why something went wrong
- Ambiguity about whether something is resolved
- Your team's workload volume as a proxy for value

## Section 2: The Weekly Status Report

This is one of your highest-leverage communication tools. Sent every Friday. Delivered before the executive's Monday morning. Written from their perspective, not from your team's.

One page maximum. If you cannot say what matters on one page, you have not finished thinking.

### Template

```
Week of [Date] — Operations Summary

Highlights (3 bullets — what went well or materially progressed):
- Service X availability held at 99.97% through the peak traffic window for the holiday campaign.
- Problem Management: 2 long-running P1 problems closed this week; rolling MTBI improved 22% month over month.
- Environment team completed all 4 sprint trueups ahead of schedule; no environment-related delivery delays this sprint.

Risks (2 bullets — what I am watching, with owner and next update):
- Vendor Y SLA at risk: response time SLA breached twice this month. Meeting with Vendor Y VP on Tuesday.
  Owner: [Senior Manager Name]. Update by Thursday.
- On-call load: Team A averaging 7 pages/night. Hiring requisition submitted for 2 FTE.
  Risk: coverage gap persists until Q2 hire completes onboarding. Mitigation: contractor backfill in evaluation.

Decision needed (1 item or "none this week"):
- Requesting approval for $45K unbudgeted spend on monitoring tooling capacity expansion.
  Business case attached. Needed by end of month to meet Q2 implementation schedule.

Metrics snapshot:
| Metric                | This Week | Target   | Trend |
|-----------------------|-----------|----------|-------|
| P1 incidents          | 0         | 0        | ✓     |
| MTTR (P1/P2 average)  | 47 min    | <60 min  | ✓     |
| SLO compliance        | 99.91%    | 99.9%    | ✓     |
| Change failure rate   | 3.1%      | <5%      | ✓     |
| Open P1 problems      | 2         | <3       | ✓     |
```

### What Makes This Report Work

The report is written from the executive's perspective, not from the operational team's perspective. Your team had 847 tickets closed this week. That is irrelevant to an executive who needs to know whether the operation is healthy and whether anything requires their attention.

Numbers tell the story. Prose supports the numbers. If a metric is in the yellow or red, the prose explains what you are doing about it — not why it happened.

Risks are called out proactively. The executive should never have to ask "are you watching this?" They should already know from your report that you are, who owns it, and when the next update is coming.

Tone is confident, not defensive. Bad reports hedge. Good reports state what is true and what is being done. "We experienced 3 P2 incidents this week. MTTR was within target on all three. Root cause on the largest is identified; remediation scheduled for next week's maintenance window." That is confident. "Unfortunately we had some incidents this week, but the team worked really hard and…" — that is defensive and it makes the executive wonder what you are not saying.

## Section 3: The Monthly Operational Review

More comprehensive than the weekly status. Presented verbally in 15 minutes with the written report as a leave-behind. The written report exists so the executive can refer to it later — it is not a script for your verbal presentation.

### Written Report Structure

1. **Executive summary** — 3 sentences: what is healthy, what is at risk, and what is trending in the right direction
2. **Availability scorecard** — actual vs. target by service tier, 3-month rolling
3. **Incident summary** — volume by severity, MTTR trend (rolling 3-month chart), notable incidents with one-line description
4. **Problem management** — open P1/P2 problem count, average age, fix delivery rate vs. target, problems closed this month
5. **Change management** — total changes, failure rate, emergency change percentage, DORA lead time and deployment frequency trend
6. **SLA compliance** — services at risk, breach count, credits issued or at risk of issuance
7. **Environment health** — environment incidents, MTTE, trueup completion rate, delivery impact count
8. **Team health** — headcount vs. plan, on-call load by team, open requisitions, attrition this quarter
9. **Top 3 risks** — with owner, mitigation status, and target resolution date
10. **Next quarter preview** — what the org is focused on and why

### Presenting It Verbally

Lead with the headline. Do not bury it. "This was a strong month — availability held, problem backlog decreased, and we closed the two most expensive P1 problems. One risk I want to flag before we go into detail." Then go into detail.

Do not spend 12 of your 15 minutes on what happened and 3 minutes on what is next. Executives know what happened — they read the weekly reports. The monthly meeting is for analysis, risk discussion, and forward-looking decisions. Spend 5 minutes on the past, 10 minutes on what it means and what comes next.

Common mistake: reading the report aloud. The executive can read. Your verbal presentation should add interpretation, not transcript.

## Section 4: The Quarterly Business Review

This is the highest-stakes presentation most Directors run. Audience: your VP plus potentially SVP, CPO, CTO, or CFO. You have a 30-minute slot in a packed executive agenda. Use it precisely.

### Slide Structure (8 slides maximum)

**Slide 1 — Operational health dashboard**  
Green/amber/red by domain. One number per domain if possible. This slide should communicate the state of the operation in 10 seconds. Executives make a subconscious assessment of your org before you say a word — make that assessment accurate.

**Slide 2 — Quarter in review**  
Two bullets per domain: what improved and what is still in progress. Not what the team did — what changed as a result of what the team did. "Deployed automated rollback capability; emergency change lead time reduced 40%" not "team completed automated rollback project."

**Slide 3 — Top 3 things that went well (with data)**  
Not bragging — evidence that the org is capable and improving. Each point has a number attached. "Achieved 99.94% availability in Q3, up from 99.89% in Q2. MTTR for P1 incidents improved 28% year over year."

**Slide 4 — Top 2 risks**  
What you are watching and what you are doing about it. Not a comprehensive risk register — the 2 things that would concern the executive if they knew about them and would concern you if they did not have a mitigation in place.

**Slide 5 — DORA trend**  
Four metrics on one chart: deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, MTTR. Rolling 4-quarter trend. This is the closest thing to a single-slide health indicator for an engineering operations org.

**Slide 6 — Investment summary**  
Budget vs. actuals. Top 3–4 investments made this quarter and what they produced. This answers the implicit executive question: "Are we spending the right money on the right things?"

**Slide 7 — Next quarter priorities**  
Three to five things the org is focused on, explicitly tied to company OKRs or executive priorities. This demonstrates you understand how your work connects to what leadership cares about.

**Slide 8 — What I need from you**  
Specific decisions, resource requests, or air cover. Not vague asks ("your continued support"). Concrete items: "Approval for 2 additional FTE headcount to close the on-call coverage gap" or "Air cover for the cross-functional dependency on Platform to complete the API migration before our SLA review in April."

### The Slide 1 and Slide 8 Rule

Executives remember two things from a QBR: the headline of slide 1 and whatever you asked for on slide 8. Everything in between is supporting evidence. If slide 1 is unclear and slide 8 is vague, the meeting produced nothing. Spend more time on those two slides than on all the others combined.

## Section 5: Communicating Bad News

When something goes wrong — a P1 outage, SLA breach, missed commitment, team failure, vendor collapse — the instinct is often to wait until it is resolved before communicating upward. This is almost always the wrong call.

**Rule: executives hear bad news from you, not from someone else first.**

The moment a VP learns about a problem in your org from a customer escalation, a peer, or their own boss — before you told them — your credibility is materially damaged. It signals either that you did not know (competence concern) or that you knew and withheld it (trust concern). Neither is recoverable quickly.

### The Bad News Communication Model

The goal is to communicate quickly, calmly, and with enough information that the executive can decide whether they need to act or whether you have it covered.

**Step 1 — Acknowledge immediately**  
"I want to flag an issue before you hear about it elsewhere." This single sentence changes the dynamic. You are the one bringing it. That matters.

**Step 2 — State the impact**  
"Service X has been degraded for 90 minutes. Estimated N customers affected. SLA is at risk if not resolved within 2 hours."

**Step 3 — State current status**  
"Incident command is [Name]. Mitigation is in progress — estimated resolution by [time]. We are tracking in [channel/ticket]."

**Step 4 — State next update time**  
"I will update you in 30 minutes or when resolved, whichever is first." Then deliver that update on time, even if there is nothing new to report. "Still in progress, no change, next update in 20 minutes" is a full update when the commitment is active.

**Step 5 — Post-resolution brief**  
Within 24 hours: what happened, impact summary, what fixed it, and what prevents recurrence. This does not need to be long — half a page is fine. It should be written and sent, not delivered verbally.

### What Executives Hate More Than Bad News

They hate discovering bad news themselves and realizing you knew. This ends careers.

Proactive communication of bad news, delivered calmly with a plan attached, builds credibility. It communicates: "I am in control of this org. I know what is happening. I have a plan. I am not hiding from you." That is a Director they can trust.

Delayed communication of bad news communicates: "I was hoping this would resolve itself before I had to tell you." That is not a Director they can trust.

## Section 6: Managing Up — The VP Relationship

Your VP is a human being with their own pressures, KPIs, political landscape, and stakeholders. Managing this relationship actively is part of your job description, not an optional add-on.

### What Your VP Needs from You

**To not worry about your org.** This does not mean never surfacing problems — it means they trust that you have visibility, that you will surface things early, and that when something requires their attention, you will tell them in time to act.

**Your honest assessment.** VPs who receive sanitized views make bad decisions. Your job is to give them the real picture — what is actually healthy, what is fragile, and where you are managing a real risk. Executives who are protected from bad news inside their org eventually get surprised by it from outside.

**Infrequent but significant escalations.** If you escalate everything, your VP stops trusting your judgment — because you apparently cannot distinguish between things you can handle and things you cannot. If you escalate nothing, you are not using the relationship you have, and you will eventually hit a blocker you cannot clear at your level that has been festering.

### What You Need from Your VP

**Air cover.** Protection from cross-functional interference that would slow your team down. Other Directors who create friction, upstream teams that consistently miss commitments, executive stakeholders who bypass your chain and assign work directly to your engineers — these are things your VP can help with when you cannot.

**Budget authority clarity.** Know exactly what spending decisions are yours to make vs. what requires their sign-off. Ask explicitly. Ambiguity here leads to either unnecessary escalations that waste their time or unauthorized commitments that damage trust.

**Strategic context.** What is coming in the next 6 months that will affect your org's work? Your VP has visibility into roadmap, org changes, and executive priorities that you may not. Build the relationship where they share this context with you early, not after decisions are made.

**Decision support on cross-functional conflicts.** When a conflict with a peer Director cannot be resolved at your level, your VP should be able to help resolve it at theirs. Use this sparingly — every escalation costs some political capital.

### The Monthly 1:1 with Your VP

30 minutes. Not a status report — that comes in writing before the meeting. The 1:1 is for things that do not fit in a written format:

- Strategic alignment: "Here is what I'm planning to prioritize next quarter. Does that match what you need from my org?"
- Escalations requiring their attention: one or two, framed as decisions needed, not just problems
- Relationship building: understand their priorities, their pressures, and what they are being measured on

Listen 40% of the time. Ask: "What is the thing you are most worried about related to my org right now?" Then take the answer seriously and address it. Executives notice when you asked and then acted on the answer.

### Reading Your VP's Communication Style

Some VPs want daily contact. Some want you to surface things only when you need them. Some want data-heavy reports. Some want a 30-second verbal brief. You need to figure this out in the first 30 days and then adapt.

The fastest way to find out: ask directly. "How do you prefer to receive updates from your Director-level team? Do you want me to reach out proactively on P2s or only P1s? Do you prefer written updates or quick calls?" Most VPs appreciate the question. It signals you are thinking about their experience, not just your own.

## Section 7: The Skip-Level Relationship

You will occasionally present to or interact with the SVP, CTO, or COO above your VP — in QBRs, executive staff meetings, or ad hoc conversations.

Three rules for skip-level interactions:

**Your VP should never be surprised by what you say.** Brief them before any skip-level presentation or meeting. Tell them what you plan to say and what you might be asked. This protects them from being caught flat-footed in front of their boss, and it protects you from saying something that contradicts their narrative.

**One version of the truth.** Your message to the skip-level executive should be consistent with what you tell your VP. Any divergence — even accidental — signals political maneuvering and erodes the trust of both. If you find yourself wanting to say something to the SVP that you have not said to your VP, that is a signal to have the conversation with your VP first.

**You are being evaluated on your VP's behalf.** In a skip-level setting, the senior executive is partly assessing whether your VP has made a good hire and is running a capable org. Represent that accurately. Your performance in those rooms reflects on your VP's judgment.

## Section 8: The Communication Habits That Build Credibility Over Time

Credibility with executives is built through repetition, not through a single great presentation. The habits that build it:

| Habit | Why It Matters |
|-------|---------------|
| Weekly status delivered consistently, before Monday | Shows discipline and respect for their time |
| No surprises on bad news | Signals you have visibility and trust the relationship |
| Commitments kept or explicitly revised before they miss | Shows you take your word seriously |
| Metrics that are consistent week over week | Prevents "why is this measured differently this month?" conversations |
| Recommendations with the "what I need from you" explicit | Respects their time; executives appreciate decisiveness |
| Follow-through on action items from their meeting | Shows you listen and execute; many Directors do not |

And the habits that erode it:

| Habit | Why It Damages |
|-------|---------------|
| Status reports that are inconsistent or skipped | Signals disorganization or avoidance |
| Learning about your org's problems from other sources | Signals poor visibility or worse — withholding |
| Vague asks ("your support") instead of specific requests | Forces them to synthesize; feels like you do not know what you need |
| Presenting data without interpretation | Makes them do your job in the meeting |
| Reversing a position without explaining what changed | Signals instability or political maneuvering |

## Self-Assessment

Answer these before moving to Module 03.

1. Does your VP ever learn about a problem in your org from a source other than you? If yes, what is the gap?
2. Is your weekly status report something your VP would find useful on its own — or does it require context from previous conversations to make sense?
3. When you last communicated bad news upward, did you do it before or after it was resolved? Before or after the VP heard about it elsewhere?
4. Do you know what your VP is most worried about right now regarding your org? If not, have you asked?
5. In your last QBR or major executive presentation, was slide 1 (the state of the operation) clear within 10 seconds? Was your ask on the last slide specific and decision-ready?

---

## What Good Looks Like

Your VP expresses confidence in your org when speaking to their peers. They do not worry about what is happening in your domain because you have made it predictable. When something goes wrong, they hear it from you first. The frequency of your escalations is low enough that when you do escalate, it is taken seriously immediately.

## What Failure Looks Like

Your VP discovers problems from other sources. They receive inconsistent information across different meetings. They have to ask for status instead of receiving it. They are not sure what you need from them and have to guess. They hedge when asked by their own leadership whether your org is under control.
