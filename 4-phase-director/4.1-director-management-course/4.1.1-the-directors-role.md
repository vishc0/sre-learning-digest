# Module 01 — The Director's Role

**Estimated time**: 2–3 hours  
**Apply for**: 1 week before moving to Module 02  
**Core question**: What changes when you become a Director — and what do you stop doing?

---

## The Identity Shift

Most people become Directors because they were excellent Senior Managers or Principal Engineers. The skills that made them excellent at that level can actively work against them at Director level.

The promotion is not a reward for past performance. It is a signal that the organization believes you can operate differently. Whether you actually do is on you.

This module is about the identity shift — what changes, what you stop doing, and what you must become.

---

## Section 1: What Changes When You Become Director

### From Executor to Designer

At every level below Director, your value was partly in the quality of your execution. As a Senior Manager, you designed solutions and oversaw their execution. As a Director, your job is to design the **system** in which others design and execute.

The difference is concrete:

- A Senior Manager asks: "Is this incident being resolved correctly?"
- A Director asks: "Is the incident management system producing the results we need — and if not, what is wrong with the system?"

The Senior Manager improves the answer. The Director improves the question-answering machine.

This feels uncomfortable at first because it puts distance between you and the thing you are good at. That discomfort is the signal you are doing it right. If you are still diving into incident resolution, you are executing, not designing.

### From Expert to Enabler

Your value at Director level is not your technical depth. It is your ability to multiply others.

The Director who always has the best answer trains their team to wait for the Director's answer. Every time you solve the problem instead of helping your senior manager solve it, you have:
- Taken on work that should not be yours
- Robbed your senior manager of a development opportunity
- Subtly signaled that their judgment is not trusted
- Created a dependency that will cost you time the next time a similar problem appears

You must actively suppress the impulse to solve the problem yourself. This is not passive — it requires discipline and practice. The target behavior: when a senior manager brings you a problem, your first move is a question, not an answer. "What options have you considered?" or "What would you do if I weren't available?" — and then hold the silence.

### From Individual Accountability to Systemic Accountability

As a Senior Manager, you were accountable for your team's results. If your team had a bad incident, you owned it.

As a Director, you are accountable for the **capability** of multiple teams to produce results. Not the results themselves, moment to moment — the machinery that produces them.

The practical difference:
- If one team fails, a Senior Manager fixes that team.
- If one team fails, a Director asks: "What about the system allowed this to happen, and where else is that condition present in my other teams?"

A Senior Manager fights the fire. A Director asks whether the fireproofing is adequate across the entire building.

## Section 2: The Three Jobs of a Director

Every Director has three jobs, running simultaneously. When something is going wrong, it is usually because one of these three is being neglected.

### Job 1: Set Direction

Direction is not strategy (that comes from the VP and above). Direction is the translation layer between executive strategy and what your senior managers work on every quarter.

Setting direction means:
- Defining the **principles** the org operates by — not rules, but the reasoning behind rules. Rules change; principles survive context changes. "We prioritize availability over feature delivery" is a principle. "All changes require two approvals" is a rule derived from a principle.
- Translating executive strategy into operational priorities your senior managers can act on. If the company's OKR is "improve customer trust," your direction sets what that means for your org: MTTR targets, SLO commitments, incident communication standards.
- Making the hard calls on what **not** to do. This is the hardest part of direction-setting. Your org has more possible work than capacity. Deciding what to deprioritize — and holding that line against pressure — is a core Director function.
- Maintaining consistency across multiple senior managers who might otherwise drift. If two of your three senior managers are running their teams with different standards, the inconsistency will surface as conflict, unfairness, and confusion. Your direction prevents the drift.

Signs your direction-setting is weak: each senior manager is running their team according to their own interpretation of what matters, decisions that should be fast require your involvement to break ties, and the org's priorities shift based on who shouted last.

### Job 2: Remove Obstacles

During execution, your senior managers will hit walls. Cross-functional resistance. Budget constraints that are unclear. Process dysfunction in another part of the organization. Personnel issues that exceed their authority. Technology decisions that require sign-off they cannot get.

Your primary value-add during execution is not direction — it is clearing the path so your senior managers can move.

This requires three things:
1. **Knowing what's blocking them** — this comes from weekly 1:1s that are genuinely about blockers, not status updates
2. **Having the relationships to clear it** — your peer network (other Directors, VPs in adjacent functions) is a resource. Build it before you need it.
3. **Having the authority to escalate when needed** — your VP relationship determines how far you can clear. Know where your authority ends.

The Director who is not removing obstacles is either unaware of blockers (a 1:1 problem), lacks the relationships to clear them (a network problem), or lacks the authority to escalate (a VP relationship problem). Diagnose which one and fix the root cause.

### Job 3: Develop People

The org's long-term capability equals the people you develop.

Your senior managers must be developing the managers who report to them. Those managers must be developing the ICs. Your job is to develop the developers — senior managers who can grow teams, not just manage them.

Development at the Director level does not mean running training programs. It means:
- Giving your senior managers stretch assignments that require capabilities they don't yet have
- Providing honest feedback on the gaps between their current performance and Director-level performance (because some of them aspire to your role)
- Creating the conditions for them to fail safely on decisions that are recoverable, then debriefing what happened
- Protecting high-potential people from being consumed by operational noise

The Director who does not develop people eventually has a team that cannot function when someone leaves, cannot scale to take on more work, and cannot produce senior managers ready for the next level. This is a slow-moving organizational failure that is invisible until it is severe.

## Section 3: What You Stop Doing

This section requires honesty with yourself. The things listed here are things you were likely good at. That is exactly why they are hard to give up.

### Delegate and Never Take Back

These activities belong to your senior managers. If you are doing them, you are doing two jobs and doing neither well.

| Activity | Who Owns It Now |
|----------|----------------|
| Incident command for P2 and below | Your Senior Manager of Incident Management |
| CAB review and approval for standard changes | Your Change Manager or Senior Manager of Engineering |
| Individual performance management below direct reports | The relevant manager, coached by their Senior Manager |
| Day-to-day resource scheduling and prioritization | Your Senior Managers |
| Technical design reviews for routine work | Your Senior Managers and their engineering leads |
| On-call scheduling and rotation management | Team leads, not you |
| Ticket triage and backlog prioritization | Product/engineering leads under your Senior Managers |

Delegation is not giving someone a task. Delegation is giving someone the **authority** to make decisions in a domain and holding them accountable for the outcomes. If you delegate a task but require your approval at each decision point, you have not delegated — you have created an approval process with extra steps.

### The "Still Doing It" Trap

Signs you have not fully transitioned out of Senior Manager mode:
- You are on every P2 bridge call, not just to observe
- You are reviewing every RFC or major change before it goes to CAB
- Your engineers route questions to you instead of to their manager
- Your calendar is blocked by operational meetings that your senior manager attends and you do not need to
- You are the one who knows the most detail about any operational situation at any given time

Why this happens: control, comfort, expertise. These are real feelings. The org is familiar territory. Being the expert feels valuable. The discomfort of not knowing the detail is real. None of these feelings make it correct.

The fix is explicit delegation with clear authority — not "let them handle it," but "this decision is yours to make, here is the boundary, come to me when you hit it." Then hold yourself to the same standard you would hold a senior manager to: if you made the call, you own the result.

## Section 4: Decision Architecture at the Director Level

Not all decisions are equal. The Director's job is to make the **right** decisions, not all decisions.

### Decision Types

| Type | Example | Who Decides |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Irreversible + high-consequence | Hire/fire, major vendor contract change, architecture direction, Tier 1 SLA revision | Director with VP alignment before deciding |
| Reversible + high-consequence | Process redesign, team restructure, technology adoption trial | Director with senior manager input |
| Irreversible + low-consequence | Naming a team, establishing a recurring meeting cadence | Push to senior manager; you should not be involved |
| Reversible + low-consequence | Sprint priority, which incident to work first, tooling preference | Delegate entirely; do not want to know unless asked |

The decision test: "If this decision is wrong, how long does it take to undo and how much does it cost?" That answer determines who should make it.

### Decision Debt

Every decision the Director makes that their senior manager could have made creates a dependency. That dependency costs the Director time on the next similar decision, and it limits the senior manager's development because they never built the judgment required.

Track this deliberately. When you find yourself making a decision, ask: "Could a senior manager have made this call with information they already have?" If yes, you have accumulated decision debt. The solution is not to retroactively undo the decision — it is to name the pattern to your senior manager, transfer the authority explicitly, and not repeat it.

### The Decision Escalation Ladder

Build this with your senior managers explicitly. Define what they can decide unilaterally, what requires a heads-up to you, and what requires your approval. Make it concrete.

Example:

| Decision Category | SM Decides | SM Notifies | Director Approves |
|------------------|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| Incident severity classification | P3 and below | P2 | P1 |
| Emergency change approval | Standard urgency | High urgency | Critical / crisis |
| Vendor engagement | Existing SOW, within budget | Out-of-scope asks | New contract or scope extension >$25K |
| Headcount utilization | Task assignment | Contractor extension | New headcount requisition |
| Process changes | Team-level | Cross-team | Cross-org or SLA-impacting |

Without this ladder, your senior managers will either escalate everything (safe for them, expensive for you) or decide everything (efficient, but you lose visibility where it matters). Build the ladder once. Revisit it every six months.

## Section 5: The Director's Relationship with Uncertainty

At IC and Senior Manager level, uncertainty is managed by finding more information. Research, test, prototype, escalate. The solution to not knowing is more investigation.

At Director level, many decisions must be made with incomplete information, under time pressure, with significant consequences. Waiting for certainty is often not an option — and sometimes the refusal to decide is itself a decision with consequences.

### Frameworks for Deciding Under Uncertainty

**Reversibility first**: Prefer reversible options even if they are not optimal. A reversible decision preserves optionality. An irreversible one forecloses it. All else being equal, reversibility is worth a meaningful performance discount.

**Least regret**: "Which of these options will I regret most if I am wrong about the conditions?" This is not the same as expected value. Regret maps to accountability. The option that produces the least regret if wrong is often the more defensible choice.

**Asymmetric downside**: "If I am wrong about Option A, the cost is X. If I am wrong about Option B, the cost is 10X." Default to Option A unless there is an overwhelming reason not to. This is not risk avoidance — it is risk calibration.

**Signal vs. noise**: Directors are bombarded with information. Most of it is noise — things that feel urgent but do not actually require a decision right now. The skill is rapid triage: Is this a real signal requiring action from me, or is it noise that belongs to a senior manager? Build this habit deliberately. Every item that comes to your attention should trigger the question: "Is this mine, and does it require a decision now?"

### The "Good Enough to Act" Standard

The Director who needs certainty before deciding will be perpetually paralyzed. The standard at your level is: good enough to act, with a watch-and-adjust plan.

This means:
- Make the call with the information you have
- Define explicitly what you will watch for that would indicate the call was wrong
- Define what you will do if that signal appears
- Set a review date

"We will move forward with Vendor A for the 12-month contract. I will watch their CSAT score and SLA adherence monthly. If either drops below threshold two months in a row before month 6, we initiate a termination-for-cause review. Review at month 3 and month 6."

That is not certainty. That is disciplined action under uncertainty.

## Section 6: Accountability vs. Responsibility

This distinction is where many new Directors get confused, and where getting it wrong causes the most damage.

**Accountability**: You answer for the outcome regardless of who executed it. You cannot transfer accountability downward — only responsibility.

**Responsibility**: You execute the task. Responsibility can and should be transferred to your people.

If your environment manager misses a trueup and a QA environment is broken for a week, you own that outcome to your VP. Not "my environment manager failed." You, as the Director, own it. Your VP hired you to produce a functioning org. One of your org's leaders made an error. That is your outcome.

This is uncomfortable for new Directors, especially those who are used to owning what they personally built. Work through the discomfort. Accepting accountability without doing the work is the job definition.

### The Shield Pattern

You absorb accountability pressure from above so your senior managers can work without executive interference.

When something goes wrong and your VP is concerned, your job is to take that concern, respond to it directly, and let your senior managers focus on fixing the problem — not on managing upward. You translate "the VP is upset" into "here is what we are doing, here is the timeline, here is my commitment" and return the environment to normal so the team can execute.

This is not about hiding failures or protecting people from consequences. It is about filtering the noise so the org can function. Your senior managers should know that when something goes wrong, you will handle the executive layer. They focus on resolution. You handle communication and pressure. That division of labor is part of why having a Director matters.

### What Accountability Is Not

Accountability is not blame. If your team fails to meet an SLA, you answer for it — and then you fix the system that produced the failure, not just the individual who executed it. Blame-first cultures produce people who hide problems. Accountability-first cultures produce people who surface problems early so they can be fixed.

The public posture is: "My org owns this. Here is what we are doing." The internal posture is: "What about the system produced this outcome, and what do we change?"

## Section 7: Your First 90 Days

The first 90 days of a Director role is the highest-leverage period you will have in that role. Use it deliberately.

### Days 1–30: Listen

Your job in the first 30 days is to understand before you act. Do not restructure. Do not announce priorities. Do not make commitments you cannot keep.

Do:
- Meet every direct report (your senior managers) 1:1 in the first two weeks. Listen for: what they are proud of, what frustrates them, what they think is broken, and what they wish leadership understood.
- Meet every peer Director and key stakeholder. Ask: "What do you need from my org that you are not getting?" Listen without defending.
- Read every operational report, SLA scorecard, incident trend, and team health signal for the past 6 months. Form your own view before accepting anyone else's.
- Understand the budget: what was approved, what is committed, what is discretionary, and what is already at risk.

Do not:
- Announce that things will change
- Publicly agree or disagree with your predecessor's decisions
- Make staffing changes
- Commit to timelines without understanding the current state

### Days 31–60: Assess

Build your own view of the org's health. Not the inherited view — yours.

For each major function your org owns, assess:
- Is the team producing the outcomes it should? (Look at metrics, not activity)
- Is the senior manager running it in control of the operation? (Can they answer questions about risk, blockers, and next steps without looking at notes?)
- What is the biggest systemic risk in this domain?

By the end of day 60, you should have a clear view of: what is healthy, what is fragile, and what is broken. Share this view with your VP before publishing it internally.

### Days 61–90: Act

Now you have enough context to begin acting with intention.

Define:
- The 3 things your org will focus on in the next 6 months (not 15 things — 3)
- The 1 systemic risk you are addressing first
- What success looks like at 6 months and 12 months

Communicate this to your senior managers directly, then let them communicate it to their teams. You set direction at the senior manager level. They set direction at the team level.

## Self-Assessment

Answer these before moving to Module 02. Be honest.

1. In the past week, how many decisions did you make that your senior manager could have made without you?
2. Can your org function for 3 business days without your input? If not, what specifically breaks?
3. Name one thing you are still doing that you should have delegated. What is stopping you?
4. When your VP asks you about an operational detail, are you the first person in the chain to know it — or does it come to you through your senior managers?
5. In a recent failure or missed target, did you answer for it directly to leadership before explaining what went wrong operationally? Or did you lead with the explanation?

If questions 1, 3, and 4 are uncomfortable to answer, Module 01 is doing its job.

---

## What Good Looks Like

Your senior managers make decisions confidently without needing your approval for routine matters. The org produces consistent results whether you are in the building or not. When something goes wrong, your VP hears about it from you, not from another source. You spend your time on things only a Director can do: direction-setting, obstacle removal, and people development.

## What Failure Looks Like

Your calendar is full of operational details. Your senior managers route everything through you before acting. You feel busy but the org does not seem to be improving. When you are out of office for three days, things that should not be urgent become urgent.
