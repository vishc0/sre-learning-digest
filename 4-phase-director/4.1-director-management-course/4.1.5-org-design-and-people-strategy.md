# Module 05 — Org Design and People Strategy

**Building and Structuring the Team That Runs Your Org**

---

## Section 1: Org Design Principles at the Director Level

The org structure you design is the single biggest lever you have on what work gets done and how well. Every structure creates a set of conversations that happen naturally and a set that don't happen at all. Choose your structure to make the most important conversations the easiest ones.

### The Four Org Design Principles

**1. Clear domain ownership**

Every domain — incident management, change management, SLM, observability, platform engineering — has one team that owns it end-to-end. Shared ownership without a defined primary is an accountability vacuum. When something goes wrong in a shared space, both teams assume the other team is handling it. Neither is.

The test: for any domain in your org, you should be able to name the manager who owns it, what the ownership means in practice, and what happens when something goes wrong. If you can't, the domain is at risk.

**2. Span of control**

- Senior Manager: 4–7 direct reports max
- Manager: 5–8 direct reports max

Beyond the upper bound, development suffers. Managers become coordinators, not developers of people. They spend their time in status meetings instead of 1:1s that matter. Below the lower bound, the management layer isn't justified — the Director is carrying overhead without commensurate return.

Span of control also signals org health. A Senior Manager with 10 direct reports isn't managing them — they're triaging them. A Manager with 2 direct reports is underutilized and the team structure is probably wrong.

**3. Career path visibility**

The structure must show where a junior SRE can go in three to five years. A well-designed path looks like: Associate SRE → SRE → Senior SRE → Staff SRE (individual contributor track) or Senior SRE → Manager (people manager track).

If the path isn't visible from inside the org, your best people will look for orgs where it is. Career path visibility is a retention mechanism, not an administrative formality. Engineers who can see where they're going stay. Engineers who can't see anything beyond "more of the same" leave — usually to a smaller company where the path is forced into existence by scale.

**4. Bus factor**

No single person should be the only one capable of a critical function. This applies to technical knowledge (the one engineer who understands the legacy alerting pipeline), operational knowledge (the manager who is the only person with vendor relationship context), and process execution (the analyst who is the only one who runs the monthly SLA report).

Team design must anticipate absence — planned (vacation, conference) and unplanned (illness, attrition). The bus factor test: if this person is unavailable for 30 days starting tomorrow, what breaks? Document it. Fix the ones that would cause operational failures.

---

### The Four Structures a Director Chooses Between

**Functional**

Teams organized by operational domain: an Incident Management team, a Change Management team, a Platform Reliability team, an Observability team. Each team has a clear charter and owns their domain across all application stacks.

- Strengths: clean ownership, clear accountability, domain expertise concentrated in the right place
- Risks: silos — domain managers who optimize their area without coordinating with adjacent domains; cross-domain work falls through the cracks

Mitigation: structured cross-domain forums (weekly ops review, joint incident reviews, shared problem management backlog) make the lateral conversations happen by design rather than by accident.

**Product-aligned**

Teams organized by the application stack they support: Team A owns ecommerce services, Team B owns payment services, Team C owns identity services. Each team handles the full operations lifecycle for their product.

- Strengths: deep service knowledge, strong relationships with development counterparts, fast response to product-specific issues
- Risks: inconsistent practices across teams (Team A does incident management one way, Team B does it differently), tool sprawl, redundant capabilities in each team

Mitigation: a centralized standards function or chapter lead model that sets practices even when teams are product-aligned.

**Platform + product hybrid**

A central platform team (environment management, tooling, observability infrastructure) plus embedded SREs in product teams. The platform team sets the standards and builds the shared capabilities; embedded SREs apply them to specific product stacks.

- Strengths: good balance of consistency (platform team) and context (embedded SREs)
- Risks: the embedded SREs drift from platform standards when product pressure is high; the platform team becomes disconnected from actual product pain

Mitigation: explicit rotation (embedded SREs rotate through platform team periodically), platform team has a service owner model where they're accountable for tool adoption in product teams.

**Matrix**

SREs report to a central Director for line management (performance, career, compensation) but are embedded in product squads for day-to-day work. Two reporting lines — HR reporting up through the SRE Director, functional reporting up through the product squad lead.

- Strengths: SRE line management stays coherent, SREs have product context
- Risks: SRE identity fragmentation (am I an SRE or a product team member?), inconsistent on-call governance, conflicting priorities when product squad and SRE Director disagree

Matrix works when the organization has strong executive alignment on how matrix authority is divided. It fails when that alignment isn't explicit — the SRE ends up caught between two managers with competing priorities.

---

### Which Structure to Choose

For a 20–100 person operations org supporting a multi-service enterprise stack: **functional with strong cross-domain forums** is the right default. It gives you clean ownership, predictable accountability, and a management structure you can hold. The forum layer (weekly ops review, joint incident review, problem management working group) ensures the silos don't calcify.

Product alignment works at larger scale — typically 200+ engineers — where the coordination overhead of a central ops team exceeds the benefit, and where the product teams are large enough to sustain their own operational expertise.

Pure matrix should be used only when the parent organization mandates it. It creates coordination complexity faster than it creates value at this scale.

## Section 2: When to Restructure

Org structures drift out of alignment with the work. Restructuring is expensive — it consumes management attention, creates uncertainty in the team, and takes 90+ days before the new structure finds its operating pattern. Do it when the current structure is actively limiting the org, not when you want a change.

### Signs You Need to Restructure

- **Constant cross-domain friction that process fixes aren't resolving**: if the Incident Management team and the Change Management team are in recurring conflict and you've tried process interventions without improvement, the structure itself may be creating the conflict. Look at whether ownership boundaries are ambiguous.
- **Span of control exceeded**: a manager has grown to 10 direct reports. The team is larger than the structure accommodated. You need a new management layer.
- **Two domains have become inseparable**: SLM and observability were separate when you had 20 people. Now they're interdependent enough that running them as separate teams creates constant coordination overhead. Merge them.
- **A domain has shrunk below manager-level**: a function that once justified a manager now has two ICs. Consolidate under an adjacent manager.
- **A new capability needs a home**: chaos engineering, AI observability, FinOps — new disciplines that don't fit cleanly in existing team charters. Create a home rather than forcing them into an ill-fitting existing team.

### How to Restructure Without Breaking Things

- **Timing**: never restructure during high operational tempo. Before a major product release, during a post-incident period, or during peak season is not the time. Wait for a stable window.
- **Involve Senior Managers in the design**: not as voters, but as input sources. They will see failure modes you won't. A Senior Manager who knows their team composition will tell you that the proposed restructure puts two people with significant conflict on the same team.
- **Communicate the reason, not just the change**: "we're reorganizing the incident and change teams" tells people nothing useful. "The current structure creates a hand-off gap between incident detection and change authorization that's adding 20 minutes to our P2 resolution time on average — here's how the new structure fixes it" gives them a reason to believe in the change.
- **90-day evaluation**: structures take time to form their operating patterns. Judge the new structure at 90 days, not at 30. At 30 days, the team is still adjusting. At 90 days, you can see whether the structure is working.

### The Restructure Anti-Pattern

Reorganizing as a response to a performance problem. "This team isn't working well, so let's restructure them" is almost always the wrong move. Restructuring adds change disruption on top of an existing problem and rarely addresses the root cause, which is usually management quality, team dynamics, or unclear expectations — not team boundaries.

Diagnose first. If the problem is structural, restructure. If it's not, address the actual problem directly.

## Section 3: The Headcount Lifecycle

### Creating a Role

Business case (Module 03 covers this) → VP approval → HR job classification → job description written → sourcing begins.

The time from executive approval to offer accepted typically runs 45–90 days depending on market conditions, role seniority, and your hiring process efficiency. Senior Manager roles tend to take longer — 60–90 days — because the pool is smaller and the Director is directly involved in selection.

The planning implication: if you need someone in Q3, the hire process needs to start in Q1. If you wait until Q2 to get approval, you will be hiring into Q3 and onboarding into Q4. Model this in your HC planning every year.

### Hiring

Define the role and level before interviewing begins — not during, not after. A job description that says "senior or manager level depending on candidate" guarantees you'll have inconsistent interviews and will struggle to calibrate your decision.

Director participation in interviews:
- All Senior Manager hires: Director participates in the final interview
- Manager hires: Director participates when the role is high-stakes or the pool is competitive; delegates to Senior Manager otherwise
- IC hires two levels below: Director does not typically interview, but reviews the hiring bar periodically through skip-levels and new-hire feedback

### Onboarding

New team members take 45–90 days to contribute at full productivity in an operations role. The ramp is longer in operations than in pure engineering because the context is high — incident process, change workflow, tooling, vendor relationships, team norms.

A formal onboarding plan reduces the ramp by 30–40%. This means an assigned buddy (peer, not manager), a structured 30-60-90 day plan with explicit checkpoints, and deliberate introduction to the key cross-team relationships they'll depend on.

The first 30 days set the pattern. If a new team member spends their first 30 days trying to find documentation and figure out where to ask questions, they will be slower, more hesitant, and less likely to stay. Invest in the first 30 days.

### Performance Management

Annual review cycles are too infrequent to drive development. Quarterly or semi-annual mid-cycle check-ins are the minimum for any team member. Director review cadence:

- Senior Managers: Director reviews performance directly. At minimum, a formal mid-year and end-of-year conversation plus ongoing 1:1 feedback.
- Managers: Senior Managers own this; Director spot-checks through skip-levels.
- ICs two levels below: Director does not review directly, but conducts quarterly skip-levels to calibrate on team health, development quality, and talent.

Documentation rule: if a performance conversation happened — especially a course correction or high-stakes feedback conversation — it should be documented. Not punitively, but so both parties have a shared record of what was discussed, what was agreed, and what the timeline is. Memory is unreliable. Documentation protects both the manager and the employee.

### Attrition

Exit interviews: ask Senior Managers to share themes (not names) from exit conversations with you. If three people leave in a quarter and all three mention on-call load, that's a signal you need to act on — regardless of whether each individual departure seemed explainable on its own.

Attrition rate above 15% annually in an operations team is a serious signal. The causes in order of frequency: on-call load that isn't recovering, growth stall, and management quality issues. Address the cause, not the symptom.

Distinguish voluntary attrition: regrettable (you would have worked to keep this person) vs. non-regrettable (the departure is actually fine for the team). Track them separately. Losing your top performers at a 10% rate is categorically worse than losing underperformers at a 25% rate.

### Succession Planning

Every Senior Manager role should have an identified successor — someone who could step into the role within 30–90 days if needed. This is not about creating anxiety ("you're going to leave"). It is about organizational resilience.

The succession review question is simple: if [Senior Manager name] called me tomorrow and said they were leaving in 30 days, what would I do? If the answer is "panic and post a job req," the succession gap is real.

Review succession annually at minimum. Update it when the team changes, when individuals develop, or when new people are hired who change the picture.

## Section 4: People Strategy — Beyond Headcount

Headcount is table stakes. People strategy is how you build a team that improves over time rather than just sustaining current capability.

### Retention Strategy

The three reasons operations engineers leave, in order of frequency:

**1. On-call load**

Paged too often. Too little recovery time between incidents. The same problems recurring because there's no investment in fixing the root cause. Engineers who are on-call every other week and getting paged 4–5 times per rotation will leave within 12–18 months — to a company where on-call is a rotation of 15 people not 6, or to a role that doesn't have on-call at all.

Interventions: error budget policy with enforcement, problem management investment (fixing the root causes, not just closing the tickets), and hiring before you burn people out — not after.

**2. Growth stall**

No visible path forward. Skills not developing. Same work year after year with no new challenge. This is insidious because it doesn't generate complaints — people just quietly disengage and start looking.

Interventions: explicit career ladder (what does Staff SRE look like, what does a Manager track require), rotation opportunities (try environment engineering for a quarter, join a transformation project as an embedded SRE), and training investment that actually leads somewhere.

**3. Management quality**

The immediate manager, not the Director, is usually the driver here. Engineers don't leave companies — they leave managers. If a Senior Manager isn't developing their team, isn't giving real feedback, or is managing by microcontrol, the best ICs in that team will leave first.

This is why you do skip-levels and why you develop your managers. You cannot fix management quality problems you can't see. Skip-levels surface them. Deliberate manager development prevents them.

### Toil Tracking

Ask each team to estimate their toil percentage quarterly: the fraction of their work that is operational, repetitive, manual, and could be automated — but currently isn't.

- Target: less than 50% toil for any engineer
- Amber: 50–70% toil — this team is spending more time keeping the lights on than improving the system
- Red: above 70% toil — this is a burnout and attrition signal; engineering time is being consumed by operational work that should be automated or eliminated

High toil almost always traces to one of three causes:

1. **Problem management failure**: the problems that create the toil (recurring alerts, manual escalation paths, repeated data corrections) aren't being fixed because no one owns fixing them systematically
2. **Tooling failure**: automation exists somewhere but isn't being used, or the right tooling hasn't been built yet
3. **Staffing failure**: not enough people, so there's no time to invest in reducing toil — the team is always reactive

Track toil trends, not just snapshots. A team moving from 70% to 55% over two quarters is improving. A team stuck at 65% for a year isn't.

### Culture

Operations culture has a specific character challenge: the work rewards reactive heroes and penalizes systematic improvement.

The IC who saved the production system at 2am gets recognized, thanked in the post-incident review, and mentioned in the all-hands. The engineer who spent three months reducing page volume by 40% through better alerting thresholds and problem management rarely gets the same moment of recognition — the work is invisible precisely because it worked.

If you don't actively counter this, the culture optimizes for heroics. Engineers learn that the path to recognition is incident response, not incident prevention. The structure you want — a team that makes the system more reliable over time — cannot emerge from a culture that rewards the opposite.

The Director's role in this:
- When you talk about incidents, thank the IC who responded and then explicitly name the problem management work that's going to prevent the next one
- In QBR, create a recognition category for reliability improvement work alongside availability metrics
- When you make promotion decisions, examine what the engineer actually improved — not just what they responded to

## Section 5: Performance Conversations for the Senior Manager Layer

The four performance conversations you will have most often with your Senior Managers:

### 1. Recognition

"The SLA compliance improvement this quarter was real and I want to name it. What you did — [specific action: restructuring the on-call rotation, investing in the problem management backlog, pushing the vendor on their MTTR commitments] — directly drove that result. I want you to know I see it."

Specific, behavioral, timely. Recognition that isn't specific isn't useful — it reads as a reflex, not as genuine observation. If you can't name what they did, the recognition lands as noise.

### 2. Course Correction

"I'm seeing a pattern I want to address before it becomes a bigger issue. [Specific pattern: the last three cross-team deliverables you committed to came in late, the incident review cadence has slipped from weekly to monthly over the past quarter]. Walk me through what's happening from your perspective."

Not accusatory. Diagnostic first. You name the specific observable pattern, then create space for the Senior Manager to explain the context. Sometimes the pattern has a good explanation. Sometimes the explanation reveals something else going on. Either way, you're starting from evidence, not inference.

### 3. Development

"You're strong in [area — incident command, team development, vendor management]. The next thing I want you to develop is [area — financial management, executive communication, technical architecture credibility]. Here's why that matters: [career reason — this is the gap between your current profile and a Director-level role; operational reason — your team is heading into a budget cycle and you need to own more of that]. What would be useful support?"

Future-focused, specific about the gap, and explicitly connected to why it matters. Development conversations that don't explain the why ("you should work on your executive presence") land as criticism. Development conversations that connect to the Senior Manager's own ambitions and the org's needs land as investment.

### 4. High-Stakes Feedback

"I need to be direct. [Specific behavior or outcome — the last two vendor renewals went to auto-renewal without a performance review, the on-call attrition problem has not improved in two quarters despite three conversations about it]. This is not at the level this role requires. Here's the specific change I need: [precisely what needs to be different, by when]. What support do you need from me to make that happen?"

Clear, respectful, and documented immediately after the conversation. The documentation matters not because you're building a case, but because alignment on what was discussed and agreed is what makes the feedback actionable. Memory diverges. Documentation doesn't.

---

### The Core Rule

Every Senior Manager should know roughly where they stand with you at all times. If a performance review contains a surprise — a rating or feedback they weren't expecting — that surprise is a Director failure. It means the real-time feedback wasn't happening throughout the year.

The performance review should be a summary of what has already been said, not a revelation of what you've been thinking.
