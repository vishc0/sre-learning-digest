# Module 10 — The Director's Complete Operating System

**Integrating Everything: The Director's Complete Operating Model**

This final module ties together the previous 9. A Director doesn't apply one framework at a time — they run all of them simultaneously. This module is the integration layer: what the Director does across a day, week, month, quarter, and year, and how all the domains connect.

---

## Section 1: The Director's Operating Cadence — The Complete Calendar

### Daily (30–45 minutes)

**Morning (first 30 min of the day):**

1. **Morning metrics check (10 min):**
   - SLO burn rate for Tier 1 services
   - Open P1 incidents (anything new overnight?)
   - Overnight change outcomes (anything failed, rolled back?)
   - On-call page volume last 24 hours (anyone > 5 pages?)

2. **Communications triage (10 min):**
   - Anything that requires Director response vs. can route to a Senior Manager
   - Any vendor/partner escalation that landed overnight

3. **Day's focus (5 min):**
   - What is the ONE most important thing I accomplish today?
   - What decisions are waiting for me?

**End of day (10 min):**
- Did the most important thing get done? If not, why?
- What needs to go in tomorrow's plan?
- Any VP communication needed today?

---

### Weekly (the Director's week)

**Monday:**
- Weekly ops review (50 min) — all Senior Managers present: incidents, problems, change calendar, environment status, risks, decisions needed
- After ops review: write weekly VP status (15 min)
- Review open risk register: anything moved to red over the weekend?

**Tuesday–Wednesday:**
- 1:1s with each Senior Manager (30 min each)
- Cross-functional sync with Product, Engineering, Security peers (30 min)

**Thursday:**
- Skip-level as needed (quarterly rotation)
- Vendor/partner calls if scheduled
- Decision processing: make all decisions that have been waiting

**Friday:**
- Week-end metrics snapshot
- Plan for next week
- Any attrition risk conversations following the week's 1:1s?

## Section 2: The Decisions That Define a Director

Some decisions define your tenure. You won't make many of them, but how you make them determines your reputation.

### The Staffing Decision

When you hire or promote someone to Senior Manager, you are betting on them. You are also betting on the org they will build. These decisions take 12–24 months to play out. Hire slowly — these decisions are hard to reverse and expensive to get wrong.

Test: "Would I be comfortable if this person's leadership decisions represented mine?" If not, don't hire or promote.

### The Vendor Exit Decision

Exiting a major vendor is expensive, disruptive, and often necessary. The Director who avoids it because it's hard is paying a higher cost over time.

Test: "Is this vendor making our org better, or are we accommodating our practices to their tool's limitations?"

### The Restructure Decision

Changing the org structure is one of the highest-leverage and highest-disruption decisions available to you. Used well, it unlocks velocity. Used as a response to a performance problem, it creates chaos while the performance problem remains.

Rule: restructure the org to serve the work, not to signal change.

### The "This Isn't Working" Conversation

When a Senior Manager is not performing at level and coaching hasn't moved the needle, the decision to move them out or to move them to a different role is yours. Most Directors wait too long — the team sees the underperformance and loses confidence in the Director for tolerating it.

The direct conversation, done with respect and specificity, is an act of leadership. Avoidance is an act of cowardice dressed as kindness.

## Section 3: How the Domains Connect — The Director's Integration View

Each operational domain is not independent — they form a system. The Director's unique visibility is seeing how they interact.

```
Change Management ──→ Environment Management ──→ Incident Management
       ↓                        ↓                        ↓
  DORA Metrics         Config Parity Health          MTTR / Repeat Rate
       ↓                        ↓                        ↓
Problem Management ◄──────────────────────────────────────────────────
       ↓
  SLM / SLO Health ──→ Executive Reporting ──→ Architecture Decisions
```

### The Integration Insight

Problems that appear in one domain often originate in another.

| Symptom | Apparent Domain | Actual Source Domain |
|---------|----------------|----------------------|
| High incident rate on same services | Incident Management | Problem Management — recurring issues not being closed |
| "Works in QA, fails in Prod" | Incident Management | Environment Management — config parity failure |
| High emergency change rate | Change Management | Change Management — normal process too slow; people work around it |
| SLA breaches discovered after the fact | Incident Management | SLM — no real-time SLO burn monitoring |
| Architecture decisions that create operational debt | Any | Architecture Governance — no operational review in ADR process |

Director's diagnostic model: when a metric is red, trace it to its source domain before reacting. A high P1 incident count might look like an incident management problem — but if the same services are involved every time, it's a problem management problem. Fix the right thing.

## Section 4: The Director's Relationship with Time

Time is your scarcest resource. How you allocate it is a management decision.

### Director Time Allocation Model

| Activity | Target % | What It Delivers |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| 1:1s with Senior Managers | 20% | The most important management activity you do |
| Weekly ops review + monthly reporting | 15% | Visibility and accountability |
| Cross-functional relationships | 15% | Air cover, information, alignment |
| Vendor/partner management | 10% | Financial value, risk management |
| Escalation resolution | 10% | Clearing blockers for the org |
| Strategic thinking / planning | 15% | Direction, roadmap, next quarter |
| Skip-levels and team visibility | 10% | Culture, retention, early warning |
| Personal development | 5% | Staying current, growing |

### The Operating Level Check

If operational detail (incident command, change review, technical problem solving) is consuming > 20% of your time, you are operating below your level.

Action: identify what you're doing that a Senior Manager should own and explicitly transfer it. State the transfer out loud — "I'm handing this back to you. This is your decision to make." Implicit handoffs don't stick.

## Section 5: The Director as a Point of Stability

Organizations go through turbulence: executive changes, reorgs, major incidents, vendor failures, economic pressure. Your team watches how you handle it and calibrates their own response accordingly.

### What Your Team Needs from You During Turbulence

- **Clarity**: "Here's what we know, here's what we don't know yet, here's what it means for us."
- **Calm**: if you're panicking, they will too. Your emotional thermostat sets the room temperature.
- **Direction**: "Here's what we're focusing on despite the uncertainty." Keep the team pointed at the work.
- **Honesty**: don't promise what you don't know. "I don't have that answer yet. I'll have more by [date]." This is more stabilizing than false confidence.

### What Destabilizes an Org

- Conflicting signals from the Director in different conversations
- The Director avoiding difficult news until it becomes unavoidable
- The Director visibly stressed without acknowledging it or providing context
- The Director changing direction without explaining the change

The pattern: your team will tolerate almost any difficult reality if you are honest and consistent about it. What breaks trust is inconsistency and concealment — not the difficulty itself.

## Section 6: Measuring Yourself as a Director

Most Directors are measured by their org's metrics. But there's a deeper assessment: is the organization getting better over time?

### Indicators That You Are Building Something Durable

- MTTR is declining across all severity levels
- On-call load is manageable (< 3 pages/night average per engineer)
- Senior Managers are making decisions confidently without you
- Your skip-levels reveal a team that understands the direction and believes in it
- Attrition is < 10% annually and you're not losing your best people
- The org performed well during a period when you were unavailable (vacation, illness, conference)

### The Most Honest Assessment

"If I left this role today, how long would it take for my departure to materially affect the org's performance?"

A Director who has done their job well has built a system that functions without them. A Director who hasn't has built a dependency.

This is not modesty — it is the actual goal. The org that depends on a single Director is fragile. The org that has internalized the Director's operating model, principles, and standards into its systems and its Senior Managers is resilient. Build toward resilience from day one.
