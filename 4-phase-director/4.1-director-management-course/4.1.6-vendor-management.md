# Module 06 — Vendor Management

**Governing External Partners: Contracts, SLAs, Reviews, and Escalation**

---

An operations team at a mid-size enterprise typically manages 10–30 vendor relationships: monitoring tooling, ITSM platforms, cloud providers, managed services, professional services firms. Poor vendor management costs money (auto-renewals at full price), creates operational risk (SLA breaches discovered too late), and wastes engineering time (tools nobody uses, integrations nobody maintains). This module gives you the framework to govern all of it.

---

## Section 1: Types of Vendors an Operations Director Manages

| Vendor Type | Examples | Primary Risk | Management Lever |
|-------------|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Observability / Monitoring | Splunk, Datadog, Dynatrace | License cost, data volume overage | Usage optimization, competitive negotiation |
| ITSM / Ticketing | ServiceNow, Jira Service Mgmt | Feature lock-in, customization debt | Upgrade roadmap alignment |
| Cloud providers | AWS, Azure, GCP | Cost creep, commitment discount expiry | FinOps practice, commitment reviews |
| Managed services | NOC, SOC, DR providers | SLA compliance, response quality | Monthly SLA review, escalation path |
| On-call / Alerting | PagerDuty, OpsGenie | License cost, integration sprawl | Consolidation, seat audit |
| Professional services | SI partners, consultants | Scope creep, knowledge transfer | SOW specificity, handoff requirements |
| Hardware / Infrastructure | Storage, networking vendors | Support tier, EOL risk | Tier SLA, lifecycle tracking |

Each vendor type has a different risk profile and a different management posture. Managed services get a monthly SLA review because they're providing an ongoing operational capability — a miss this month matters. Professional services get SOW scrutiny upfront because the cost and scope risk is front-loaded. Cloud providers get FinOps treatment because cost creep is gradual and invisible until it isn't.

Know which type each of your vendors is. Manage them accordingly.

## Section 2: The Vendor Contract Lifecycle

Every vendor contract has five phases. Managing each phase deliberately prevents the most common vendor failures.

### Phase 1 — Evaluation and Selection (Before Contract)

**Define requirements before looking at vendors.** Requirements written by vendors are not requirements — they're marketing. Start with what you need the tool or service to do, in your environment, for your team, at your scale. Write that down before anyone talks to a sales rep.

**Include your operations team in evaluation.** They will use it. They will tell you things the vendor demo won't show you: whether the on-call integration is actually reliable, whether the alert fatigue reduction claims hold in a high-volume environment, whether the documentation is good enough for a new hire to learn from. Their input is not optional.

**Reference checks.** Ask for references, then go beyond them. The references a vendor provides are their best customers. Ask those references who else they know using the product at your scale. Talk to a customer the vendor didn't introduce you to. The unfiltered view is worth the extra hour.

**Total cost of ownership.** License cost is one number. The real number includes: implementation cost, integration cost (engineering time to build and maintain the integration), training cost, and migration cost if you ever switch. A tool that's 20% cheaper on license and 3x harder to integrate is not cheaper.

**Exit cost.** Before you sign, ask: what does it take to leave this vendor? Can you export all your data in a standard format? How long does migration typically take? Can you terminate early and at what penalty? High exit cost equals high vendor leverage over you at renewal. If you can't leave without significant pain, you will pay more to stay.

### Phase 2 — Contracting

The Director should review — or have legal review explicitly — the following clauses before signing:

**SLA commitments**: what is the vendor's uptime SLA? What is the credit mechanism if they breach it? Most enterprise vendor SLAs offer 10% of the monthly contract value as a service credit. On a $200K annual contract, that's $1,700 per month of credit for a service outage that may have cost you $50,000 in engineering time and customer impact. Understand the gap between the credit and your actual exposure.

**Data portability**: can you export all your data in a standard, non-proprietary format? At any time, including before renewal? Some vendors make data export difficult by design — it increases your switching cost. Know what you're agreeing to before you generate three years of operational data that you can't easily move.

**Pricing structure**: is this fixed fee, consumption-based, or per-seat? Consumption-based is particularly dangerous in operations tooling — your data volumes and seat counts grow with your org. Model the pricing against your 12- and 24-month growth projections before signing. "We'll figure out the cost as we grow" is a budget planning failure.

**Auto-renewal clause**: almost every enterprise contract has one. The standard notice period is 30–90 days before expiry. Put the renewal review date on your calendar the day you sign. This is the single most preventable vendor management failure — discovering a $200K auto-renewal three days after the window to cancel closed.

**Audit rights**: can you audit the vendor's security practices? For any vendor touching your operational data, SOC 2 Type II compliance is the minimum bar. The right to audit means you can verify it, not just take their word for it.

**Liability cap**: if the vendor causes you a material loss — an SLA breach that triggers a customer SLA penalty, a data breach affecting your environment — what is their maximum liability? The standard is 1–3x the annual contract value. On a $100K contract, the liability cap is $100K–$300K. If the realistic exposure from a vendor failure in your environment is larger than that, you need to know before you sign, because you're self-insuring the gap.

### Phase 3 — Onboarding and Integration

**Assign an internal owner.** One person in your org is the vendor owner — they manage the relationship day-to-day, track SLA compliance, escalate issues, and own the renewal decision timeline. The vendor owner is not the Director. It is typically a Senior Manager or a designated lead in the affected team.

**Define success criteria before going live.** "By 90 days, we expect [X]: alert noise reduced by 30%, all P1 incidents routed through the new ITSM workflow, on-call rotation migrated from the old tool." Put this in writing. The vendor agrees or you negotiate. Without documented success criteria, 90-day performance reviews are subjective.

**Integration documentation.** Every integration between the vendor's tool and your stack must be documented: what connects to what, through which API or connector, with what authentication, and what breaks if the integration goes down. When you renegotiate, migrate, or upgrade, you need this. When the engineer who built the integration leaves, you need this. Write it at onboarding, not retroactively.

### Phase 4 — In-Contract Management

Your ongoing vendor management cadence:

| Cadence | Activity |
|---------|----------|
| Monthly | SLA compliance review — is vendor meeting their contractual commitments? |
| Monthly | License utilization — are you using what you're paying for? |
| Quarterly | Business review — vendor presents roadmap, you present usage trends and satisfaction |
| Quarterly | Cost vs. value assessment — is this tool earning its cost? |
| 90 days before renewal | Renegotiation window opens |
| 60 days before renewal | Decision made: renew at current terms, renegotiate, or replace |

The monthly SLA review is the most important and the most skipped. Running it monthly means you catch a breach in month one, not after six months of accumulated data that's now harder to dispute.

The quarterly business review is your opportunity to signal your position before renewal. A vendor who sees that your utilization is declining, your satisfaction is neutral, and your team has been evaluating alternatives will negotiate harder at renewal than a vendor who thinks you're locked in.

### Phase 5 — Renewal or Exit

**Never let a contract auto-renew without a performance review.** An auto-renewal at current terms is an implicit statement that you're satisfied with the vendor's performance and that the market hasn't changed. Both assumptions should be verified, not assumed.

**The 90-day window is your leverage.** Vendors will negotiate to retain your business during this window. After you renew, the leverage shifts — you've committed, and the next negotiation opportunity is 12 months away. Use the window.

**Competitive quotes.** Even if you intend to stay, get a competitive quote from at least one alternative. This is not bad faith — it's information. It tells you whether the market has moved and gives you pricing leverage. Vendors know their customers do this. The ones who won't negotiate against a competitive quote are telling you something about how they view the relationship.

**Exit planning.** If you're switching vendors, build the migration plan before you terminate the current contract. Migrating under pressure — because you terminated first and the deadline is now real — always costs more in engineering time and always introduces more operational risk. Overlap the contracts by 30–60 days if the migration is non-trivial.

## Section 3: Vendor SLA Management

Most enterprise vendor SLAs are written to favor the vendor. The uptime percentage sounds impressive until you read the definitions: what counts as "downtime," what's excluded (maintenance windows, customer-caused outages, third-party dependencies), and what the credit mechanism is. Your job is to understand what you actually contracted for and enforce it consistently.

### Monthly SLA Review Process

1. **Pull the data.** Get actual SLA data from the vendor's portal. If you can measure the same metric independently through your own monitoring — and often you can — do so. Vendor-reported SLA data has a conflict of interest. Your monitoring of the vendor's availability is cleaner.

2. **Compare to commitment.** Does the measured availability meet the contractual target? Document this regardless of whether there's a breach — a 12-month trend tells a story that a single month doesn't.

3. **If SLA breached**: issue a formal credit request within the window specified in the contract. Missing the window forfeits the credit. This is a process step that must be calendared — don't rely on someone remembering to do it.

4. **Document everything.** Breach date, measured availability, contractual commitment, credit request issued, vendor response. Keep this in the vendor record.

### The SLA Escalation Chain

| Breach Pattern | Action |
|---------------|--------|
| Single breach | Issue credit request, log it |
| Second breach (same metric) | Written notice to vendor account manager, request root cause analysis, demand improvement plan with timeline |
| Third consecutive breach | Escalate to vendor VP, put contract termination clause review on the table |
| Persistent pattern | Competitive evaluation, exit planning |

This escalation chain is a signal to the vendor that you're managing the relationship seriously. Most vendors respond at the "second breach" level because that's when they realize you're tracking. The vendors that don't respond are telling you that they don't expect consequences — which is a reason to ensure there are consequences.

### The SLA That Doesn't Cover What You Need

Many vendor SLAs commit to "99.9% uptime" for the platform but exclude the specific APIs, integrations, or features your team depends on. A monitoring platform with 99.9% uptime for the web console is not useful if the alerting API — the one that pages your on-call engineers — isn't covered by the same commitment.

Before you sign and before you measure, confirm: which services, APIs, and features does the SLA actually cover? Ask for it in writing. If the answer is ambiguous, negotiate an addendum that specifies the components your operations depend on.

## Section 4: Vendor Escalation

When your account manager can't or won't resolve an issue, you escalate. Most vendor issues can be resolved at account manager level — that's what the relationship is for. But when it can't, the escalation path matters.

### The Escalation Path

```
Your account manager
       ↓ (if unresolved)
Their Regional Account Director or Manager
       ↓ (if unresolved)
Their VP of Enterprise Sales or Customer Success
       ↓ (if unresolved)
Executive escalation — your VP or Director to their VP or CTO
```

Most issues resolve at the first or second level. The VP-level conversation is a significant relationship signal — it tells the vendor that you've exhausted the normal channel and you're prepared to make this a consequential issue. Use it when warranted; don't use it for routine friction.

### When to Escalate

- The same issue recurs without resolution across two or more conversations with the account manager
- A P1-level service disruption isn't getting adequate response time or communication
- A contractual obligation — SLA credit, data export, committed feature delivery — is being ignored
- The account manager says they'll escalate internally but nothing changes

### How to Have the Escalation Conversation

Direct and business-focused: "Your team has not resolved [specific issue] in [timeframe]. This is causing [specific business impact — our on-call engineers are missing pages, our P1 MTTR has increased by 15 minutes, our SLA compliance to our own customers is at risk]. I need a VP-level owner assigned by end of day today and a resolution plan with a committed date by [date]. If this isn't resolved by [date], we will be reviewing the contract, including termination rights."

The specificity matters. A vague complaint is easy to absorb. A complaint with a specific business impact, a specific timeline, and a specific consequence is harder to absorb.

### What Not to Do

Never escalate a vendor issue to your VP without giving the vendor one opportunity to resolve it at account manager level. Escalating past the account manager on the first contact damages the relationship and removes your escalation leverage — you've used it before the vendor has had a chance to respond.

Never escalate without documentation. If the conversation goes to VP level, both sides need a clear record of what the issue is, what's been tried, and what the commitment is going forward.

## Section 5: Vendor Consolidation Strategy

Over time, organizations accumulate tools. A new problem emerges, someone evaluates three options, one gets purchased. Six months later, a different team has the same problem and buys a different tool. After five years, you have overlapping tools, redundant capabilities, and licenses nobody is fully using.

The operations Director has both the authority and the incentive to audit and consolidate. The incentive is straightforward: consolidation reduces cost and reduces the operational overhead of managing tool sprawl.

### Identifying Consolidation Targets

1. **List all tools by function.** Monitoring, alerting, ITSM, observability, documentation, on-call management, change management. Group them.

2. **Identify overlapping capabilities.** Two tools that both do alerting. Three tools that all have dashboard capability. One team's ITSM and another team's ITSM that were supposed to be consolidated two years ago.

3. **Measure actual usage.** Ask teams: "What tools do you use day-to-day?" vs. "What tools are you licensed for?" The gap between those two answers is your consolidation opportunity. A tool with 30% seat utilization is a strong consolidation candidate.

4. **Licensing utilization below 50%**: candidate for reduction or elimination. The question is whether the low utilization reflects low value or low adoption. If low value — the tool isn't doing what it promised — eliminate it. If low adoption — the team hasn't been trained or the integration isn't complete — fix that before paying full price at renewal.

### Consolidation Business Case

| Element | What to Include |
|---------|----------------|
| Savings | Annual license cost of eliminated tools + reduced integration maintenance |
| Cost | Migration effort (engineering hours × loaded cost rate) + transition risk |
| Payback period | Months until savings exceed one-time migration cost |
| Contract timing | Which contracts renew when — migrate before renewal, not after |

The contract timing is the most practical constraint. If the tool you're eliminating has eight months left on its contract, you're paying for it either way. Plan the migration to coincide with the renewal window, not arbitrarily.

### The Consolidation Anti-Pattern

Consolidating onto a "strategic" tool because it's a VP's preference, because it's the enterprise standard on paper, or because a vendor relationship exists at the CTO level — rather than because it's the operationally better choice.

The decision should be based on: capability fit for your use case, integration quality with your existing stack, and total cost of ownership over three years. If those factors point to the "strategic" tool, consolidate. If they don't, make the case for the better tool. Vendor relationships and executive preferences are real factors in enterprise decisions — but they should be inputs to the decision, not replacements for analysis.

## Section 6: The Vendor Register

Every Director should maintain a vendor register. This is the master list of all vendors, contracts, and relationships your org owns. It is the single document that prevents the most common vendor management failure: discovering a $200K auto-renewal after the cancellation window has closed.

### Vendor Register Fields

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Vendor name | — |
| Tool or service | What they provide |
| Internal owner | Your team member who owns the day-to-day relationship |
| Annual contract value | — |
| Contract start date | — |
| Contract end date | Including auto-renewal date if different |
| Notice period | Days required to cancel or not renew |
| Renewal decision date | Contract end minus notice period — this is the action date |
| SLA commitment | Key metric and target (e.g., 99.9% uptime, 4-hour response time) |
| Current SLA status | Green / Amber / Red based on last review |
| Last review date | When the last formal SLA or business review occurred |
| Competitive alternatives | One or two named alternatives — useful when negotiating |
| Notes | Non-standard terms, known issues, pending negotiations |

### How to Use the Register

Review it monthly. The renewal decision date column drives your calendar. When a date falls within 90 days, the renegotiation window opens and the vendor owner is responsible for initiating the review.

The register is not a bureaucratic artifact — it is operational intelligence. A Director who can look at this document and immediately see which contracts are coming up, which vendors are in SLA breach, and which tools have internal owners is a Director who is not going to be surprised by a renewal, a breach, or an ownership gap.

---

## What Good Looks Like

Every vendor has a named internal owner. SLA compliance is reviewed monthly against the contractual commitment. No contract auto-renews without a deliberate decision — either a conscious choice to renew or an active negotiation. You have pricing leverage at renewal because you've built a track record of active management and you have competitive quotes.

Your team is using the tools they're paying for at a utilization rate above 70%. The consolidation candidates have been identified and are on a roadmap tied to contract renewal dates. Escalation has been used twice in the past year, and both times it resolved the issue.

## What Failure Looks Like

You discover a $150K auto-renewal three days after the cancellation window closed. Your team is using 30% of a monitoring tool's capacity but paying for 100% because nobody audited the license count after a team reduction. A vendor has been in breach of SLA for four months and no credit request has been filed because nobody was tracking it. Three monitoring tools are doing overlapping work because they were each bought to solve the same problem at different moments in time by different managers.

Both states are reachable from where you are today. The register, the cadence, and the escalation path are what determine which one you end up in.
