# Chapter 01 — Why Layer Taxonomy Matters

**Position in framework**: Foundation chapter. Read this before any other chapter. Every governance decision in ch08 and every routing decision in the incident playbooks rests on the model established here.

---

## The VP's Problem: An Incident Occurs — Who Do You Call?

It is 2AM. SEV1 declared. The bridge has 20 people — platform SRE, middleware SRE, DBRE, app team leads, a network engineer, and two product managers who joined because their dashboards are red. The customer-facing checkout flow is down.

Nobody on the bridge knows the answer to this question: **is this a database, a message broker, an API gateway, or an application service failure?**

The next 20 minutes are not debugging. They are organizational improvisation. Someone runs a query. Someone checks Splunk. Someone asks "who owns the Order Service?" Someone else says "it depends on what part of Order Service." Meanwhile the customer-impact clock runs.

Layer taxonomy eliminates this scene. The model is simple:

1. Every CI in the CMDB has a layer classification.
2. Layer classification determines incident lead team — immediately, without discussion.
3. The incident commander checks the layer classification of the first failing CI, calls that team's on-call lead, and the bridge has a commander within 5 minutes.

At T-Mobile scale — hundreds of microservices, Kubernetes service mesh, 50+ Kafka topics, 8 RabbitMQ vhosts, MuleSoft integration flows, Kong and Apigee at the edge, multiple RDBMS clusters — **incident routing without taxonomy is tribal knowledge**. Tribal knowledge does not scale past 30 services. At hundreds of services, tribal knowledge means the person who built the thing three years ago is the only one who knows who to call. That person is on vacation.

## Layer Taxonomy as Organizational Architecture

Conway's Law states that systems reflect the communication structures of the organizations that build them. The inverse is also operationally true: **how you classify layers shapes how you structure teams**.

If you classify RabbitMQ, Kafka, MuleSoft, and REST adapters as a single "Middleware" category, you will build a single Middleware team. That team will be paged for broker failures (platform-severity), integration adapter failures (application-severity), and consumer lag alerts (operational tuning) — all at the same time, at the same on-call weight. The team's on-call burn rate will be unsustainable within 18 months.

If you classify by function — broker infrastructure (platform) versus integration adapters (domain) — you get two ownership models with appropriate SLA tiers.

**The VP decision**: "Does my team structure match my layer structure?" If your teams are organized by technology (the Middleware Team, the Database Team) rather than by function and blast radius, your incident routing and your on-call load distribution will be misaligned. One or the other needs to change. The layer taxonomy tells you which is wrong.

| Layer | Team Model | On-Call Rotation | Budget Line |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Persistent Storage | Dedicated DBRE team | DBRE primary, Platform SRE secondary | Platform engineering |
| Storage Abstraction | Platform SRE | Platform SRE primary | Platform engineering |
| Data Services | Domain team, embedded SRE | Domain on-call, Platform SRE escalation | Domain product budget |
| Middleware / Integration | Dedicated Middleware SRE team | Middleware SRE primary, Platform SRE secondary | Platform engineering |
| Application Services | Product team + embedded SRE | Domain on-call primary | Product / domain budget |
| API Gateway / Edge | Platform SRE, Network SRE | Platform SRE primary | Platform engineering |
| Presentation / UI | Product team | Product on-call primary | Product budget |

## The Two Classification Axes

Every component must be evaluated on two axes before a layer is assigned.

**Axis 1 — Functional**: What does this component DO?
- Store data durably → Persistent Storage or Storage Abstraction
- Route or transform messages between producers and consumers → Middleware / Integration
- Execute business logic → Application Services
- Expose APIs to external consumers or aggregate requests → API Gateway / Edge
- Render interfaces for humans → Presentation / UI

**Axis 2 — Operational**: Who RUNS it? What SLA does it carry? How does it fail — and how widely?
- Managed by a platform team with infrastructure-grade SLAs → Platform classification
- Managed by a product/domain team with service-grade SLAs → Product classification

**When a component spans axes**: classify by primary function. Ambiguity is the enemy. A component with two valid classifications will receive split ownership, split monitoring, and split alerting — which means no single team has the full picture during an incident.

**Common dual-use example — Kafka**: At T-Mobile, Kafka is used simultaneously as (a) an event streaming bus for domain event propagation (Middleware / Integration) and (b) a durable audit log for compliance records (Persistent Storage). These are different consumers with different SLOs. The resolution: classify the Kafka cluster by its **primary** operational function as defined in the architecture decision record (ADR). If compliance audit is the primary contract, classify as Persistent Storage; apply DBRE-equivalent retention and backup governance. If event streaming is primary, classify as Middleware / Integration; apply consumer lag and DLQ monitoring. Do not allow both classifications to coexist on a single CI — that produces two teams, two SLOs, and two on-call rotations for the same infrastructure.

**Common dual-use example — Redis**: Redis used as an application cache (Storage Abstraction) versus Redis Streams used as a message queue (Middleware / Integration). Primary function wins. Most Redis deployments at application scale are caches; classify accordingly and apply Storage Abstraction governance. If Redis Streams is the primary delivery mechanism for inter-service messages, reclassify.

## Monolith vs. Microservice Layer Differences

In a monolithic application, multiple architectural layers collapse into a single deployable artifact.

A typical Spring Boot WAR file contains:
- The ORM layer (Hibernate) — Storage Abstraction
- Business logic classes — Application Services
- REST controllers — API Gateway equivalent (internal edge)
- Frontend Thymeleaf templates — Presentation / UI

All four layers are in one JVM. Layer ownership in a monolith is therefore a **code-level concern**, not an infrastructure concern. There is no separate team for "the ORM layer" — the application team owns all layers simultaneously. This is manageable at small scale. It becomes ungovernable when the monolith reaches 500k+ lines of code and 20+ developers.

In a microservices architecture, layers are made explicit at the infrastructure level. Each service is one layer component. It owns its own SLO. It has its own deployment pipeline. It has a named team.

**The strangler fig migration risk**: when extracting a monolith into microservices, layer boundaries must be declared explicitly in the first sprint of extraction — not discovered organically. The failure mode is that teams extract services by technical boundary (database access layer becomes one service, business logic becomes another) rather than by functional layer boundary. You end up with a distributed monolith: the coupling is preserved but now it crosses network calls. Every inter-service call is now a latency source and a failure boundary that did not exist in the monolith.

The extraction rule: before writing a single line of the extracted service, answer these two questions:
1. What layer does this service occupy?
2. Who owns it, and what SLO does it carry?

If you cannot answer both in two minutes of discussion, the extraction boundary is wrong.

## The "It's Complicated" Failure

**Symptom**: CMDB query returns CIs classified as "Application" with no further detail. The incident management system routes all alerts from these CIs to an "App Team" distribution list that contains 40 engineers across 6 product domains.

**Failure sequence**:

1. SEV1 declared. Incident commander pages "App Team on-call."
2. The on-call engineer for the Order domain joins. The issue is with the Customer Profile service, which is owned by the Retail domain.
3. 15 minutes lost while the Order engineer determines they are in the wrong incident.
4. Retail domain on-call joins. They investigate the Customer Profile service and determine that it is returning 500s due to connection pool exhaustion — but the pool is connecting to a PostgreSQL cluster owned by DBRE.
5. DBRE joins. They determine the database itself is healthy; the connection pool configuration in the application tier is wrong.
6. Total routing loss: 65–90 minutes. The database was never the problem. The application was never the problem. The problem was a configuration value in the connection pool layer (Storage Abstraction, owned by Platform SRE).

**The fix**: Every CI carries a layer classification AND a named owning team, not a distribution list. The CMDB record for the Customer Profile connection pool reads: "Layer: Storage Abstraction. Owner: Platform SRE. On-call: platform-sre-primary. SLO: 99.95% connection availability, P99 acquisition time < 5ms."

With that record, the incident commander routes to Platform SRE in minute 2, not minute 90.

> **VP ownership decision**: Audit the CMDB. Every CI without a layer classification is a routing time-bomb in the next incident. Assign a one-sprint classification effort. Any CI that cannot be classified within 2 minutes of discussion is a sign of architectural debt — document it as a problem ticket and schedule an architecture review.

## Layer Taxonomy as Investment Guide

Reliability engineering investment should follow blast radius, not equal distribution. Giving every layer the same investment — the same team size, the same on-call headcount, the same monitoring depth — is a misallocation that produces the worst possible outcome: marginal coverage everywhere, excellence nowhere.

| Layer | Blast Radius | Recovery Complexity | Investment Tier | Team Model |
|-------|-------------|--------------------|-----------------|-----------| 
| Persistent Storage | Total — all consumers affected | High — data recovery, replication resync | Tier 1 — dedicated DBRE team, automated backup validation, replication monitoring | Dedicated specialist team |
| Middleware / Integration | Total for async — all async consumers stop | High — consumer replay, DLQ drain, offset reset | Tier 1 — dedicated Middleware SRE, consumer lag alerting, automated DLQ remediation | Dedicated SRE team |
| API Gateway / Edge | Total for traffic — all inbound requests affected | Medium — routing config, cert rotation, rule rollback | Tier 2 — Platform SRE ownership, runbook automation, fast rollback tooling | Platform SRE sub-team |
| Storage Abstraction | Domain-wide — all consumers of a pool affected | Medium — pool drain and reconfigure | Tier 2 — Platform SRE, automated pool monitoring | Platform SRE ownership |
| Data Services | Domain-scoped — one domain's consumers | Medium — depends on downstream fan-out | Tier 3 — embedded SRE, domain on-call | Domain team + embedded SRE |
| Application Services | Service-scoped — one service or dependency chain | Low-Medium — restart, config change, rollback | Tier 3 — embedded SRE, domain on-call | Domain team + embedded SRE |
| Presentation / UI | Customer-perceived — high visibility, contained impact | Low — rollback deploy, CDN purge | Tier 3 — product on-call, SRE escalation path | Product team primary |

**Investment principle**: Tier 1 layers receive dedicated headcount and platform-grade tooling investment regardless of cost. A Tier 1 failure stops the entire platform. The cost of a 4-hour storage outage — in transaction loss, incident management overhead, and customer trust — exceeds the annual cost of a DBRE team.

## Common Misclassifications

| Component | Wrong Classification | Correct Classification | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| RabbitMQ broker | "Application infrastructure" | Middleware / Integration (Platform) | Wrong team owns incidents; no consumer lag monitoring; DLQ goes unnoticed |
| Redis (cache mode) | "Database" | Storage Abstraction (Platform) | DBRE team gets paged for cache eviction events; cache SLO treated as data SLO |
| MuleSoft integration flow | "API" | Middleware / Integration (Platform) | Edge team (Kong/Apigee SRE) responds to integration failures with wrong tooling |
| REST-to-SOAP adapter | "Middleware" | Application Services (Domain) | Middleware SRE is paged at 2AM for a stateless HTTP adapter owned by a product team |
| BFF (Backend for Frontend) | "Backend service" | Presentation / UI (Product) | Mobile-specific incidents routed to backend platform team; wrong SLO tier applied |
| Kafka audit log topic | "Middleware" | Persistent Storage (Platform) | Audit log retention governed as streaming — data lost on compaction; compliance risk |
| Connection pool (HikariCP) | "Part of the app" | Storage Abstraction (Platform) | Pool exhaustion incidents routed to application team; Platform SRE never notified |
| API gateway plugin (auth) | "Application service" | API Gateway / Edge (Platform) | Auth plugin failures routed to product team; platform SRE not in loop |

> **Incident routing implication**: every misclassification above represents a wrong first call in the next incident. The "REST adapter as Middleware" error means the Middleware SRE is paged at 2AM for a stateless HTTP adapter failure that the Application domain team should own — and has the context and rollback authority to resolve in 10 minutes. Instead, 40 minutes are lost while Middleware SRE determines they cannot fix it and escalates to the correct team.

> **VP ownership decision**: schedule a one-sprint classification audit. Every CI in CMDB gets a layer tag using the definitions in this framework's Key Definitions table. Any CI that cannot be classified within 2 minutes of discussion is architectural debt — log it as a problem ticket with the question: "Why is this component's function ambiguous?" The answer to that question is always a design deficiency, not a classification deficiency.

## What You Must Be Able to State in a VP Interview

1. **The organizational model**: Layer structure drives team structure drives on-call structure drives budget allocation. These are not independent decisions.

2. **The cascade constraint**: Storage SLO must be stricter than every layer above it. This is a mathematical requirement, not a best practice. You cannot architect your way around it without full fallback paths.

3. **The blast radius investment rule**: Tier 1 layers (Storage, Middleware) get dedicated teams regardless of headcount pressure. The cost of a one-hour total outage exceeds the annual cost of the team. This is not a budget argument — it is an expected-value argument.

4. **The misclassification consequence**: Every misclassified CI is a wrong first call in the next incident. MTTR impact is 40–60% on cross-layer failures in the absence of classification. You can cite this in cost terms.

5. **The audit action**: The first 90 days in a new VP/Director role includes a CMDB layer classification audit. It is the fastest way to identify architectural debt and organizational misalignment simultaneously.
