# Chapter 06: Application Transformation In Flight

Applications caught mid-transformation are the hardest operational state to govern. They are not legacy — they are not yet modern. They occupy a liminal zone where two systems run simultaneously, two on-call rotations must understand different architectures, and organizational inertia conspires to make "temporary" permanent. This chapter covers how to manage, measure, and close out transformations that are actively in progress.

---

## 1. The Dual-Running Problem

Every day you run old and new in parallel, you pay five separate taxes:

**Infrastructure cost.** Two systems means two compute footprints, two persistence layers, two networking stacks, two monitoring configurations. The infrastructure cost of a dual-run is typically 80–120% of the legacy system cost on top of the new system cost — not a rounding error.

**Operational overhead.** Your on-call rotation must be proficient in both systems. This is not additive — it is multiplicative. Rotating engineers who understand the new system need to re-learn legacy failure modes. Engineers who know legacy need to ramp on the new system. The effective operational overhead is 1.5–2x single-system operations.

**Cognitive load.** Every architectural decision during the dual-run period gets made twice: "how does this affect legacy behavior?" and "how does this affect new system behavior?" Meeting time doubles. Documentation forks. Onboarding complexity compounds.

**Security surface.** Two systems mean two vulnerability surfaces, two dependency trees to patch, two TLS certificate rotation schedules, two sets of secrets to rotate. Each is a separate audit finding if not maintained.

**Compliance footprint.** If your application is in a regulated domain (PCI, SOX, HIPAA), you now have two systems subject to audit controls. Double the control evidence, double the audit prep, double the risk of a control gap finding.

The dual-running cost must be made visible — not assumed away as a cost of doing business. "We started this transformation 14 months ago and we're still running both systems. That costs us $X per month in infrastructure and the equivalent of 2 FTE SRE hours per day in dual-system operational overhead. Every month this continues is another month of sunk cost that produces no new business value."

Dual-running is a transition state. It is not a steady state. It requires a mandatory exit date.

## 2. Transformation State Tracking

For every in-flight transformation, the VP needs to know seven things:

1. **Current phase** — one of the eight phases defined below
2. **Exit criteria for the current phase** — specific, measurable, binary
3. **Projected date to reach the next phase** — based on current velocity, not wishful thinking
4. **Active blockers** — what is preventing phase advancement today
5. **Daily cost of delay** — infrastructure + operational overhead, in dollars
6. **Escalation trigger** — what event or date automatically escalates to VP
7. **Owner** — the named individual accountable for this transformation progressing

Track this in the transformation status dashboard. Reviewed weekly by the transformation team, monthly by the VP, quarterly by the board as part of the portfolio review packet.

### The Eight Transformation Phases

**Design.** Architecture confirmed, transformation pattern selected (rehost/replatform/refactor/replace/retire), team assigned, budget approved, target state documented. Exit criteria: architecture decision record (ADR) signed off by architecture lead and VP.

**Build.** New system under active development. Legacy is production-only — no new features, no architectural changes. Exit criteria: new system passes all functional test suites, load tests at 110% of peak legacy traffic, and security scan with no critical CVEs.

**Testing.** New system in production-equivalent environment with production data (anonymized if required). Not yet receiving production traffic. Exit criteria: quality gates passed (functional, performance, security), runbook written for new system, on-call trained and signed off on new system readiness.

**Canary.** Ten percent or less of production traffic routed to new system. Legacy serves the remainder. Exit criteria: 7 consecutive days with error rate at or below legacy baseline, P99 latency within 20% of legacy P99, no data reconciliation anomalies.

**Progressive.** Ten to ninety-nine percent of production traffic on new system, legacy serving the remainder. Exit criteria: 14 days at current traffic split with all canary gates sustained, new system demonstrated capable of handling 110% of current traffic (headroom for spike if legacy is fully cut over).

**Cutover.** One hundred percent of production traffic on new system. Legacy stopped (not decommissioned). Exit criteria: 30 days of SLO compliance on new system at full traffic. After this gate, decommission is approved.

**Parallel-run.** Both systems receive 100% of production traffic simultaneously. Used only for financial, billing, or settlement systems where output comparison is a regulatory or risk requirement. Not a traffic migration mechanism — a correctness validation mechanism. Exit criteria: N consecutive business days (typically 30–60) with zero divergence between system outputs above defined tolerance. This phase must have a hard end date set before it begins.

**Decommission-pending.** Cutover complete, decommission approved. Old system in the decommission queue per the process defined in ch05. Exit criteria: decommission complete — all data migrated or archived, all dependencies removed, infrastructure released, cost savings confirmed in FinOps tooling.

## 3. The "Never Done" Transformation

The most dangerous failure mode in portfolio governance is a transformation that enters parallel-run and never exits. The team is technically "migrating" but has no committed cutover date and no defined acceptance criteria that could ever force the question.

Warning signs that a transformation has stalled:

- "We're not quite confident in the new system yet" — stated for more than two consecutive quarters with no specific technical gap identified
- Exit criteria keep expanding: the confidence bar moves every time the team approaches it
- The team is adding new features to the new system instead of proving it handles production load
- No VP-level deadline has been set or communicated to the team
- The legacy system is receiving bug fixes or minor enhancements, indicating the team has mentally re-adopted it

### Governance Intervention

A stalled transformation requires VP-level intervention, not team-level encouragement.

**Set a hard cutover date.** "Traffic moves to 100% new system on [date]. This date does not move without written executive escalation and explicit risk acceptance." The date is not a target — it is a boundary condition.

**Define objective, measurable acceptance criteria.** Acceptance criteria that depend on team confidence are not acceptance criteria — they are proxies for organizational anxiety. Replace them with:

```
Acceptance criteria for cutover approval:
- 30 consecutive days of SLO compliance at full production traffic
- P99 latency within 10% of the legacy baseline at equivalent traffic levels
- Zero data reconciliation errors (financial systems) or < N errors per million transactions (other)
- Zero Severity-1 or Severity-2 incidents attributable to new system in the 30-day window
```

These criteria are observable, measurable, and binary. Either they are met or they are not.

**If criteria are not met by the deadline, escalate.** Do not silently extend the timeline. Escalation means: VP reviews blockers, assigns resolution owners, adjusts resourcing, or makes a documented decision to extend with explicit risk acceptance. Silence is not governance.

## 4. Incident Handling for Dual-Running Systems

The first question in any incident involving a dual-running system: who owns it?

**The rule:** The new system team is on-call primary. The legacy system is on-call secondary. Never invert this. Inverting the on-call ownership creates an organizational incentive to not migrate traffic — if legacy is primary, the new system team has no urgency to make the new system production-ready.

### Incident Decision Tree

**Incident is on the new system.**
Incident commander from the new system team leads. Legacy system team is on standby for rollback support. The IC decides whether to roll back traffic to legacy. Rollback must be executable in under 5 minutes.

**Incident is on the legacy system.**
Route any traffic still on legacy to the new system (if safe to do so). Treat as a legacy incident. Do not delay cutover based on this incident — if the legacy system is having incidents, that accelerates the case for cutover, not against it.

**Incident is caused by dual-run overhead itself** — data sync failure, routing misconfiguration between old and new, split-brain between datastores.
Escalate immediately to the transformation lead. This is a transformation risk event, not a normal production incident. The IC documents the incident as a transformation blocker.

### Rollback Mechanism Requirements

Before routing any production traffic to the new system, the rollback mechanism must exist and be tested. Rollback is: routing 100% of traffic back to legacy. The mechanism used to split traffic initially is the rollback mechanism.

Acceptable rollback mechanisms (in order of preference):
- Service mesh routing weight (Istio VirtualService, Linkerd traffic split): weight change takes effect in seconds, no DNS involved
- API gateway routing rule: weight change via gateway config, typically seconds to minutes
- Load balancer weight (AWS ALB target group weights, Nginx upstream weights): effective in under a minute

Unacceptable rollback mechanism:
- DNS-based traffic split: DNS TTL means rollback takes minutes to hours to propagate. For a P1 incident, this is unacceptable.

Test the rollback before you route any production traffic. Specifically: with 10% of traffic on the new system, execute a rollback to 0% and confirm 0% is achieved within 5 minutes, with no errors during the transition.

## 5. Data Consistency During Parallel Run

The hardest technical problem in dual-running is data. When two systems write to independent datastores, keeping those datastores consistent is a distributed systems problem with no clean solution — only trade-offs.

### Read-Only Parallel Run

New system serves reads. Legacy serves writes. New system reads from the legacy datastore (or a replica of it).

This is the simplest configuration. Data consistency is guaranteed by construction — there is one writer. The risk is semantic: the new system may have different read behavior (query optimization, caching layer, data projection logic) that produces different results from the same underlying data. Surface this through reconciliation queries that compare new system read output against legacy read output for the same inputs.

### Full Parallel Run

Both systems receive writes. This is where it gets hard.

**Dual-write at application layer.** The application writes to both datastores in the same request. Consistency risk: if one write succeeds and the other fails, the datastores diverge. Mitigation requires two-phase commit or compensating transactions — both are complex and add latency. Use only if you have no alternative and the divergence tolerance is non-zero.

**Event-driven sync.** Writes go to legacy. Changes are published as events (Kafka, RabbitMQ). New system consumes events and updates its datastore. This is the pattern most compatible with existing middleware expertise. The risk: the new system is eventually consistent. There is always a lag between legacy write and new system update. For financial systems, this is typically unacceptable. For other domains, define and enforce a maximum acceptable lag (e.g., 500ms P99 event processing lag).

**Database replication.** Legacy database replicates to new database via native replication (Postgres streaming replication, MySQL binlog replication, DynamoDB global tables). Risk: replication lag (typically sub-second but can spike under load) and schema differences (if the new system has a different schema, replication requires a transformation layer, which re-introduces lag and complexity).

### Data Reconciliation

Regardless of which sync pattern is used, run continuous reconciliation. A reconciliation job queries both systems for key metrics and alerts on divergence.

Minimum reconciliation checks:
- Record counts by entity type (orders, users, transactions) — should match within replication lag tolerance
- Aggregate values (total order value, account balances) — should match exactly for financial systems
- Sample-based spot checks: pick N random records from legacy and verify they exist and match in new system

Alert thresholds:
- Financial/billing systems: divergence tolerance is zero. Any divergence is a P1.
- Other systems: define based on business impact. Typical starting point is < 0.01% record divergence.

## 6. Traffic Migration Strategy

Use the 0%/10%/50%/100% pattern. Do not skip steps. Do not compress timelines by skipping observation periods.

### 0% — Operational Readiness Baseline

New system is deployed to production but receives no traffic. This is not a staging environment — it is a production deployment with zero traffic.

Required before advancing to 10%:
- Dashboards deployed and reviewed by on-call team
- Alerts configured, tested (fire test alerts, confirm routing), and confirmed reaching the correct paging destination
- Runbook written, reviewed, and accessible in the team's runbook system
- On-call team trained on new system — not just "read the runbook" but active training with simulated scenarios
- Rollback mechanism deployed and tested (per Section 4)
- Data reconciliation job running and producing baseline output

### 10% — Canary

Route 10% of production traffic to the new system. The 10% should be representative of the full traffic mix — not cherry-picked low-risk requests. Random sampling is preferred. Geographic or user-segment canaries are acceptable if the segment is representative.

Minimum observation period: 7 days. Do not advance based on 24 hours of clean metrics — traffic patterns vary by day of week.

Gate criteria to advance from 10% to 50%:
- Error rate on new system at or below legacy baseline for all 7 days
- P99 latency within 20% of legacy P99 at equivalent traffic levels
- No data reconciliation anomalies detected
- No P1 or P2 incidents attributable to new system

### 50% — Progressive

At 50%, the new system must be capable of handling 100% of production traffic. If legacy fails while you're at 50%, and you need to route all traffic to new system, the new system must absorb 2x its current load. Load test to 110% of peak production traffic before advancing to 50%.

Minimum observation period: 14 days.

Gate criteria to advance from 50% to 100%:
- All 10% gate criteria sustained at 50% load for 14 days
- Load test at 110% of full production traffic passed with SLOs maintained

### 100% — Cutover

Full traffic on new system. Legacy is stopped (not decommissioned). Keep legacy infrastructure in place for the 30-day observation period — it is the rollback option of last resort.

Minimum observation period: 30 days.

Gate criteria to approve decommission:
- 30 consecutive days of SLO compliance at full traffic
- No P1 or P2 incidents attributable to new system
- Data reconciliation confirms clean state (where applicable)

After 30 days, initiate decommission per ch05 process.

### Kubernetes Implementation

For Kubernetes-hosted services, use Istio VirtualService weight routing or Nginx Ingress canary annotations. Both support weight-based traffic splitting with immediate-effect configuration changes.

Istio example pattern:
```yaml
apiVersion: networking.istio.io/v1alpha3
kind: VirtualService
metadata:
  name: payment-service
spec:
  http:
  - route:
    - destination:
        host: payment-service-legacy
      weight: 90
    - destination:
        host: payment-service-new
      weight: 10
```

Weight changes take effect within seconds — no rolling restart required. This is the correct mechanism for canary routing. DNS-based approaches (split at Route53 or equivalent) are not acceptable for this use case because TTL makes rollback slow.

## 7. Feature Flag Governance During Transformation

Transformation teams use feature flags to control old-vs-new routing, enable dark launches, and manage progressive rollout within the application layer. Feature flags are useful. They also accumulate as technical debt when not actively retired.

### Flag Naming Convention

Every transformation routing flag follows this pattern:

```
FF_MIGR_[APP]_[CAPABILITY]_[YYYYMM]
```

Where `[YYYYMM]` is the planned retirement date — not the creation date. This makes overdue flags immediately visible in a flag registry query.

Examples:
- `FF_MIGR_PAYMT_CHECKOUT_202506` — payment service checkout routing flag, planned retirement June 2025
- `FF_MIGR_NOTIF_EMAIL_202504` — notification service email path flag, planned retirement April 2025

### Flag Retirement SLA

Transformation routing flags must be retired within 90 days of cutover completion. A flag still in the codebase 90 days post-cutover is a technical debt finding. At 180 days, it is a blocker for the team's next debt cleanup sprint.

### Flag Registry

Every flag is registered in a central flag registry with:
- Flag name and owning team
- Purpose (transformation routing, dark launch, killswitch, etc.)
- Created date
- Planned retirement date
- Current state (active, disabled, retired)
- Retirement status (on track, overdue, escalated)

The VP receives a monthly report: flags planned for retirement this quarter, flags overdue for retirement, flags older than 6 months that are not yet retired. Unretired flags older than 6 months are escalated to Engineering Director for resolution timeline.

## 8. Transformation Health Metrics

The VP reviews these metrics weekly for every in-flight transformation. These are not engineering metrics — they are transformation governance metrics.

| Metric | What It Measures | Alert Threshold |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| % traffic on new system | Actual migration progress | Below expected rate for current phase by > 10 percentage points |
| Incident rate: new system vs. legacy baseline | Relative reliability of new system | New system incident rate > 120% of legacy rate |
| Data reconciliation match rate | Data consistency between systems | < 99.99% (non-financial); any divergence (financial/billing) |
| Daily dual-run cost (infra + ops) | Pace of cost accumulation | > approved budget for current phase |
| Days in current phase vs. plan | Schedule health | > 30% over planned phase duration |
| Rollback exercise freshness | Readiness of rollback capability | Last tested > 30 days ago |

A transformation that fails two or more of these metrics in a single weekly review is automatically escalated to VP for a 30-minute review session. The transformation lead must come prepared with: root cause of metric failure, specific remediation steps, revised timeline.

Transformation health is reviewed as a standing agenda item in the weekly SRE staff meeting, not as an ad hoc escalation. By the time an escalation happens informally, the problem has usually been known to the team for weeks.
