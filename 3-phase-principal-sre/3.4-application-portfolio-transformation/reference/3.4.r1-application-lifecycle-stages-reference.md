# Application Lifecycle Stages Reference

The five lifecycle stages define an application's investment level, governance requirements, and operational posture. Every application in the portfolio is assigned exactly one stage at any given time. Stage assignment is reviewed quarterly as part of the health scorecard process.

An application's lifecycle stage is not the same as its health score. A Mature/Stable application can have a high health score. A Legacy application must by definition have an elevated technology currency risk, but may have a high reliability score. Both dimensions — stage and health — are required to make portfolio decisions.

## Stage Detail

### Inception

An application in Inception has been approved and is being built. It is not yet in production. Investment is high — the team is building at full velocity. Governance at this stage is focused entirely on what happens at the transition to production, not on production operations.

**Portfolio management focus**: ensuring the new application meets platform standards before it reaches production. An application that bypasses the production readiness checklist at Inception becomes a dark matter application by its first birthday — it exists in production but nobody knows its full dependency graph, alert coverage, or recovery procedures.

**Production readiness checklist** (required before first production deployment):
- SLO defined and agreed with business owner
- Dashboards deployed (service-level and infrastructure-level)
- Alerts configured with correct routing to on-call rotation
- Runbooks drafted for the three most likely failure modes
- On-call coverage assigned and trained
- Security scan passing (no critical CVEs in dependency tree)
- Secrets management confirmed (no hardcoded credentials)
- Application registry entry created

**Governance**: no production change records required during development. Standard change records apply from first production deployment forward.

---

### Active Development

The highest-velocity stage. The application is in production and receiving regular features. Deployment frequency is high — weekly or more. The team's attention is on feature delivery, not operational stability, which creates accumulation risk.

**Portfolio management focus**: early warning. The most cost-effective intervention is catching technical debt accumulation while the application is in Active Development — the team is present, the codebase is understood, and the effort to correct course is low. A dependency that drifts to EOL during Active Development costs hours to upgrade. The same drift caught at Legacy stage may cost months.

**Quarterly health scorecard** is mandatory for all Active Development applications. Focus on technology currency (is the team keeping dependencies current as they add features?) and security posture (is the CI/CD pipeline catching new CVEs before they reach production?).

**Common failure modes at this stage**:
- Dependencies pinned at minor versions during initial development, never updated
- Security scanning added to the pipeline but failures set to warn-only, not block
- Runbooks drafted at Inception but not updated as new failure modes are discovered
- On-call rotation limited to the core development team (bus factor not growing)

**Governance**: standard change records. Weekly deployments normal. ARB review not required for feature changes. ARB review required for architectural changes (new external dependencies, database schema changes affecting other consumers).

---

### Mature/Stable

The most populous stage in most portfolios. The application is valuable — it works reliably and delivers business function. Feature investment has declined because the application has reached functional completeness for current business needs. The SRE team spends most of its time on operations, not on enabling new features.

**The core risk at Mature/Stable**: gradual currency drift. Each quarter without a dependency upgrade is a quarter of accumulated EOL exposure. Because the application is "working," there is organizational pressure to avoid touching it. This pressure is the direct cause of applications entering the Legacy stage without anyone making an explicit decision to let them drift.

**Portfolio management focus**: mandatory annual technology currency review, regardless of application health score. A Mature/Stable application with a health score of 85 can still have three dependencies approaching EOL — the high reliability and team knowledge scores can mask the currency risk. The scorecard's technology currency dimension (scored separately, 0–20) surfaces this before it becomes a Legacy classification decision.

**Governance**: standard change records for routine changes. Major changes — architectural modifications, new external service dependencies, database schema changes affecting other consumers — require ARB review. The ARB review is not a blocker; it is a forcing function for documentation and dependency analysis before changes are made to a system that other teams depend on.

---

### Legacy

A Legacy application is one where the combination of declining technology currency and confirmed operational risk (high change blast radius, or knowledge concentration below safe threshold) makes it a portfolio liability rather than a portfolio asset. It is still in production, still delivering value, but it is no longer safe to treat as a normal application.

**The L1–L4 classification** applies within the Legacy stage (defined in ch03). Classification determines the urgency and type of intervention:
- L1: Knowledge concentration risk only. Bus factor < 2. Intervention: knowledge preservation sprint within 30 days.
- L2: Technology currency risk, manageable blast radius. Intervention: planned upgrade cycle within 90 days.
- L3: Architecture risk, high change blast radius, or tightly-coupled dependency on other legacy components. Intervention: protective wrapping before any modernization. Change freeze recommended for non-essential changes.
- L4: Active instability, regular SLO breaches, or critical unmitigated CVEs. Intervention: immediate VP notification, change freeze, emergency stabilization before any other work.

**Portfolio management focus**: accurate classification, protective wrapping before modernization begins, and sequencing the application into the modernization roadmap. A Legacy application should not simply sit in the Legacy stage indefinitely — it must have an active modernization plan or a VP-approved decision to remain in Legacy with compensating controls.

**Governance**: mandatory ARB review for every change. The purpose is not bureaucracy — it is blast radius analysis. Because Legacy applications have high coupling and unpredictable failure modes, every change requires a documented understanding of what else could break. L3 and L4 classification trigger change freeze: no changes except those required for incident remediation or security patching.

---

### Sunset

Sunset is a fixed-duration stage with a defined end state: decommission complete. It is initiated after the replacement system has been proven stable for 90 days (full SLO compliance) and VP decommission approval has been obtained.

**The 8-phase decommission process** (from ch05) applies to all Sunset applications:
1. Dependency mapping (confirm all consumers identified)
2. Consumer migration (all consumers migrated to replacement or decommissioned)
3. Traffic validation (zero traffic confirmed via telemetry)
4. Data archival (data retained per retention policy, archived to cold storage)
5. Data deletion (data beyond retention policy deleted)
6. Infrastructure teardown (compute, network, storage deprovisioned)
7. DNS and certificate cleanup
8. Application registry updated to Decommissioned state

**Applications that get stuck in Sunset**: if an application has been in Sunset stage for more than 6 months without completing decommission, it is returned to Legacy stage. The decommission stall is a signal that either consumers have not been migrated (consumer discovery was incomplete), data archival is blocked (retention policy conflict or data volume), or the replacement system has not been proven stable. Each of these requires active management, not passive waiting.

A new decommission plan with VP sign-off is required before the Sunset stage can be re-entered.

**Governance**: no new changes to the application. Traffic routing changes to support consumer migration are permitted. Infrastructure changes to reduce operational cost during the wind-down period are permitted with standard change records. No feature changes, no dependency upgrades — the application is being wound down, not maintained.

## Governance Requirements by Stage

| Requirement | Inception | Active Dev | Mature/Stable | Legacy | Sunset |
|-------------|-----------|------------|---------------|--------|--------|
| Application registry entry | Required before first deploy | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes — marked Sunset |
| SLO defined | Before prod deploy | Yes | Yes | Yes | Not required |
| On-call coverage | Before prod deploy | Yes | Yes | Yes | Legacy team (wind-down) |
| Quarterly health scorecard | Not required | Yes | Annual minimum | Quarterly | Monthly (tracking decommission) |
| ARB review for changes | Not required | Standard | Major changes only | All changes | No new changes |
| VP sign-off for lifecycle stage change | Not required | Not required | Not required | Required to enter Sunset | Required to extend beyond 6 months |

## Stage Transition Triggers

**Active Development → Mature/Stable**

Triggered when: feature delivery rate falls below 1 significant feature per quarter for 6+ consecutive months. This is not a failure — it is a signal of functional completeness. Prompt: the technical owner updates the application registry stage and the quarterly scorecard moves to annual cadence for the technology currency dimension.

**Mature/Stable → Legacy**

Triggered when: technology currency score falls below 10 AND either (a) change risk is confirmed high (ARB assessment) or (b) knowledge concentration is confirmed below safe threshold (bus factor < 2). Both conditions must be present: low currency score alone can be remediated with an upgrade cycle. The combination of low currency AND high change risk or knowledge risk is the signal that normal remediation pathways are insufficient.

**Legacy → Sunset**

Triggered when: (a) replacement system has been proven stable for 90 days with full SLO compliance AND (b) a migration plan covering all consumers is in place AND (c) VP decommission approval is obtained. All three conditions are required. A replacement that is not yet stable is not grounds for Sunset entry — it is grounds for accelerating the Legacy application's stabilization.

**Sunset → Decommissioned** (terminal state)

Triggered when: all 8 decommission phases are complete, application registry is updated, and infrastructure costs reach zero.

**Sunset → Legacy** (regression)

Triggered when: application has been in Sunset stage for more than 6 months without completing decommission. Requires new VP-approved decommission plan before re-entering Sunset.
