# Application Portfolio Transformation

## What This Is

Application portfolio management is the discipline of treating applications as business assets with explicit lifecycles, ownership, investment levels, and retirement criteria — not as software that happens to be running. An organization that manages its portfolio knows what it owns, what each asset costs to operate, where each asset sits in its lifecycle, and what the plan is when that lifecycle ends. An organization that does not manage its portfolio discovers its risk posture through incidents.

The distinction matters at Director and VP level because portfolio decisions are financial and strategic decisions, not just technical ones. When a VP of Operations approves a modernization program, they are making a capital allocation decision: invest now in transformation to reduce future operational cost and risk. When they defer that decision indefinitely, they are making a different capital decision — accepting increasing run cost, increasing technical debt, and increasing probability of a catastrophic legacy failure. Portfolio management makes that trade-off explicit, quantified, and governable. Without it, the trade-off still exists but is invisible until it materializes as a P0.

## Three Failure Modes of Portfolio Management

### Failure Mode 1: No Portfolio Visibility

The organization does not have an authoritative inventory of what runs in production. Teams know their own applications. Nobody has a complete picture. The VP knows the top 10 systems. The other 190 are where risk concentrates.

This failure mode manifests as: incidents involving systems nobody thought were critical, decommission attempts that fail because undocumented consumers break, modernization programs that stall when they discover unexpected dependencies, and budget conversations where nobody can justify the run cost because nobody knows what they're running.

The recovery is a portfolio audit (see [ch08-portfolio-audit-and-recovery.md](ch08-portfolio-audit-and-recovery.md)) and a governance commitment to maintaining the inventory.

### Failure Mode 2: Transformation Without Governance

Projects start but do not finish. Applications enter parallel run and never exit. The new system is declared "ready" repeatedly without meeting defined exit criteria. The old system was supposed to be decommissioned 18 months ago but still serves 5% of traffic because one consumer never migrated.

This failure mode is characterized by: parallel-run cost doubling the budget for extended periods, teams maintaining two systems indefinitely, migration project "completion" dates that slide quarterly, and organizational fatigue that kills the next transformation proposal.

The recovery is governance discipline: defined exit criteria before parallel run begins, executive accountability for completion dates, and a decommission-first mindset (see [ch06-transformation-governance.md](ch06-transformation-governance.md) and [ch07-decommission-and-sunset.md](ch07-decommission-and-sunset.md)).

### Failure Mode 3: Never Sunsetting Anything

Applications are added to the portfolio continuously. Nothing is ever removed. The portfolio grows year over year. Every system has a constituency that resists retirement. "We might need it." "One team still uses it." "It's too risky to turn off."

This failure mode accumulates zombie applications: systems with declining traffic, no active investment, no clear owner, consuming SRE on-call time, carrying unpatched CVEs, and adding blast radius to every change. Each zombie is a security risk, an operational tax, and a distraction from systems that matter.

The recovery is a decommission discipline and an EOL governance process. Every application with a declining lifecycle stage must have a documented sunset date. Sunset dates are not suggestions — they are commitments with executive visibility (see [ch07-decommission-and-sunset.md](ch07-decommission-and-sunset.md) and [templates/eol-risk-register-template.md](templates/eol-risk-register-template.md)).

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Application Portfolio** | The complete set of applications an organization operates in production, managed as a collection of business assets with lifecycles, ownership, and investment levels — not an ad hoc list of systems. |
| **Transformation** | Any change to an application's fundamental architecture, platform, or technology — as opposed to feature additions (Grow) or operational maintenance (Run). Covers migration, modernization, and replatforming. |
| **Migration** | Moving an application from one hosting environment, platform, or infrastructure to another — without necessarily changing its internal architecture. |
| **Modernization** | Changing an application's internal architecture, data model, or technology stack to align with current standards — typically to improve scalability, deployability, or cost profile. |
| **Legacy System** | A production system whose technology stack, architecture, or operational model has declined below the organization's current standards, such that the cost and risk of maintaining it increases over time relative to alternatives. Not a pejorative — legacy systems often deliver critical business value. |
| **Technical Currency** | The degree to which an application's technology stack, dependencies, and architecture align with current organizational and industry standards. An application with low technical currency uses EOL frameworks, unmaintained libraries, or architectural patterns the organization has moved away from. |
| **Application Lifecycle Stage** | A defined phase in an application's life: Inception, Active Development, Mature/Stable, Legacy, Sunset. Each stage implies different investment levels, change governance, and SLO expectations. |
| **Strangler Fig** | A migration pattern where a replacement system is built incrementally alongside the legacy system, with traffic routed to new components as they're proven, until the legacy system is fully replaced and decommissioned. Preferred pattern for most large legacy modernizations due to incremental risk reduction. |
| **Strangler Vine** | A variant of Strangler Fig where the replacement wraps the legacy system at the API layer, presenting a clean interface to consumers while routing to legacy for capabilities not yet replaced. Useful when clean API exposure is needed before full capability replacement is possible. |
| **Big Bang Migration** | Replacing an entire system in a single cutover event. Highest execution risk due to no rollback path and requirement that all functionality be proven before cutover. Necessary only when regulatory or architectural constraints prohibit parallel operation. |
| **Parallel Run** | Operating both legacy and replacement systems simultaneously, routing traffic to both and comparing outputs, before committing production decisions to the replacement. Highest confidence pattern; highest cost due to double infrastructure and operations. |
| **Application Scorecard** | A structured assessment of application health across multiple dimensions (reliability, security posture, technology currency, operational cost, team knowledge) producing a composite score that informs lifecycle stage and investment priority. |
| **Run/Grow/Transform Budget Framework** | A budget allocation model that categorizes all technology spending into three buckets: Run (keeping existing systems operational), Grow (adding features to existing systems), and Transform (fundamental architecture or platform change). Used to assess portfolio health and drive modernization investment decisions. |
| **Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL)** | A translation layer between a legacy system and a replacement system that prevents the new system's domain model from inheriting the legacy system's data structures, terminology, and constraints. Required when the legacy data model would corrupt the new system's design. |
| **Dark Matter Application** | A production system with no documented owner, no SLO, no on-call coverage — typically discovered during incidents. Accumulates through reorganizations, acquisitions, and shadow IT. |
| **EOL (End of Life)** | The date at which a technology component (framework, runtime, operating system, vendor platform) exits vendor support — receiving no security patches. Applications running EOL components carry unmitigable security risk. |
| **Application Tier** | A classification of criticality: Tier 1 (business-critical, direct revenue or customer impact, highest SLO), Tier 2 (significant internal dependency or indirect customer impact), Tier 3 (supporting systems, limited blast radius), Tier 4 (non-critical, dev/test, internal tooling). Tier drives investment priority, on-call requirements, and change governance. |
| **Decommission** | The controlled shutdown of an application: traffic drained, consumers migrated, data archived or deleted per retention policy, infrastructure released, system removed from the portfolio registry. A completed decommission returns Run budget and eliminates the associated risk. |

## Relationships to Other Knowledge Domains

**[Architecture Principles and Governance]** — Portfolio management operates within the architectural standards set by governance. The transformation pattern selected for any application must comply with architectural principles (e.g., "all new services must be event-driven," "no direct database sharing between services"). The portfolio review and the architecture review board have overlapping authority over modernization decisions — define the boundary explicitly to avoid conflicting mandates.

**[Change Management]** — Every application that changes lifecycle stage (e.g., Legacy to Sunset) requires a formal change record. Consumer migrations, parallel run cutover, and decommission events are high-risk changes that require change advisory board approval. The portfolio registry must be updated as a required deliverable of any change that alters an application's hosting platform, ownership, or operational status.

**[Environment Management]** — Transformation programs introduce environment complexity: legacy and replacement systems must coexist in staging, pre-production, and production environments simultaneously during parallel run. Environment management must account for this multiplicity in capacity planning, environment promotion gates, and access controls. Decommission must include environment cleanup — not just production, but all non-prod environments the legacy system occupied.

**[Performance Engineering]** — Application health scoring includes an operational cost dimension that requires baseline performance data. The modernization business case depends on cost and performance benchmarks from the legacy system and projections for the replacement. Performance engineering provides the measurement instrumentation that makes these comparisons valid. During parallel run, performance comparison between legacy and replacement is a primary exit criterion — performance engineering owns that measurement.
