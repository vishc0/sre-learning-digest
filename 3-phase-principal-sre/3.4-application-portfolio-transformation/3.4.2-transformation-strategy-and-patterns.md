# Chapter 2: Transformation Strategy and Patterns

## 1. Why Transform?

Transformation is not a technology initiative. It is a business decision driven by four categories of business pressure, each with specific, quantifiable consequences. A VP of Operations who cannot articulate the business case for transformation in these terms will not get the budget for it.

### Cost

Legacy systems cost 3–5x what equivalent modern services cost to operate. The cost premium is not a single factor — it is a stack of compounding costs:

**Specialized skill premium**: The supply of engineers who can operate TIBCO BusinessWorks, COBOL mainframe applications, or Oracle Forms is shrinking as that generation of engineers retires and newer engineers are not trained on these platforms. Supply contraction drives salary and contractor premiums. A TIBCO BW 5.x administrator commands a 40–60% premium over an equivalent MuleSoft or Kafka engineer in most US markets. The premium will increase over time, not decrease.

**Legacy licensing**: Many legacy platforms use perpetual license models with annual maintenance fees that are not usage-elastic. You pay the same license cost whether the system processes 10,000 transactions/day or 1,000. Modern cloud-native platforms and open-source middleware scale cost with usage — you pay for what you consume. A fixed-license TIBCO deployment serving 30% of its licensed capacity is paying 3x the per-transaction cost of a right-sized modern equivalent.

**Manual operational procedures**: Legacy systems were designed before the era of automated operations. Deployments require manual steps. Scaling requires manual intervention. Incident response requires specialized knowledge that cannot be scripted. Each of these manual procedures has a labor cost that modern platforms eliminate through automation. A Kubernetes-based service with a GitOps deployment pipeline deploys in minutes with no human involvement after merge. An equivalent legacy deployment may require a 2-hour change window with three engineers present.

**Infrastructure that can't be right-sized**: Legacy applications are often tied to specific hardware or VM configurations that cannot be elastically scaled. You provision for peak load and pay for peak capacity 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. Cloud-native applications scale down during off-peak hours and scale up during traffic spikes. For applications with variable traffic patterns — retail, media, consumer internet — the cost difference is material.

### Risk

Legacy carries two categories of risk that compound over time:

**Reliability debt**: Legacy systems have worse MTTR than modern equivalents, not because they fail more often (though they often do), but because when they fail, the blast radius is larger and recovery is harder. Monolithic architectures fail completely when any component fails. ESB-centric integration patterns mean a single middleware failure can take down all integrations simultaneously. Rollback from a failed legacy deployment often requires manual steps that take hours. A microservice in Kubernetes can be rolled back in seconds; a TIBCO BusinessWorks application requires a manual rollback procedure that was last tested 18 months ago.

**Security debt**: Legacy systems carry security posture that is structurally incompatible with modern requirements. Applications built before zero-trust security models assumed network perimeter security — they authenticated at the edge and trusted everything inside the network. Service-to-service authentication, certificate-based identity, secrets rotation, and audit logging at the API boundary are not retrofit-able to applications that were not designed for them. EOL components (JVM versions, OS, middleware platforms) receive no security patches — every new CVE disclosed is a permanent vulnerability in that application. This is not a risk that gets better with time. It is a risk that compounds: each new CVE on an EOL platform is a new permanent exposure.

Frame this to an executive audience as: "Legacy is a loaded probability distribution. The longer you hold it, the more likely it fires. Every month you defer modernization, you are accepting a higher probability that this application delivers your next major security incident."

### Velocity

Legacy architectures impose a speed ceiling on feature delivery that grows lower over time as complexity accumulates.

**Undocumented dependencies**: Every change to a legacy application carries the risk of breaking something in the dependency graph that nobody fully understands. Engineers respond rationally by being conservative — smaller changes, longer regression testing, more review cycles. The blast radius of a legacy monolith change is unknown; the blast radius of an independent microservice change is bounded to that service's consumers.

**Deployment coupling**: A monolith requires that all teams' changes are deployed together. One team's release schedule becomes every team's release constraint. Coordinating 15 teams to align on a release window is expensive in both calendar time and engineering overhead. Independent services deploy independently — team A's release does not require team B's approval or alignment.

**Testing cost**: When blast radius is unknown, you test everything on every change. A comprehensive regression suite for a monolith can take 4–6 hours to execute. A service with bounded scope can be fully tested in 10 minutes. The mathematics of this difference compounds: a team that can run a test suite in 10 minutes can iterate 30x faster per day than one that requires a 5-hour test suite.

### Capability

Legacy architectures are structurally incapable of supporting requirements that the current business expects as table stakes:

**Real-time event streaming**: Batch-oriented architectures cannot deliver real-time. A nightly batch feed cannot support a real-time fraud detection model or a live customer notification. The architecture is the constraint — you cannot add real-time capability to a batch system without replacing its core processing model.

**AI/ML integration**: Machine learning models require access to data at scale, with low latency, in formats that legacy data stores were not designed to provide. Integrating an ML model into a workflow that depends on a legacy relational database with a normalized schema and batch export patterns requires bridging an architectural gap that is expensive and fragile.

**API-first product strategy**: ESB-centric integration was designed for internal system-to-system connectivity. It does not naturally expose clean, versioned, externally consumable APIs. Building an API program on top of an ESB architecture requires a facade layer that adds latency, operational complexity, and a translation problem every time the underlying system changes.

**Cloud-native scaling**: Legacy applications that were designed for vertical scaling (more CPU, more RAM) cannot horizontally scale (more instances). Kubernetes is horizontal-first — it manages pools of instances. An application that cannot run as multiple concurrent instances cannot take advantage of Kubernetes' core scaling capability. You end up with a large single instance on Kubernetes, which is worse than a large single instance on a VM because you've added orchestration overhead without gaining the orchestration benefit.

## 2. The Transformation Pattern Taxonomy

Nine patterns. Each has a place in the toolkit. The mistake is having a default — applying Strangler Fig to everything, or defaulting to Big Bang because it feels like a single coherent project. Pattern selection is driven by constraints, not preference.

---

### Pattern 1: Lift and Shift (Rehost)

**What it is**: Moving an application to new infrastructure without changing the code, architecture, or application configuration in any material way. The application runs in a new place; it behaves identically to how it behaved before.

**Operational characteristics**: Fastest to execute. Lowest risk during migration (the application is unchanged, so failure modes are infrastructure-only). No new capabilities are gained. No technical debt is retired. The application arrives at the new infrastructure with all of its original problems intact.

**When to use**:
- Datacenter exit deadline: you must vacate a physical location by a date, and there is not enough time to refactor before that date. Lift and shift buys time.
- Short-term cost reduction: moving from expensive co-location to cloud IaaS can reduce infrastructure cost even without any application changes, if the current hosting contract is the primary cost driver.
- Prerequisite to deeper work: sometimes you need the application on the new infrastructure before you can refactor it. Lift and shift gets it there; a subsequent phase does the architectural work.

**When NOT to use**:
- When the architectural problem is in the code. An application that crashes under load because it has a memory leak does not benefit from running on cloud infrastructure — it still crashes under load.
- As a modernization strategy. It is a migration strategy. It does not modernize anything.

**Rollback ability**: High. Infrastructure is the only variable. If the new infrastructure fails, route traffic back to original infrastructure. Application state is unchanged.

**T-Mobile/middleware example**: Moving a TIBCO BusinessWorks deployment from a physical datacenter to cloud-hosted VMs as part of a datacenter exit. The TIBCO deployment is unchanged; the infrastructure beneath it is now in cloud compute. The TIBCO licensing, the manual deployment procedures, and the architectural constraints all remain — they are now running in a different facility.

**Key risks**: You have moved technical debt to a new address. All application-level problems (EOL components, security posture, operational cost) remain and will need to be addressed in a subsequent phase. Organizations that treat Lift and Shift as a destination rather than a waypoint discover this when the EOL and security risk comes due anyway.

### Pattern 2: Re-platform

**What it is**: Moving to new infrastructure with minor platform-level changes — changes that affect how the application is packaged and deployed, without changing the application's internal architecture or business logic.

**Operational characteristics**: Moderate execution complexity. The application is containerized or adapted for the new platform runtime, but its internal structure is unchanged. You gain the operational benefits of the new platform (Kubernetes deployment automation, resource efficiency, observability) without refactoring the application's architecture.

**When to use**:
- You want the benefits of Kubernetes (resource efficiency, deployment automation, horizontal scaling capability) without taking on the risk of a full rewrite.
- The application's architecture is acceptable — the problem is purely the deployment and operations model.
- The team has limited Kubernetes experience and needs to build it against an application they understand before tackling architectural changes.

**When NOT to use**:
- When the application's architecture is the problem. Containerizing a monolith that OOMs due to a memory leak gives you a containerized monolith that OOMs. Kubernetes will keep restarting it, which is better than manual intervention, but the root cause is unaddressed.
- When the application cannot run as multiple concurrent instances. If the application has in-memory state, singleton patterns, or other characteristics that prevent horizontal scaling, containerizing it does not unlock Kubernetes' core value.

**Rollback ability**: Moderate. The infrastructure change is reversible; the containerization work represents a non-trivial investment.

**T-Mobile/middleware example**: Moving a Spring Boot microservice from PCF (Pivotal Cloud Foundry) to EKS. The application code is unchanged. The deployment manifest moves from a PCF manifest.yml to a Kubernetes Deployment and Service. The application is containerized with a Dockerfile. It runs in Kubernetes with all the operational benefits that entails — rolling deployments, health checks, horizontal pod autoscaling. The application's internal architecture is the same as it was in PCF.

**Key risks**: The gap between re-platform and full re-architecture is often underestimated mid-project. Teams discover during containerization that the application has characteristics (singleton state, local filesystem dependencies, hardcoded hostnames) that prevent it from running correctly in Kubernetes without application-level changes. When those changes surface, the project scope expands beyond its original definition.

### Pattern 3: Re-architect / Modernize

**What it is**: Changing the application's internal architecture — extracting microservices from a monolith, migrating from synchronous REST to event-driven Kafka patterns, decomposing a shared database into service-owned data stores, replacing a stateful design with a stateless one.

**Operational characteristics**: Highest execution complexity and duration of the single-application patterns. The application is fundamentally redesigned during this process. The output is a new system with the same business capability as the original but with a new architecture that eliminates the constraints of the original.

**When to use**:
- When the current architecture is the blocker — the application cannot independently scale, independently deploy, or independently fail without taking other systems down.
- When technical debt in the architecture is the primary driver of operational cost and change velocity degradation.
- When the application must gain capabilities (real-time streaming, cloud-native scaling) that are structurally incompatible with the current architecture.

**When NOT to use**:
- As the scope of a time-boxed project without clear boundary definition. Re-architect projects are the most susceptible to "while we're in here" scope expansion.
- When the team does not have the architectural expertise to define the new design before starting execution. Discovering the new architecture mid-project is a reliable path to a failed program.

**Rollback ability**: Low. The application is fundamentally changed. Rollback to the original architecture requires reverting an architectural transformation, which may not be feasible if consumers have already adapted to the new patterns.

**T-Mobile/middleware example**: Extracting the order management domain from a retail monolith into an independent microservice with its own PostgreSQL database. Order events are published to Kafka for downstream consumers (fulfillment, billing, inventory) that previously called the monolith's internal functions directly. Each downstream consumer is updated to consume from Kafka rather than making synchronous calls into the monolith.

**Key risks**: Scope creep. The project boundary must be defined in terms of specific capability domains, with a hard constraint that "while we're in here" changes require a separate project approval. Teams that do not enforce this constraint discover that a 6-month project has become a 3-year program when they resurface.

### Pattern 4: Strangler Fig

**What it is**: Incrementally replacing a legacy system by building the replacement alongside it, routing traffic to new components as they're ready and proven, until the legacy system receives no traffic and can be decommissioned.

Named after the strangler fig tree (Ficus aurea), which germinates in the upper canopy of a host tree, sends roots down to the ground, and gradually wraps around the host tree until the host is fully enclosed and eventually replaced. The fig tree is the new system; the host tree is the legacy system.

**Why this is the preferred pattern for most large legacy modernizations**: It addresses the three primary failure modes of legacy transformation programs. First, it eliminates big-bang risk — at any point in the process, you can stop routing additional traffic to the new system and route everything back to legacy. The rollback path is always available until you complete the final decommission step. Second, it delivers value incrementally — each slice of functionality that moves to the new system is independently valuable and can be operated and measured before the next slice begins. Third, it builds team confidence progressively — the team proves the new system at low traffic before committing high-stakes traffic to it.

**Mechanics**:
1. Identify a decomposable slice of legacy functionality — a domain, a transaction type, a consumer segment, or a traffic category that can be handled independently.
2. Build the replacement for that slice. The replacement does not need to cover all functionality — only the slice.
3. Route a subset of traffic for that slice to the new system. The routing mechanism (API gateway, service mesh, DNS-based, feature flag) is a project-level decision based on the traffic type and infrastructure available.
4. Operate the new system for that slice, measure against agreed criteria, and prove it at the traffic level you've committed.
5. Route more traffic — either increase the percentage for the same slice or move to the next slice.
6. Repeat until the legacy system receives zero traffic.
7. Decommission the legacy system.

**T-Mobile/middleware example**: Migrating customer account management from a legacy CRM to a new microservice. Start with read traffic (profile lookups — lowest risk, easily compared against legacy output). Route 5% of read traffic to the new service, compare responses, prove correctness. Scale to 100% of reads. Then introduce write traffic starting with low-risk writes (address updates, preference changes). Scale to 100% of writes. Then move complex transactions (billing changes, plan modifications). When all traffic categories are fully on the new service, decommission the legacy CRM.

**Rollback ability**: High throughout execution, declining to zero at decommission. Until decommission, the legacy system remains available as a rollback target.

**Key risks**: The parallel run cost during execution. While both systems coexist, you are operating two systems. If the migration timeline extends (it usually does), parallel run cost extends with it. Establish a decommission date before you start and treat it as a governance commitment, not an estimate.

### Pattern 5: Strangler Vine

**What it is**: A variant of Strangler Fig where the new system wraps the legacy system at the API layer, presenting a clean interface to consumers immediately, while routing to the legacy system for capabilities that have not yet been replaced.

The vine grows from the outside in. Consumers see the new API from day one. Behind that API, the new system proxies to legacy for capabilities it hasn't yet replaced, and serves from its own implementation for capabilities it has replaced. The legacy system is strangled from behind the API facade.

**Why it differs from Strangler Fig**: In pure Strangler Fig, consumers may interact with both old and new systems during the transition, or the routing logic lives at the infrastructure level. In Strangler Vine, the API facade provides a clean, stable interface to consumers regardless of which system serves the response. Consumers adapt once (to the new API) and never need to change again as capabilities migrate behind the facade.

**When to use**:
- When you need to present a clean API contract to consumers immediately — either for product reasons (external partners need a stable API) or strategic reasons (the organization has committed to an API-first program and the current API surface is not acceptable).
- When the capability replacement timeline is long (18–36 months) and you cannot ask consumers to maintain compatibility with both old and new interfaces for that duration.
- When the legacy system has no clean API boundary — it's called directly via proprietary protocols — and you want to introduce an API layer as the first step.

**T-Mobile/middleware example**: Building a new API gateway facade over a TIBCO ESB integration layer. External consumers (mobile app, partner APIs, customer service tooling) are migrated to call the new API gateway. The API gateway routes to the TIBCO ESB for integration flows that have not yet been replaced, and routes directly to new microservices for flows that have been rebuilt. The TIBCO ESB is gradually replaced behind the facade, invisible to consumers. Each retired TIBCO flow reduces the ESB's traffic; the ESB is decommissioned when its traffic reaches zero.

**Rollback ability**: High at the capability level (repoint a specific capability's routing back to legacy); moderate at the program level (unwinding the facade itself is a significant consumer migration).

**Key risks**: The facade can become a new form of technical debt if it is not architected cleanly. A facade that accumulates business logic, transformation code, and routing complexity becomes the new legacy system you will eventually need to replace.

### Pattern 6: Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL)

**What it is**: A translation layer between a legacy system and a replacement system that prevents the new system's domain model from being corrupted by the legacy system's data structures, terminology, naming conventions, and constraints.

The ACL is not a pattern that replaces or migrates — it is a defensive pattern that is applied alongside whatever migration pattern you choose. It is almost always required when the legacy system has a bad data model, and legacy systems almost always have bad data models.

**Why legacy data models corrupt new systems**: Legacy applications were built incrementally over years or decades, often before domain-driven design, API-first thinking, or even consistent naming conventions were organizational standards. The data model reflects the decisions of people who are no longer at the organization, solving problems that may no longer exist, under constraints that have since changed. Common patterns:
- A "customer" in the legacy system is actually an account record — the data model conflates person and account. The new system needs to distinguish them.
- A "product" in the legacy billing system is a billing construct (a charge code with rules) rather than what the customer understands as a product. The new product catalog needs a different model.
- Phone numbers are stored in five different formats across four tables, depending on when the record was created and which team added the column.
- Fields that should be null are stored as the string "NULL" or the integer -1 because the original database schema did not allow nulls.

If the new service consumes legacy data structures directly — without translation — it inherits these problems. The "account is a customer" model leaks into the new service's API. The billing product model leaks into the new product domain. The new system has cleaner code but the same conceptual problems.

**The ACL translates**:
- Legacy field `ACCT_TYP_CD = 'P'` → new model `AccountType.PERSONAL`
- Legacy phone number format `(503) 555-1234` → canonical E.164 format `+15035551234`
- Legacy null representation `VOID_DT = '9999-12-31'` → new model `expiryDate: null`
- Legacy product code `PROD_CD = 'UWBP-4G-STD'` → new model `Product { id: "unlimited-basic", tier: STANDARD, technology: "4G" }`

**When an ACL is not optional**: The ACL is not optional when the legacy data model would corrupt the new system's domain. That is most legacy systems. The question is not "do we need an ACL?" but "where does the ACL live?" Options: as a separate service (cleanest, most independently evolvable), as a module within the new service's integration layer (simpler, harder to maintain boundary discipline over time), or as a component within the Strangler Vine facade.

**Key risks**: ACL scope expansion. The ACL should translate, not transform business logic. When teams start putting business rules in the ACL ("if the legacy system returns X, apply rule Y"), the ACL becomes a business logic layer and loses its architectural clarity. Translation only.

### Pattern 7: Big Bang Migration

**What it is**: Replacing an entire system in a single cutover event. All traffic moves from the old system to the new system on a defined date, after which the old system is decommissioned.

**Why it is the highest-risk pattern**: Big Bang violates the fundamental principle of incremental risk reduction. The new system is unproven at production scale before the cutover — you are discovering production failure modes during the cutover window, not before it. The rollback path is expensive: rolling back a Big Bang migration requires reversing a full-system cutover, which may include data migrations, consumer reconfiguration, and DNS or routing changes that take longer to reverse than the allowed rollback window. If the new system has a critical defect discovered 4 hours into the cutover, you may not be able to roll back in time to meet your RTO commitments.

**When it is sometimes necessary**:

- **Regulatory systems that cannot legally run in parallel**: Some regulatory frameworks require that there is a single system of record for a given dataset at any point in time. Running two billing systems simultaneously with split traffic may violate financial reporting requirements. Running two identity management systems simultaneously may violate data residency or access control requirements. In these cases, parallel operation is not compliant and Big Bang is the only legal option.
- **Systems with no API boundary that makes traffic splitting impossible**: If the legacy system receives traffic through a proprietary protocol with no proxy capability, and the new system uses a different protocol, there may be no mechanism to split traffic incrementally. The cutover must be complete because the routing mechanism cannot be partial.
- **Data model changes so fundamental that dual-write is not feasible**: If the old system and new system store the same data in structures so different that maintaining consistency through dual-write would require a transformation engine more complex than either system, the cost and risk of dual-write may exceed the cost and risk of a well-planned Big Bang.

**Requirements when Big Bang is unavoidable**:
- Minimum 12-month planning horizon from decision to cutover date.
- At least three full dress rehearsal cutovers in pre-production, each run with production-representative data volumes and traffic patterns.
- Documented rollback procedure, with rollback tested end-to-end in pre-production. If you haven't tested the rollback, you don't have a rollback — you have a plan.
- Executive sign-off on the cutover plan, including explicit acceptance of the rollback risk.
- War room staffing for a minimum of 72 hours post-cutover, with defined escalation paths and decision authority clearly assigned before the cutover begins.

**Rollback ability**: Low to none post-cutover start, depending on how far the cutover has progressed when a decision to roll back is made.

**Key risks**: Everything. Undiscovered defects in the new system that only manifest at production scale. Data migration errors discovered post-cutover. Consumer systems that were certified compatible but exhibit unexpected behavior when live traffic flows. Cascading failures in dependent systems that weren't fully covered by integration testing.

### Pattern 8: Parallel Run

**What it is**: Operating both the legacy system and the replacement system simultaneously, routing all traffic to both, and comparing the outputs. Production decisions are made based on the legacy system's output until the parallel run exit criteria are met — the new system's output is used for validation, not for production decisions.

**Why it provides maximum confidence**: The new system is proven at full production load, with real production data, over a sustained period. You are not extrapolating from pre-production test results to production behavior — you are measuring production behavior directly, with no production impact if the new system gets the wrong answer.

**Operational cost**: Parallel run is the most expensive operational posture in the taxonomy. Double the infrastructure. Double the operational monitoring. Engineering effort to maintain the comparison framework and investigate every divergence. This cost is appropriate for systems where correctness is the non-negotiable requirement.

**When to use**: Financial systems, billing, settlement, payment processing — any system where an incorrect answer from the new system has a real-world consequence (a customer was charged the wrong amount, a trade was settled at the wrong price) that cannot be undone by a simple rollback. "Our billing system processes $2B in charges per year. I need proof — not confidence, proof — that the new system gives the same answer before I stop running the old one."

**Exit criteria from parallel run**: Not time-based alone. Time-based exit criteria ("we'll exit after 30 days") do not account for divergences discovered during the run. Exit criteria must be output-based:
- 30 consecutive days of 100% output match across all transaction types, OR
- All divergences have been investigated and have a documented explanation accepted by the business and risk owners. "The new system rounds at a different decimal place than the legacy system, and the business has accepted this as equivalent for the purposes of this run" is an acceptable explanation if properly documented and signed off.

Undocumented, uninvestigated divergences are never acceptable exit criteria for a financial system.

**Rollback ability**: Complete, at any point during the parallel run. The legacy system remains the production decision system until exit criteria are met.

**Key risks**: Discovery of divergences that are expensive to resolve — cases where the legacy system's behavior is technically incorrect (e.g., a rounding error that has been applied for 10 years, which means customer ledgers depend on the incorrect behavior) and the "correct" behavior change cannot be accepted without business process changes. These are program-stopping discoveries that require executive decision, not engineering decisions.

### Pattern 9: Database Migration Patterns

Database migration is the highest-risk component of most application modernizations because data is stateful, data migrations fail in ways that are difficult to roll back, and data quality issues discovered post-migration often cannot be resolved without manual remediation at scale.

**Expand-Contract**

The safest schema change pattern for applications that cannot tolerate downtime. Executed in two phases:
- *Expand phase*: Add new columns or tables without removing or renaming existing ones. The old schema and new schema coexist. The application is updated to write to both old and new columns simultaneously.
- *Contract phase*: After all data has been migrated to the new structure and the application has been updated to read from the new structure, remove the old columns.

This pattern allows schema changes to be deployed without downtime and with clean rollback capability at any step. It is the standard pattern for any schema migration on a production database that cannot be taken offline.

**Dual-Write**

The application writes state changes to both old and new datastores simultaneously during the migration period. Reads are served from the old datastore (authoritative) until the new datastore is validated. At validation, the read source is switched to the new datastore. Writes to the old datastore are then stopped.

Critical requirement: the old datastore must remain authoritative for reads until validation is complete. Running dual-write with the new datastore as the read source before validation is a data integrity risk — you are using unvalidated data for production decisions.

**Event Sourcing Migration**

Capture all state changes as events (in a Kafka topic or equivalent event log) and replay those events into the new system to build equivalent state. Useful when migrating from a traditional relational model to an event-sourced architecture, or when the new system needs to backfill historical state that does not exist in its native format.

The validation challenge: replayed event state in the new system must match current state in the legacy system. Discrepancies require root cause investigation — either a replay bug, a missing event, or a state mutation in the legacy system that was not captured as an event.

**CQRS Split**

Separate the write (command) path from the read (query) path during migration. Migrate read queries to the new system first (lower risk — reads cannot corrupt state), prove correctness of read results against the legacy authoritative source, then migrate writes. The read migration gives you a validation period of real production read load on the new system before any write risk is introduced.

## 3. Pattern Selection Framework

### Decision Matrix

| Primary Driver | Downtime Tolerance | Data Complexity | Regulatory Constraint | Recommended Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost (infrastructure only) | Any | Low | No | Lift and Shift |
| Cost (infrastructure + operations) | Low | Low | No | Re-platform |
| Cost + Velocity + Capability | Low | High | No | Strangler Fig (preferred) |
| Clean API surface needed immediately | Low | High | No | Strangler Vine |
| Correctness-critical replacement | None | High | No | Parallel Run + Strangler Fig |
| Regulatory prevents parallel | None | High | Yes | Big Bang (with full planning rigor) |
| Legacy data model corruption risk | Any | High | Any | Add ACL to whichever pattern applies |
| Schema change on live system | None | Any | Any | Expand-Contract |
| State migration to new architecture | Low | High | No | Dual-Write or Event Sourcing Migration |

### Simplified Decision Tree

```
Start here: What is your primary constraint?

├── < 3 months to complete
│     └── Lift and Shift — buy time; address architecture in a subsequent phase
│
├── Need clean API surface for consumers immediately
│     └── Strangler Vine — facade first, capability migration behind it
│
├── Can decompose by capability and prove each slice
│     └── Strangler Fig — preferred pattern; incremental risk, incremental value
│
├── Correctness must be proven before committing (financial, billing, settlement)
│     └── Parallel Run — operate both, compare output, exit on proven equivalence
│
├── Regulatory or architectural constraint prevents parallel operation
│     └── Big Bang — required; execute with full planning rigor and tested rollback
│
└── Legacy data model would corrupt the new system
      └── Add Anti-Corruption Layer to whichever pattern you selected above
            (ACL is not an alternative — it is a supplement)
```

The decision tree is a starting point, not a final answer. Real transformations often combine patterns: Strangler Vine at the API layer with Strangler Fig behind it for capability migration, with an ACL at the integration boundary and Expand-Contract for the database schema changes. The taxonomy gives you the vocabulary; the specific combination requires analysis of the application's actual constraints.

## 4. Transformation Anti-Patterns

These are recurring failure patterns in transformation programs. They are not theoretical — every one of these has produced a failed or severely delayed transformation program in organizations with capable engineering teams.

### "Big Bang because it's simpler to plan"

Big Bang is simpler to plan because it has a clean, simple structure: one project, one cutover date, one team. The planning work is bounded and the governance is straightforward. Incremental migration (Strangler Fig) requires coordinating routing changes, partial traffic splits, consumer migration sequences, and multiple exit criteria over an extended period. Planning it is genuinely harder.

This is the wrong trade-off. Big Bang is simpler to plan and catastrophic to execute. Strangler Fig is harder to plan and dramatically safer to execute. The cost of a failed Big Bang cutover — an emergency rollback from a P1 incident during a full-system cutover, potentially followed by an extended parallel period while root cause is addressed — dwarfs the cost of the additional planning work required for incremental migration.

The anti-pattern is selecting a high-risk execution pattern because the planning is easier. Risk lives in execution, not in planning.

### "We'll modernize while adding features"

A team proposes to modernize an application while simultaneously delivering the product roadmap on it. The logic is: we're touching the code anyway, so we'll refactor as we go. We don't need a separate modernization track.

This produces scope explosion with certainty. Modernization work and feature work have fundamentally different objectives, timelines, and risk profiles. Modernization work requires architectural decision-making that interrupts feature delivery. Feature work imposes delivery pressure that shortcuts architectural discipline. The result is a codebase that has been partially modernized and partially extended with new features, in a state that is neither clean legacy nor clean modern architecture. It is harder to operate than the original, has more defects than either a clean legacy or clean modern system, and has neither delivered the modernization nor the feature roadmap on schedule.

Modernization programs are separate from product delivery programs. They share a team's capacity and require explicit trade-off decisions at the portfolio level. "We will reduce feature delivery rate by 30% for 6 months to complete the modernization, and then feature delivery will be faster because we've removed the architectural constraints" is a coherent business case. "We'll do both simultaneously without slowing down" is not — it is schedule optimism that produces architectural chaos.

### "The new system is ready when code is complete"

Code completion is not readiness. A system is ready for production traffic when it has been proven at production load, with production data patterns, for a sustained period under observation. Code complete means the engineers have finished writing it. Those are not the same thing.

The consequences of conflating them: the new system goes live with defects that only manifest at production scale or with production data diversity. The defects are discovered during or immediately after cutover, in a state where rollback is expensive or impossible. The root cause is not engineering failure — the engineers built what was specified. The root cause is a governance definition of "ready" that did not include production-proven as a criterion.

Exit criteria for production readiness must be specified before the project begins and must include: sustained performance testing at production load profile, chaos engineering or fault injection testing where applicable, security review and penetration test completed, SLO defined and measured in pre-production, runbook written and reviewed, on-call training completed, parallel run or canary period completed with defined success criteria. Code complete is not on that list because it is a prerequisite to starting readiness validation, not a measure of it.

### "We don't need the ACL — we'll just be disciplined"

Teams consistently underestimate how quickly domain model corruption propagates. The decision to consume legacy data structures directly in the new service is made early in the project, when the team is confident in their ability to maintain conceptual boundaries through coding discipline. Six months later, the legacy field names and data structures are embedded throughout the new service's code, tests, API contracts, and documentation. The team adapted to the legacy model without noticing, because each individual accommodation seemed small.

By the time the corruption is recognized, removing it requires a refactoring effort that touches everything in the new service. The cost of adding the ACL after the fact is higher than the cost of building it at the start. The discipline argument fails because discipline is not a scalable substitute for structural enforcement. Teams are never as disciplined as they think they will be, especially under delivery pressure. The ACL is the structural enforcement that makes discipline unnecessary because the translation boundary is code, not intent.
