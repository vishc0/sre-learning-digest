# Chapter 3: Legacy System Management

## 1. What Makes a System "Legacy"

Age is not the criterion. A 20-year-old system with a maintained codebase, current runtime, and active dependency updates is not legacy — it is mature. A 3-year-old system built on an abandoned framework with no documentation and no automated tests is already legacy. The distinction matters because "legacy" is often used as a synonym for "old," which leads to wrong prioritization: the 20-year-old billing system that is operationally rock-solid gets modernization budget while a 4-year-old microservice nobody understands sits paging at 2am.

The four true criteria for legacy classification:

**Technical currency**: how far behind current standards has this system drifted — runtime EOL, abandoned frameworks, deprecated APIs, unpatched dependency vulnerabilities. A Java 8 application running on Tomcat 7 with Spring 3.x is technically currency-impaired regardless of its age or stability.

**Knowledge concentration**: how few people understand the system well enough to safely change or recover it. A bus factor of 2 or below is a legacy risk signal. When the two people who understand the system leave or are unavailable during an incident, the organization has lost its ability to respond safely.

**Change risk**: what is the blast radius of every change. Systems without automated tests, without deployment pipelines, and requiring manual regression cycles carry compounding change risk — every change is a potential incident, which means changes are delayed, which means the system falls further behind, which increases the blast radius of the next change.

**Operational cost**: disproportionate support burden measured in SRE on-call time, MTTR, toil, and manual intervention for routine operations. A system that requires a human to restart a process every Monday morning because "that's just how it is" is imposing legacy tax on your team.

**Classification rule**: a system is legacy when it exhibits 3 or more of these 4 criteria. A system meeting only 1 or 2 criteria is flagged for monitoring but not yet classified as legacy — the 20-year-old mainframe that meets only "technical currency" is mature with a known risk, not legacy.

## 2. Legacy Risk Classification

Four tiers, each with a specific operational meaning and a specific response:

| Classification | SLO Status | Change Risk | Knowledge State | Incident Rate | Modernization Priority | Immediate Action |
|----------------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| L1 — Operationally Stable | Met | Moderate | Adequate, thinning | Below SLO threshold | Queue for next planning cycle | Maintenance only; document knowledge gaps |
| L2 — Knowledge-Concentrated | Met | High | Concentrated in 1–2 people | Below SLO threshold | Elevated — business continuity risk | Mandatory knowledge transfer program |
| L3 — Unstable | Breached or at risk | Very high | Thin | Exceeding SLO threshold | Accelerated | Protective wrapping; change freeze |
| L4 — Critical and Fragile | Breached | Extremely high — no safe change path | Near-zero organizational knowledge | Chronic P1/P2 | Emergency | Dedicated modernization pod; executive sponsorship |

**L1 — Operationally Stable Legacy**: the system runs reliably and the SLO is met. Technology currency is declining — the runtime may be approaching EOL, the framework is no longer actively maintained — but none of this is causing incidents today. Team knowledge is adequate but thinning: the original developers are gone, current team members can operate it but are not confident in deep changes. This is the system that makes every architect nervous but hasn't yet caused pain. Investment: maintenance budget only. No new features, no architectural changes. Queue for modernization in the next planning cycle.

**L2 — Knowledge-Concentrated Legacy**: stable but entirely dependent on 1–2 people who hold the institutional knowledge. The SLO is met. Change risk is high because nobody else can safely modify the system. If those people are unavailable — vacation, illness, resignation — the team cannot safely respond to incidents. This is an existential business continuity risk disguised as a stable system. Investment: mandatory knowledge transfer program before any other modernization activity. Architecture capture sessions, recorded, stored, accessible. Runbook completeness audit. Shadow incident rotations. Modernization priority is elevated because the risk is not visible in metrics — until it catastrophically materializes.

**L3 — Unstable Legacy**: this is the system that pages at 2am regularly and takes 4 hours to resolve because nobody deeply understands it anymore. The SLO is breached or chronically at risk. Change risk is very high — any change risks making things worse. SRE time is disproportionately consumed by this system. Investment: protective wrapping immediately (API facade or Anti-Corruption Layer in front of the system), change freeze for everything except incident-driven remediation, and accelerated modernization priority. Every week this system stays in L3 is a week of avoidable incidents.

**L4 — Critical and Fragile**: the nightmare scenario. A Tier-1 or Tier-2 application (one that the business cannot operate without) that is simultaneously L3 (unstable, high incident rate) and has no safe modernization path because it is too complex to understand and too risky to change. The system is fragile enough that attempting to replace it is dangerous, but leaving it running is also dangerous. Investment: emergency response. A dedicated modernization pod with executive sponsorship, removed from normal portfolio sequencing. This cannot wait for the next planning cycle.

## 3. Managing L3/L4 Legacy: Immediate Actions

**Change freeze policy**: no changes to L3/L4 systems except incident-driven remediation, approved by the Director or VP. The reasoning: every unnecessary change is a new incident risk in a system that is already generating too many incidents. Engineers will argue that they need to make changes to fix the instability — some of those changes are valid, but they go through a higher approval gate so the risk is conscious and deliberate, not opportunistic.

**Protective wrapping (API facade / Anti-Corruption Layer)**: place a modern API gateway or thin facade service in front of the legacy system so that downstream consumers depend on the facade's interface, not on the legacy internals. This decouples consumers from the system's implementation, which gives you the freedom to replace the internals without breaking every consumer simultaneously. This step must happen before modernization begins, not during. If you start replacing internals before the facade is in place, every consumer must coordinate with every replacement milestone.

```
Before wrapping:
[Consumer A] → directly → [Legacy System]
[Consumer B] → directly → [Legacy System]
[Consumer C] → directly → [Legacy System]

After wrapping:
[Consumer A] → [API Facade] → [Legacy System]
[Consumer B] → [API Facade] → [Legacy System]
[Consumer C] → [API Facade] → [Legacy System]
                   ↓ (during replacement)
              [New System] (receives traffic as it is migrated)
```

**Incident priority uplift**: L4 legacy incidents are automatically treated as P1 regardless of the initial reported blast radius. The reason: fragile systems have unpredictable failure modes — a P2-looking incident on a fragile L4 system can cascade into a P1 within 20 minutes. Do not apply normal triage logic. Assume worst case, staff accordingly, and step down the priority once you have confirmed containment.

**Accelerated modernization priority**: L4 systems jump the portfolio modernization queue. Normal portfolio sequencing does not apply when a critical system is failing regularly. Every week of delay in starting the L4 modernization is a week of additional P1 risk.

**War gaming**: quarterly tabletop exercise — "what happens if this system goes down and does not recover for 72 hours?" This forces dependency mapping, contingency planning, and organizational awareness before the event forces it. The output of a war game is not a presentation — it is an updated dependency map, a confirmed fallback procedure, and an assigned owner for each fallback step.

## 4. The Legacy Tax

The legacy tax is the measurable operational overhead that legacy systems impose on the organization. Quantifying it is the VP-level responsibility because it is the business case for modernization investment. Without the numbers, modernization competes against feature work with no objective basis for prioritization.

**SRE on-call time consumed by legacy systems**: measure the percentage of total SRE on-call hours (pages responded to, bridge time, investigation time, manual remediation time) attributable to legacy systems. Benchmark: healthy organizations keep this below 30%. Legacy-heavy organizations routinely exceed 60%. If your SRE team spends 60% of on-call time on 15% of the application portfolio, that concentration is the finding.

**P1/P2 incident concentration**: what percentage of P1 and P2 incidents are traceable to legacy systems? The comparison: if legacy systems represent 20% of the portfolio but cause 70% of P1 incidents, the concentration risk is extreme and visible. This metric also drives the business case: "our legacy systems cause $X in incident cost per year."

**Feature delivery velocity ratio**: how long does it take to add a comparable feature to a legacy system vs. an equivalent modern service? Legacy feature delivery is typically 5–10x longer due to: manual regression testing cycles, coordination overhead, deployment windows, and change review escalations. This is a velocity tax on the entire engineering organization.

**Cost per supported transaction**: legacy infrastructure is typically 3–5x more expensive per transaction than equivalent modern services on current cloud infrastructure. This includes: older instance types with lower compute efficiency, licensing costs for commercial middleware, and the SRE labor cost embedded in higher MTTR.

**The business case format**: present the legacy tax as a return on investment, not as a technical problem.

> "Our 15 legacy systems represent 8% of the application portfolio. They consume 45% of SRE on-call time, cause 62% of P1/P2 incidents, and cost $2.3M annually in direct infrastructure and labor overhead above what equivalent modern services would cost. Modernizing them over 3 years at an investment of $4M will return approximately 35% of current SRE capacity to productive work and reduce annual incident costs by $900K."

That framing gets budget. "Our systems are old and we need to modernize" does not.

## 5. TIBCO/MuleSoft-Specific Legacy Management

The Enterprise Service Bus is the most common legacy system in large telecom, retail, and banking organizations. TIBCO BusinessWorks, MuleSoft 3.x, IBM MQ-based integration layers, and similar ESBs were the integration backbone of the 2000s–2010s. They are now classic L2/L3 legacy in most enterprises — stable enough to not demand emergency intervention, but too knowledge-concentrated and too far behind technical currency to safely ignore.

**The ESB knows everything**: in most large organizations, the ESB has 200+ integration flows touching 50+ systems. It is not a peripheral system — it is connective tissue. You cannot shut it off or replace it in a single project. The Strangler Vine pattern is the correct approach: route new consumers through a modern API gateway, gradually migrate flows behind the gateway, and reduce the ESB's scope incrementally until it can be decommissioned.

**ESB knowledge concentration**: TIBCO BusinessWorks and MuleSoft developers are a small, aging skillset. If your organization has 2 TIBCO administrators who understand the full flow library, you have a single-point-of-failure. This is an L2 classification by definition. Knowledge preservation is the first action, before any modernization work begins.

**The wrapping approach — Strangler Vine in practice**:

```
Step 1: Deploy Kong/Apigee/AWS API Gateway in front of the ESB
[Consumer] → [API Gateway] → [TIBCO ESB] → [Backend Systems]

Step 2: New consumers use the API Gateway, which proxies to ESB
[New Consumer] → [API Gateway] → [TIBCO ESB] → [Backend]
[Legacy Consumer] → [API Gateway] → [TIBCO ESB] → [Backend]

Step 3: Replace ESB flows one group at a time, behind the gateway
[Consumer] → [API Gateway] → [New Integration Service] → [Backend]
                          → [TIBCO ESB remaining flows] → [Backend]

Step 4: Decommission TIBCO ESB when zero flows remain
[Consumer] → [API Gateway] → [Modern Integration Services] → [Backend]
```

**EOL dates are hard deadlines**: TIBCO BusinessWorks 5.x reached end of life. MuleSoft 3.x is end of life. Running EOL software in a production environment is a compliance and security risk, not just a technical currency issue. Know the EOL date for your specific ESB version and treat it as a hard cutoff, not a guideline.

**RabbitMQ legacy considerations**: older RabbitMQ deployments (pre-3.8) lack quorum queues, streams, and current management capabilities. Upgrading RabbitMQ in place is feasible but requires careful queue migration planning. Classic queues cannot be directly migrated to quorum queues — consumer coordination is required. The migration sequence: add new quorum queue alongside classic queue, migrate producers to write to both, migrate consumers to read from quorum queue, drain and delete classic queue. This is a rolling migration, not a cutover.

## 6. Legacy Knowledge Preservation

Knowledge preservation is a prerequisite to any modernization. A team that attempts to replace a system they do not understand will build the replacement wrong and migrate the bugs along with the functionality.

**Requirements before a senior team member responsible for a legacy system can transfer out of the team**:

1. Architecture capture session (recorded, stored in the team's knowledge base, accessible to all engineers)
2. Runbook completeness audit: every known failure mode has a runbook. The audit is conducted by running the top 10 incident types against the available runbooks and identifying gaps.
3. Dependency map documented: every upstream dependency (systems this application calls), every downstream dependency (systems that call this application), every data dependency (databases owned, databases shared), every infrastructure dependency (which RabbitMQ exchange, which TIBCO flow, which Kafka topic).
4. Bus factor audit: can two engineers who were not the legacy expert independently resolve the top 5 incident types without escalating to the expert? If no, knowledge transfer is incomplete.

**Bus factor mitigation program**:

- **Shadow incident rotations**: engineers shadow incidents alongside the legacy expert. They observe the diagnosis, ask questions during the post-incident review, and participate in runbook updates. After three shadow rotations, they attempt to lead an incident with the expert available but silent.
- **Documentation sprints**: quarterly 2-day sprints dedicated exclusively to documenting the legacy system. Output: updated architecture diagram, new or revised runbooks, dependency map updates.
- **Controlled failure injection**: deliberately inject known failure modes in a lower environment to build team experience. The goal is for the team to encounter failure modes for the first time in a controlled environment, not during a production incident. For RabbitMQ: inject consumer group failure, queue depth exhaustion, dead-letter queue overflow. For TIBCO: inject adapter connectivity failure, flow timeout, schema validation failure.

## 7. Legacy Decommission Readiness Criteria

A legacy system is ready for decommission when all of the following conditions are confirmed — not estimated, not assumed, confirmed:

- **Replacement proven stable**: 90 days of SLO compliance in production under real traffic. Not in a test environment. Not under synthetic load. Under production traffic patterns including peak load periods.
- **Traffic fully migrated**: 100% of production traffic confirmed on the new system via service mesh telemetry and application logs. Not "we think all consumers have migrated" — confirmed by telemetry.
- **No confirmed active consumers**: API gateway confirms zero calls to legacy endpoints for 30 consecutive days. Any remaining traffic triggers a new consumer discovery cycle before decommission proceeds.
- **Data archived or migrated**: all operational data either migrated to the new system with reconciliation confirmed, or archived to cold storage per the retention policy. No data deletion without regulatory sign-off.
- **Monitoring cleanup documented**: all legacy-specific dashboards, alerts, on-call routing rules, and Splunk saved searches identified and scheduled for retirement. Leaving orphaned alerts generates noise that degrades on-call signal quality over time.
- **Regulatory sign-off**: legal and compliance confirm no regulatory hold on the application or its data that requires the system to remain operational or accessible.
- **VP sign-off**: formal written decommission approval for Tier 1/2 systems. This is not bureaucracy — it creates the audit trail that compliance, legal, and audit will ask for.
