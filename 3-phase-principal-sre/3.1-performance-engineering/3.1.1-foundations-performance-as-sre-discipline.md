# Chapter 1: Foundations — Performance as an SRE Discipline

## The Three Disciplines: Performance Engineering, Testing, and Monitoring

These three are frequently conflated. They answer different questions, apply at different points in the lifecycle, and fail in different ways when absent. Conflating them is why organizations run load tests without performance engineering programs and wonder why the same incidents recur every quarter.

| Dimension | Performance Engineering | Performance Testing | Performance Monitoring |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Question answered** | What capacity should this system have, and does it meet that requirement? | Does the system meet its capacity requirements right now, under controlled conditions? | Is the system performing within acceptable bounds right now in production? |
| **When applied** | Design → Development → Release → Production, continuously | Pre-launch, post-significant-change, quarterly validation | 24/7 in production |
| **Primary artifact** | Performance SLOs, capacity models, PE program governance | Load test report, pass/fail result against baseline | Dashboards, alerts, SLO burn rate reports |
| **Owner** | PE team / Principal SRE | QE / PE / SRE depending on org structure | SRE / Ops |
| **Tooling** | APM + load test infrastructure + baseline repository + CI/CD gates | JMeter, Gatling, k6, Locust + APM during test | Datadog, Dynatrace, Splunk ITSI, New Relic, Prometheus/Grafana |
| **Failure to do it** | Repeated performance incidents with no learning loop; no accountability when SLOs are missed | Surprise capacity limits discovered in production; reactive tuning post-launch | No early warning before user impact; incidents discovered by users, not engineers |
| **Produces** | Engineering standards, SLO contracts, capacity projections, governance cadence | A point-in-time measurement with pass/fail verdict | Continuous signal of current system health |

The relationship between the three: Performance Engineering defines what "good" means (the SLO). Performance Testing validates that the system achieves "good" before it reaches users. Performance Monitoring detects when "good" degrades in production. An organization that does testing without engineering has no definition of "good." An organization that does monitoring without testing finds out what "good" was only after production fails.

## Why Performance Matters at the VP Level

Engineering teams instinctively measure performance in latency and throughput. Executive teams measure it in revenue and risk. The translation between these two frames is the VP's job. The data exists to make this translation concrete — these are not hypotheticals.

**Amazon**: A 100ms increase in page load time reduced revenue by 1% (Bezos shareholder letter, 2006, later confirmed in internal studies). At Amazon's scale that translates to approximately $1.8B annually at current revenue. The mechanism: slower pages reduce conversion rate and session depth.

**Google**: A 500ms increase in search latency reduced traffic by 20% (Marissa Mayer, Google I/O 2006). The mechanism: users abandon slow searches and return to the search box or navigate away. Slower results produce fewer subsequent queries — compounding revenue impact.

**Walmart**: A 1-second improvement in page load time increased conversion rate by 2% (Walmart Labs research, 2012). This was measured on a commerce platform at scale, not in a lab. The mechanism: faster pages reduce abandonment at each step of the purchase funnel.

**T-Mobile context**: At 25M daily transactions across digital channels, a 200ms latency increase in high-value flows (account management, upgrade checkout, device activation) represents approximately 50,000 degraded user experiences per day at p95 impact. At a conservative 2% conversion impact on degraded sessions, that is approximately 1,000 lost conversions daily. At an average transaction value of $50–$200 (device activation, plan upgrade), a sustained 200ms latency regression in production represents $50,000–$200,000 in daily revenue exposure — before accounting for churn signal from degraded NPS.

> **VP Governance Callout**: The performance contract is a revenue commitment. When an engineering team ships a service without defined performance SLOs, they are making an implicit promise they cannot measure. The system will either perform or it will not — but without a contract, there is no accountability when it doesn't. The VP's job is to make that implicit promise explicit, funded, and governed. "We don't know what our performance SLO is" is equivalent to "we don't know what we promised our customers."

## The Performance Contract

A performance contract is the formal statement of what a service promises regarding capacity and latency, under what conditions, and what happens when it fails to deliver. It is distinct from an SLA (which is a legal/commercial agreement) — a performance contract is an internal engineering commitment with defined consequences.

**Components of a performance contract:**

**Latency SLO**: The service will process 95% of requests within 200ms under sustained load of 500 TPS. This is not a best-effort target — it is a threshold. Breaching it means the service is in SLO violation.

**Throughput capacity**: The service will sustain 2,000 TPS for a minimum of 60 minutes without throughput degradation exceeding 5% or latency exceeding the p95 SLO target. This defines the capacity ceiling that must be validated before every major release.

**Error rate ceiling**: Error rate will remain below 0.1% at normal operating load (≤ 1x peak projection). Error rate will remain below 0.5% at 2x peak load. Above 2x peak load, the behavior contract shifts: the service must degrade gracefully (circuit break, queue backpressure, or reject with 503) rather than fail uncontrolled.

**Degradation behavior contract**: Beyond 2x peak load, the service will apply one of: circuit breaking (reject new requests immediately with a defined fallback), queue-based backpressure (accept requests but signal slow consumers to reduce submission rate), or graceful rejection (return HTTP 503 with Retry-After header). The contract specifies which mechanism. A service that crashes under 2.5x peak has violated its degradation behavior contract regardless of whether its latency SLO was met.

**Recovery time**: After a traffic spike subsides below 1.5x peak, the service will return to baseline latency (within 10% of p95 baseline) within 5 minutes. This validates that the system releases resources (threads, connections, heap) and does not require restart to recover.

**The performance debt concept**: The gap between the performance contract and actual measured performance in production is performance debt. An organization that ships services without contracts has infinite performance debt — they simply cannot see it. An organization with contracts and no baseline measurement has debt that is accumulating but unmeasured. Performance engineering's job is to make the debt visible, quantify it, and drive it to zero at launch.

## Performance SLOs: p50, p95, p99 — Why Percentiles, Not Averages

**The core problem with averages:**

Consider 10 requests with these response times in milliseconds: 50, 60, 55, 70, 65, 80, 90, 100, 150, 2000.

- Average: 272ms — appears to indicate a slow system, pulled up by one outlier
- p50 (median): 72ms — what a typical user experiences, the actual middle
- p95: 150ms — the latency threshold 95% of users are within
- p99: 2,000ms — what the slowest 1 in 100 users experience

The average is misleading in both directions: it overstates the typical experience (72ms vs. 272ms) while the single 2,000ms outlier is invisible to anyone tracking only the average. In production at 1,000 TPS, one 2,000ms request per second means 60 severely degraded requests per minute — a real user impact that the average cannot surface.

**The compound p99 problem in microservice chains:**

Assume a request path traverses 5 services sequentially, each with an independent p99 of 200ms.

- The probability that a single service hits its p99 on a given request: 1%
- The probability that at least one of 5 services hits its p99: 1 − (0.99^5) = 4.9%
- At 1,000 TPS, 49 requests per second experience at least one service at p99 latency
- If those services are in the critical path and latencies are additive: compound worst-case latency = 5 × 200ms = 1,000ms for those 49 requests per second
- The end-to-end p99 is not 200ms — it is approximately 1,000ms for ~5% of requests

This is why microservice architectures require tighter per-service SLOs than monoliths to deliver equivalent end-to-end SLOs. A 5-service chain targeting end-to-end p99 ≤ 500ms must budget each service at approximately p99 ≤ 100ms with zero headroom for network and sidecar overhead.

**SLO target setting framework:**

| Traffic Type | p50 Target | p95 Target | p99 Target | Rationale |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Interactive UI (checkout, login, account mgmt) | ≤ 100ms | ≤ 300ms | ≤ 1,000ms | Human perception threshold: >300ms feels slow, >1s feels broken. Nielsen's 1-second rule. |
| API (mobile app backend, T-Mobile app) | ≤ 50ms | ≤ 200ms | ≤ 500ms | Mobile UX: total round-trip including radio latency must complete in <1s. Service must budget ≤500ms. |
| Async messaging (Kafka consumer, RabbitMQ) | ≤ 100ms processing | ≤ 500ms end-to-end | ≤ 2s end-to-end | Depends on SLA to downstream consumer. End-to-end includes queue wait time, not just processing time. |
| Internal service-to-service (synchronous) | ≤ 20ms | ≤ 80ms | ≤ 200ms | Must leave budget for the calling service's own processing and the external-facing SLO. |
| Batch (nightly reconciliation, billing run) | ≤ 30min total | ≤ 45min | Must complete <60min | Hard deadline: must complete before market open, next-day cutoff, or billing window. |
| Read-heavy cache-backed API | ≤ 5ms | ≤ 20ms | ≤ 50ms | Cache hit path. Cache miss must be tracked separately and SLO'd independently. |

**Setting the T value for Apdex:**

Apdex requires a satisfaction threshold T. Rules:
- Interactive checkout/login: T = 500ms (Satisfied ≤500ms, Tolerating 500ms–2s, Frustrated >2s)
- Mobile API: T = 200ms
- Internal APIs: T = 100ms
- Async processing: Apdex is not the right metric — use lag and processing time SLOs instead

An Apdex score below 0.85 indicates a service in systematic performance trouble. Below 0.7 is a business problem. Below 0.5 means more than half of users are frustrated.

## Microservice vs. Monolithic Performance: Different Failure Modes

Understanding the failure mode is required before diagnosing the problem. The same symptom (latency spike) has different root causes and different remediation paths depending on architecture.

**Monolith failure modes:**

- **JVM heap exhaustion → OOM kill**: Single heap, single GC domain. When Old Generation fills, Full GC runs — potentially minutes of stop-the-world pause. If GC cannot reclaim enough, OutOfMemoryError kills the JVM. Blast radius: total service outage. Single restart fixes it (until the next cycle). Diagnosis: heap size vs. live data set size; GC log analysis.

- **Shared thread pool exhaustion**: All request processing shares one thread pool. If a slow backend (database, external API) causes threads to block, the pool fills. New requests queue. Queue fills. New requests timeout. Latency spike then error spike, in that order. Diagnosis: thread dump — look for blocked/waiting threads on I/O operations. Remediation: separate thread pools per backend, timeouts on all blocking calls, circuit breakers.

- **Database connection pool saturation**: A monolith with one database connection pool. Under high load, all connections are in use. New requests wait in the pool queue (HikariCP default wait: 30s). Requests that wait 30s time out with a connection acquisition timeout — a 30s p99. Users see this as timeout errors. Diagnosis: pool metrics — `hikari.connections.active` vs. pool max size. Remediation: pool size increase (bounded by database connection limits), query optimization to release connections faster.

- **Stop-the-world GC pause**: G1GC mixed collections or Full GC halt all application threads simultaneously. During a 500ms GC pause, every request in flight either queues (if upstream holds the connection) or times out (if upstream gives up). Diagnosis: GC logs, `jstat -gcutil`, JVM monitoring. All users experience the pause simultaneously — it shows as a latency spike across all p-values simultaneously for a narrow time window.

- **Blast radius is total, diagnosis is targeted**: The entire service fails together. Every user is affected equally. But the root cause is localized (heap, one thread pool, one connection pool, one GC cycle). Fix the root cause, fix the service.

**Microservice failure modes:**

- **Latency fan-out through the call chain**: Service A calls B which calls C. If C's p99 is 800ms, every request that flows through C experiences ≥800ms at the A level, even if A itself responds in 10ms. The slowest service in the critical path dominates the end-to-end latency distribution. There is no averaging — it is additive.

- **Connection pool fan-out multiplication**: 10 instances of Service A, each with a pool of 20 database connections = 200 total connections to the database. Add Service B (10 instances × 20 connections = 200) and Service C (10 × 20 = 200). Three microservices with modest pool sizing = 600 database connections — identical load that would require 60 connections in a monolith. This is the N × pool_size fan-out problem. It limits horizontal scaling because the database connection limit becomes the ceiling, not compute.

- **Network hop overhead**: Each synchronous service-to-service call adds network latency. In a Kubernetes pod network: 0.5–2ms within the same node, 2–5ms across nodes in the same AZ, 5–15ms cross-AZ. With Istio/Envoy sidecar injection: add 1–5ms per hop for proxy processing. A 5-service chain with Istio: minimum 5–25ms of pure sidecar overhead, before any application processing. This overhead is fixed — it cannot be optimized away, only minimized by reducing hops.

- **Distributed GC pauses — cumulative but not simultaneous**: Each JVM has its own GC. GC pauses are not synchronized across services. However, if Service A has a GC pause while it is waiting for Service B to respond, and Service B has a GC pause 200ms later, the two pauses add on the critical path of a request that spans both. Under load, the probability of hitting a GC pause on any given cross-service request increases with the number of services in the chain.

- **Circuit breaker misconfiguration causes cascade**: A circuit breaker configured with too-high failure threshold opens too late — after the downstream is already down and requests have been failing for 30 seconds. A circuit breaker with too-low threshold opens on transient errors, causing upstream services to fail fast on workloads that would have recovered naturally. Misconfigured circuit breakers transform partial failures into complete ones.

- **Service mesh overhead — quantified:**

| Overhead Type | Typical Range | Conditions |
|--------------|--------------|------------|
| Envoy proxy latency per hop | 1–5ms | Depends on policy complexity (mTLS, retry logic, header manipulation) |
| Envoy proxy memory per pod | 50–200MB | Depends on Envoy config, number of upstream clusters |
| Envoy CPU overhead | 5–15% of pod CPU | At sustained high TPS |
| Istio control plane sync lag | 100ms–5s | Time for updated policy to propagate to all sidecar proxies |

At T-Mobile EKS scale with hundreds of pods and Istio enabled, sidecar memory overhead alone can represent significant node resource consumption — this is a capacity planning input, not just a performance footnote. See [Capacity Management].

**Performance budget implication:**

A monolith has one performance budget for the entire request. A microservice architecture has N service budgets that must sum to ≤ the user-facing SLO, minus network overhead for all hops. The budget must be allocated before services are built, not after they are measured. Retrofitting a latency budget onto a service that was built without one requires rearchitecting, not tuning.

## The Performance Budget

A performance budget is an explicit latency allocation per architectural layer. It is the mechanism for enforcing the performance contract across multiple teams each owning different services. Without a budget, each team optimizes independently and no one is accountable for the end-to-end SLO.

**Worked example — E-commerce checkout API with p95 SLO of 500ms:**

```
Total user-facing budget:      500ms  (p95 SLO target)

Layer-by-layer allocation:
  DNS resolution:                  ~5ms  (client-side, not controllable by service)
  TLS handshake:                  ~20ms  (mitigated by TLS session resumption)
  API Gateway (auth + rate limit): ~15ms  (Kong, AWS API GW, or Istio IngressGateway)
  Service A — Cart Validation:    ~80ms  [internal DB call: 40ms, logic: 40ms]
  Service B — Inventory Check:    ~60ms  [internal DB call: 40ms, Kafka publish: 20ms]
  Service C — Payment Auth:      ~150ms  [external PSP API call: 100ms, internal: 50ms]
  Service D — Order Creation:     ~80ms  [DB write: 50ms, event publish: 30ms]
  Response serialization:         ~10ms
  Network return path:             ~5ms
                                 ------
  Total allocated:               ~425ms

  Headroom reserve:               ~75ms  (15% buffer against p95 measurement variance)
```

The headroom reserve is not slack — it is a structural budget reserve. When any service overruns its allocation (Service C goes from 150ms to 220ms under load), the headroom is consumed. When headroom reaches zero, the p95 SLO is at risk. The budget forces the conversation about optimization to happen before production.

**T-Mobile Kafka consumer budget example:**

```
End-to-end Kafka message processing budget:   2,000ms  (p99 SLO)

  Kafka producer → Kafka broker:               ~5ms   (network + broker ack)
  Kafka broker queue wait:                    ~50ms   (depends on consumer lag)
  Consumer poll-to-process handoff:            ~5ms
  Consumer business logic:                    ~80ms   (enrichment, validation)
  Downstream DB write:                        ~40ms
  Downstream event publish (if any):          ~20ms
  Consumer ack to broker:                      ~5ms
                                             ------
  Total:                                     ~205ms   (well within 2,000ms budget)

  Available for consumer lag under backpressure: ~1,795ms
  Consumer group lag threshold alert: >500ms average lag = alert; >1,500ms = page
```

The consumer budget has two components: per-message processing time (the service's internal performance), and queue wait time (the throughput-to-consumer-capacity ratio). A consumer that processes each message in 80ms but has a 1,500ms queue wait has a TPS capacity problem, not a processing performance problem. These require different interventions.

> **VP Governance Callout**: The performance budget is the architectural equivalent of a financial budget. Each service team has an allocation. Overruns must be approved, funded for optimization, or offset by savings elsewhere in the chain. Without a budget, teams make independent decisions that collectively breach the user-facing SLO — and no single team is accountable because each one was "within limits" by their own measurement. The performance budget makes the accountability chain explicit and distributes it to the team level.

## Director Decision Framework

Three decisions that require Director or VP-level ownership in a performance engineering program:

### Decision 1: Performance Target Commitment

**The decision**: What p95 latency and TPS capacity does the organization commit to per service? Who is authorized to set, modify, or waive these targets?

**What makes this a leadership decision, not an engineering decision**: A performance SLO is a promise — to users, to the business, and implicitly to every team that calls that service. Engineers can measure and advise, but accepting the promise on behalf of the organization is a leadership accountability. An engineer cannot commit the organization to a p95 = 200ms SLO because they cannot authorize the investment required to achieve and maintain it.

**Decision inputs**:
- p95 and p99 targets per service tier (interactive, API, async, batch)
- TPS capacity target and seasonal multiplier (peak-to-average ratio)
- Error rate ceilings at normal load and 2x peak load
- Degradation behavior contract (circuit break vs. queue vs. reject)

**Who authorizes**: Principal SRE proposes. Product confirms it aligns with user experience expectations. VP signs off, accepting accountability for funding the engineering required to achieve it.

**Red flag**: If nobody can name the p95 SLO for your highest-traffic production service without looking it up, the organization is operating without a performance contract. This is a governance gap.

### Decision 2: Launch Halt Criteria

**The decision**: Under what performance test results do we halt a scheduled launch?

**Why this requires a pre-committed decision**: If halt criteria are determined at launch time rather than pre-committed, they are subject to launch-day pressure, relationship dynamics, and recency bias ("the numbers look better than last week"). Performance-degraded software ships because nobody pre-committed to a number that would stop it.

**Recommended halt criteria** (Director/VP pre-commits to these before the launch cycle):

| Condition | Halt Threshold |
|-----------|---------------|
| p95 latency vs. SLO | >20% over SLO target under nominal load → halt |
| Error rate at nominal load | >0.1% → halt |
| Error rate at 2x peak | >0.5% → halt |
| Saturation point vs. expected peak | Saturation point < 1.5x projected peak load → halt |
| Regression vs. baseline | p95 increased >10% vs. prior release baseline → halt (with override process) |
| Soak test heap growth | Heap size growing >10% per hour during 4-hour soak → halt (memory leak) |
| Recovery time | Does not return to baseline within 10 minutes after spike subsides → halt |

**Override process**: A halt can be overridden by the VP or Director responsible for the service — documented decision, accepted risk, post-launch monitoring plan with defined rollback trigger. No anonymous overrides.

### Decision 3: Performance Governance Cadence

**The decision**: How does leadership know whether service performance is stable and trending correctly over time?

**The cadence that works:**

- **Weekly**: SRE team produces a performance anomaly report — any service where p95 moved >10% week-over-week, any SLO burn rate acceleration, any new performance incident. 1-page, automated from APM data. Director reviews.

- **Monthly**: Per-service performance scorecard — current p50/p95/p99 vs. baseline, TPS headroom vs. peak, top 3 optimization wins, top 3 open performance risks. Presented by PE lead to engineering directors.

- **Quarterly**: Full performance capacity review — current capacity vs. projected demand for next quarter, saturation point mapping, infrastructure investment recommendations, PE program maturity assessment. Presented to VP/Directors. Drives budget decisions.

- **Pre-launch**: Every launch that modifies a service's critical path requires a load test report signed off against halt criteria. No exceptions. The halt criteria table above is the contract.

See [ch08 — PE Program Governance and Reporting] for the full program governance model, scorecard format, and reporting templates.

See [Capacity Management] for how the quarterly performance review feeds the capacity planning cycle — TPS headroom measurements from performance tests become the input to infrastructure scaling decisions.
