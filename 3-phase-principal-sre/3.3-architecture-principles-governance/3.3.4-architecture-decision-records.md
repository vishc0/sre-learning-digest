## 1. What an ADR Is

An Architecture Decision Record is a document that captures four things: the context that forced a decision, the options that were considered (not just the option that won), the decision that was made, and the consequences that were accepted as a result.

That sequence is deliberate. The "options considered" section is the most valuable part of an ADR — and it is the section most often omitted. When you document only the decision and not the rejected alternatives, you guarantee that every rejected option will be re-proposed within 18 months. The people who considered and rejected Cassandra in 2022 will have left by 2024. The engineers who join in 2023 will have used Cassandra at their previous company. Without an ADR recording why Cassandra was rejected — the specific expertise gap, the operational support calculation, the incident data from the Trial — the proposal returns, the debate restarts, and the same conclusion is reached after another three weeks of evaluation. This is not a hypothetical. It is the default outcome when organizations skip ADRs.

ADRs are not design documents. Design documents describe what was built — the component diagram, the data model, the API contract. ADRs capture why this approach was chosen over alternatives. A design document answers "how does this work?" An ADR answers "why does it work this way instead of any of the other ways we considered?"

ADRs are not meeting notes. Meeting notes capture what was discussed, who said what, and what the open questions were. ADRs capture what was decided — the single, bounded outcome of a decision process. Meeting notes are inputs to ADRs. They are not substitutes.

An ADR is permanent by design. Once written and accepted, it is never deleted. It may be superseded — a newer ADR may overturn it — but the original record stays because it is historical context. The history of why the organization was where it was at a point in time is part of the record.

## 2. The Institutional Memory Problem at VP Level

The average tenure of a senior engineer at a technology company is 2-3 years. The average life of an architectural decision — the point at which its consequences are fully embedded in the system and cannot be reversed without significant effort — is 5-7 years. This gap between personnel tenure and decision lifetime is the institutional memory problem. ADRs are the only practical solution at organizational scale.

Every question of the form "why was this built this way?" that takes more than 30 minutes to answer is an ADR that was never written. When the answer requires scheduling a meeting, finding the one engineer still at the company who was in the room, and reconstructing the decision from Slack history and half-remembered JIRA comments — the cost of the missing ADR is being paid in the present.

**The archaeology cost**: When a new VP arrives, the first 6 months are frequently spent doing architecture archaeology — discovering what exists, why it was built, and what constraints it imposes. In organizations with ADR discipline, this takes weeks. In organizations without it, it takes months and is never fully complete. The VP who inherits a system without ADRs is operating with an incomplete map. They will make decisions that violate constraints they cannot see because those constraints were never written down.

**The re-decision cost**: Without ADRs, the same decisions get made repeatedly. A team evaluates Kafka versus RabbitMQ for their use case. The evaluation takes three weeks. They reach a conclusion. Two years later, a different team evaluates the same question — they cannot see the prior decision because it was never recorded — and spends three more weeks reaching the same conclusion. The organizational cost of this repeated evaluation is not just the three weeks. It is also the inconsistency that results when two teams making independent decisions reach different conclusions from equivalent evidence.

**Decision debt**: The accumulation of undocumented decisions is a form of technical debt. Each undocumented decision adds weight to the system's future — future engineers cannot reason about the system fully, future changes carry hidden risks because the constraints are invisible, and future VPs start from a worse position than their predecessor. Decision debt compounds. Organizations that let it accumulate for five years find it genuinely difficult to change direction because no one knows which changes would violate which constraints.

## 3. ADR Lifecycle

An ADR moves through four statuses. Each status has a specific meaning and a specific transition condition.

| Status | Meaning | Transition |
|---|---|---|
| **Proposed** | The ADR is under review by the ARB or architecture team. The decision is not yet binding. Any engineer may propose. | Moves to Accepted via ARB approval for Tier 1 decisions; architecture team approval for Tier 2 decisions. |
| **Accepted** | The decision is current and binding. Supersedes any prior ADR that conflicts with it in this domain. The default state for ADRs in force. | Moves to Superseded when a newer ADR overturns or replaces it. |
| **Superseded** | Replaced by a newer ADR. Must reference the superseding ADR by number and title. The record stays — it is historical context. | Terminal state — a superseded ADR does not return to Accepted. |
| **Deprecated** | The system or pattern this decision governed no longer exists. The record stays for archaeology. | Terminal state — triggered by system decommissioning or migration completion. |

**Why the history is never deleted**: A new engineer joining in 2025 looking at a system built in 2019 should be able to trace the architectural decisions that shaped it — including decisions that were made and later changed. The sequence of decisions is context. Deleting superseded ADRs removes that context and recreates the institutional memory problem the ADR practice was designed to solve.

## 4. ADR Content — What Goes in Each Section

An ADR has eight sections. Each section has a purpose. What follows is an explanation of what "good" looks like versus what "bad" looks like for each section — because the failure mode is an ADR that exists but contains nothing useful.

**Title**

Format: verb-noun, present tense. Should be findable by keyword search.

Good: `Use RabbitMQ for task queue messaging in the billing domain`  
Bad: `Messaging decision` — unsearchable, not self-describing

Good: `Reject Cassandra for time-series storage due to operational support gap`  
Bad: `Database choice` — contains no information about the decision

**Date**

The date of the decision, not the date of writing. If the decision was made in a November ARB session but the ADR was written in December, the date is November. Decisions are not retroactively altered by when they were documented.

**Status**

One of: Proposed, Accepted, Superseded (→ ADR-NNNN), Deprecated. Nothing else. This field must be kept current — an ADR with status Proposed that has been in force for two years is a governance failure.

**Context**

The forces that made this decision necessary. External constraints, business requirements, team capability at the time, timeline pressure, vendor conditions. This section should make any reader — including a reader with no context — understand why the decision could not be avoided and why it needed to be made when it was made.

Good context: `The billing service and notification service both require async communication with guaranteed delivery semantics. The billing service processes approximately 50,000 messages per day at peak with bursts to 80,000. The notification service requires dead-letter handling for failed deliveries and retry policies configurable per message type. Three distinct teams will implement consumers. The SRE team has operational expertise in RabbitMQ from the prior payments integration. We evaluated whether Kafka met these requirements — Kafka's streaming semantics are a better fit for high-throughput event log use cases than for task queue use cases at this volume. RabbitMQ's per-message acknowledgment, dead-letter exchange, and per-queue TTL configuration is a better fit for this workload.`

Bad context: `We needed to choose a message broker.` — this tells the reader nothing about why this decision was necessary, nothing about the constraints, nothing about the trade-offs being navigated. It is a placeholder, not context.

**Options Considered**

At least two options, ideally three. For each option:
- Name and one-line description
- Why it was evaluated (what capability it offered)
- Pros in this specific context
- Cons in this specific context
- Reason for rejection (or reason for selection)

This is the most important section for the institutional memory purpose. A rejected option with a documented reason for rejection cannot be re-proposed without addressing that reason. Without this section, every option is always back in play.

**Decision**

One sentence. The specific, bounded outcome. Not a paragraph, not a list, not a summary of the options section.

Good: `We will use RabbitMQ for all task queue messaging within the billing and notification domains.`  
Bad: `After careful consideration of the available options and their relative strengths, the team has decided to proceed with RabbitMQ as our preferred messaging solution going forward, particularly given our existing expertise...` — this is padding. One sentence.

**Consequences**

The trade-offs accepted. This section has three parts:

1. What becomes easier as a result of this decision
2. What becomes harder as a result of this decision
3. What must be built, changed, or accepted as a result

A consequences section that lists only positive outcomes is a marketing document, not a governance record. Every decision has a cost. If this section doesn't identify a cost, the ADR is incomplete.

Good: `Easier: Dead-letter handling, per-queue TTL configuration, consumer acknowledgment patterns. Harder: This architecture does not scale to event streaming workloads above ~100K messages/second — if billing volume grows by 10x, this decision must be revisited. We are accepting a per-message routing overhead that Kafka's partition-based model avoids. Must be built: SRE runbook for RabbitMQ management UI, Splunk alert for consumer lag exceeding 5 minutes, capacity planning model for queue depth.`

Bad: `RabbitMQ will serve our needs well and teams will be able to implement their use cases effectively.` — this is not a consequences section, it is a summary of the decision.

**Review Date**

When this decision should be formally revisited. For most technology decisions: 12-24 months, or when a specific condition is met (e.g., "revisit if monthly message volume exceeds 10 million"). This date is tracked in the ARB Decision Log. An ADR with a review date that has passed without review is an overdue governance action.

## 5. When to Write an ADR

**Write an ADR when:**

- The decision is hard or expensive to reverse — technology selection, data model schema design, public API contracts, authentication patterns
- The decision has cross-team impact — any integration pattern, any shared platform capability, any message topic or schema that multiple teams will consume
- The decision involves a tension between two architecture principles — consistency versus availability, security versus developer experience, operational simplicity versus feature richness
- The decision selects a technology from the Trial ring of the Technology Radar
- The decision requires a standards exception
- The ARB reviewed and approved the proposal — every ARB-reviewed decision requires an ADR regardless of other criteria
- The decision overturns a prior decision — a superseding ADR is required

**Do not write an ADR for:**

- Implementation choices within an approved architecture — which JSON parsing library, which HTTP client library, code organization within a service
- Standard technology use following established patterns — standing up a new PostgreSQL database following the approved database provisioning runbook
- Decisions that are trivially reversible — naming conventions, log format within a service, local development environment configuration
- Team-internal conventions with no external interface implications — internal code review policy, internal testing approach for a single service

The test: does this decision create a constraint that engineers outside my team will encounter without knowing why it exists? If yes, write the ADR.

## 6. ADR Governance — Who Proposes, Who Reviews, Who Approves

ADR governance operates in three tiers. The tier determines the approval path and the timing requirement.

**Tier 1 — VP and ARB approval required**

Scope: new service architectures, cross-domain integrations, technology radar changes (any ring movement), decisions that affect multiple teams or Tier 1 critical systems, standards exceptions.

Timing: ADR must be written and reviewed before implementation begins. The ADR is the approval artifact — work begins when the ADR is Accepted, not before.

Process: team proposes ADR → architecture team review (1 week SLA) → ARB session review → VP sign-off → status set to Accepted → implementation begins.

**Tier 2 — Architecture team review, ARB notification**

Scope: single-team architectural decisions with external interface implications (a new API endpoint that other teams will call, a new message topic that other teams will consume), decisions that affect an existing Tier 1 system's interface without changing its core topology.

Timing: ADR required within 2 weeks of implementation decision. Not before — this tier acknowledges that some decisions need to be made in motion — but the documentation obligation is real and tracked.

Process: team makes decision and begins implementation → ADR written within 2 weeks → architecture team review → ARB notified at next session → status set to Accepted.

**Tier 3 — Team-internal, optional ADR, ARB notification if promoted**

Scope: implementation decisions within an approved architecture, team-internal conventions with no external interface implications.

Timing: ADR optional. Encouraged for decisions the team expects to be questioned later.

Process: team decides → writes ADR if warranted → notifies ARB only if decision later gets escalated to Tier 2 or Tier 1 scope.

## 7. ADR Repository and Discoverability

ADRs that cannot be found are equivalent to ADRs that were never written. Discoverability is a governance requirement, not an operational nicety.

**Repository structure:**

Cross-team and cross-domain ADRs live in a central architecture repository, not in individual service repositories. Service-specific ADRs live adjacent to the service code. The naming convention is universal:

```
adr-NNNN-short-title.md
```

Where NNNN is a zero-padded sequential number and short-title is lowercase kebab-case. Example: `adr-0042-use-rabbitmq-for-billing-task-queues.md`.

Numbers are never reused, even for superseded ADRs. The number is a permanent identifier.

**Index and discovery:**

The central ADR repository maintains an index file with the following columns: ADR number, title, date, status, systems affected, technologies referenced, author. The index is machine-readable (CSV or YAML) so it can be queried. An engineer should be able to run a search for "Kafka" and receive all ADRs that reference Kafka — regardless of their status — within seconds.

A search should return:
- All ADRs by system name (give me everything that touches the billing domain)
- All ADRs by technology (give me every decision that references RabbitMQ)
- All ADRs by date range (what decisions were made in the 6 months before this incident?)
- All ADRs by status (what Proposed decisions are currently awaiting ARB review?)

**New engineer onboarding:**

The 10 most important ADRs for any system must be linked in that system's README. Not the full ADR text — the link. This is the first governance act when onboarding a new team member to a service: here are the decisions that shaped this system, read these before making architectural changes.

**The ADR graveyard anti-pattern:**

ADRs written and never referenced. The signs: engineers in an interview or design session say "I didn't know there was an ADR for that" or "where do I find the ADRs?" If engineers do not know ADRs exist, or cannot locate the relevant ADR for their system in under two minutes, the ADR practice is producing documentation without producing governance. The documentation is not the outcome — the shared knowledge is.

> **What the VP decides**: Which decisions require Tier 1 ADRs. Where the central ADR repository lives and who owns it. Who is accountable for ADR coverage across the organization — this is an architecture team accountability that the VP holds through quarterly governance reports.

> **What happens without ADRs**: A new VP spends 6 months doing architecture archaeology — discovering what was built, why it was built that way, and what constraints it imposes. This 6-month cost should have been 6 hours of documentation. The VP who inherits an organization without ADR discipline does not just face a knowledge problem. They face a decision-making handicap: every choice they make may violate constraints they cannot see because those constraints were never written down.

## 8. Bad ADRs — What to Avoid

An ADR that exists but contains bad content is more dangerous than no ADR. It creates false confidence — "we have an ADR for that" — while providing none of the institutional memory value.

**Vague context**: `We needed to make a decision about databases.` What forces? What constraints? What was the triggering event? Vague context means the reader cannot understand why the decision was necessary and therefore cannot evaluate whether it is still appropriate.

**Missing alternatives**: Only one option listed — the winner. This is a justification document, not a decision record. A decision record without rejected options cannot prevent those options from being re-proposed. The "options considered" section exists specifically to record the evaluation, not to catalog the winner.

**No trade-off acknowledgment in consequences**: The consequences section says only positive things about the chosen option. "PostgreSQL gives us ACID transactions, strong consistency, and familiar query semantics." These are reasons the option was selected — they belong in the decision section. The consequences section must also answer: what does this make harder? What must now be built or accepted? What cannot be done under this decision that could have been done under an alternative?

**Decision by default**: `We used PostgreSQL because it was already installed on the host.` This is a discovery, not a decision. A decision made by default (what was already there, what the team knew, what was easiest to provision) without explicit evaluation of whether it was the right choice should be written up as "decision made by default — no evaluation of alternatives was performed — recommended for explicit review." Passing it off as a real decision creates false governance coverage.

**Stale status**: An ADR with status Proposed that has been in force for 18 months. An ADR marked Accepted that governs a system that was decommissioned two years ago. Stale statuses mean the ADR index cannot be trusted, which means the ADR index will not be used, which means the ADR practice has collapsed into a documentation exercise with no governance value.

## Summary

| Concept | Operational Meaning |
|---|---|
| ADR purpose | Capture the context, options, decision, and consequences — not just the outcome |
| "Options considered" section | The most valuable section — prevents infinite re-proposal of rejected options |
| ADR ≠ design document | Design docs describe what was built; ADRs explain why this approach instead of alternatives |
| Institutional memory problem | Average engineer tenure (2-3 years) < average decision lifetime (5-7 years) |
| ADR lifecycle | Proposed → Accepted → Superseded or Deprecated — never deleted |
| Tier 1 ADR | Required before implementation for cross-team, cross-domain decisions |
| Tier 2 ADR | Required within 2 weeks of implementation for external-interface decisions |
| Discoverability | ADRs that cannot be found provide no governance value |
| Bad ADR | Vague context, missing alternatives, no trade-offs, decision by default, stale status |
