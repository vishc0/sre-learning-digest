# Architecture Principles & Governance

## What This Is

Architecture governance is the constitutional layer of engineering. It defines the non-negotiable constraints that shape every system design decision made across your organization — and the mechanisms that ensure those constraints are actually followed.

Without this layer, engineering organizations default to tribal knowledge, local optimization, and eventually the big ball of mud. With it, teams make faster decisions, incidents have traceable architectural root causes, and the VP can answer the CTO's hardest question: "Why does our architecture look like this?"

This framework covers:

- **Architecture Principles** — the constraints every system must satisfy, regardless of team, technology, or timeline pressure
- **Technology Standards** — the approved technology choices teams build on, and the process for exceptions
- **Architecture Decision Records** — the institutional memory layer that captures *why* decisions were made, not just what was decided
- **The Architecture Review Board** — the governance body, its operating model, and how to run one that teams trust rather than route around
- **Technical Debt Governance** — how to make debt visible, measurable, and a managed risk item rather than accumulated shame
- **Policy Hierarchy** — how principles, standards, policies, procedures, and guidelines relate to each other and who owns what
- **Governance Metrics** — what to measure to know if governance is working

The governance mechanisms exist to enforce the principles at scale. Principles without enforcement are philosophy. Enforcement without principles is bureaucracy.

## How to Navigate

**An architecture review is happening now — you need to prepare or evaluate a submission.**
Go to `playbook/architecture-review-request-playbook.md` for the submission process and review criteria. Use `templates/architecture-review-request-template.md` to structure the request. Reference `ch02-foundational-architecture-principles.md` to evaluate principle compliance. If the proposal requires a technology not on the current radar, use `playbook/standards-exception-playbook.md`.

**You are establishing architecture principles for a new organization or a newly formed VP scope.**
Start with `ch01-why-architecture-governance-matters.md` to build the governance case for leadership. Work through `ch02-foundational-architecture-principles.md` to draft the principles document. Use `ch07-policy-hierarchy-and-document-governance.md` to structure the document hierarchy correctly before publishing. Then stand up the ARB using `ch05-architecture-review-board.md`.

**You are building or reforming the ARB.**
Read `ch05-architecture-review-board.md` in full — it covers charter, membership, operating cadence, and the three failure modes. Then set the operating model with `playbook/arb-session-playbook.md`. Check `ch08-governance-metrics-and-reporting.md` to define what success looks like before the first session runs.

**You are conducting a governance audit — inherited a new organization or preparing for a board-level review.**
Start with `ch08-governance-metrics-and-reporting.md` to understand what maturity looks like. Work through each chapter to assess current-state gap. Use `ch06-technical-debt-governance.md` to make debt visible and structured. Map findings to the governance maturity model in `ch01`.

## Three Failure Modes of Architecture Governance Programs

Every architecture governance program fails in one of three ways. Knowing which failure mode you are in — or inheriting — determines the recovery strategy.

### Failure Mode 1: Ivory Tower ARB

The ARB says no to everything that deviates from the approved architecture, which was last meaningfully updated three years ago. Review cycles take six weeks. Teams learn to route around the process — they build first, ask for forgiveness later, or reframe requests to look compliant. A shadow architecture proliferates in Slack channels and one-on-one conversations. The ARB has nominal authority over the official architecture and zero authority over what is actually being built.

**Signature behaviors:** Teams have a shared Slack channel specifically for discussing how to avoid the ARB. Approved standards include technologies the vendor has deprecated. ARB rejection rate is over 30% and rising. Engineering leadership privately refers to the ARB as the "architecture police."

**Recovery path:** Cut the review cycle to two weeks or less. Publish decision criteria in advance so teams can self-assess before submitting. Shift the ARB's role from gatekeeper to advisor. Update the technology radar immediately — retire the holds that no longer make sense.

### Failure Mode 2: Rubber Stamp ARB

The ARB approves everything to avoid conflict and maintain team goodwill. Every review ends with "approved." Principles are referenced in documents no one reads. Standards exist on a wiki page that was last edited before the current VP arrived. The governance program is theater — it provides the appearance of oversight without its substance.

**Signature behaviors:** The ARB has never rejected a request. The principles document has not been updated in 18+ months. When asked which principle governs a specific decision, ARB members give different answers. The first significant incident always reveals that the violated system was "ARB approved."

**Recovery path:** Audit the last 12 months of ARB decisions against the current principles document. Any approval that cannot be traced to principle compliance is a governance failure. Establish minimum documentation requirements — an ADR is required before approval. Run a principles refresh: which of the current principles would you actually enforce if enforced?

### Failure Mode 3: Absent Governance

There is no ARB. There is no principles document. Each team is a sovereign nation with its own technology choices, integration patterns, and risk tolerance. The VP inherits an archipelago of incompatible systems when they arrive. The first 90 days are forensic archaeology — mapping what exists, who owns it, and what would happen if it failed. The governance program must be built from scratch while the organization continues to ship.

**Signature behaviors:** No one can name the architecture principles. "Who owns the integration between X and Y?" triggers a three-week investigation. Significant incidents cannot be root-caused because the architecture is undocumented. The word "legacy" is used to describe systems that are less than four years old.

**Recovery path:** Inventory before you govern. Map the actual architecture — every integration, every data flow, every undocumented dependency. This is the baseline. Then establish principles before standards: principles change rarely, standards change frequently, and you need the hierarchy before you can enforce either.

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Architectural Principle** | A non-negotiable constraint on all system design decisions across the organization. A principle is not a preference or a best practice — it is a rule that applies to every team, every project, and every technology choice. Violated only by VP/CTO exception, in writing, with documented risk acknowledgment. |
| **Standard** | An approved technology or pattern choice that teams must use, or formally request an exception from. Standards change more frequently than principles — they are the operational expression of principles in specific technology choices. A standard for message brokering (e.g., Kafka for event streaming) is derived from the principles of loose coupling and eventual consistency. |
| **Policy** | A mandatory rule with a defined compliance mechanism and defined consequences for violation. Policies are auditable — they have a compliance state (compliant/non-compliant) and a remediation timeline. A policy without a compliance mechanism is a guideline wearing a suit. |
| **Guideline** | An advisory best practice that teams are recommended to follow. Not mandatory. No compliance tracking. No consequences for deviation. Guidelines represent accumulated wisdom — they are what the organization has learned works well — but teams may deviate without a formal exception process. |
| **Architecture Decision Record (ADR)** | A document capturing a significant architectural decision: the context that made the decision necessary, the options considered, the option chosen, and the rationale for the choice. ADRs are the institutional memory layer. They answer the question "why does this work this way?" — a question that becomes critical at 2am during an incident or during a new VP's first 90 days. |
| **Architecture Review Board (ARB)** | The governance body that reviews significant architectural decisions for alignment with principles and standards. The ARB is not a veto committee — it is a structured review mechanism. Its output is a recommendation with documented rationale, not a rubber stamp or a rejection. Membership spans architecture, security, operations, and senior engineering leadership. |
| **Technology Radar** | A quadrant model for classifying technology maturity and organizational endorsement level. Four quadrants: **Adopt** (use by default; proven at scale), **Trial** (actively evaluate; limited production use), **Assess** (monitor and understand; not yet ready for production), **Hold** (do not adopt for new projects; migrate away from existing use). The radar is a living document reviewed quarterly. |
| **Technical Debt** | The accumulated cost of design shortcuts, outdated technology choices, undocumented integrations, and past decisions that no longer fit current scale, security requirements, or business needs. Technical debt generates interest in operational load — SRE time on undocumented systems, on-call fatigue from brittle integrations, and incident cost from systems that no one fully understands. Debt is not inherently bad; unmanaged, invisible debt is. |

## The Governance Hierarchy

Architecture governance operates through a five-level document hierarchy. Each level has a distinct authority model, change velocity, and enforcement mechanism. The hierarchy is not aesthetic — it maps to different governance functions that must not be collapsed.

| Level | Document Type | Description | Change Authority | Enforcement | Example |
|-------|--------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | **Principles** | Non-negotiable constraints that define the character of all system design. Change rarely (annually at most). | VP/CTO sign-off; board awareness for major changes | Exception required; VP-level approval for any deviation | "Design for Failure: every component will fail; design so failures are isolated and recoverable." |
| 2 | **Standards** | Approved technology and pattern choices derived from principles. Change quarterly with technology landscape. | Architecture Council or ARB; published on radar | Exception process with ARB review; deviation logged and tracked | "Kafka is the standard for event streaming. RabbitMQ is approved for task queues only." |
| 3 | **Policies** | Mandatory rules with compliance mechanisms and defined consequences. Auditable. | VP or delegated policy owner; legal/compliance review where applicable | Compliance tracking; remediation timelines; escalation path | "All production services must emit structured logs to the centralized SIEM within 30 days of deployment." |
| 4 | **Procedures** | Step-by-step instructions for performing governed activities. Change frequently as tooling evolves. | Team leads or process owners; ARB for architecture procedures | Process adherence tracked in delivery metrics | "How to submit an architecture review request: step 1, complete the ARB template; step 2..." |
| 5 | **Guidelines** | Advisory best practices. Not mandatory. No compliance tracking. | Any senior engineer or architect; published through knowledge management | None — advisory only; deviation requires no justification | "Consider using circuit breakers on all synchronous service-to-service calls." |

**Why collapsing these into one document is the most common governance failure:** When principles, policies, and guidelines coexist in the same document without clear hierarchy labels, three things happen. First, teams treat all of it as advisory because they cannot distinguish the non-negotiable from the recommended. Second, compliance is impossible to measure because the document mixes auditable rules with philosophical intent. Third, the document becomes impossible to maintain — principles that should change annually are revised whenever a guideline needs updating, and vice versa. The governance hierarchy exists so that "Design for Failure" never appears on the same level as "consider adding a circuit breaker." One is a constraint on every decision you will ever make. The other is a suggestion for how to implement it.

## Cross-References

This framework connects to and depends on adjacent governance domains:

- **[Incident Management]** — Incidents are the most honest signal of architecture governance failures. Every significant incident with an architectural root cause is a governance finding. The PIR process must have an explicit "architectural principle violated" field.
- **[Change Management]** — Every significant change should pass an architecture review before it reaches the CAB. The CAB evaluates operational risk; the ARB evaluates architectural alignment. These are sequential gates, not competing processes.
- **[Problem Management]** — Recurring problems that trace to architectural root causes escalate from the Problem Record to the ARB. The Problem Manager and the Chief Architect need a standing interface.
- **[Application Transformation]** — Modernization decisions are the highest-stakes architecture decisions a VP makes. The ARB must be the governance layer for transformation prioritization — application rationalization without architecture governance produces a different mess than the one you started with.
