## 1. ARB Purpose

The Architecture Review Board is the governance body that ensures significant architectural decisions align with the organization's foundational principles, technology standards, and strategic direction. It is the institutional mechanism that prevents architectural drift — the slow accumulation of decisions that individually seem reasonable but collectively degrade the system's coherence, operability, and security posture.

The ARB is not an approval committee for all technical decisions. That is the ivory tower failure mode. When the ARB must review every technology choice, every library selection, every database schema change, it becomes a bottleneck that kills engineering velocity. Teams route around it. Shadow architecture proliferates outside the governance boundary. The ARB reviews and approves architectural decisions that cross team boundaries, select or change technology standards, or create architectural debt the whole organization will carry.

The ARB is not a technical authority that tells teams how to build their systems. The team proposing an architecture retains technical accountability. The ARB reviews the decision against principles and standards. It does not redesign the system.

**ARB authority**: the ARB can approve, approve with conditions, defer for more information, or reject with an alternative recommendation. It cannot make the architectural decision for the team — only review the decision the team has made. An ARB that starts redesigning proposals in session has left its governance role and entered a consulting role. These are different things with different accountabilities.

**The VP as ARB chair**: the VP of Operations typically chairs or co-chairs the ARB. This is not ceremonial. The VP chair gives the ARB direct access to strategic context — the VP knows which systems are on the CTO's critical path, which compliance obligations are hardening, which vendor relationships are changing — and gives the VP direct visibility into the architectural decisions that will determine operational outcomes 12-18 months from now. Architecture debt approved by an ARB the VP never attends will be owned by the VP when it surfaces as an incident.

## 2. ARB Composition

The ARB is a deliberate assembly. Every seat exists to contribute a specific lens. Members are not selected for seniority — they are selected for the blind spots they prevent.

| Role | Why They're Here | Decision Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **VP Operations (Chair)** | Sets strategic direction, holds final authority on Tier 1 decisions, ensures the ARB remains a decision body and not a discussion forum, reports governance health to executive leadership | Final call on tied votes, Tier 1 approvals, standards changes |
| **Principal / Staff Engineer (2 seats)** | Deep technical authority — evaluate feasibility, identify integration risks, catch assumption failures, provide peer review for the proposing team. These are the engineers who have seen failure modes the proposing team hasn't considered | Binding technical vote; can request deferral for insufficient technical evidence |
| **Security Architect** | Evaluates every proposal for compliance with Defense in Depth, Zero Trust, least privilege, and encryption standards. Security is not a secondary review — it is a first-class ARB lens on every proposal | Veto authority on proposals that violate security principles; cannot be overridden by majority vote without VP escalation |
| **Data Architect** | Evaluates data sovereignty, schema impact, cross-service data access patterns, and API-First compliance. Critical for proposals that create new data flows between systems | Binding vote on proposals creating or modifying cross-service data contracts |
| **Platform Architect** | Evaluates infrastructure standards compliance, operational readiness, Kubernetes/EKS patterns, observability requirements, IaC standards | Binding vote on infrastructure and platform proposals |
| **Rotating Domain Representative** | One domain engineering lead rotates quarterly. Ensures the ARB hears from teams on the receiving end of governance — prevents the ARB from becoming disconnected from what teams actually build and operate | Advisory — provides domain context, does not vote on decisions affecting their own domain to avoid conflict of interest |

**Total**: 5 voting members (VP, 2 Principal Engineers, Security Architect, Data Architect, Platform Architect) plus 1 non-voting rotating representative. Six people with defined responsibilities.

**Why odd-number voting matters**: Tied votes are resolved by the VP chair. An even voting body without a tie-breaker creates deadlock — the worst governance outcome is a decision that cannot be made because the process is stuck. Odd-number voting plus a named tie-breaker eliminates this failure mode.

**Quorum requirement**: 4 of 5 voting members for Tier 1 decisions. The Security Architect is a required quorum member for any decision with a security principle implication — which is most decisions. 3 of 5 for Tier 2 decisions. Below quorum, the session may continue for discussion, but no binding decisions are made. Decisions requiring Tier 1 quorum that have not reached quorum are scheduled for the next session.

**Who is NOT on the ARB**: the proposing team's manager (conflict of interest — they want their team's proposal approved), individual contributors without cross-domain technical authority (they have a seat in the rotation, not a permanent seat), and vendor representatives (no vendor has a seat on an internal governance body, regardless of strategic partnership status).

## 3. What Goes to the ARB

The scope question — "does this need ARB review?" — is the first governance decision an engineering lead or VP makes about any architectural change. Getting this wrong in either direction has costs: too broad and the ARB becomes a bottleneck; too narrow and governance doesn't reach the decisions that matter.

| Goes to ARB | Does NOT Go to ARB |
|---|---|
| New service introduction that interfaces with more than one existing domain | Implementation choices within an approved architecture |
| Cross-domain integrations — new API contracts, new message topics, new shared data stores | Standard technology use following established patterns from the Adopt ring |
| Technology selection outside the approved Technology Radar (requires standards exception) | Single-team decisions with no external interface implications |
| Core topology changes to Tier 1 critical systems | Bug fixes and performance improvements within an existing approved design |
| API versioning decisions with breaking changes for external consumers | Technology choices from the Adopt ring following established runbooks |
| Technology Radar reclassification proposals (any ring movement) | Team-internal conventions — code structure, internal naming, test organization |
| Architecture exception requests | Capacity increases within an approved architecture (scale up, not redesign) |
| New shared platform capabilities a team wants to build for other teams to depend on | Deployment pipeline changes within an approved CI/CD standard |
| ARB-condition follow-up reviews | New team members being given access to existing approved systems |
| Post-incident architectural findings requiring governance response | — |

**The decision tree for "does this go to ARB?"**:

1. Does this decision create a dependency that other teams must carry? → **ARB review required**
2. Does this decision deviate from any Technology Radar standard? → **ARB review required**
3. Does this decision affect a Tier 1 critical system's interfaces or topology? → **ARB review required**
4. Does this decision create a new shared capability (other teams will call this) that does not already have an approved pattern? → **ARB review required**
5. Does this decision require a formal standards exception? → **ARB review required**
6. None of the above → **ARB not required; team decides; ADR if warranted**

When an engineering lead is uncertain whether their proposal meets the ARB threshold, the answer is to ask the architecture team — not to make the call unilaterally and submit after implementation. Pre-implementation scoping conversations with the architecture team are not a process failure; they are the process working correctly.

## 4. ARB Meeting Cadence

The ARB operates on three rhythms. The cadence is designed to make governance fast for routine decisions and thorough for complex ones.

**Weekly lightweight review** (60 minutes, async-first)

Agenda circulated 48 hours prior to the session. Simple Tier 2 decisions — those meeting clear criteria and requiring no contentious judgment — are reviewed asynchronously via the ARB review channel. If 4 of 5 voting members approve asynchronously before the session, the decision is made without requiring the synchronous meeting. The synchronous session then covers: ratifying async approvals, scheduling complex proposals, tracking condition follow-ups, and reviewing architecture health signals.

The async-first approach is the mechanism that prevents the ARB from becoming a bottleneck. Most governance value is delivered by ensuring a proposal was reviewed by the right eyes — not by requiring synchronous deliberation for every decision.

**Monthly deep-dive** (2-3 hours)

Reserved for: new architecture proposals requiring substantive debate, standards evolution discussions, Technology Radar quarterly review, architecture health review with metrics from ch08, and retrospectives on decisions that surfaced unexpected consequences. All proposing teams attend in person — the ARB does not review architecture without the ability to ask the proposing team direct questions.

**Ad hoc review** (as needed, VP-triggered)

Triggered by: security vulnerabilities requiring immediate architecture response (CVE ≥ 9.0 affecting an Adopt-ring technology), post-incident architecture findings that require urgent governance action, or CTO-level strategic changes that have immediate architecture implications. Ad hoc reviews bypass the scheduling queue but retain full quorum requirements.

**Agenda template — weekly session:**

```
1. Async decisions to ratify (10 min)
   - List each async decision with vote tally
   - Chair calls for any objections before ratifying
   
2. New review requests — intake and scheduling (15 min)
   - Each new request presented in 2 minutes: proposing team, scope, decision timeline
   - ARB determines: async review, next weekly session, or monthly deep-dive
   
3. Prior decisions — condition follow-up and ADR status (15 min)
   - For each open condition: named owner, deadline, current status
   - For each approved decision: is the ADR filed? Status updated?
   
4. Architecture health signals (10 min)
   - Exception count — open, new this week, closed
   - ADR coverage — decisions made without ADRs flagged this week
   - Standards compliance — any Hold-ring technology adoption detected in scans
   
5. Open items (10 min)
```

The agenda template is published before every session. Decisions not on the agenda are not made in session — they are scheduled for a future session. This prevents the ARB from becoming an open forum where informal decisions are made without the review process.

## 5. ARB Decision Framework

Every proposal reviewed by the ARB is evaluated against the same five-part framework. This framework is not a checklist to be completed and forgotten — it is the structured interrogation that surfaces the risks a proposal may have missed.

**1. Principle alignment**

Does the proposal follow the foundational architecture principles? For each principle that the proposal implicates, the reviewing member must state: compliant, exception acknowledged, or violation. Where two principles are in tension — API-First conflicts with a performance requirement, Defense in Depth conflicts with a development velocity requirement — the proposal must explicitly name the tension and explain how it was resolved. A proposal that acknowledges the tension is reviewable. A proposal that ignores a tension is incomplete.

**2. Standards compliance**

Does the proposal use technologies from the Adopt ring? For each technology in the proposal, the ARB verifies its radar status. Technologies in the Trial ring are acceptable with evaluation commitments. Technologies in the Hold ring require a formal exception filed separately. Technologies not on the radar at all require either an exception or immediate assessment-track placement. No technology may appear in a production system without a known radar status.

**3. Operational readiness**

Can SRE operate this system without heroics? This is the question the Platform Architect and VP ask of every proposal. Operational readiness requires:
- Observability: is the system instrumented? Can an on-call engineer determine within 5 minutes whether the system is healthy?
- Alerting: are alert thresholds defined? Is there a Splunk search or Datadog monitor that detects the primary failure modes?
- Runbook: does a runbook exist or have a committed delivery date?
- Rollback: what is the rollback plan if this deployment fails?
- On-call coverage: which team owns incidents? Are they trained? Do they have access to the monitoring tools?

A proposal with excellent architecture and no operational readiness plan is not approvable. The architectural decision is inseparable from the operational commitment.

**4. ADR quality**

Is the ADR adequate for this decision? The ARB reviews the ADR as part of the proposal review — not after. An ADR with vague context, missing alternatives, or no consequences section is returned for revision before the decision is made. The ADR is the governance record of the ARB's decision — if the ADR is inadequate, the governance record is inadequate.

**5. Dependency risk**

What new dependencies does this proposal create? What systems must be available for this system to function? What breaks if this component fails? Cross-domain dependencies — where one team's system depends on another team's system for core functionality — receive particular scrutiny. Every new dependency is a potential cascade failure path and a potential SLA conflict.

**Decision outcomes:**

- **Approve**: proposal meets all five criteria. ADR is ratified. Team may proceed immediately. Decision logged in the ARB Decision Log.

- **Approve with conditions**: proposal meets criteria with specific required modifications. Conditions are documented with named owners and deadlines. A follow-up review is scheduled. Work may begin on components not affected by the conditions. Conditional components wait for condition resolution.

- **Defer**: insufficient information to decide. The ARB documents the specific questions that must be answered before the decision can be made. The proposing team returns with answers — this is not a rejection, it is a request for completeness. A deferred proposal does not restart the review queue; it is scheduled directly for the session after the questions are answered.

- **Reject with alternative recommendation**: the proposal violates a principle or standard in a way that cannot be resolved through conditions. The ARB provides a specific alternative path — not just a "no," but a "not this way; here is a way that would be approvable." This is not a final no on the problem being solved; it is a redirect on the approach.

**The ARB never returns a bare "No" without an alternative.** A bare rejection without an alternative path is the ivory tower failure mode. It tells the proposing team that the governance body cannot be reasoned with — and teams learn to route around it. A rejection with an alternative path tells the proposing team that the ARB is a partner in finding a workable solution, not a veto machine.

## 6. ARB Governance — Decision Log and ADR Requirement

The ARB's own governance must be rigorous. A governance body that does not govern itself cannot credibly govern others.

**ARB Decision Log**: every ARB decision is recorded in the ARB Decision Log [reference/arb-decision-log-template.md] within 48 hours of the session. The log entry includes: proposal ID, proposing team, decision date, decision outcome (Approve / Approve with conditions / Defer / Reject), conditions if any, named owners for conditions, condition deadlines, and the ADR number for approved decisions.

The Decision Log is readable by any engineer in the organization. It is not a confidential document. Transparency in governance decisions builds trust — teams that can see why proposals were approved or rejected understand the principles the ARB is enforcing, which improves the quality of future proposals.

**ADR requirement for approved decisions**: every approved Tier 1 decision requires a finalized ADR with status Accepted within 2 weeks of ARB approval. The ARB does not close the governance loop on a decision until the ADR exists. This is tracked as an open item in the ARB Decision Log. Decisions approved without a follow-up ADR within the 2-week window are escalated to the VP for acknowledgment — not as a punitive measure, but because undocumented approved decisions create the institutional memory gap the ADR practice exists to close.

**Condition tracking**: conditional approvals are tracked with named owners and deadlines. At every weekly session, the condition tracking section reviews all open conditions. Conditions that have passed their deadline without resolution are escalated to the VP. The VP then makes the call: extend with explanation, rescind approval pending condition resolution, or accept the condition as unresolvable and determine next steps. Conditions that are never tracked become conditions that are never met — and the approval that depended on them becomes a governance gap.

**Rejected proposals**: rejected proposals are recorded with the alternative recommendation. This prevents the same proposal from returning in a future session without having addressed the ARB's feedback. The Decision Log entry for a rejection includes: the specific principles or standards violated, the specific alternative recommendation, and the conditions under which the rejection could be reconsidered. A proposing team that returns with the same proposal without addressing the recorded feedback will have the log entry cited in the new session.

**Quarterly ARB health review metrics** (reported to VP, delivered to ch08 governance metrics):
- Decision volume: total proposals reviewed, split by Tier 1 / Tier 2
- Cycle time: average days from proposal submission to decision (target: ≤ 5 business days for Tier 2, ≤ 10 for Tier 1)
- Rejection rate: proposals rejected or deferred as a percentage of total — too high suggests either a quality problem in proposals or a standards problem in the ARB; too low suggests the ARB is rubber-stamping
- ADR coverage: percentage of approved decisions with finalized ADRs within the 2-week window
- Exception count: open standards exceptions, new exceptions this quarter, exceptions converted to standards
- Condition close rate: percentage of conditional approvals with conditions resolved within the deadline

## 7. The VP as ARB Chair

The VP's role in the ARB is the most important governance role the VP holds. It is also the role most frequently mishandled — either through over-involvement (the VP becomes the de facto architecture reviewer on every proposal) or under-involvement (the VP delegates the chair to a principal engineer and never attends).

**What the VP does in the ARB:**

Sets the agenda — the VP's priorities determine which proposals receive monthly deep-dive time versus weekly lightweight treatment. A VP who does not set the agenda has ceded governance priority-setting to the architecture team, which may not have the business context to prioritize correctly.

Ensures the ARB does not become a bottleneck — the VP monitors cycle time actively. When a Tier 2 decision takes more than 2 weeks from submission to outcome, the VP investigates the cause. Cycle time is a governance health signal. Long cycle times do not reflect complex decisions — they reflect a broken process. Complex decisions take longer to prepare; they do not take longer to review once prepared.

Makes final calls on tied decisions — the VP holds the tie-breaker not to be the technical authority but to be the strategic authority. "Which way aligns with our 18-month direction?" is a question the VP can answer and the principal engineers cannot, because they don't have the business context.

Escalates principle violations to consequences — when a team bypasses the ARB and ships an architectural decision that required review, the VP holds the consequence conversation. Not the architecture team. The architecture team surfaces the violation; the VP determines the response. Without VP-level consequence, ARB bypass has no cost.

Reports architecture health to the executive team — the quarterly governance metrics are a VP deliverable to the CTO and CFO. The VP translates ARB metrics into business terms: "We have 7 open standards exceptions, 2 of which have missed their 12-month review window. This represents risk to our compliance posture. Here is the remediation plan." This is the governance reporting that justifies the existence of the ARB to non-technical leadership.

**What the VP does NOT do in the ARB:**

Review every architecture decision personally — the VP is the final authority on Tier 1 decisions. For the vast majority of Tier 2 decisions, the VP's role is to be informed, not to be the reviewer. Reviewing every decision means the VP is doing architecture work, not governance work.

Approve implementation details — the ARB approves architectural decisions. The VP does not weigh in on which Kafka serialization format to use, which Helm chart structure is preferred, or which retry strategy a specific service should implement. These are implementation decisions within an approved architecture.

Substitute for technical judgment on feasibility — the VP cannot and should not override a principal engineer's judgment that a proposal is technically infeasible. The VP can override a governance judgment ("this doesn't meet our standards"), not a technical judgment ("this cannot be built to meet these latency requirements in this architecture"). If the VP overrides the principal engineers on technical feasibility, the ARB has become a management override mechanism, not a governance body.

**The bottleneck test**: if ARB cycle time for a standard Tier 2 decision consistently exceeds 2 weeks, the VP investigates immediately. This is a governance failure, not a capacity problem. The causes are: ARB members reviewing too many proposals (scope is too broad — reduce what goes to ARB), ARB members not preparing adequately before sessions (process problem — agenda distribution lead time needs to increase), or proposals arriving in inadequate states (quality problem — the architecture team's intake review is not working). Each cause has a different fix. The VP identifies which cause applies and fixes it.

**The rubber stamp test**: if the ARB has not rejected or applied conditions to a proposal in the last quarter, either every proposal this quarter was impeccably prepared (possible but unlikely) or the ARB is not doing its job. A governance body that never says "not this way" is not governing. The VP examines: are the right proposals coming to the ARB? Are members preparing adequately? Is there social pressure to approve so as not to be seen as a blocker? Each of these is a different culture problem with a different fix.

**What the VP reports to the board or executive committee:**

- Architecture compliance rate: percentage of Tier 1 decisions made with ARB review (target: 100%)
- Open exception count: number of active standards exceptions and their ages
- Technical debt trend: direction and rate of architectural debt accumulation (from ch08 metrics)
- Governance cycle time: average time from proposal to decision, trending
- ADR coverage: percentage of significant decisions with documented ADRs

These metrics make architecture governance legible to executives who will not read an ARB Decision Log but need to understand organizational risk.

## 8. ARB Anti-Patterns

Each of these failure modes has a distinct signature. A VP who can recognize the signature early can intervene before the anti-pattern is fully embedded.

**ARB as rubber stamp**

Signature: every proposal is approved without conditions. Sessions are short. No proposals have been rejected in memory. Teams view the ARB as a bureaucratic formality.  
Root cause: social dynamics — members do not want to be seen as blocking team velocity, or members do not prepare adequately and default to approval.  
Consequence: governance becomes decorative. Principles are violated systematically and accumulate as architectural debt. The VP inherits the consequences in 18 months when the debt surfaces as incidents.  
Fix: the VP personally reviews the last 10 ARB decisions and asks: "Were there no trade-offs, no tensions with principles, no missing elements in the operability plan?" Almost certainly there were. Introduce the expectation that conditions or deferrals on at least some percentage of proposals is normal governance, not a failure.

**ARB as blocker**

Signature: cycle time exceeds 2 weeks routinely. Teams complain that the ARB is slowing delivery. Engineering leads submit proposals late or frame them to avoid ARB review. Shadow architecture proliferates.  
Root cause: scope too broad (everything goes to ARB), or the ARB rejects unfamiliar approaches without a genuine alternative, or the VP does not enforce the cycle time standard.  
Consequence: teams route around governance. The highest-risk architecture decisions are the ones made outside the process.  
Fix: the VP audits what is going to the ARB and identifies what should not be there. Simultaneously, the VP audits rejections to determine whether they included actionable alternatives. A rejection without an alternative is a failure of the ARB's responsibility.

**ARB that only reviews greenfield**

Signature: the ARB reviews new projects and new services. Existing systems are never reviewed except post-incident. The highest-risk architecture in the organization — legacy systems with accumulated debt — never receives governance attention.  
Root cause: no mechanism for reviewing existing systems proactively; ARB agenda driven entirely by new proposals.  
Fix: include an architecture health section in the monthly deep-dive that proactively surfaces existing systems for review. Post-incident architecture reviews are a required ARB agenda item — the incident finding "this architecture did not follow principle X" must produce an ARB review of that system.

**ARB without enforcement**

Signature: the ARB decides, teams acknowledge, nothing changes. Conditions are never met. Exceptions run past their 12-month window without review. Standards violations are discovered in code review or incidents, never in ARB governance.  
Root cause: governance has no connection to consequences. There is no audit mechanism. The VP does not make compliance visible or consequential.  
Consequence: governance becomes a ritual without substance. Engineers learn that ARB decisions are optional.  
Fix: the VP must personally hold the first few consequence conversations when bypass is discovered. Not the architecture team. The VP. Once teams understand that governance bypass has a VP-level consequence — not a punitive one, but a visible, named one — the cost of bypassing changes.

**ARB too large**

Signature: 10+ members. Sessions produce little because everyone defers to consensus. Accountability diffuses. The rotating representatives vote on decisions outside their domain expertise. Sessions run long and reach weak conclusions.  
Root cause: governance body was grown to include stakeholders rather than decision-makers.  
Fix: reduce to 5-7 members. Stakeholders who want visibility can attend as observers with no vote. Every member must be able to make a decision, not just attend a meeting.

> **What the VP decides**: ARB composition, what categories of decision require ARB review, what the VP's own accountability is for ARB decisions — including decisions made while the VP was chair, and decisions made in bypass of the ARB while the VP was accountable for governance.

> **What happens without the ARB**: the first 90 days of a new VP's tenure is often spent discovering what was built without governance — the archaeology tax. Services using Hold-ring technologies because no one checked. Cross-domain dependencies that no one mapped. Standards exceptions that were never formally requested, so they were never formally reviewed, so they were never formally sunset. This is recoverable with time and resources. It is not recoverable on a delivery timeline.

## Summary

| Concept | Operational Meaning |
|---|---|
| ARB purpose | Review cross-team, standards-impacting, or principle-relevant decisions — not all decisions |
| ARB composition | 5 voting members; specific roles, not seniority; odd-number to prevent ties |
| What goes to ARB | Cross-domain, standards-deviating, Tier 1 system-affecting, shared-capability decisions |
| ARB cadence | Weekly async-first lightweight; monthly synchronous deep-dive; ad hoc for security/incidents |
| Decision framework | Principle alignment → standards compliance → operational readiness → ADR quality → dependency risk |
| Decision outcomes | Approve / Approve with conditions / Defer / Reject with alternative — never bare "No" |
| VP's ARB role | Agenda, bottleneck prevention, tie-breaking, escalation, executive reporting |
| ARB anti-patterns | Rubber stamp, blocker, greenfield-only, no enforcement, too large |
| Governance health metrics | Cycle time, rejection rate, ADR coverage, exception count, condition close rate |
