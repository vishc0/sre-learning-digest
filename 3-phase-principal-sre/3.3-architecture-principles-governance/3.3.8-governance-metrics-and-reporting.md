## 1. Why Metrics Matter for Governance

Governance without metrics is hope-based management. You cannot improve what you do not measure. More specifically: you cannot *defend* what you do not measure. When the board asks whether architecture governance is working, "we have an ARB" is not an answer — compliance rate, cycle time, and debt trend are answers.

The VP needs two classes of governance metrics:

- **Leading indicators** — governance process health. Are we doing the right things? Are the mechanisms functioning? These predict future outcomes.
- **Lagging indicators** — governance outcome quality. Are the right things working? Did our processes produce the outcomes we intended? These confirm past decisions.

Neither class alone is sufficient. Leading indicators without lagging confirmation means you are measuring effort, not effect. Lagging indicators without leading indicators means you only discover failures after they produce visible damage.

### The Three Reporting Contexts

Governance metrics serve three distinct audiences with different information needs:

| Context | Audience | Cadence | Primary Need |
|---------|---------|---------|-------------|
| Operational rhythm | Engineering leadership, architecture team | Weekly / Monthly | Early warning, course correction |
| Executive reporting | CTO, CPO, CFO, VP peers | Quarterly | Trend, investment decisions, risk posture |
| Board-level risk reporting | Board, audit committee | Annual (or triggered) | Risk materiality, investment ask, compliance posture |

The mistake is using the same metrics package for all three audiences. The board does not need ARB cycle time. Engineering leadership does not need the financial translation of debt interest rates. Tailor the package to the decision the audience needs to make.

## 2. The 8 Core Governance Metrics

---

### Metric 1: Architecture Compliance Rate

**Definition**: The percentage of new systems and significant changes that passed architecture review with no principle violations at launch.

**Calculation**:
```
Compliance Rate = (Compliant Launches) / (Total Launches Reviewed) × 100
```

A "compliant launch" is one where no principle violations were identified in the ARB review or in the post-launch PIR review. Violations discovered post-launch that were missed in ARB review count against compliance — the ARB review quality is also being measured.

**Leading or Lagging**: Lagging — measures the outcome of the governance process, not the process health itself.

**Target**: >90% for Tier 1 systems; >80% overall across all tiers.

**Bad Trend**: Compliance rate declining quarter-over-quarter. Three possible causes:
1. Principles are unclear — teams cannot apply them consistently
2. ARB is rubber-stamping — reviews are not substantive
3. Teams are bypassing the review process — systems are launching without going through ARB

**When It Goes Wrong**: Audit which principles are most frequently violated. If one principle accounts for 60% of violations, the problem is likely that principle's clarity or enforceability, not team behavior. If violations are distributed across principles and domains, the ARB review process itself needs examination.

---

### Metric 2: Standards Exception Count and Trend

**Definition**: The number of active approved exceptions to technology standards, tracked as a running balance and a quarterly flow (new exceptions added, exceptions closed/expired).

**Calculation**:
```
Active Exceptions (balance) = exceptions approved and not yet closed
New Exceptions (flow) = exceptions approved this quarter
Closed Exceptions (flow) = exceptions retired/expired this quarter
Net Exception Change = New - Closed
```

**Leading or Lagging**: Leading — exceptions today become operational debt and support complexity tomorrow. A rising exception count predicts future platform fragmentation.

**Target**: Fewer than 10 active exceptions total; net exception change trending toward zero or negative (more exceptions closing than opening) year-over-year.

**Bad Trend**: Exception count rising consistently. This has two possible root causes that require different responses:
- Standards are too rigid — teams cannot realistically comply, so exceptions are the norm. Trigger a standards review.
- Teams are choosing non-standard technology without sufficient justification — exceptions are being granted too readily. Tighten ARB exception criteria.

**When It Goes Wrong**: The first action is to audit exceptions without sunset plans. An exception with no committed retirement date is a permanent deviation masquerading as a temporary one. Force a sunset date on every open exception. Then assess whether the standard the exception departs from remains the right standard.

---

### Metric 3: ADR Coverage Rate

**Definition**: The percentage of significant architectural decisions — as defined by the ARB review criteria — that have a completed Architecture Decision Record (ADR).

**Calculation**:
```
ADR Coverage Rate = (Decisions with ADRs) / (Total Decisions Requiring ADRs) × 100
```

The denominator requires an ARB decision log as the authoritative count of decisions requiring ADRs. Without the decision log, the denominator is unknown and the metric is unmeasurable.

**Leading or Lagging**: Leading — ADR coverage predicts the organization's institutional memory quality. Low coverage means decisions are being made, executed, and forgotten. The institutional knowledge walks out the door with the engineer who made the decision.

**Target**: >95% for ARB-reviewed decisions; >80% for Tier 2 decisions made outside ARB scope.

**Bad Trend**: ADR coverage falling — decisions are being made and executed without documentation. This frequently accompanies high delivery pressure: teams skip ADRs when moving fast, and the organizational memory debt accumulates quietly until a key engineer leaves or a system needs to be understood by someone new.

**When It Goes Wrong**: Assign the ADR backlog remediation to the architecture team — retroactively documenting decisions from the ARB decision log and engineering records. Make ADR completion a gate on the next quarter's architectural planning cycle: no new decisions logged until outstanding ADRs for prior decisions are complete.

---

### Metric 4: ARB Cycle Time

**Definition**: The median time from architecture review request submission to ARB decision (approved, rejected, or returned for revision).

**Calculation**:
```
ARB Cycle Time = median(Decision Date - Submission Date) across all ARB decisions in the period
```

Use median, not mean — outlier decisions (complex cross-domain proposals, contested decisions) will skew mean cycle time and obscure the typical engineer experience.

**Leading or Lagging**: Leading — cycle time predicts whether ARB will become a delivery bottleneck. If engineers learn that ARB takes 3 weeks, they will either submit requests earlier (good) or start work before approval (bad, and common).

**Target**: Median <5 business days for Tier 2 decisions; median <10 business days for Tier 1 decisions.

**Bad Trend**: Cycle time rising. This is the governance equivalent of a queue building up. When cycle time rises consistently, teams respond by routing around the ARB process — submitting after decisions are already made, presenting fait accompli proposals, or simply not submitting at all. By the time cycle time is visibly high, bypass behavior is already established.

**When It Goes Wrong**: Three levers — review ARB agenda management (are meetings frequent enough relative to submission volume?), consider async approval for standard-compliant decisions (not every decision needs a synchronous meeting), and check whether submissions are arriving with complete information (incomplete submissions cause iteration cycles that inflate cycle time).

---

### Metric 5: Technical Debt Ratio

**Definition**: The total outstanding debt balance (engineer-weeks to remediate all items in the debt register) compared to the prior quarter baseline.

**Calculation**:
```
Debt Balance = sum of Fix Cost estimates for all non-Retired items in the debt register
Debt Ratio Change = (Current Quarter Balance - Prior Quarter Balance) / Prior Quarter Balance × 100
```

**Leading or Lagging**: Lagging — measures the accumulated outcome of governance decisions over time. The debt ratio reflects whether the governance system is controlling debt accumulation.

**Target**: Debt ratio declining or stable year-over-year. A stable ratio means debt generation equals debt retirement — the balance is not growing. A declining ratio means the organization is net-retiring debt.

**Bad Trend**: Debt ratio rising quarter-over-quarter for three or more consecutive quarters. Single-quarter increases are acceptable (a new platform debt item was identified and added to the register). Consecutive increases indicate systemic debt accumulation that the 20% debt budget is not controlling.

**When It Goes Wrong**: Two immediate questions — (1) is the 20% capacity allocation actually being honored, or is it being consumed by feature work? (2) Is a specific team or domain generating disproportionate debt? Domain-level debt breakdown is essential for the VP to direct the intervention at the right level.

---

### Metric 6: Debt Burn Rate

**Definition**: The engineer-weeks of debt retired per quarter — how fast the organization is paying down the outstanding debt balance.

**Calculation**:
```
Debt Burn Rate = sum of Fix Cost estimates for items moved to "Retired" status in the quarter
```

**Leading or Lagging**: Leading — burn rate predicts whether debt will be brought under control. If burn rate equals or exceeds debt generation rate, the debt balance stabilizes. If burn rate consistently lags generation rate, the balance grows without bound.

**Target**: Burn rate ≥ debt generation rate (net debt change ≤ 0 each quarter).

**Bad Trend**: Burn rate consistently below generation rate. The debt balance is accelerating. At some compounding factor, this trajectory ends in a forced crisis — the "rewrite everything" moment or a major operational failure.

**When It Goes Wrong**: Identify which teams are generating the highest debt volume. Review their sprint allocation to confirm the 20% policy is being honored. Escalate to VP-level conversation: if the product roadmap is consuming the debt budget, the VP must make the call on what gets traded — not the engineering team.

---

### Metric 7: Policy Violation Rate

**Definition**: The number of confirmed policy violations per quarter, broken down by policy and by domain.

**Calculation**:
```
Violation Rate = count of confirmed violations identified through audit, incident review, and ARB findings per quarter
```

Track at two levels: total violations (trend) and violations by domain (accountability) and by policy (enforceability).

**Leading or Lagging**: Lagging — violations are after-the-fact observations. However, violation patterns across quarters become leading indicators of systemic non-compliance.

**Target**: Declining trend overall; fewer than 5 confirmed violations per domain per quarter.

**Bad Trend**: Two distinct bad patterns:

1. **Domain concentration** — the majority of violations come from one domain. This is a domain leadership conversation. Either the domain VP is not enforcing policies, or the domain has unique constraints that make compliance genuinely difficult and the policies need revision.

2. **Policy concentration** — the majority of violations involve one specific policy. This means either the policy is unclear (engineers do not know what compliance looks like), unenforceable (no mechanism to detect violations until after the fact), or uncommunicated (engineers are not aware the policy applies to them).

**When It Goes Wrong**: Root-cause each violation cluster. Ask four questions: Is the policy clear enough to audit? Is the policy enforceable? Is the policy communicated to the teams it governs? Is the policy still the right policy? If any answer is "no," the policy needs revision — not stronger enforcement of an unfit rule.

---

### Metric 8: Document Freshness Rate

**Definition**: The percentage of governance documents reviewed within their required cadence.

**Calculation**:
```
Freshness Rate = (Documents Reviewed On Time) / (Documents with Review Due in Period) × 100
```

A document is "reviewed on time" if its review was completed by the "Next Review Due" date in the registry.

**Leading or Lagging**: Leading — stale documents are governance gaps waiting to be discovered. Document freshness predicts whether the governance system remains grounded in operational reality.

**Target**: >95%.

**Bad Trend**: Freshness rate below 90% indicates systemic review debt. Documents are accumulating past their review dates without being assessed. The governance system is running on outdated rules.

**When It Goes Wrong**: Three interventions — (1) assign review ownership more specifically (if the owner is "the architecture team," no individual is accountable; assign a named reviewer), (2) add automated calendar reminders for review dates linked to the governance registry, (3) VP personal review of the overdue document list as a signal that the governance maintenance function is not operating. If the VP needs to review the list personally, the system is not self-sustaining and requires structural attention.

## 3. Executive Reporting Package

The quarterly governance report to the executive team contains five elements. One page of dashboard, one appendix of supporting data. The executive team needs the summary and the trend — not the raw metrics.

### Element 1: Architecture Compliance Scorecard

- Compliance rate by domain and tier, with trend vs. prior quarter
- Exceptions requiring executive attention (exceptions that are overdue, contested, or represent significant risk)
- Any principle violations discovered post-launch and their remediation status

### Element 2: Technical Debt Summary

- Total debt balance (engineer-weeks and cost equivalent)
- Net debt change this quarter (generated vs. retired)
- Burn rate vs. generation rate — are we winning or losing?
- "Kills" category items and their current status (open / in remediation / retired)

### Element 3: Governance Process Health

- ARB cycle time trend
- ADR coverage rate
- Policy violation rate by domain — the accountability signal

All three with quarter-over-quarter trend. Green/yellow/red against the targets in Section 2.

### Element 4: Top 3 Architecture Risks

The VP's qualitative assessment of the three architecture risks most likely to generate operational impact in the next 12 months. Not generated by a metric — the VP synthesizes the metrics and applies operational judgment. Format:

```
Risk: [description]
Likelihood: H/M/L
Impact: H/M/L
Current Mitigation: [what is in place]
Gap: [what is not in place]
Recommended Action: [investment or decision needed]
```

### Element 5: Standards Evolution

Any significant Technology Radar changes proposed for the next quarter. Any major standard being deprecated and the migration timeline for teams using it. The executive team needs advance notice of standard changes that affect roadmap planning.

## 4. Board-Level Architecture Risk Reporting

Board reporting is annual for baseline posture and triggered by: a major operational incident with broad customer or financial impact, a regulatory finding related to architecture or technology, or a material change in the technology risk environment.

### Risk Framing for the Board

Architecture risk is presented in the three categories established in ch06, translated to financial and strategic terms:

**Reliability Risk**: "Our current architecture posture generates an estimated X major incidents per year with an average cost of $Y. Our debt reduction program targeting Z items is projected to reduce this by W% over 12 months."

**Velocity Risk**: "Platform debt is currently consuming approximately N% of engineering capacity in maintenance vs. the target of 20%. This represents a delivery gap of approximately M engineer-years per year in deferred product investment."

**Security Risk**: "We carry X unpatched critical CVEs across our technology estate. Y of these have known exploit paths. The estimated breach cost exposure from the highest-risk item is $Z, against a remediation cost of $W."

### Debt as a Financial Risk Statement

Do not present technical debt to the board in engineer-weeks. Present it in the financial terms the board uses to make investment decisions:

```
Outstanding Debt Balance:     $X.XM (equivalent remediation cost)
Annual Interest Rate:         $X.XM (operational cost carried per year)
Payback Period at Current     X.X years
  Burn Rate:
Probability of Forced Crisis  X% within 18 months (qualitative estimate)
  if Unaddressed:
Board-Requested Investment:   $XM over X quarters
Projected Risk Reduction:     X% reliability improvement, X% velocity improvement
```

### Governance Posture Statement

"We have formal governance covering X% of our technology surface area, enforced through the ARB, policy audit mechanisms, and the debt register. The ungoverned Y% consists of [specific description — legacy systems, acquired technology, etc.] and carries the following risk profile: [summary]. Our 12-month governance expansion plan addresses [specific items]."

This framing turns governance into a quantifiable risk position rather than a process description. Boards respond to risk positions and investment cases — not process diagrams.

---

> **What the VP decides**: which metrics to report, at what cadence, to which audience. The VP owns governance transparency — not just governance execution. A VP who cannot report clearly on governance health to the board cannot make the case for governance investment, and cannot hold the line on governance when delivery pressure peaks.

> **What happens without metrics**: governance becomes performative. The ARB exists, the principles document exists, the debt register exists — but no one can tell if they are working. You discover the failure when a major incident traces to a principle violation that should have been caught in ARB review, or when an audit finds that 40% of your active technology stack is ungoverned. By that point, the remediation cost is substantially higher than the measurement cost would have been.
