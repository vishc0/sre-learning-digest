# Technical Debt Review Playbook — VP and Architecture Team

**Audience**: VP (chairs and decides), architecture team (prepares and tracks), domain engineering leads (report)  
**Purpose**: Run a quarterly debt review that produces actual decisions, not just visibility  
**Cadence**: Once per quarter, 2 hours, VP + architecture team + domain engineering leads  
**Input artifacts**: Debt register (all domains), incident data (debt-correlated incidents), capacity data (engineer-weeks on debt vs. new work), ADR coverage report

---

## What This Review Must Produce

A debt review that ends without explicit VP decisions is a status meeting, not a governance event. This review must end with:
- Named owners for all "kills" category items
- Specific capacity allocations per domain for Q+1
- Any domains authorized to reduce feature velocity to address debt
- Board reporting package outline signed off by VP

If the session ends without these, it failed.

---

## Step 1: Debt Register Update (1 Week Before Review)

**Owner: each domain engineering lead**  
**Due: 5 business days before the quarterly review session**

Each domain lead is responsible for submitting an updated register with these actions completed:

- [ ] All known debt items entered with current status
- [ ] Fix cost estimates reviewed and updated — costs change as systems evolve; a 2-year-old estimate is unreliable
- [ ] All items scheduled for retirement this quarter: status updated to In Progress, Retired, or Delayed with explanation
- [ ] New debt discovered this quarter: assessed, scored, and entered
- [ ] Priority scores recalculated using current annual interest estimates — the score is a function of recurrence cost, not just technical severity

**Debt scoring reminder:**

| Score Component | What to Measure |
|----------------|----------------|
| Annual Interest | Engineer-hours per quarter spent working around this item × 4 |
| Fix Cost | Engineer-weeks to resolve completely |
| Operational Risk | Probability this item contributes to a production incident in the next 12 months (L/M/H) |
| Debt Ratio | Fix Cost / Annual Interest — items with ratio < 1 (pays back within the year) are highest priority |

**Leads who do not submit by the deadline:** Their domain does not get a dedicated review slot. Their debt items are included in the aggregate data only. This is enforced — it protects the session time and creates accountability for pre-work.

---

## Step 2: Pre-Review Analysis (Architecture Team, 2 Days Before)

**Owner: architecture team lead**

Prepare the following before the session. These are presented as context for VP decision-making, not debated during the session.

**Debt balance summary**
- Total debt balance across all domains (in engineer-weeks)
- Change from prior quarter: net direction (increasing / decreasing / flat)
- Burn rate vs. generation rate: are teams retiring more debt than they're generating? If not, the 20% allocation is not being honored or is not sufficient.
- Debt-to-capacity ratio: what percentage of total engineering capacity is this debt balance consuming in annual interest?

**Top 10 by priority score**
- Highest-priority items across all domains, sorted by priority score
- Flag any that have moved up significantly since last quarter (escalating items)
- Flag any that have been on the top 10 for 3+ consecutive quarters (chronic items)

**"Kills" category check**
Items classified as operational kills: their annual interest exceeds their fix cost AND they have generated at least one production incident in the last 12 months. These items get dedicated VP attention regardless of domain.

**Incident correlation**
- Which production incidents this quarter had technical debt as a contributing factor? (Source: PIR database, tagged to debt items)
- What is the aggregate incident cost attributable to debt? (Translate to engineer-hours and customer impact if possible)
- Are any debt items generating incidents at an accelerating rate?

**Domain comparison**
- Debt balance per domain (absolute and normalized by team size)
- Quarter-over-quarter change per domain
- Which domains are net-positive (retiring more than generating)? Which are net-negative?
- Any domain showing consistently worsening trend for 2+ quarters?

**ADR coverage report**
- What percentage of architectural decisions made this quarter have associated ADRs?
- Any domains with significantly below-average coverage? That is a debt signal itself.

## Step 3: The Review Session (2 Hours)

```
0:00 – 0:15  | Debt balance summary (architecture team presents, VP questions)
              |   Total balance, net direction, burn vs. generation rate
              |   "Kills" category items called out immediately
              |   VP may stop here if the trend is significantly worse than expected

0:15 – 0:45  | Domain reports (10 minutes each, max 3 domains per session)
              |   Rotate domains quarterly — every domain is in the spotlight once per year
              |   Each domain lead covers:
              |     - Current debt balance (absolute and vs. prior quarter)
              |     - Debt retired this quarter: what was fixed, how long it took, cost vs. estimate
              |     - New debt generated: what was added, why, what created it
              |       (note: "delivery pressure" is a valid and honest answer — the question is whether the debt was conscious)
              |     - Progress on items scheduled from last quarter's review
              |   VP questions after each domain report. Architecture team does not interject.

0:45 – 1:15  | Priority scoring and Tier 1 decisions (VP-led)
              |   Review top 10 items across all domains
              |   For each "kills" category item: VP assigns a named owner and a target resolution quarter
              |   For items with both high operational risk AND high fix cost: VP decides whether this requires
              |     a dedicated program (ring-fenced capacity, separate tracking) or domain-team ownership
              |   Items with active CVEs or regulatory implications: flagged for security/compliance team involvement

1:15 – 1:45  | Next quarter planning (VP makes explicit allocations)
              |   Which debt items are scheduled for Q+1 retirement? Named items, not categories.
              |   Capacity allocation per domain: what percentage of Q+1 capacity is allocated to debt retirement?
              |   Any domains authorized to reduce feature velocity? This is a VP decision, not a domain decision.
              |   Any items that need VP-level escalation to product/business stakeholders?
              |     (Debt retirement that conflicts with product roadmap requires VP to broker the conversation)

1:45 – 2:00  | Board reporting package — VP frames the narrative
              |   What is the story this quarter? Improving, stable, or deteriorating?
              |   What is the VP asking the board to understand or approve?
              |   Architecture team captures the narrative for the package
```

---

## Step 4: VP Capacity Allocation Decisions

These decisions are made at the end of the session. They are not recommendations — they are VP-level directives communicated to domain leads.

**Decision 1: Dedicated program items**  
Which debt items require dedicated engineering capacity (not just "team allocation within sprint")? These items get named program owners, separate tracking, and VP-level review checkpoints.

**Decision 2: Domain velocity adjustments**  
Which domains are authorized to reduce feature velocity to address debt? This requires VP conversation with product stakeholders — the VP owns that conversation, not the engineering lead.

**Decision 3: "Kills" category assignments**  
Every item classified as operational kills gets a named owner and a target resolution quarter. No kills item leaves the session without an owner. If no one can own it, that is an organizational capacity gap the VP resolves — not something that gets deferred.

**Decision 4: 20% allocation enforcement**  
If any domain is consistently below the 20% allocation target for debt retirement: the VP addresses this with the domain lead directly. This conversation does not happen in the review session — it happens in a 1:1. The session documents the gap; the VP closes it separately.

## Step 5: Board Reporting Package

**Owner: architecture team (drafts), VP (reviews and signs off)**  
**Due: 5 business days after the review session**

The board does not need operational detail. The board needs to understand: what is our risk posture, are we getting better or worse, and what investment is needed.

---

**Section 1: Debt Posture Snapshot**

| Metric | This Quarter | Prior Quarter | Trend |
|--------|-------------|---------------|-------|
| Total debt balance (eng-weeks) | | | ↑/↓/→ |
| Equivalent dollar value (at loaded eng cost) | | | |
| Annual interest (eng-weeks/yr) | | | |
| Debt retired this quarter (eng-weeks) | | | |
| Net change | | | |

Projected payback timeline for all currently scheduled retirement: state in quarters, not years. The board will ask "when does this go away?" — have the answer.

---

**Section 2: Risk Highlights**

"Kills" category items — present each with:
- What system it affects
- The operational risk (what happens if not addressed)
- Current mitigation in place (how we are managing around it today)
- Resolution plan and target quarter

Debt with active CVEs: name the CVE, state severity, state remediation plan and timeline.

Debt with regulatory implications (PCI-DSS, GDPR, FCC): name the regulation, state the exposure, state the remediation plan. If there is active audit risk, the board must know.

---

**Section 3: Investment Ask (if applicable)**

If the VP is requesting additional investment for debt reduction, make the ROI case:
- Debt cost this quarter (annual interest, in dollars): this is what we are paying to carry the debt
- Remediation cost (one-time, in dollars): this is the investment to eliminate it
- Risk cost (probability × impact, in dollars): this is what a debt-driven incident costs us
- Payback period: if remediation cost is $X and we save $Y per year in interest: $X / $Y = payback in years

Do not ask the board for investment without this math. "Technical debt is bad" is not an investment case.

---

**Section 4: Progress Since Last Quarter**

**Debt retired (the wins):**
- What was fixed
- Cost vs. estimate (were our estimates accurate?)
- Operational improvement resulting from the fix (reduction in incident rate, reduction in workaround time)

**New debt generated (the accountability section):**
- What was added this quarter
- Why (delivery pressure, emergency fix, architectural decision with accepted trade-offs)
- Mitigation plan (if any)

This section matters. A board that only sees progress and never sees what was added will eventually discover the omission. Transparency here builds credibility.

## Common Debt Review Failures

**Debt register not updated before the session.**  
The review becomes a conversation about what people remember, not what is actually true. Enforce the 5-day submission deadline without exception. The VP should reject the review and reschedule if the register is not ready — doing the review on incomplete data produces decisions that will need to be revised.

**Review becomes a blame session.**  
Debt was generated by teams making rational decisions under real constraints. "Who created this mess?" is the wrong question. "What is our posture and what do we do about it?" is the right question. The VP sets this tone from the opening statement. If the VP allows blame, domain leads will underreport debt to avoid scrutiny.

**No VP decisions at the end.**  
If the VP defers all decisions to "offline discussion," nothing changes. The capacity allocation is the output of this meeting. Every item in the top 10 either gets a decision or the reason for deferral is documented. Ambiguity after the meeting is a meeting failure.

**Board package that only shows progress.**  
Boards that only see improvement eventually stop believing the reports. Show the risk posture — both the progress made and the risks that remain. A VP who presents only good news to the board and then has a debt-driven outage has a credibility problem.

**The 20% allocation treated as aspirational.**  
If teams are consistently below 20% allocation for debt retirement, either the metric is not being enforced or the allocation is genuinely impossible given delivery commitments. The VP determines which: if it's enforcement, enforce it; if it's a delivery conflict, the VP brokers the conversation with product stakeholders. Treating the target as aspirational produces zero debt retirement.
