# Standards Exception Playbook — Engineering Teams

**Audience**: Engineering teams that need to use a technology outside approved standards  
**Purpose**: Navigate the exception process correctly, with full understanding of ongoing obligations  
**Owner**: Architecture team (maintains process); ARB Chair (approves Tier 1 exceptions)

---

## What Triggers an Exception

A standards exception is required when you need to use a technology that is:

- In the **Hold** ring of the Technology Radar — for any new usage, including legacy migration work
- **Not on the Technology Radar at all** — net new technology not previously assessed
- A **direct replacement** for an approved technology without going through the Trial → Adopt evaluation process

An exception is **not** required when:
- The technology is in the **Trial** ring — Trial means "use with intent to evaluate; proceed"
- The technology is in the **Adopt** ring — this is the approved path, no exception needed
- You are **continuing to operate** legacy systems built on a deprecated technology — operating existing systems is not new adoption. New workloads on that same deprecated technology require an exception.

**When in doubt:** Ask the architecture team before writing the exception request. A 15-minute conversation is faster than a rejected submission.

## The Exception Request Process

### Step 1: Confirm the Exception Is Genuinely Needed

Before investing time in the exception request, answer these questions honestly:

**Have you evaluated all approved alternatives?**  
The exception request requires this evaluation in writing. If you haven't done it yet, do it first. "We prefer this tool" is not a business case the ARB will accept.

**Does an approved technology genuinely not meet your requirements?**  
Preference, familiarity, and team experience are not technical gaps. The gap must be specific: a capability the approved technology lacks, a performance threshold it cannot meet, a licensing constraint that prevents use.

**If performance is the driver: have you benchmarked the approved alternative?**  
The ARB will ask. If you say "the approved technology can't handle our throughput" without a benchmark to back it up, expect the ARB to defer until you have data.

If after this self-check you find an approved alternative does meet your requirements: use it. No exception needed.

### Step 2: Complete the Exception Request

The exception request is a standalone document. Required sections:

---

**Business Case**

State specifically why this project needs this technology. The requirement cannot be met by any approved alternative — explain what that requirement is and why it matters.

Be specific:
- Not: "We need better performance"
- Yes: "Our order-matching service requires sub-5ms p99 latency under 50K TPS. We benchmarked [approved alternative] and achieved 12ms p99 at 40K TPS — 2.4x above our threshold."

Quantify where possible: latency targets, throughput requirements, specific feature capabilities, licensing constraints, integration requirements.

---

**Technical Assessment**

List every approved alternative evaluated. For each:

| Alternative | Requirement Tested | How Tested | Result | Conclusion |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|--------|------------|
| [Tool A] | Sub-5ms p99 latency | Benchmark at 50K TPS | 12ms p99 | Fails requirement |
| [Tool B] | Native streaming support | Architecture analysis | Does not support push model | Incompatible |

"We evaluated it and it doesn't work" is not a technical assessment. Show your work.

---

**Operational Readiness**

Who will own this technology operationally?

- Named team and individual owner (primary on-call)
- Monitoring plan: what metrics, what dashboards, what Splunk queries or Datadog monitors will exist at launch?
- Runbook location and owner: where will it live, who writes it, who keeps it current?
- On-call coverage: is this team already on-call for this service, or does rotation need to be updated?
- Training plan: does your team have the skills to operate this technology? If not, what is the ramp plan?

If you cannot name a specific owner today, the exception will not be approved. Ownerless technology is how incidents without runbooks happen.

---

**Risk Assessment**

Assess the risks your exception creates. Do not minimize them — the ARB will probe harder on risks that seem understated.

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Non-standard technology creates operational burden | M | M | Named owner, documented runbook, on-call training |
| Vendor ends support / pricing changes | L | H | Vendor commitment letter obtained; evaluated open-source fork |
| Security posture of non-standard tech is unknown | M | H | Security assessment scheduled for Q+1; CVE monitoring configured |
| Exception creates pressure for similar exceptions elsewhere | M | M | Exception scope limited to this service; ARB tracks precedent |

---

**Sunset Plan**

Is this a permanent adoption or a bridge technology?

**If permanent adoption path:** What is the process to promote this technology from exception to Trial? What evaluation criteria and timeline? Who is accountable for driving the promotion proposal?

**If bridge technology:** What is the specific migration target? What is the phased migration plan? When will the exception be resolved — state a quarter, not "eventually." Who owns the migration execution?

"We'll migrate eventually" is not a sunset plan. A sunset plan has a named owner, a target state, and a deadline.

---

### Step 3: Architecture Team Initial Review

Submit the completed exception request to the architecture team.

**Architecture team reviews within 5 business days** and provides a recommendation:

- **Support**: business case is genuine, alternatives were properly evaluated, operational plan is credible
- **Support with Modifications**: modifications required to the operational plan or sunset commitment before it goes to ARB
- **Do Not Support**: business case insufficient or approved alternatives were not genuinely evaluated — returned with specific gaps to address

The architecture team recommendation is not the final decision. It is a technical assessment that goes to the ARB alongside your request. The ARB makes the decision.

### Step 4: ARB Review

Exception requests are placed on the next available ARB session agenda.

**Your presentation: 5 minutes**
- Business case (1 minute): what requirement cannot be met by approved technology
- Alternatives evaluated (1 minute): what was tested and why it failed
- Operational readiness (1 minute): who owns it, how it will be monitored
- Risk and sunset (1 minute): what risks you're accepting and your migration/promotion plan
- Ask (30 seconds): what you are requesting the ARB to approve

**ARB questioning focuses on:**
- Was the business case genuine? Is this a real technical gap or a preference?
- Were alternatives evaluated seriously?
- Is the operational plan credible — can this team actually run this?
- Is the sunset plan binding or aspirational?

**ARB decisions:**
- **Approve**: exception granted under the terms of your sunset plan
- **Approve with Conditions**: additional conditions attached (more frequent review, security assessment deadline, etc.)
- **Reject with Alternative Recommendation**: ARB specifies a specific path using approved technology; you evaluate the recommendation and respond

### Step 5: VP Approval (Tier 1 Systems Only)

Exceptions in Tier 1 systems require VP approval in addition to ARB approval.

The VP reviews:
- The ARB recommendation (Support or Approve)
- The operational risk the exception creates for the Tier 1 system
- Whether the exception creates precedent that affects other Tier 1 decisions

VP approval is documented in the ARB decision log. The VP may add conditions beyond what the ARB specified.

## If Your Exception Is Approved — Your Ongoing Obligations

Approval is not a one-time event. These obligations begin at approval and continue until the exception is resolved.

**Technology Radar placement**  
Your exception is added to the Technology Radar in the Trial ring, scoped to your project. This makes the exception visible to the organization and prevents others from adopting the same technology without awareness of the evaluation in progress.

**90-day evaluation report**  
Within 90 days of approval, you deliver an evaluation report to the ARB:
- Is the technology performing as expected?
- Have new operational risks emerged?
- Update on the sunset plan timeline

**Sunset plan execution**  
Your sunset plan deadline is binding. Slippage requires a formal update to the ARB with a revised timeline and root cause. Repeated slippage escalates to the VP.

**Annual renewal**  
Exceptions do not auto-renew. Renewal requires:
- Evidence the technology is still needed (the original requirement still exists)
- Progress against the sunset plan (what has been completed since last renewal?)
- Operational incident record (exceptions generating incidents are scrutinized more heavily)
- Updated risk assessment

**Operational incident accountability**  
If your exception technology generates incidents, the exception cost becomes visible. High-incident exceptions accelerate review cycles and may be revoked if the operational burden is disproportionate.

---

## Exception Renewal and Sunset Enforcement

**Annual review process:**
The architecture team runs an exception audit prior to each annual cycle. For each open exception:
- Architecture team reaches out to the named owner 60 days before renewal date
- Owner submits renewal case or resolution notice
- ARB reviews at the session following the renewal date

**Escalation triggers:**
- Exception older than 2 years without a promotion path to Trial/Adopt: escalated to VP
- Sunset plan deadline missed without a filed extension: escalated to VP
- Exception generating more than 2 production incidents in a quarter: accelerated review cycle

**Escalation is not punitive — it is accountability.** The VP's involvement signals organizational priority, not disciplinary action. Named owners who proactively flag delays are treated differently from those who go silent until escalated.

## Common Exception Mistakes

**Requesting an exception without evaluating alternatives**  
The ARB returns this immediately. "We prefer Kafka" when RabbitMQ is the approved standard is not an exception request — it is a preference statement. Come back when you have benchmarks or a documented capability gap.

**No operational ownership identified**  
Exceptions without a named owner are not approved. If your team cannot commit to owning this technology operationally, you should not be adopting it.

**Sunset plan with no date**  
"We'll migrate eventually" does not meet the bar. Give a quarter. If you genuinely cannot commit to a date, state why and propose a date for that commitment instead. The ARB can work with uncertainty if you name it explicitly.

**Retroactive exception request**  
If your team has already deployed a non-standard technology without an exception, this is a policy violation, not just a process gap. Submit the retroactive exception immediately. Flag it as retroactive. The ARB will still review and decide — but the deployment status does not influence the decision. You may be required to remediate. Burying it is worse than surfacing it.

**Requesting exception for a technology in active Hold**  
Technologies in Hold are there for a reason — security risk, active CVEs, abandoned maintenance, architectural conflict. Exceptions for Hold technologies require a significantly stronger business case and typically require VP involvement regardless of system tier. Don't treat Hold as just another radar quadrant.
