# Architecture Review Request Playbook — Engineering Teams

**Audience**: Engineering teams submitting proposals to the ARB  
**Purpose**: Navigate the ARB submission process correctly, on first attempt  
**Owner**: Architecture team (maintains this guide); ARB Chair (enforces it)

---

## Do You Need an ARB Review?

Work through this checklist. If you answer YES to any item, you need ARB review before proceeding.

- [ ] You are introducing a new service that other teams will depend on
- [ ] You are creating a new integration between two existing services that did not previously communicate
- [ ] You are selecting a technology not in the Adopt or Trial ring of the Technology Radar
- [ ] You are making a breaking change to a public API
- [ ] You are changing data ownership (moving data between services, changing schema ownership, consolidating data stores)
- [ ] You are proposing a structural change to a Tier 1 system's core architecture
- [ ] You need an exception to a technology standard
- [ ] Your change materially affects the observability, security, or compliance posture of a critical system
- [ ] Your change introduces a new data residency or data sovereignty constraint

**If none of the above apply:** You do not need ARB review. Follow your team's design review process and document the decision in a team-level ADR. If you are unsure whether your change crosses any of these thresholds, ask the architecture team — a 15-minute consult is faster than a rejected submission.

## Submission Process — Step by Step

### Step 1: Complete the ADR Draft

File: `templates/adr-template.md`

Minimum for submission:

| Section | What "Complete" Means |
|---------|----------------------|
| Title | Specific enough to identify the decision — not "Choose Database" |
| Status | Set to `Proposed` |
| Context | Why this decision is needed now. What constraints exist. What happens if no decision is made. |
| Options Considered | At least 2 alternatives. For each: pros, cons, why you're not choosing it (if not chosen). |
| Decision | The option you are recommending and why. |
| Consequences | What becomes easier, what becomes harder, what you are explicitly accepting. |

**Context section failures that get proposals returned:**
- "We needed to choose a database" — this is a category, not context
- "The current approach doesn't scale" — scale to what? What is the specific bottleneck?
- "The team prefers this technology" — preference is not an architectural driver

Write context as if you are briefing someone who has never seen your system. State the problem, the constraint, and what makes this decision consequential.

### Step 2: Complete the Architecture Review Request Form

File: `templates/architecture-review-request-template.md`

The review request is separate from the ADR. It gives the ARB the operational and cross-functional view. Required sections:

**System description and scope**  
What system or component is changing? What is in scope and what is explicitly out of scope?

**Principles self-assessment**  
For each of the 12 architecture principles:
- Mark: Compliant / Not Applicable / Exception Required
- For anything marked Exception Required: brief rationale (1–2 sentences)
- Do not skip this section. The ARB uses it to focus questioning. A missing self-assessment tells the ARB you have not thought through compliance — which prolongs the discussion.

**Technology selections mapped to the radar**  
List every technology you are proposing to adopt, add, or remove. Map each to its current radar quadrant. For anything not on the radar, flag it explicitly — that is likely an exception trigger.

**Dependency map**  
- Services this change depends on (upstream)
- Services that depend on this change (downstream)
- New dependencies created by this change — highlight these explicitly
- If your change creates a new hard dependency between two previously independent services, that fact needs to be front and center

**Operational readiness statement**  
- What observability will be implemented (metrics, logs, traces)? How will it surface in Splunk/Datadog?
- Who owns the runbook? Where will it live?
- What is the rollback strategy? Has it been tested?
- "We will add monitoring after implementation" is not an operational readiness statement

**Exception request (if applicable)**  
If your proposal requires a technology standards exception, attach the completed exception request form. Do not embed it in the review request — it is a separate document.

### Step 3: Submit to the ARB Queue

- Submit via: [ARB intake channel / shared queue — fill in your organization's mechanism]
- Attach: ADR draft link, completed review request form, and any exception request if applicable
- State your proposed tier: Tier 1 (cross-domain, high-criticality) or Tier 2 (scoped, lower-risk)
- Submission deadline: **5 business days before the ARB session** you want to be included in

Why 5 days: the ARB chair reviews all materials before distribution, materials go to ARB members 48 hours before the session, and members need time to read before arriving. Submitting late means you wait for the next session — not that you get a rushed review.

**Tier determination:**

| Tier 1 | Tier 2 |
|--------|--------|
| Changes to core architecture of a Tier 1 system | Changes scoped within a single team's domain |
| New cross-domain dependencies | Minor technology additions with no cross-domain impact |
| Technology standard exceptions | ADR with complete materials, no obvious conflicts |
| Breaking API changes affecting multiple teams | Requires 4+ async ARB votes for non-session approval |
| New platform services (other teams will depend on) | |

If you are not sure which tier applies, default to Tier 1 and note your uncertainty in the submission. The ARB chair will reclassify if appropriate.

### Step 4: Pre-Read Distribution

The ARB chair or designated coordinator distributes your materials to all ARB members 48 hours before the session. You do not need to do this yourself. You will receive confirmation that your proposal is on the agenda.

If you do not receive confirmation within 2 business days of submission, follow up with the ARB coordinator — your submission may not have been received.

### Step 5: The ARB Session

**Your presentation slot: 5 minutes maximum.**

Cover:
1. The problem you are solving (30 seconds)
2. The decision you are recommending (30 seconds)
3. Why this option over the alternatives (2 minutes)
4. What you are asking the ARB to approve (1 minute)

Do not present the entire ADR. The ARB has read it. Use your 5 minutes to highlight what is not obvious from the document and to address anything you know will generate questions.

**During ARB questions:**
- Answer concisely
- If you do not know: say "we haven't evaluated that" — do not guess
- If a question reveals a gap: acknowledge it, do not defend it
- If asked to step out during deliberation: do so without comment

**What the ARB is evaluating:**
- Does the decision align with architecture principles?
- Were genuine alternatives considered, or was this a foregone conclusion?
- Is the operational plan credible — can this team actually run what they're proposing?
- Does this create risks or dependencies the broader organization needs to know about?

### Step 6: Receiving and Acting on the Decision

**Approval**  
Implementation may proceed. Finalize the ADR within 2 weeks of approval — update Status from `Proposed` to `Accepted`, fill in the ADR number, and publish to the ADR repository.

**Approval with Conditions**  
Implementation may proceed, but conditions must be met by the stated deadline. Conditions are binding.
- Track each condition — the ARB will check at follow-up sessions
- If a condition deadline is at risk: notify the ARB chair before the deadline, not after
- If conditions require ARB verification before going live: coordinate with the chair to schedule it

**Defer**  
The ARB has specified exactly what additional information is needed. This is not a rejection — it is an incomplete submission. Address what was specified, resubmit, and include the prior submission reference so the ARB can see what changed.

**Reject with Alternative**  
The ARB has provided a specific alternative path. Evaluate the alternative seriously — it is based on ARB knowledge of the broader system landscape.

If you believe the alternative recommendation is incorrect: escalate in writing to the ARB chair with your specific technical reasoning. Include what you evaluated about the recommended alternative and why it fails to meet your requirements. This escalation is a legitimate process — use it when genuinely needed, not as a reflexive response to rejection.

## SLA Commitments from the ARB

The ARB is accountable to these turnaround times. If they are missed, escalate to the ARB chair.

| Decision Type | SLA |
|--------------|-----|
| Initial review response (acknowledged, on agenda) | 5 business days from submission |
| Tier 2 decision | ≤ 5 business days from submission |
| Tier 1 decision | ≤ 10 business days from submission |

If the ARB misses these SLAs, the governance bottleneck is the VP's problem to solve — not a reason for your team to proceed without review. Escalate the SLA miss to the ARB chair directly.

## Common Reasons Proposals Are Returned Incomplete

These are the recurring failures. Do a self-check against this list before submitting.

**ADR context too vague**  
"We needed to choose a database" tells the ARB nothing. Write context that explains: what is the system doing, what constraint or problem drove this decision, and what the consequences of doing nothing would be.

**Only one option seriously considered**  
If your ADR has two options but one has three lines of content and the other has three paragraphs, the ARB will notice. Genuine alternatives require genuine evaluation. If you truly only had one viable option, explain why the alternatives were ruled out technically — that is still a real evaluation.

**No operational readiness plan**  
"We'll figure out monitoring later" is not acceptable. Observability is a non-negotiable principle, not a post-launch activity. Name the runbook owner, describe what metrics will be captured, and state the rollback approach before you arrive at the ARB.

**Principles self-assessment missing**  
This section exists to speed up the ARB session, not to create paperwork for you. When you skip it, the ARB has to derive it themselves during the session — which takes longer and produces less-informed decisions. Do the self-assessment; it will also surface your own compliance gaps before the ARB does.

**Submitting too late**  
A submission received 2 days before the session is reviewed at the next session. Plan your ARB submission into your sprint planning — it is a dependency, like any other.

**Retroactive submission**  
If your team has already implemented a change that required ARB review without going through the process, do not submit it as if it were prospective. Flag it as retroactive. The ARB will still review it and may require remediation. A retroactive submission is a policy violation — it is handled, but it is noted.
