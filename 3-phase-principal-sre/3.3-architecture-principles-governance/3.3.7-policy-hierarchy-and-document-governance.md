# Chapter 7: Policy Hierarchy and Document Governance

**Audience**: VP/Director Operations — governance architecture and document authority lens  
**Prerequisites**: [Architecture Review Board ch05]  
**Related Templates**: [Policy Document Template templates/policy-document-template.md], [Governance Document Registry reference/governance-document-registry-template.md]

---

## 1. Why Document Hierarchy Matters

The most common governance failure in large engineering organizations is not the absence of documentation — it is documentation that contradicts itself because documents exist at different authority levels without being distinguished.

A principle cannot be overridden by a team decision. A guideline can. If you cannot tell the difference by reading the document, you cannot govern.

The hierarchy answers one question: **who has the authority to change this, and under what conditions?** Without that answer embedded in the document itself, governance collapses to the individual with the strongest opinion in the room.

### The Real Failure Pattern

A "best practices" document that mixes non-negotiable security requirements with optional naming conventions. Engineers cannot tell which parts they must follow and which are advisory. The predictable outcome: everything is treated as optional, the security requirements are ignored, and the first audit finding reveals that the document existed but was not enforced.

This is not an engineer failure. It is a governance architecture failure. The VP owns it.

The hierarchy is a **decision authority map**: it tells every engineer, architect, and team lead which documents constrain their decisions and which inform them. Without the map, teams default to their own judgment — which is fine for guidelines and catastrophic for principles.

## 2. The Five-Level Hierarchy

### Level 1 — Principles

**Definition**: Non-negotiable constraints on all system design decisions. Principles define the architectural constitution of the organization.

**Characteristics**: Cannot be overridden by any team, domain, time pressure, or business urgency. A principle violation is never acceptable — it may be *discovered after the fact*, but it is always a finding that must be remediated, not accepted.

**Change Authority**: VP Operations and CTO co-sign. Board notification required for changes that affect compliance posture (security, privacy, regulatory).

**Enforcement Mechanism**: Architecture Review Board (ARB) review is a mandatory gate on every significant architectural decision. Principle violations discovered in ARB review block the decision until resolved. Principle violations discovered post-deployment are documented as findings in the Post-Incident Review (PIR) and trigger a remediation requirement.

**Owner**: VP Operations / CTO

**Example**: *"All production systems must emit observability telemetry conforming to the enterprise observability standard before entering production."* A team cannot opt out of this requirement. There is no exception process at the team level. If a team cannot comply, they escalate to the VP — who may grant a temporary waiver with a hard remediation date, but the principle itself does not bend.

**What Happens If Skipped**: Teams have no authoritative constraint. Every architectural decision becomes a negotiation. "Design for failure" is a suggestion rather than a requirement. Governance becomes advisory everywhere, which means it exists nowhere.

---

### Level 2 — Standards

**Definition**: Approved technology and pattern choices that teams must use unless they have a formally approved exception.

**Characteristics**: Standards change — technology evolves. The Technology Radar process (quarterly ARB review) is the mechanism for promoting, demoting, and retiring standards. An exception to a standard is not a violation — it is a formally approved departure with documented rationale and a defined sunset plan.

**Change Authority**: ARB vote, with VP approval required for any standard that affects Tier 1 systems or crosses domain boundaries.

**Enforcement Mechanism**: ARB review gate for any proposed use of non-standard technology. Exception approval process with mandatory elements: business justification, risk assessment, sunset plan, and owner accountability. Exceptions are tracked in the governance registry and reviewed quarterly.

**Owner**: Architecture team, ratified by ARB

**Example**: *"Approved message brokers: Apache Kafka for high-throughput event streaming; RabbitMQ for task queues and point-to-point messaging. All other message broker technologies require an ARB exception before architectural commitment."* A team that wants to use NATS for a specific use case can request an ARB exception — with justification, risk assessment, and a commitment to sunset the exception when the use case can be served by a standard technology.

**What Happens If Skipped**: Technology sprawl. On-call surface expands because every team's stack is different. Cross-team support breaks down because shared knowledge does not exist. Platform team cannot provide standardized tooling, observability, or security controls across a heterogeneous technology base.

---

### Level 3 — Policies

**Definition**: Mandatory rules derived from principles, with specific compliance mechanisms and defined consequences for violations.

**Characteristics**: Policies operationalize principles into auditable, actionable requirements. Where a principle says "design for failure," a policy says "all Tier 1 services must implement circuit breakers on all downstream dependencies." The policy is specific enough to audit — either the circuit breaker is there or it is not.

**Change Authority**: VP approval for operational policies. CISO and VP co-approval for security policies. No unilateral policy changes at the team level.

**Enforcement Mechanism**: Audit findings for policy violations (tracked in the governance registry). Change Advisory Board (CAB) review as a gate for change-related policies. Automated compliance scanning where tooling supports it (e.g., OPA policies in the deployment pipeline, SonarQube quality gates).

**Owner**: Domain VPs, ratified by the Operations Committee

**Example**: *"All Tier 1 changes must use blue-green deployment or a functionally equivalent zero-downtime deployment strategy. Exceptions require written VP approval declared in the CAB change record before the change window."* A violation of this policy is an audit finding. It goes in the quarterly governance report. The domain VP is accountable for the finding and the remediation.

**What Happens If Skipped**: Principles become unenforceable. "Design for failure" as a principle with no derived policies means every team interprets it differently. Auditors find the gap between the principle (aspirational) and the operational reality (inconsistent). The governance posture collapses under the first serious external audit.

---

### Level 4 — Procedures and Playbooks

**Definition**: Step-by-step execution guides for known operational scenarios.

**Characteristics**: Not mandatory in terms of specific steps — mandatory in terms of *having an equivalent*. A team may adapt the procedure to their specific context; they may not simply have no procedure. Procedures are living documents: they degrade if not updated when the systems they describe change.

**Change Authority**: Domain team owner with peer review and architecture team notification if the change has cross-domain implications.

**Enforcement Mechanism**: Operational readiness review (is this system ready to be on-call?) and runbook quality gate for on-call rotation entry. A system with outdated or missing runbooks should not have engineers placed on call without explicit risk acknowledgment.

**Owner**: Domain teams

**Example**: *"CAB Chair Playbook — the process for conducting a Change Advisory Board session, from agenda preparation through meeting facilitation to decision recording."* The CAB chair must follow a process. The specific facilitation techniques, agenda format, and tooling can vary by chair — but the inputs, outputs, and decision-recording requirements are fixed.

**What Happens If Skipped**: Inconsistent execution of critical processes. Tribal knowledge concentrated in individuals who leave or are unavailable at the worst moments. Key-person dependencies embedded in operational runbooks. On-call engineers who cannot execute procedures they have never seen without calling the procedure author.

---

### Level 5 — Guidelines and Rulebooks

**Definition**: Best practices and advisory content. Not mandatory. No compliance tracking.

**Characteristics**: Guidelines share accumulated operational knowledge. Teams that follow them benefit from tested patterns. Teams that deviate have not violated anything — they may be missing a better way, and a good peer review process will surface that. Guidelines are the mechanism by which practitioner knowledge becomes organizational knowledge.

**Change Authority**: Any team, with notification to the architecture team if the guideline has cross-domain applicability. No approval required.

**Enforcement Mechanism**: None. Advisory only. Peer review is recommended but not required.

**Owner**: Practitioners — the engineers and SREs closest to the work

**Example**: *"Recommended logging patterns for Java services using Logback and Splunk HEC forwarding."* A team that uses a different logging approach has not violated a policy. They may be generating logs that are harder to parse in Splunk, which is an operational quality concern — not a compliance finding.

**What Happens If Skipped**: Best practices stay in people's heads. Teams reinvent patterns that have already been learned at cost by other teams. Onboarding takes longer because new engineers have no curated starting point. The organization's operational knowledge does not compound. None of this is catastrophic in the way that skipping principles is — but the productivity cost accumulates over time.

---

### Hierarchy Summary

| Level | Type | Overridable? | Change Authority | Enforcement | Owner |
|-------|------|-------------|-----------------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Principles | No — VP/CTO exception only | VP + CTO co-sign | ARB gate; PIR documentation | VP Operations / CTO |
| 2 | Standards | Yes — with ARB exception | ARB vote + VP approval | ARB review gate; exception audit | Architecture team |
| 3 | Policies | Yes — with VP approval | VP approval | Audit findings; CAB gate | Domain VPs |
| 4 | Procedures | Yes — equivalent required | Domain team + peer review | Operational readiness review | Domain teams |
| 5 | Guidelines | Yes — no approval needed | Any team | None | Practitioners |

## 3. Document Lifecycle Governance

Every governance document — regardless of level — requires mandatory metadata fields. Without these fields, the document cannot be governed.

### Mandatory Metadata Fields

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Owner** | Named individual — not a team. Individuals are accountable; teams are not. |
| **Version** | Semantic versioning: 1.0 (initial), 1.1 (minor update), 2.0 (major principle or policy change) |
| **Status** | Active / Draft / Under Review / Deprecated |
| **Effective Date** | When this version took effect |
| **Review Date** | The date by which this document *must* be reviewed — not "when someone gets to it" |
| **Canonical Location** | The single authoritative URL in the document registry. No copies, no mirrors. |

### Review Cadences

| Level | Cadence | Triggered Review Conditions |
|-------|---------|---------------------------|
| Principles | Annual | Major incident tracing to principle gap; regulatory change affecting compliance posture |
| Standards | Quarterly | Technology Radar cycle; significant industry shift; Tier 1 exception volume rising |
| Policies | Annual | Audit finding against the policy; policy violation incident; regulatory change |
| Procedures | Bi-annual | Operational failure traceable to procedure gap; significant system or tooling change |
| Guidelines | Ad hoc, author discretion | Author awareness of outdated content; new pattern superseding existing guidance |

**Triggered review** is distinct from scheduled review. A major incident whose root cause traces to an ambiguous or unenforced policy must trigger an immediate policy review. The trigger is not about blame — it is about determining whether the policy is clear, specific, enforceable, and sufficient. If the answer to any of those four criteria is "no," the policy needs revision before the next incident tests the same gap.

## 4. Document Ownership Model

The ownership model is not arbitrary — it is designed to ensure each level of documentation reflects operational reality.

- **Principles** owned by VP Operations / CTO: because principles define what the organization will and will not compromise on architecturally, and only the executive layer has the authority to make that commitment credible.
- **Standards** owned by Architecture team, ratified by ARB: because standards require deep technical judgment to set correctly, but also cross-domain buy-in to be enforceable across teams.
- **Policies** owned by Domain VPs: because policies govern operational behavior in specific domains, and the people accountable for domain outcomes are the right owners for the rules that govern those outcomes.
- **Procedures** owned by Domain teams: because procedures must reflect how the work is actually done. A procedure owned by someone who never executes it will drift from reality within one system generation.
- **Guidelines** owned by Practitioners: because guidelines encode day-to-day knowledge, and the people generating that knowledge are best positioned to document and maintain it.

This ownership cascade ensures documents remain grounded. A principle owned by a committee gets diluted by consensus. A procedure owned by an architect who does not operate the system gets outdated by the first infrastructure change. Ownership must be assigned to the person closest to the authority and the execution for that level.

## 5. The Document Registry

All governance documents must exist in a central registry. Not "somewhere on the wiki." Not "in the team's Confluence space." One canonical location, discoverable by any engineer, auditable by any reviewer.

### Registry Fields

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Document Name** | Human-readable name |
| **Type** | Principle / Standard / Policy / Procedure / Guideline |
| **Owner** | Named individual |
| **Last Reviewed** | Date of last completed review |
| **Next Review Due** | Required review date |
| **Status** | Active / Draft / Under Review / Deprecated |
| **Canonical Location** | Authoritative URL |
| **Related Documents** | Cross-references to related governance documents |

The registry is the VP's **governance health dashboard**. Any document with a "Next Review Due" date in the past is a documented governance gap. Any document with status "Active" but a canonical location that returns a 404 is a risk. Any principle or policy without a named owner is ungoverned.

### Registry as Discovery Infrastructure

New engineers should be able to find any governance document through the registry — not through Slack archaeology, not by asking the right person, not by knowing which Confluence space the architecture team uses. If your governance cannot be discovered by an engineer who joined last week, your governance is only as good as your organizational memory, which is fragile.

The registry must be linked from:
- Every new service README
- Every ARB decision record
- Every PIR findings section
- The engineering onboarding guide

## 6. Common Governance Document Failures

These are the patterns that appear in every organization that has governance documentation but inconsistent governance outcomes.

### Failure 1: Documents That Exist But Are Not Enforced

The standards document says "use Kafka." An audit of the technology inventory reveals three teams running custom Redis pub-sub implementations, one team using AWS SNS/SQS as a primary message fabric, and one team that built their own lightweight event bus in Python.

**Detection**: cross-reference the technology inventory against the standards document quarterly. If the ARB exception log does not account for the gap, the gap is an unenforced standard.

**Fix**: retroactive ARB exception process. Each non-standard deployment gets assessed: either a formal exception is granted with a sunset plan, or a migration timeline is set. The exception register then becomes the enforcement mechanism — exceptions with expired sunset dates are policy violations.

---

### Failure 2: Documents No One Knows Exist

The architecture principles document was written three years ago by the founding architecture team. It lives on a SharePoint site that has not been linked from any resource engineers actually use. The current on-call rotation has never read it.

**Detection**: ask 10 engineers where the architecture principles are documented. If fewer than 50% can correctly locate the document within 2 minutes, discoverability is broken — and the document has no governance effect.

**Fix**: embed links to the document registry in every operational artifact that engineers touch: PRDs, RFC templates, service creation checklists, ARB submission forms. If the principles are not in the path of every architectural decision, they are not governing architectural decisions.

---

### Failure 3: Documents That Contradict Each Other

The security policy: *"All services must use mTLS for internal service-to-service communication."* The platform guidelines (written by a different team 18 months later): *"Internal services communicating within the same Kubernetes namespace can use plain HTTP for simplicity."*

Engineers read both. Some implement mTLS. Some implement plain HTTP. The security posture is inconsistent and the engineers who chose plain HTTP believe they followed the guidelines.

**Detection**: cross-reference audit across all active policies and guidelines, specifically looking for statements that could be read as contradicting one another. This should be a mandatory step in every document's annual review.

**Fix**: resolve the contradiction at the correct level. Determine which document reflects the intended governance intent. If the security policy is correct, the guideline must be updated to reflect it. If the guideline reflects a legitimate exception (e.g., namespace isolation provides equivalent security), the policy must be updated to acknowledge the exception path. The version that wins is determined by the hierarchy — a security policy at Level 3 always supersedes a guideline at Level 5.

---

### Failure 4: Documents Last Updated Three Years Ago

The deployment policy references Jenkins as the CI/CD toolchain. The organization migrated to GitHub Actions 18 months ago. The CAB process document references a "deployment freeze calendar" system that was replaced by an automated change risk scoring tool.

**Detection**: review cadence enforcement. The governance registry's "Next Review Due" column is the early warning system. Documents with overdue reviews are candidates for this failure.

**Fix**: emergency review. The document is either updated to reflect current reality within 30 days, or it is deprecated with a redirect to the current equivalent. A deprecated document left active in the registry misleads engineers. Deprecate aggressively.

---

### Failure 5: Playbooks Written for Systems That No Longer Exist

The RabbitMQ failover runbook describes a cluster topology that was replaced in a platform migration two years ago. The topic and vhost structure referenced in the runbook does not match the current deployment. The escalation contacts listed are engineers who left the organization.

**Detection**: cross-reference runbook system references against the current architecture inventory and the current on-call contact list. This should be part of the bi-annual procedure review and any post-incident review where a runbook was used.

**Fix**: deprecate immediately. Do not leave misleading operational documentation active. Write the replacement in the same session — if the runbook is needed for on-call, it must be accurate or it must not exist (an absent runbook leads to escalation; a wrong runbook leads to incorrect remediation actions that extend incidents).

---

> **What the VP decides**: which documents exist at each level, who owns which documents at each level, what the enforcement mechanisms are and whether they are functioning, and whether the document registry is being maintained as a governance artifact rather than an afterthought.

> **What happens without hierarchy**: documents that say "should" when they mean "must." Teams that selectively apply governance based on their own risk tolerance. Audit findings that reveal a consistent gap between documented intent and actual operational practice. The gap is the liability — not the missing documentation, but the false confidence that governance exists when it does not.
