# Chapter 5: Reserved Instances and Savings Plans

## The Commitment Trade-off as a Hedging Strategy

In commodity trading, a futures contract is a commitment to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price at a future date. The buyer accepts the commitment in exchange for price certainty — they know their cost regardless of how the spot price moves. Reserved Instances work on the same logic. You commit to using a certain amount of compute capacity over 1 or 3 years, and AWS gives you a discount of 30–72% below on-demand pricing in exchange for that commitment.

The risk is the same as a futures contract: if your actual usage falls below the commitment, you pay for capacity you did not consume. If usage exceeds the commitment, the excess runs at on-demand rates. The discipline is in sizing the commitment correctly — committing to your baseline, not your peak.

---

## Reserved Instances vs Savings Plans

AWS offers two commitment vehicles. Understanding the structural difference is prerequisite to choosing the right one.

### Reserved Instances (RIs)

A commitment to a specific instance type in a specific region (and optionally, AZ). In exchange: up to 72% discount.

- **Standard RI**: Highest discount, least flexible. Locked to instance family (e.g., m5), OS, tenancy, and region. Cannot change instance size within the family in some configurations.
- **Convertible RI**: Lower discount (54% max), but can be exchanged for a different instance type or family during the term. Valuable when your architecture is evolving.
- **Scheduled RI**: Deprecated — no longer sold by AWS.

**When RIs are right**: Stable, predictable workloads on specific instance families. Classic use case: your Cassandra cluster has run on r5.2xlarge for two years and will continue to for the foreseeable future.

### Savings Plans

A commitment to a spend amount ($/hour) rather than a specific instance. AWS applies the discount to any compute usage that matches the plan type.

- **Compute Savings Plans**: Broadest coverage. Applies to EC2 (any instance, any region, any OS), Fargate, and Lambda. Discount: up to 66%.
- **EC2 Instance Savings Plans**: Committed to a specific instance family in a specific region. Higher discount (up to 72%) with more flexibility than Standard RIs — can change size and OS within the family.

**When Savings Plans are right**: When your workload mix is changing (new services spinning up, others going away), or when you run across multiple instance families. The Compute Savings Plan is the most flexible commitment AWS offers and is the recommended starting point for most organizations.

## The Coverage vs Commitment Trade-off

Two metrics define your RI/Savings Plan health:

**Coverage**: What percentage of your eligible on-demand spend is covered by a commitment? Low coverage means you are leaving discounts on the table.

**Utilization**: Of the commitments you have purchased, what percentage is actually being consumed? Low utilization means you bought too much commitment and are paying for unused reservations.

The target state: **coverage ≥ 70%, utilization ≥ 90%**.

| Coverage | Utilization | Diagnosis |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Low | N/A | Under-committed — you are overpaying on on-demand |
| High | High | Healthy — well-sized commitments |
| High | Low | Over-committed — you bought more than you consume |
| Medium | High | Room to grow commitments safely |

The common mistake: buying RIs aggressively to hit high coverage, then watching utilization drop as teams right-size workloads or architecture changes. Always model utilization before purchasing.

## How to Model an RI Purchase

This is the analytical process for making a defensible commitment decision.

### Step 1: Establish Your Baseline

Pull 90 days of EC2 usage data from Cost Explorer or CUR. For each instance family, identify:
- **Minimum daily usage**: the floor of your usage — this is what you can commit to with high confidence
- **Average daily usage**: the typical level
- **Peak daily usage**: what you provision for (do not commit to peak)

### Step 2: Identify Stable vs Variable Workloads

Separate your compute into two buckets:

- **Stable workloads**: always running, predictable usage (databases, control plane components, message brokers). These are RI candidates.
- **Variable workloads**: HPA-controlled application pods, batch jobs, dev environments. These are Spot or on-demand candidates — do not commit to them.

### Step 3: Size the Commitment

Commit to the **minimum stable usage** observed over 90 days, discounted by 10–15% for safety margin. This is your initial RI purchase.

Example: Your Cassandra cluster runs 6x r5.2xlarge consistently. Your minimum observed over 90 days is 5.5 instances equivalent. Commit to 5x r5.2xlarge Standard RIs. The 6th instance runs at on-demand (or a Savings Plan covers the gap).

### Step 4: Model the Payback

| Instance | On-Demand Monthly | 1-Year RI Monthly | 3-Year RI Monthly | 1-Year Breakeven |
|----------|------------------|--------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| r5.2xlarge | $972 | $583 (40% off) | $378 (61% off) | Month 1 (paid upfront) / Month 8 (no upfront) |

For most organizations, the 1-year term with no-upfront payment is the right default. It provides significant discount while preserving budget flexibility.

### Step 5: The 1-Year vs 3-Year Decision

| Factor | 1-Year | 3-Year |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Discount (approx) | 35–45% | 55–72% |
| Flexibility | High — reassess annually | Low — 3-year bet |
| Best for | Evolving architecture | Commodity infrastructure (databases, brokers) |
| Risk | Slightly lower discount | Technology change risk (will you still need r5.2xlarge in 3 years?) |

**Director recommendation**: Default to 1-year for application compute, 3-year only for infrastructure that has been stable for 2+ years (database fleet, message broker fleet). Convertible RIs for anything where you anticipate instance type changes.

## Organizational Commitment Management

In a multi-team organization, RI and Savings Plan purchases are typically managed centrally (by a FinOps function or platform team) rather than per-team. This is because:

1. RIs purchased at the organization (payer account) level can be applied to any linked account
2. Centralized purchasing allows aggregation of baseline usage across teams, yielding higher confidence in commitment sizing
3. Individual teams making their own RI purchases creates fragmentation and reduces utilization efficiency

### The Centralized RI Model

The platform/FinOps team purchases RIs based on aggregate baseline analysis. Usage across all linked accounts is covered before any account faces on-demand pricing. Finance allocates the RI discount proportionally to each team's actual usage — this is the "blended rate" model.

**Trade-off**: Teams whose workloads consumed the RI discount feel the savings; teams that did not use committed capacity still share the cost proportionally. This creates some friction around attribution accuracy, which is why tagging quality is a prerequisite for this model.

## Savings Plan Coverage Target by Maturity

| Maturity | Savings Plan Coverage Target | RI Coverage Target |
|----------|-----------------------------|--------------------|
| Crawl | > 30% | Any coverage starts here |
| Walk | 50–70% | Stable compute fleet committed |
| Run | ≥ 70% (Savings Plans + RIs) | ≥ 90% of stable EC2 |

The FinOps Foundation finding: organizations that maintain ≥ 70% commitment coverage and ≥ 90% utilization consistently achieve 35–45% lower EC2 costs than organizations purchasing entirely on-demand.

---

## Key Takeaways

- RIs and Savings Plans are commitment instruments — like futures contracts — trading flexibility for price certainty
- Compute Savings Plans are the most flexible commitment vehicle: apply to EC2, Fargate, and Lambda across any region or instance type
- Commit to baseline usage only — never to peak; let Spot handle burst above baseline
- Coverage target: ≥ 70%; utilization target: ≥ 90% — both matter simultaneously
- 1-year no-upfront is the right default for most organizations; 3-year only for infrastructure with 2+ years of stability proven
- Centralized RI purchasing at the payer account level maximizes discount efficiency across a multi-team organization
