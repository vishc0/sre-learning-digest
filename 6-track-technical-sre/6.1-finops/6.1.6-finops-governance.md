# Chapter 6: FinOps Governance

## Governance Is the Operating System for Cost Accountability

A tagging strategy without a review cadence is a spreadsheet no one reads. A Kubecost dashboard without ownership is a metric no one acts on. FinOps governance is the operating system that converts cost visibility into cost action. It defines: who reviews what, at what frequency, with what authority to act, and what happens when something goes wrong.

The governance model for a Director-level FinOps practice has four components: the team structure, the anomaly detection system, the budget management framework, and the review cadence. Think of them as the control plane, alerting layer, policy layer, and retrospective cycle of your cost management system — directly analogous to the control plane, alerting, SLOs, and incident review cycle of your reliability practice.

---

## FinOps Team Structure

The right structure depends on organizational size and maturity. Three common models:

### Model 1: Federated (Walk Maturity, < 200 Engineers)

A central FinOps function (1–2 people, often in the platform/SRE team) sets standards, tools, and practices. Individual engineering teams own their cost optimization. The FinOps function is an enabler and aggregator, not a gatekeeper.

```
FinOps Engineer (Platform Team)
  ├── Maintains Kubecost + Cost Explorer dashboards
  ├── Runs monthly review (agenda + facilitation)
  ├── Publishes tagging standards and enforcement rules
  └── Escalates anomalies to engineering leads
  
Each Team Lead
  ├── Reviews their team's weekly cost dashboard
  ├── Responds to anomaly alerts within 24 hours
  └── Owns their team's quarterly optimization target
```

### Model 2: Hub and Spoke (Run Maturity, > 200 Engineers)

A dedicated FinOps team (3–5 people) owns the practice centrally. Each major engineering domain has a designated FinOps champion (an engineer who owns cost accountability for that domain).

### Model 3: Platform-Embedded (Common in SRE-led Organizations)

FinOps is a function within the Platform Engineering or SRE team. No separate FinOps org — the platform team owns cost tooling, anomaly detection, and monthly cadence as part of its charter.

**Director recommendation**: For a 15-person platform team at Series B–D, Model 1 (Federated) is the right starting point. One dedicated FinOps engineer embedded in your team returns 5–10x their cost in the first year through Right-sizing, RI optimization, and anomaly catch rate. Promote to Model 3 as the organization grows past 100 engineers.

## Cloud Cost Anomaly Detection

An anomaly detection system is the pager-equivalent for cost. Without it, you learn about cost problems from the monthly invoice — which is 2–4 weeks after the event. With it, you learn within hours.

### AWS Cost Anomaly Detection (Native)

AWS Cost Anomaly Detection uses machine learning to establish a spending baseline and alert when actual spend deviates significantly.

**Configuration checklist**:
1. Enable Cost Anomaly Detection in Cost Explorer → Anomaly Detection
2. Create monitors for each dimension you care about: by Service, by Linked Account, by Cost Category
3. Set alert threshold: absolute dollar amount (e.g., > $500 above baseline) OR percentage threshold (e.g., > 15% above rolling average)
4. Configure SNS topic for alert delivery → Slack via Lambda or PagerDuty integration

**Tuning the thresholds**:
- Too sensitive (low threshold): alert fatigue — every traffic spike triggers a notification
- Too insensitive (high threshold): a runaway job burns $5K before you see it
- Starting point: $200 absolute threshold for teams spending < $10K/month; 15% relative threshold for teams spending > $10K/month

### Custom Anomaly Detection with CUR + Athena

For more granular detection (per-namespace, per-deployment), build a lightweight anomaly detection pipeline:

1. CUR exports daily to S3
2. Athena query calculates 7-day rolling average per tagged dimension
3. Lambda runs daily, compares latest day to rolling average
4. If variance > threshold: post to Slack channel with cost breakdown

This is a 2-day build. It is the FinOps equivalent of writing a custom alerting rule in Prometheus — more targeted than the generic AWS tool, at the cost of maintenance overhead.

## Budget Alerts and Hard Limits

Budget alerts are preventative; anomaly detection is reactive. Use both.

### AWS Budgets Configuration

Create budgets at three levels:

**Organization total budget**: Monthly cloud spend target. Alert at 80% consumed and 100% consumed. Notify: VP Engineering, CFO, and FinOps lead.

**Account/team budget**: Per-team monthly budget. Alert at 85% consumed. Notify: team lead and FinOps engineer.

**Service budget**: For high-cost services (EC2, RDS). Alert at 90% consumed. Notify: FinOps engineer only (operational, not executive).

**Budget enforcement (hard limits)**: AWS Budgets can trigger actions — attaching an SCP that denies EC2 RunInstances when a budget is breached. Use this only for non-production accounts. Never put a hard spend limit on production infrastructure without an emergency override path — a production database restoration should never be blocked by a budget SCP.

### The Budget Reset Protocol

At the start of each quarter, review and update budgets based on the previous quarter's actuals + roadmap growth forecast. Budget amounts that are never adjusted are not budgets — they are monuments to a historical decision.

## Monthly FinOps Review Cadence

The monthly review is the heartbeat of the governance practice. Without a regular forcing function, cost conversations become reactive (after a bill shock) rather than proactive.

### Review Structure

**Frequency**: Monthly, within the first 5 business days after month-end (when AWS finalizes billing).

**Attendees**: Engineering leads (one per team), Platform/SRE lead, FinOps engineer, Engineering Director (quarterly — every third meeting). Finance optional but valuable.

**Duration**: 45–60 minutes. Longer = unfocused.

### Standard Agenda

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|------|---------|---------|
| 0–5 min | Month summary | Total spend, vs budget, vs prior month, vs same month last year |
| 5–20 min | Top cost drivers | Top 5 services/teams by spend and by change from prior month |
| 20–30 min | Anomaly review | What fired, what was investigated, what was root-caused |
| 30–45 min | Optimization actions | What was implemented, what's in progress, what's planned |
| 45–55 min | Commitments review | RI/Savings Plan coverage and utilization — any purchases needed? |
| 55–60 min | Next actions | Owner, action, due date for each item |

### Monthly Review Metrics Dashboard

Prepare this data before the meeting. These numbers must be immediately available:

| Metric | Format | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Total monthly spend | $ and vs budget % | Cost Explorer |
| Month-over-month change | $ and % | Cost Explorer |
| Top 5 services by spend | Service name + $ | Cost Explorer |
| Tag coverage rate | % of spend tagged | Config + CUR |
| Savings Plan coverage | % | Cost Explorer |
| Savings Plan utilization | % | Cost Explorer |
| Cluster idle cost | $ and % of EC2 | Kubecost |
| Cost per [unit] this month | $ | CUR + business metrics |

## Escalation When Teams Overspend

The escalation model defines what happens when a team exceeds their budget. Without a defined escalation path, overspend either gets ignored or triggers political escalation with no structure.

### Escalation Levels

**Level 1 — Advisory (80% budget consumed)**: Automated Slack notification to team lead. No action required — awareness only. FinOps engineer documents in the review tracker.

**Level 2 — Review Required (100% budget consumed mid-month)**: Team lead and FinOps engineer conduct a 30-minute triage. Identify root cause. If caused by legitimate growth, request budget increase through formal process. If caused by waste, implement remediation within 48 hours.

**Level 3 — Director Escalation (> 120% budget consumed)**: Engineering Director is notified. Team must present root cause and remediation plan in the next monthly review. If pattern repeats for two consecutive months, formal budget governance action (spending freeze on non-critical resources, mandatory FinOps review of all new provisioning requests).

**Level 4 — CFO Visibility (> 150% budget consumed OR > $50K over-budget)**: CFO and VP Engineering are notified. Incident-style post-mortem required. Finance may impose hard limits on the account.

## Cost Forecasting

Forecasting is a governance tool, not an accounting exercise. Its purpose is to identify future cost problems before they become budget surprises.

**Rolling 3-month forecast**: Project current spend forward using:
- Current run rate (last 30 days × 3)
- Known growth factors (new service launching, traffic growth from marketing campaigns)
- Known reduction factors (planned optimizations, RI renewals, deprecations)

**Annual budget cycle input**: In October/November (for January budget), present the FinOps team's cloud cost forecast to finance. This forecast should include:
- Baseline: current run rate × 12
- Growth adjustment: expected traffic/user growth percentage
- Optimization offsets: planned RI purchases, right-sizing campaigns
- New initiative costs: any major architectural changes planned for next year

A Director who arrives at the annual budget conversation with a model — not a guess — is demonstrating the financial leadership that defines the role.

## Key Takeaways

- Governance converts visibility into action — without a cadence, dashboards are decorations
- Federated model (central FinOps function + distributed team ownership) is the right starting point for most platform teams
- Anomaly detection is the cost equivalent of on-call alerting — configure it before you need it
- Budget alerts at three levels: total org, per team, per service — with escalation paths defined for each
- The monthly review is the forcing function — 45–60 minutes, structured agenda, named action owners
- Cost forecasting is a governance deliverable, not just an accounting exercise
