# Chapter 4: Unit Economics for SRE

## Why Unit Economics Is a Director-Level Discipline

An SRE Manager responds to incidents and runs capacity. An SRE Director justifies investments. The instrument of justification — in a language that a CFO and a CTO both understand — is unit economics. When you can say "our platform delivers 25 million messages per day at a cost of $0.00074 per message, and our target is $0.0006 by Q3," you are doing two things simultaneously: demonstrating engineering rigor and speaking the language of business value.

Unit economics translates the complexity of distributed infrastructure into a single, comparable number: what does it cost to deliver one unit of business value? That number is a lever. Make it visible, and every engineering decision (add a replica, use a smaller instance, cache this response) becomes a trade-off with a calculable outcome.

---

## Defining the Right Cost Unit

The cost unit must map to something the business cares about. Generic choices like "cost per EC2-hour" are meaningless to a product manager or a CFO. Business-aligned choices:

| Platform Type | Natural Cost Unit |
|--------------|------------------|
| Messaging / notification platform | Cost per message delivered |
| API platform | Cost per API call (or per 1M calls) |
| Data pipeline | Cost per GB processed |
| E-commerce | Cost per order fulfilled |
| Streaming | Cost per hour streamed per user |
| SaaS product | Cost per active user per month |

For an SRE Director at a T-Mobile-scale notification platform, the right unit is **cost per message delivered**. This connects directly to the business metric (messages sent), survives capacity changes (you normalize by volume), and makes cost trends comparable month over month regardless of growth.

## Case Study: Cost Per Message at a T-Mobile-Scale System

This is a worked example based on a notification platform running 25 million messages per day on EKS + Cassandra + RabbitMQ.

### Infrastructure Baseline (Monthly)

| Component | Resource | Monthly Cost Estimate |
|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| EKS worker nodes | 12x m5.2xlarge On-Demand | $4,320 |
| EKS management fee | Per cluster | $73 |
| Cassandra cluster | 6x r5.2xlarge (memory-optimized) | $5,832 |
| EBS volumes for Cassandra | 6x 2TB gp3 | $1,440 |
| RabbitMQ brokers | 3x m5.large | $324 |
| S3 (message archival) | 50TB at standard tier | $1,150 |
| Data transfer | ~10TB/month outbound | $900 |
| CloudWatch / logging | Log ingestion and storage | $620 |
| **Total** | | **~$14,659/month** |

### Volume Normalization

25 million messages/day × 30 days = **750 million messages/month**

### Raw Cost Per Message

$14,659 / 750,000,000 = **$0.0000195 per message** (approximately $0.00002)

Or equivalently: **$19.54 per million messages delivered**

### Breaking Down the Cost Drivers

| Cost Component | Monthly $ | % of Total | Cost per Message |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| EKS compute | $4,320 | 29.5% | $0.0000058 |
| Cassandra compute | $5,832 | 39.8% | $0.0000078 |
| EBS (Cassandra) | $1,440 | 9.8% | $0.0000019 |
| RabbitMQ | $324 | 2.2% | $0.0000004 |
| S3 archival | $1,150 | 7.8% | $0.0000015 |
| Data transfer | $900 | 6.1% | $0.0000012 |
| Observability | $620 | 4.2% | $0.0000008 |

**Insight from this breakdown**: Cassandra compute is the largest single cost driver. Before any optimization effort, the highest-ROI target is Cassandra right-sizing and Reserved Instance purchase for the r5.2xlarge fleet.

### Setting a Cost SLO

Once you have the baseline: $0.0000195/message. Set a target:

- **Current**: $0.0000195/message (baseline)
- **Target (Q2)**: $0.0000160/message (18% reduction via RI purchase + VPA right-sizing on EKS)
- **Target (Q4)**: $0.0000130/message (33% reduction, adds Spot nodes and Cassandra tier optimization)

This is a cost SLO. It is tracked in the monthly FinOps review the same way error rate is tracked in the SRE review.

## Calculating the Cost of Reliability

This is the differentiating analysis for Director-level interviews. "What does an additional nine of availability cost?" is a question most SRE candidates cannot answer quantitatively. Knowing the framework — and a real number from your platform — is a significant differentiator.

### The Cost of 9s Framework

Each nine of availability (99.9% → 99.99% → 99.999%) requires a different architecture and a proportionally larger infrastructure investment. The progression is roughly exponential, not linear.

| Availability Target | Architecture Required | Relative Cost Multiplier |
|--------------------|-----------------------|--------------------------|
| 99% (~87h downtime/year) | Single region, some redundancy | 1.0x baseline |
| 99.9% (~8.7h downtime/year) | Multi-AZ, load balanced, health checks | 1.5–2.0x |
| 99.99% (~52min downtime/year) | Active-active multi-AZ, automated failover, redundant control plane | 3.0–5.0x |
| 99.999% (~5min downtime/year) | Multi-region active-active, chaos engineering validated | 8.0–15.0x |

### Applied to the Notification Platform

If the baseline 99.9% architecture costs $14,659/month:

- Moving from 99.9% → 99.99% requires: multi-AZ EKS with redundant control plane, Cassandra cross-AZ replication (adds nodes), RabbitMQ HA configuration (mirror queues or quorum queues), and automated failover tooling.
- Estimated additional monthly cost: +$8,000–12,000 (second AZ nodes, additional Cassandra replicas, ALB changes, DR tooling)
- **Cost of going from 99.9% to 99.99%: approximately $10,000/month** for this workload at this scale

This translates to: **each additional nine costs roughly $10,000/month.** That is the number you bring to the CFO. That is the number that makes the SLA negotiation concrete. The business can now decide: is avoiding 52 minutes of downtime per year worth $120,000/year?

### The SRE Investment Case

When you present a budget request for SRE investment (headcount, tooling, chaos engineering), you frame it against this number:

"Our current platform has a $10,000/month cost-per-nine between 99.9% and 99.99%. We are requesting $15,000/month in additional SRE capacity (one senior engineer). The return: we believe we can achieve 99.99% availability through proactive reliability work, eliminating an average of one P1 incident per month that currently costs $50,000 in engineering time, customer impact, and SLA credits. Payback period: 3 months."

This is a business case. Not a request.

## Cost Per Transaction at Different Maturity Levels

As a benchmarking tool, knowing what your cost per message (or equivalent unit) implies about your infrastructure maturity:

| Cost per Message | Maturity Signal |
|-----------------|----------------|
| > $0.00005 | Likely over-provisioned, no Savings Plans, no right-sizing |
| $0.00002–0.00005 | Typical Walk maturity, some optimization done |
| $0.00001–0.00002 | Good Run maturity, active right-sizing, Spot + RI coverage |
| < $0.00001 | Excellent — likely at scale with strong FinOps practice and workload-specific optimization |

These benchmarks are rough and workload-dependent. A Cassandra-heavy architecture will always cost more per message than a Redis-backed one. The value is not the absolute number but the trend: is your cost per message going up, down, or flat as volume grows?

---

## Key Takeaways

- Unit economics translates infrastructure complexity into a business-legible number: cost per unit of value delivered
- Choose the cost unit based on what the business cares about — for a notification platform, it is cost per message delivered
- The cost-per-nine framework ($X/month per additional nine of availability) is the most powerful tool in Director-level budget conversations
- Cost SLOs — targets on cost per transaction — make FinOps a first-class engineering discipline alongside reliability SLOs
- The investment case for SRE is: reliability improvement ROI > cost of SRE investment. Calculate both sides.
