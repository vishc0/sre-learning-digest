# Chapter 8: Director Decision Framework

## The Gap This Chapter Fills

Everything in chapters 1–7 is operational knowledge. This chapter is about the judgment layer — the decisions that only a Director makes, the conversations only a Director has, and the framing only a Director can bring to an executive table.

An SRE Manager optimizes Kubernetes. An SRE Director explains to the CFO why the Kubernetes spend is strategically justified, proposes the right tooling investment, and builds the FinOps maturity roadmap that takes the organization from Crawl to Run. This chapter teaches that layer.

---

## Presenting Cloud Cost to the CFO

The CFO does not think in GB-seconds, vCPU-hours, or EC2 instance families. They think in margin, growth, risk, and return. The Director's job is translation — converting infrastructure economics into business economics.

### The CFO Conversation Framework

Three scenarios you will face as a Director:

**Scenario 1: Annual budget review — you are defending next year's cloud spend request.**

Wrong framing: "We need $4.2M for cloud next year, up from $3.8M."

Right framing: "Last year we delivered X billion API calls, Y million notifications, and Z petabytes of data processed at $3.8M. Next year, with 25% traffic growth, our cost will be $4.2M — a 10.5% cost increase for a 25% volume increase. Our cost per transaction is improving because of the FinOps investments we made in Q2 and Q3. Here is the model."

The narrative: cost is growing slower than the business because the engineering team is managing it intelligently.

**Scenario 2: A CFO is asking why cloud costs spiked last month.**

Wrong framing: "We had some unexpected EC2 usage due to a misconfigured autoscaler."

Right framing: "We had a cost anomaly of $X driven by [root cause]. Our anomaly detection caught it on day N. We contained it by [action]. Here is what we have done to prevent recurrence. Total impact: $X, which is Y% of the monthly budget. Our forecast for this month is back within budget."

The narrative: the team has controls, the team detected it, the team fixed it. This is a managed system, not a surprise.

**Scenario 3: Requesting a FinOps investment (tooling or headcount).**

Wrong framing: "We want to hire a FinOps engineer and buy Kubecost."

Right framing: "We currently spend $X/month on cloud. At our current maturity level, we are likely 20–30% over-optimized relative to Run-maturity organizations. The FinOps Foundation benchmarks this gap as $Y/month in recoverable savings. A dedicated FinOps engineer ($Z/year) and Kubecost ($W/year) will cost $V/year. Conservative first-year ROI: $Y × 12 months × 0.5 (50% realization) = $R. Payback period: [R/V] months."

The narrative: this is a return on investment, not a cost center request.

## The Cost of a Nine: The CFO Pitch Frame

This is the most powerful FinOps concept a Director can bring to an executive conversation. Most SRE Directors never quantify it.

### The Core Argument

Reliability is not free. Every additional nine of availability requires architectural redundancy — more nodes, more AZs, more replicas, more failover automation. Each of those requirements has a dollar cost. The Director who can put a number on that cost is the one who can have a rational conversation with the business about what SLA level to target and what it is worth paying for.

### The Pitch

"Our current platform delivers 99.9% availability on a $14,659/month infrastructure budget. Here is what different availability targets would cost:

| Target | Architecture Change | Additional Monthly Cost |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| 99.9% (current) | Multi-AZ, load balanced | Baseline |
| 99.99% | Active-active multi-AZ, automated failover | +$10,000–12,000/month |
| 99.999% | Multi-region active-active, validated DR | +$80,000–120,000/month |

The question is not 'do we want high availability?' The question is 'what is the business impact of a P1 outage, and how does that compare to the cost of preventing it?'

Our last P1 outage lasted 2.3 hours. Engineering time to resolve: 8 engineers × 2.3 hours at loaded cost = $4,600. Customer impact: estimated $X in SLA credits and Y% churn risk. Total incident cost: $Z.

If we upgrade from 99.9% to 99.99%, we expect to eliminate 80% of P1 frequency. Cost: +$11,000/month. At our current P1 rate of 1.2 per month, the savings in incident cost alone is approximately $Z × 1.2 × 0.8 = $W/month. The upgrade pays for itself in M months."

This is a Director-level conversation. Bring this framing to interviews and it will differentiate you from every candidate who only talks about MTTR.

## Build vs Buy: FinOps Tooling Decision

Every FinOps practice faces this decision: which tooling do you build and which do you buy?

### Tooling Landscape

| Tool | Category | Pricing (approx) | Best For |
|------|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **AWS Cost Explorer** | Native | Included (some features cost extra) | Starting point, Cost Anomaly Detection |
| **AWS Budgets** | Native | Free (basic); $0.02/day for advanced actions | Budget alerts, cross-account controls |
| **Kubecost** | Third-party | Free (open source) to $499/cluster/month (enterprise) | Kubernetes namespace cost attribution |
| **Apptio Cloudability** | Enterprise | $$$$ (custom, typically $50K–$200K+/year) | Large enterprises, multi-cloud, chargeback |
| **CloudHealth by VMware** | Enterprise | $$$$ (custom) | Multi-cloud governance, mature FinOps programs |
| **Grafana + CUR + Athena** | Build | Engineering time + AWS query costs | Maximum customization, data sovereignty |
| **OpenCost** | Open source | Free | Kubernetes, CNCF project, Kubecost alternative |

### Decision Framework

**At Crawl maturity**: Use AWS native tools (Cost Explorer + Budgets + Cost Anomaly Detection). They are free, available today, and sufficient for establishing baseline visibility. Do not buy enterprise tooling until you have exhausted native capabilities.

**At Walk maturity**: Add Kubecost (open source or standard tier). The Kubernetes cost attribution gap is the primary limitation of native tools. Kubecost is the minimum viable solution for EKS shops.

**At Run maturity**: Evaluate Apptio or CloudHealth only if you need multi-cloud consolidation (AWS + Azure + GCP in one view), sophisticated chargeback with automated billing, or finance-grade reporting for a large organization (> 500 engineers, > $5M annual cloud spend). Below that threshold, Kubecost + Cost Explorer + a custom Grafana dashboard is almost always sufficient.

### Director-Level Build vs Buy Criteria

| Criterion | Buy (tooling) | Build (custom) |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Time to value | Days | Weeks–months |
| Maintenance burden | Vendor | Your team |
| Customization | Limited to vendor roadmap | Unlimited |
| Multi-cloud support | Strong in enterprise tools | Requires custom integration |
| Cost of tooling vs cloud spend | Evaluate ROI rigorously | Engineering time is not free |
| Organizational credibility | Pre-built tools are auditable | Custom tools require internal trust |

**Recommendation for a platform team at Series B–D**: Buy Kubecost standard + use AWS native. Build a Grafana dashboard on top of CUR for executive reporting. Do not buy enterprise tooling until annual cloud spend exceeds $3–5M.

## FinOps Maturity Roadmap: Crawl to Run in 12 Months

This is the roadmap you present to leadership when asked "how are we going to get our cloud costs under control?"

### Quarter 1: Establish Visibility (Crawl → Walk)

| Week | Action | Owner | Output |
|------|--------|-------|--------|
| 1–2 | Audit current tagging coverage | FinOps engineer | Baseline tag coverage rate |
| 2–4 | Implement tagging standard + AWS Config enforcement | Platform team | Tag policy in SCPs or Config rules |
| 4–6 | Deploy Kubecost | Platform team | Namespace cost dashboards live |
| 6–8 | Enable Cost Anomaly Detection + configure Slack alerts | FinOps engineer | Anomaly alerting operational |
| 8–10 | Run first monthly FinOps review | Director | Monthly cadence established |
| 10–12 | Establish team cost dashboards | FinOps engineer | Self-service cost visibility |

**Q1 target**: Tag coverage > 80%, cost anomaly detection active, monthly review running.

### Quarter 2: Optimize Compute (Walk)

| Action | Expected Savings |
|--------|-----------------|
| VPA right-sizing campaign on top 5 over-provisioned deployments | 10–15% EC2 reduction |
| Spot migration for stateless workloads | 40–60% on affected nodes |
| Non-prod environment scheduling (stop at night/weekends) | 30–50% non-prod EC2 reduction |
| Purchase Savings Plans at 60% coverage | 30–40% on covered compute |

**Q2 target**: Cluster idle cost < 20%, Savings Plan coverage > 60%, 15% overall cost reduction from Q1 baseline.

### Quarter 3: Unit Economics and Commitment Optimization

| Action | Expected Outcome |
|--------|-----------------|
| Define and implement cost per transaction metric | SLO for cost per message/call established |
| RI purchase for stable database/broker fleet | 40–60% on committed fleet |
| Topology-aware routing for inter-AZ reduction | 30–50% data transfer reduction |
| Ingress consolidation (reduce load balancer count) | $200–500/month depending on count |

**Q3 target**: Cost per transaction metric live, RI utilization > 90%, Savings Plan coverage > 70%.

### Quarter 4: Governance and Run Maturity

| Action | Expected Outcome |
|--------|-----------------|
| Cost SLOs added to team OKRs | FinOps embedded in team accountability |
| Architecture review includes cost modeling | Cost-aware design from inception |
| Annual RI planning cycle completed | Q1 next year commitments sized and purchased |
| Annual FinOps maturity assessment | Publish maturity level to leadership |

**Q4 target**: FinOps Foundation Run maturity criteria met, 25–30% total cost reduction vs Q1 baseline.

## Director Interview: FinOps Questions and Answers

**"How have you managed cloud costs at scale?"**

Answer framework: State the maturity you inherited, the program you built, and the metric improvement you drove. Use the cost per unit metric if you have it. Reference the governance cadence you established. Quantify the savings.

**"How would you justify a FinOps investment to the CFO?"**

Answer: Use the ROI model — current spend, estimated waste based on FinOps Foundation benchmarks for your maturity level, cost of the program, conservative first-year savings, payback period.

**"What does each nine of availability cost in your current platform?"**

Answer: Walk through the cost-of-reliability framework. Even if you do not have the exact number, demonstrating the analytical approach — infrastructure delta cost per maturity level, cost of downtime vs cost of prevention — shows Director-level thinking.

**"How do you make engineering teams accountable for cloud cost?"**

Answer: The federated model — central standards, distributed ownership, showback first (then chargeback when trust is established), monthly review cadence as the accountability mechanism, cost SLOs as team metrics.

## Key Takeaways

- The CFO pitch for cloud cost is ROI-framed, not technical — cost per transaction, cost per nine, payback period for investments
- The cost-of-reliability framework ($X/month per additional nine) is a genuine Director-level differentiator in interviews and budget conversations
- Build vs buy FinOps tooling: AWS native + Kubecost for most organizations; enterprise tools only above $5M annual spend
- The Crawl-to-Run roadmap is a 12-month program with quarterly targets — present it as a roadmap, not a wish list
- FinOps maturity at Run saves 25–35% vs Crawl — this is the ROI anchor for every FinOps investment conversation
