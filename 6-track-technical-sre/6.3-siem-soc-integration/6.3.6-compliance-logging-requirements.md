# Chapter 6: Compliance Logging Requirements

## Why This Is a Platform Engineering Problem

The compliance audit does not interrogate the audit team. It interrogates the systems. And the systems are owned by the platform engineering team.

If the authentication logs are missing for a three-hour window because a forwarder crashed and nobody noticed, the compliance finding is against the organization — not against the specific team whose forwarder failed. If logs were retained for 90 days when the applicable framework requires 12 months, the finding is against the platform that configured the retention policy, not against the compliance officer who did not know the policy was wrong.

Platform engineers and SREs who operate the logging infrastructure are, in practice, the owners of compliance logging. The sooner that reality is internalized, the sooner the design decisions get made correctly the first time instead of being retrofitted under audit pressure.

This chapter covers what the four major compliance frameworks require from a logging perspective and how to design a logging architecture that satisfies all of them simultaneously.

---

## SOC 2 Type II

SOC 2 is an auditing standard developed by the American Institute of CPAs (AICPA) for service organizations. Type II means the audit covers a period of time (typically 6–12 months), not just a point in time. The auditor is verifying that your controls operated continuously throughout the audit period.

**What must be logged:**
- Logical access events: who accessed what systems, when, and from where
- Changes to system configuration and security settings
- Anomalies and security events that triggered alerts or investigation
- Administrative actions on systems holding customer data

**Retention requirement:** Minimum 12 months. Auditors need to review the full audit period, plus some buffer.

**Key requirements from an SRE perspective:**
- Log access must itself be controlled and logged. If an admin can delete Splunk logs, that is a finding.
- Availability of logs for audit — auditors need to query log data, not just receive reports. Your Splunk access model needs to accommodate this.
- Evidence that anomalies were detected and responded to. Splunk notable events with closure status and analyst notes satisfy this requirement.

## PCI-DSS v4.0

PCI-DSS applies to any system that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data. In a platform engineering context, this typically means the API gateway handling payment processing, the Kubernetes namespaces running payment microservices, and the databases holding payment records. The Cardholder Data Environment (CDE) is defined by scope, and everything that touches the CDE or could affect its security is in scope.

**What must be logged:**
- All individual user access to system components (Requirement 10.2.1)
- All actions taken by anyone with root or administrative privilege (10.2.1.2)
- Access to all audit logs (10.2.1.3)
- Invalid logical access attempts (10.2.1.4)
- Use of and changes to identification and authentication mechanisms (10.2.1.5)
- Initialization, stopping, or pausing of audit logs (10.2.1.6)
- Creation and deletion of system-level objects (10.2.1.7)

**Retention requirement:** 12 months total, with the most recent 3 months available for immediate analysis. This maps to: 90 days in hot Splunk storage, remaining 9 months in warm/cold storage accessible within a defined SLA.

**Time synchronization (Requirement 10.6):** All systems in the CDE must synchronize clocks to a defined NTP source. Log timestamps that cannot be trusted destroy the audit trail. This is a platform infrastructure requirement, not a logging requirement per se, but it shows up as a logging compliance finding constantly. Your NTP configuration must be auditable.

**Log integrity:** PCI-DSS v4.0 (Requirement 10.3) requires that audit logs are protected from destruction and unauthorized modifications. Splunk's own storage is insufficient — a Splunk admin can delete index data. PCI requires a mechanism that prevents even administrators from modifying logs after ingestion. This requires immutable archival storage.

## HIPAA

HIPAA applies to covered entities (healthcare providers, health plans, healthcare clearinghouses) and their business associates. In platform terms, if you operate infrastructure that processes, stores, or transmits electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI), HIPAA's Security Rule applies.

**Audit Controls (§164.312(b)):** "Implement hardware, software, and/or procedural mechanisms that record and examine activity in information systems that contain or use ePHI."

HIPAA's logging requirement is notably less prescriptive than PCI-DSS. It does not mandate specific event types or retention periods. This flexibility is intentional — HIPAA applies across a wide variety of covered entities. The practical standard is: log enough to be able to answer "who accessed ePHI, when, and why" during an audit or breach investigation.

**What to log for HIPAA compliance:**
- All access to ePHI (successful and failed)
- System and application logins
- Modifications to ePHI (create, update, delete)
- Administrative actions on systems containing ePHI
- Security events (intrusion detection alerts, unusual access patterns)

**Retention:** HIPAA requires that documentation of security policies and procedures be retained for 6 years from creation or last effective date. This is documentation retention, not necessarily raw log retention. In practice, retaining audit logs for 6 years is the safe interpretation, particularly for breach investigation scenarios.

**Minimum necessary standard:** Users should only access the ePHI necessary for their job. Logging must be sufficient to detect and demonstrate violations of this standard (unusual query patterns, bulk access that exceeds typical need).

## FedRAMP

FedRAMP (Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program) applies to cloud services used by U.S. federal agencies. It is based on NIST Special Publication 800-53. FedRAMP Moderate is the most common authorization level for SaaS and platform services.

**Applicable NIST 800-53 controls — AU (Audit and Accountability):**

- **AU-2 (Event Logging):** Define and document auditable events. The control requires that you have specified, in writing, which events are logged and why.
- **AU-3 (Content of Audit Records):** Each log record must contain: type of event, date/time, location, source, outcome, identity of users.
- **AU-4 (Audit Log Storage Capacity):** Allocate audit log storage to prevent capacity failures that would cause log loss.
- **AU-6 (Audit Record Review, Analysis, and Reporting):** Review and analyze audit logs at a defined frequency. Alert on findings. This is the control that Splunk ES notables directly satisfy.
- **AU-9 (Protection of Audit Information):** Protect audit logs from unauthorized access, modification, and deletion. Again, this requires immutable storage.
- **AU-11 (Audit Record Retention):** Retain audit logs for 90 days online (immediately accessible) and one year archive (accessible within a defined SLA, typically 24 hours).
- **AU-12 (Audit Record Generation):** All systems in scope must be capable of generating audit records for the events defined in AU-2.

## Immutable Log Storage: Why Splunk Alone Is Insufficient

This is the architectural requirement most platform teams miss. Splunk is an excellent analysis platform. It is not an immutable log archive.

A Splunk administrator with the `delete_by_keyword` capability or direct access to the index buckets can delete data. In most compliance frameworks, logs that can be deleted by an administrator do not satisfy the integrity requirement. The requirement is that logs cannot be modified or deleted after ingestion, even by the highest-privileged users.

The solution is a write-once, read-many archival tier running in parallel with Splunk:

- **AWS S3 with Object Lock (WORM mode):** Logs are written to S3 with Object Lock enabled. Once written, the objects cannot be deleted or overwritten for the configured retention period. Even the AWS account root user cannot bypass WORM mode during the lock period.
- **Azure Immutable Blob Storage:** Equivalent to S3 Object Lock on Azure.
- **GCP Cloud Storage with Retention Policies:** Similar WORM capability on GCP.

The architecture: logs arrive at the Splunk Heavy Forwarder, which fans them out to both Splunk indexers and the immutable S3 bucket simultaneously. Splunk provides the analysis capability. S3 provides the compliance-grade archival.

This is the architecture that satisfies the integrity requirement across all four frameworks.

## Tiered Retention and Cost Management

Retaining everything in Splunk hot storage for 7 years is cost-prohibitive. A tiered strategy:

| Tier | Storage | Retention | Access Latency | Use Case |
|------|---------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| Hot | Splunk indexers (local SSD) | 90 days | Seconds | Active investigation, correlation searches |
| Warm | Splunk SmartStore (S3) | 12 months | Minutes | Historical investigation, compliance queries |
| Cold | S3 Standard-IA or Glacier | 7 years | Hours (Glacier: hours to days) | Compliance archival, legal hold |

For PCI-DSS: 90 days hot + 9 months warm covers the immediate-access requirement. Cold handles the remaining retention obligation.

For HIPAA: 6-year cold retention for ePHI-adjacent logs.

SmartStore is Splunk's tiered storage feature: index buckets roll from local SSD to S3 automatically based on age. The Splunk search layer handles the retrieval transparently — analysts query the same way regardless of whether the data is in hot or warm tier.

## Director Frame

Compliance logging is infrastructure, not an audit team problem. If the logging architecture is not designed with compliance requirements in mind from day one, you will spend three times as much retrofitting it under audit pressure. The specific cost multiplier: a compliance finding that requires retroactive log collection from systems that were not onboarded costs months of engineering time, the audit remediation timeline, and the business cost of the delayed authorization.

The Director-level decision: when onboarding a new log source or a new system, the compliance logging check happens at the design phase, not after production deployment. This means the platform team has a standing checklist (see the Log Source Onboarding Playbook) that includes compliance classification, retention requirement, and immutable storage configuration as required steps before any new data source goes to production.

That checklist is a forcing function. It ensures compliance logging is a design constraint, not an afterthought.
