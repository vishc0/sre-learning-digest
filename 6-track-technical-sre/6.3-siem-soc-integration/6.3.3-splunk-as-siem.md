# Chapter 3: Splunk as SIEM

## The Architecture Decision That Makes This Work

Splunk Enterprise Security is not a separate product that competes with your observability platform. It is a premium application layer that runs on top of your existing Splunk deployment. Same indexers. Same forwarders. Same data. ES adds three things the base platform does not have: a security-specific data model, a correlation search engine, and an analyst workflow (the notable event queue). 

Understanding this architecture is critical for a Director. The business case for ES is not "we need to buy a new security tool." It is "we can extend our existing Splunk investment to serve the SOC without duplicating infrastructure." That is a fundamentally different conversation with a CFO.

---

## Splunk Enterprise Security Architecture

At the base layer, Splunk indexes data: raw events land on indexers, get time-stamped, and get written to index buckets. This is identical whether you are running base Splunk or ES. The indexers do not know or care that ES is installed.

ES installs on a dedicated search head (or search head cluster). It introduces:

**The Security Data Model:** ES uses the Common Information Model (CIM) to normalize disparate log sources into standard field names. Authentication events from Active Directory, Okta, and Linux PAM all map to the same `Authentication` datamodel fields: `user`, `src`, `action`, `app`. This normalization is what allows a single correlation search to detect authentication anomalies across every auth source simultaneously.

**Correlation Searches:** Scheduled SPL searches that run on a defined interval, look for patterns across time windows, and promote matching events into the notable event queue when the pattern fires. A correlation search is the SIEM equivalent of an alert rule, except it can span multiple data sources, look for sequences of events, and apply risk scoring rather than just binary fire/no-fire logic.

**The Notable Event Queue:** The analyst's work queue. Notables are the output of correlation searches. Each notable carries: the rule that fired, the involved entities (user, system, IP), a severity score, a status (New, In Progress, Closed), and links to the contributing events. Analysts work the notable queue, not the raw alert stream.

**Assets and Identities:** ES maintains lookup tables mapping IP addresses and hostnames to business context (owner, criticality, environment) and mapping usernames to employee profiles (department, title, risk tier). This enrichment is what makes "authentication failure from 10.20.30.40" become "authentication failure from the CEO's laptop in the Finance subnet, classified as critical asset."

## Notable Events: The Fundamental SIEM Concept

Understanding notables is the single most important concept for someone new to SIEM but familiar with Splunk alerting.

A raw Splunk alert fires when a condition is met. It can fire thousands of times per day. Most alerts in observability are fine-grained and high-volume by design — you want to know the moment a threshold is crossed.

A notable event is different. A notable is a correlated observation that has passed through a threshold of accumulated evidence before being promoted to an analyst's queue. A single authentication failure does not produce a notable. Ten thousand authentication failures against five hundred distinct usernames from three source IPs in thirty minutes produces a notable.

The notable lifecycle:

```
Correlation Search fires → Notable created (status: New)
    → Analyst picks it up (status: In Progress)
    → Analyst investigates and closes:
        - Resolved: true positive, incident handled
        - False Positive: rule tuned for next time
        - No Action Required: true but benign
```

The goal of a well-tuned SIEM is to produce notables that are almost always worth an analyst's time. A poorly tuned SIEM produces thousands of notables per day, analysts ignore them, and the SIEM provides no security value. Alert fatigue in SIEM is a system design problem, not an analyst discipline problem.

## Risk-Based Alerting: Why It Reduces Alert Fatigue

Risk-Based Alerting (RBA) is ES's most powerful concept for an SRE audience because the intuition maps directly to SLO error budget thinking.

The idea: instead of alerting on individual events, assign a risk score to each suspicious event and accumulate that score against the entity involved (a user, a system, an IP address). Only when an entity's accumulated risk score crosses a threshold does a notable event get created.

This is directly analogous to error budget burn rate alerting. You do not alert the moment a single request fails. You alert when the burn rate indicates the error budget will be exhausted within a time window. Individual events are noise; the rate of accumulation against a threshold is signal.

A suspicious login from an unusual location might add 30 risk points to a user. A privilege escalation attempt might add 50 points. Access to a sensitive system outside business hours might add 40 points. None of these individual events, in isolation, necessarily warrants an analyst's immediate attention. But a user who accumulates 120 risk points in 24 hours — across three distinct suspicious events — definitely warrants review.

Risk score accumulation query:

```spl
| from datamodel Risk.All_Risk
| stats sum(risk_score) as total_risk, values(source) as contributing_alerts by risk_object, risk_object_type
| where total_risk > 100
| sort -total_risk
```

Risk scores decay over time (configurable). An entity that accrued points three weeks ago and has been clean since should not have stale risk driving a false notable.

## Correlation Searches: The Engine Room

A correlation search is a scheduled SPL search. It runs every N minutes, looks back M minutes, and evaluates whether the result set meets a threshold condition. If it does, it creates a notable.

Example correlation search detecting brute force authentication:

```spl
| tstats count from datamodel=Authentication 
    where Authentication.action="failure" 
    by Authentication.user, Authentication.src, _time span=5m
| where count > 50
| eval risk_score=40, risk_object=Authentication.user, risk_object_type="user"
```

The `tstats` command is critical here. It queries the accelerated data model (pre-aggregated at index time) rather than scanning raw events. This is what makes correlation searches performant at scale. An SRE who understands that `tstats` is to security queries what `mcollect` is to metrics — a pre-aggregation layer that makes time-series queries fast — has the right mental model.

## ESCU: Pre-Built Correlation Content

The Enterprise Security Content Update (ESCU) is Splunk's library of pre-built correlation searches, mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework. Installing ESCU gives you several hundred pre-built detection rules covering the most common adversary techniques.

Not all ESCU content applies to every environment. A rule detecting Windows Event ID 4624 is irrelevant if your environment is Linux-only. Evaluation of ESCU content should follow this sequence:

1. Which MITRE ATT&CK tactics are relevant to your threat model?
2. Which ESCU rules cover those tactics?
3. Of those rules, which require log sources you actually have?
4. Of the applicable rules, which produce acceptable false positive rates against your data?

ESCU rules are a starting point, not a finished product. They require tuning against your specific environment before they produce useful notables.

## SIEM-Specific SPL Patterns

**Authentication anomaly detection using CIM datamodel:**

```spl
| tstats count from datamodel=Authentication 
    where Authentication.action=failure 
    by Authentication.user, Authentication.src 
| where count > 50
| join type=left Authentication.user 
    [| inputlookup identity_lookup_expanded | rename identity AS Authentication.user]
```

**Risk score drill-down for a specific entity:**

```spl
| from datamodel Risk.All_Risk 
| where risk_object="suspicious-service-account-name" 
| sort -_time 
| table _time, source, risk_score, risk_message
```

**Threat intelligence lookup against outbound connections:**

```spl
index=network_traffic direction=outbound
| lookup threatintel_by_cidr dest AS dest_ip 
    OUTPUT threat_category, confidence, threat_group
| where isnotnull(threat_category)
| stats count by dest_ip, threat_category, threat_group
```

**Notable event review for new, high-severity items:**

```spl
| `notable` 
| search status=new severity=high OR severity=critical
| table _time, rule_name, src, dest, user, severity, owner
| sort -_time
```

## Director Frame

Splunk ES is not a separate product to buy — it is a deployment decision on your existing Splunk investment. The ROI conversation is precise: what is the cost of a SOC analyst alert-fatiguing through ten thousand raw alerts per day versus reviewing fifty notable events that have been pre-correlated, risk-scored, and enriched with asset context?

The answer, in most organizations, is that the analyst reviewing raw alerts misses the critical events because they are buried in noise. The notable event model, done correctly, means the analyst who reviews fifty notables catches real threats. The analyst reviewing ten thousand raw alerts does not.

When you own the Splunk platform as a Director, the ES licensing conversation is: "We are already paying for the indexing infrastructure. ES licensing is the incremental cost to turn our SRE observability platform into a platform that serves the SOC as well. The alternative is the SOC buying a separate SIEM, running separate infrastructure, ingesting duplicate data, and delivering worse results because they do not have our platform expertise."

That is a compelling argument when you can back it with the architecture understanding this chapter provides.
