# Chapter 1 — Foundations: On-Call as a Reliability Mechanism

## The Wrong Mental Model

Most engineers inherit the mental model that on-call is a tax — an unavoidable cost of working in operations. Some organizations explicitly or implicitly treat it as a punishment: "you broke it, you page for it." That mental model produces exactly the outcomes you'd expect. Engineers minimize their connection to production systems. Nobody volunteers for on-call. The engineers who do get paged become a separate, resentful caste.

The correct mental model: on-call is a feedback loop. It is the mechanism by which the people who make architectural decisions feel the consequences of those decisions in production. When the engineer who chose an eventually-consistent storage model gets paged at 2am because "eventually" is taking six hours, they make better architectural decisions in the future.

This is the "skin in the game" principle applied to engineering. It is not punitive. It is informational. The page is data.

## The Three Failure Modes of On-Call Programs

Every broken on-call program fails in one or more of three ways. Identifying which failure mode is present is the first diagnostic step.

**Failure Mode 1: Alert Fatigue**
Engineers are receiving more pages than they can meaningfully respond to. The natural adaptation is to lower their engagement threshold — they acknowledge the page, check whether it resolves itself, and go back to sleep. The alert stops being informational and becomes ambient noise. This is the same phenomenon as a car alarm going off in a parking lot: the first time, people look. The hundredth time, people assume it is a false alarm and do nothing. The day a real car gets stolen, nobody responds.

Alert fatigue is almost never an alerting problem — it is a cultural signal that nobody is doing alert quality work, and that the cost of that debt is being paid by individual engineers rather than being visible to management.

**Failure Mode 2: No Runbooks**
Every incident becomes a first-time event. The engineer is paged, stares at a dashboard, and begins a process of discovery that should have been documented from the last three times this exact thing happened. This is the equivalent of an airline that has never written a checklist — experienced pilots can land safely, but every new crew member reinvents the procedure from scratch.

The absence of runbooks is also a knowledge concentration problem. When the one engineer who knows how to restart the notification pipeline leaves, they take that knowledge with them. The runbook is the organizational memory.

**Failure Mode 3: Invisible Cost**
Burnout and attrition are attributed to individual character rather than system design. "They just couldn't handle the pressure." "Some people are not cut out for ops work." This framing protects the system from accountability. The real question is: what does the data say about page rate? What does the after-hours burden look like per engineer? Is the rotation large enough? Is there compensation?

When the cost of on-call is invisible — not measured, not reported, not discussed — it accumulates silently until someone leaves. At that point the rotation shrinks, which increases burden on remaining engineers, which accelerates the next departure. This is the on-call attrition spiral.

## What Healthy Looks Like from the Outside

A Director evaluating an on-call program should be able to answer these questions from data within the first week:

- Pages per engineer per week: is it trending down over the last quarter?
- Actionable rate: are more than half of pages requiring human action?
- After-hours burden: what fraction of pages fire outside business hours?
- Engineer sentiment: do on-call engineers describe the rotation as manageable?
- Runbook coverage: do the top 20 most-fired alerts have runbooks?

Healthy programs have low page rates, high actionable rates, and engineers who describe on-call as occasionally stressful but fundamentally manageable. Engineers in healthy rotations often describe it as a growth experience — they learn the system deeply, they feel trusted, and they see a clear connection between their on-call response and service improvement.

## The Director's Entry Question

When you inherit an on-call program, the first question is not "what tools are we using?" That is an implementation detail. The first question is: **how do engineers feel about on-call?**

Not "how is the on-call program performing?" — that invites metric-level answers. "How do engineers *feel* about on-call?" invites honest sentiment that is the leading indicator of everything else. If engineers describe it with resignation, resentment, or dread, you have found your first signal. The metrics will confirm it.

The tools, the rotation schedule, the runbooks — those are all fixable. Engineer sentiment, once it has curdled, takes much longer to recover. Fix the program first. The sentiment follows.
