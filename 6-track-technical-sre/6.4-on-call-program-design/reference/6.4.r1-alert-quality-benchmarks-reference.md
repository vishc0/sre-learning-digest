# Reference: Alert Quality and On-Call Health Benchmarks

*Sources: Google SRE Book (Beyer et al., O'Reilly), DORA State of DevOps Report (2023), PagerDuty State of Digital Operations Report (2023-2024), industry practitioner consensus. Specific numbers are approximate and represent practitioner consensus rather than exact citations — use as directional benchmarks, not contractual standards.*

---

## Pages per Engineer per Week

| Rating | Pages/Engineer/Week | Interpretation |
|--------|--------------------|-|
| **Excellent** | <3 actionable | Engineers can handle each page with full attention; alert catalog is well-maintained; team has capacity for deep investigation |
| **Good** | 3-5 actionable | Acceptable operational load; engineers maintain investigative quality; sustainable long-term |
| **Acceptable** | 5-7 actionable | Noticeable but manageable; engineers may begin to feel pressure during busy weeks; alert quality work recommended within 60 days |
| **Warning** | 7-10 actionable | Alert fatigue beginning; investigation quality degrades; engineers start to feel on-call as a burden; alert reduction campaign warranted |
| **Critical** | >10 actionable | Engineers are drowning; institutional alert-ignore behavior is developing; attrition risk is active; immediate intervention required |

*Note: These thresholds apply to actionable pages. If your actionable rate is <50%, your total page count will be higher — what matters is the actionable subset. However, even non-actionable pages generate context-switches and sleep disruption.*

## Actionable Rate

| Rating | Actionable Rate | Interpretation |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Excellent** | >80% | Alert catalog is well-curated; nearly all pages require human action; engineers trust the pager |
| **Good** | 70-80% | Strong signal; some noise remaining; ongoing alert quality work is working |
| **Acceptable** | 50-70% | Marginal; more than half of pages are real, but up to half are noise; engineers beginning to develop skepticism |
| **Warning** | 30-50% | Majority of pages are noise; alert fatigue is developing; engineers are conditioning toward ignoring alerts |
| **Critical** | <30% | Engineers can rationally ignore pages because 70%+ are false alarms; this is the state where real incidents get missed; immediate alert audit required |

## After-Hours Page Ratio

| Rating | After-Hours % of Total Pages | Interpretation |
|--------|------------------------------|----------------|
| **Excellent** | <15% | Most incidents surface during business hours; strong observability and proactive monitoring catching issues early |
| **Good** | 15-25% | Acceptable distribution; on-call engineers sleep most nights; sustainable rotation |
| **Acceptable** | 25-35% | Significant after-hours burden; engineers are losing sleep regularly; rotation size and alert quality should be evaluated |
| **Warning** | 35-50% | Nearly as many after-hours pages as business-hours pages; severe quality-of-life impact; attrition signal |
| **Critical** | >50% | More than half of pages are after-hours; engineers are effectively working a night shift without scheduling or compensation for it; immediate action required |

*After-hours defined as: 10pm–7am local time for weekday pages; all hours Friday 10pm – Monday 7am for weekend pages.*

## MTTA (Mean Time to Acknowledge) Targets by Severity

| Severity | SLA Target (Acknowledge) | Industry Median | P95 (High-Performing Teams) | Note |
|---------|------------------------|----------------|---------------------------|------|
| **P0 / SEV1** | 5 minutes | 4-7 min | <3 min | Any P0 miss >10 min warrants escalation to manager and postmortem item |
| **P1 / SEV2** | 10 minutes | 8-12 min | <6 min | Missed P1 MTTA should be reviewed in weekly ops sync |
| **P2 / SEV3** | 30 minutes | 20-35 min | <15 min | P2 MTTA matters less for customer impact but signals engineer engagement |
| **P3 / SEV4** | Business hours (no page SLA) | N/A | N/A | P3 is ticket-based; no pager expectation |

| Severity | SLA Target (Resolve) | Industry Median | P95 (High-Performing Teams) | Note |
|---------|---------------------|----------------|---------------------------|------|
| **P0 / SEV1** | 60 minutes | 45-90 min | <30 min | Extended P0 (>2 hours) should trigger executive escalation and real-time communication to affected customers |
| **P1 / SEV2** | 4 hours | 2-6 hours | <90 min | P1 resolution >4 hours should trigger postmortem |

## On-Call Compensation Benchmarks

| Company Type | Level | Explicit Stipend Range | Implicit Compensation Notes |
|-------------|-------|----------------------|----------------------------|
| **Startup (<500 employees)** | Engineer | $0-200/week | Often equity-framed; less formal; recovery time informal |
| **Startup (<500 employees)** | Senior/Staff | $150-400/week | More likely to have written policy at Staff+ level |
| **Mid-size (500-5000)** | Engineer | $200-500/week | More formalized; HR-approved policies more common |
| **Mid-size (500-5000)** | Senior/Staff | $300-600/week | Often tied to on-call expectations in job description |
| **Enterprise (5000+)** | Engineer | $300-600/week | Formal comp framework; may be baked into total comp |
| **Enterprise (5000+)** | Senior/Staff/Principal | $400-800/week | Often part of annual total comp calculation |
| **FAANG/Tier-1** | All levels | $500-1500+/week | Well-established programs; often include significant time-off provisions |

*Note: T-Mobile as a large enterprise should fall in the $300-600/week range for SRE engineer-level on-call stipend. If your team currently has no stipend at above-warning page rates, this is the benchmark to use in a business case.*

**Implicit compensation that high performers expect (regardless of explicit stipend):**
- Written recovery time policy enforced by manager (not just informal understanding)
- On-call performance visible in performance review as a named contribution
- Career growth opportunities (incident command, postmortem authorship) tied to on-call experience
- Named acknowledgment in team communications for significant on-call contributions

## Rotation Size Benchmarks

| Rating | Rotation Size | Engineer On-Call Frequency | Sustainability |
|--------|--------------|---------------------------|----------------|
| **Excellent** | 8+ engineers | ~1 week per 2 months | Very sustainable; engineers maintain skills without excessive burden |
| **Good** | 6-7 engineers | ~1 week per month | Sustainable; acceptable for most teams |
| **Acceptable** | 5 engineers | ~1.2 weeks per month | Borderline; vacation coverage is tight |
| **Warning** | 4 engineers | ~1 week per month | Minimum viable; any departure or leave creates acute pressure |
| **Critical** | 3 engineers | ~1.3 weeks per month | Unsustainable with any variance; single departure makes rotation non-functional |
| **Emergency** | 2 engineers | ~2 weeks per month | This is not a rotation — this is two engineers sharing a pager indefinitely; immediate action required |

*Rotation size benchmark assumes 7-day rotation periods. Adjust if your rotation period differs.*

## Runbook Coverage Benchmarks

| Runbook Coverage | Rating | Interpretation |
|-----------------|--------|----------------|
| 100% of P0 alerts have runbooks | Excellent | Every critical incident has a documented response path |
| 100% of P0/P1 alerts have runbooks | Good | Strong coverage for pages that wake engineers |
| >80% of P0/P1 alerts have runbooks | Acceptable | Minor gaps; fill within next quarter |
| 60-80% of P0/P1 alerts have runbooks | Warning | Significant coverage gaps; major incidents may lack documented response |
| <60% of P0/P1 alerts have runbooks | Critical | Engineers routinely respond to major incidents without documented guidance; reinventing response every time; high MTTR impact |

**Runbook freshness benchmark**: Runbooks not reviewed in >90 days are considered potentially stale. Runbooks not reviewed in >180 days should be treated as unreliable until verified. Industry benchmark for top-20 runbook review cadence: quarterly.

## Alert Review Cadence Benchmarks

| Practice | Frequency | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Review top-20 noisiest alerts | Monthly | Identify candidates for reduction; track trend |
| Full alert catalog review | Quarterly | Classify all alerts; delete/downgrade stale/noisy |
| Alert reduction campaign | As needed (at least annually) | Structured campaign with defined targets and change log |
| New alert review (before adding) | Per-alert, before publishing | New alerts require: symptom-based framing, runbook link, service owner, actionable rate baseline |
| Runbook review (top-20 alerts) | Quarterly | Assign owner; test during game day or real incident |
