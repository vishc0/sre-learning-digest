# Chapter 4 — Escalation Policy Design: Who Gets Woken Up and When

## Why Escalation Policy Is Not Tribal Knowledge

In most organizations, the de facto escalation policy lives in the heads of senior engineers. "If you can't figure it out in 20 minutes, ping Alex. If it's really bad, text the manager." This works when the team is small, stable, and experienced. It fails completely when an engineer is new, when Alex is on vacation, when the incident is novel, or when the person on-call at 2am has only been in the rotation for three weeks.

An escalation policy is a written document that defines, for every incident type and severity level, who gets contacted, through what channel, at what time threshold, and with what information. If your escalation policy is not written down, it does not exist in the moments when it matters most — high-pressure situations where a junior engineer is trying to make a fast decision about whether to wake someone up.

## The Four Escalation Tiers

**Tier 1 — Primary On-Call Engineer**
- Who: the rotating primary on-call
- Response SLA: acknowledge within 5 minutes of page
- Resolution SLA: initial assessment within 15 minutes
- Authority: can resolve most P2/P3 incidents independently; should not hesitate to escalate for P0/P1
- Contact method: PagerDuty page → phone call if no acknowledge in 5 min

**Tier 2 — Secondary On-Call (Senior Engineer or Tech Lead)**
- Who: the rotating secondary, typically senior SRE or service tech lead
- When to escalate: after 10-15 minutes with no Tier 1 acknowledgment, OR explicit request from Tier 1
- Escalation trigger: automatic (timeout) or manual (Tier 1 judgment)
- What to provide when escalating: current state of incident, steps already taken, hypothesis about cause
- Contact method: PagerDuty secondary escalation or direct Slack message

**Tier 3 — On-Call Manager**
- Who: the SRE manager or designated management coverage
- When to escalate: for any P0 (customer-impacting), any P1 not resolved within 30 minutes, any incident requiring resources or cross-team authority, any incident the primary/secondary disagrees on how to handle
- Expectation: the manager is not expected to debug — they are expected to remove obstacles, make decisions about trade-offs, coordinate communication, and escalate further if needed
- Contact method: phone call or PagerDuty; never just Slack for P0/P1

**Tier 4 — Director / VP**
- Who: the executive on-call or designated senior leadership
- When to escalate: Severity 1 events affecting material business metrics (revenue, regulatory reporting, media-visible outages), sustained P0 incidents exceeding 60 minutes, security incidents with data exposure risk, incidents requiring external vendor escalation at an executive level
- Expectation: situational awareness and decision-making authority; not operational involvement
- Contact method: phone call; provide a 3-sentence situation summary before calling

## Severity Matrix: Who Gets Paged at What Threshold

| Severity | Description | Tier 1 | Tier 2 | Tier 3 | Tier 4 |
|---------|-------------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| P0 / SEV1 | Full outage, data loss, regulatory impact | Immediate | Immediate | Immediate notify | If >60 min or material |
| P1 / SEV2 | Partial outage, significant customer impact | Immediate | If P1 >20 min | Notify within 15 min | No |
| P2 / SEV3 | Degraded performance, limited customer impact | Immediate | On explicit request | Async notification | No |
| P3 / SEV4 | Minor issue, minimal impact, no SLO breach | Ticket (no page) | No | No | No |

The table must be written into the escalation policy document and must be reviewed with the team during onboarding and during quarterly program reviews.

## Escalation Paths by Incident Type

Not all incidents follow the same path. Define escalation paths by incident category, not just by severity.

**Infrastructure outage (EKS node failure, network partition, storage degradation)**
- Start with platform SRE on-call
- Escalate to cloud provider support (AWS TAM) for EKS control plane events
- Parallel-notify application SRE if services are impacted

**Data incident (data corruption, pipeline backlog, message loss)**
- Start with application SRE on-call
- Immediate escalation to Tier 3 (manager) regardless of confirmed scope — data incidents have regulatory implications
- Trigger data incident response protocol in parallel with technical triage

**Security incident (unauthorized access, credential exposure, anomalous API behavior)**
- Security incidents bypass the standard escalation chain
- Tier 1 notifies security team lead and on-call manager simultaneously at time of detection
- Standard SRE on-call is support, not lead — security team leads the response

**Third-party dependency failure (carrier outage, payment processor degraded, SMS gateway down)**
- Tier 1 identifies as external when no internal metrics show degradation
- Escalate to Tier 3 for vendor escalation authority
- Customer communications may need to start within 15 minutes — Tier 3 makes that call

## The No-Skip Escalation Rule

The most common escalation anti-pattern is bypassing Tier 2 to escalate directly to the manager or Director. This happens when an engineer does not feel confident, wants visible authority in the incident, or has not been trained on what Tier 2 is for.

Skipping Tier 2 creates learned helplessness in two directions. The engineer never develops the confidence to resolve incidents at the Tier 2 level because they always hand off early. The manager becomes the de facto technical resolver for incidents that should not require management involvement. The Tier 2 engineer is never developed because they are never in the incident.

The rule: you may skip a tier only when the tier is explicitly unavailable (Tier 2 engineer is the same person as Tier 1 because of rotation coverage; an emergency contact has been pre-designated for the skip). Otherwise, escalate in sequence.

## Director Framing: Written Policy vs. Head Knowledge

The escalation policy test: if you asked the newest engineer on your rotation right now, at midnight, "who do you call if this incident is not resolved in 20 minutes?" — could they answer without looking anything up? If the answer requires them to know which senior engineer knows which service, you do not have a policy. You have accumulated institutional knowledge that exists only as long as the people who hold it do.

A written policy that is published, reviewed at onboarding, and revisited quarterly is the minimum. PagerDuty/Opsgenie schedules should reflect the policy exactly — not approximate it. The tool is the enforcement mechanism for the written policy.
