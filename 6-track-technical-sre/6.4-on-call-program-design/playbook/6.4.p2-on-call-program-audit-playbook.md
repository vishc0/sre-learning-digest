# Playbook: On-Call Program Audit

## Purpose and When to Use

Use this playbook when:
- You have inherited an on-call program and need to establish its current health within your first 30 days
- Conducting a quarterly health review of an existing program
- Following an attrition event where on-call burden may have been a contributing factor
- Preparing a business case for headcount, tooling, or compensation changes

**Expected time to complete**: 3-5 business days (data collection intensive; analysis and reporting take 1-2 additional days)

**Output**: On-Call Program Audit Report (use the on-call program design template as the target-state document after findings are reviewed)

---

## Roles

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|----------------|
| **Auditor** (you / Director / SRE Manager) | Owns the process, conducts interviews, synthesizes findings |
| **PagerDuty/Opsgenie Admin** | Exports raw page data, escalation policy config, service lists |
| **Splunk Admin** | Runs SPL queries if PagerDuty data is indexed in Splunk |
| **Team Leads** | Provide service ownership context, runbook inventory, current rotation membership |
| **HR/People Partner** | Provides attrition data for on-call engineers (request separately; may require approval) |

---

## Phase 1 — Data Collection

### Step 1: Export 90-Day Page History
**Who**: PagerDuty/Opsgenie Admin

From PagerDuty:
- Analytics → Incidents → Filter last 90 days → Export CSV
- Include fields: incident ID, service, severity, created time, acknowledged time, resolved time, assigned to, was escalated (Y/N)

From Opsgenie:
- Reports → Alert Reports → Custom date range → Download CSV
- Include fields: alert ID, team, priority, created at, acknowledged at, closed at, owner, escalated

**Decision gate**: If no export is possible (permissions issue, data retention gap), escalate to PagerDuty/Opsgenie account owner before proceeding. Do not estimate this data.

---

### Step 2: Calculate Per-Engineer Page Rates
**Who**: Auditor (using exported data)

For each engineer in the rotation, calculate:
- Total pages in 90 days
- Pages per week (total ÷ 13)
- After-hours pages (10pm–7am local time)
- Weekend pages (Fri 10pm – Mon 7am)

Create a table sorted by total pages descending. Flag any engineer at more than 2x the team average.

If PagerDuty data is indexed in Splunk, use this SPL:
```spl
index=pagerduty sourcetype=pagerduty_webhook earliest=-90d
| eval hour=strftime(_time, "%H")
| eval is_after_hours=if(hour<7 OR hour>=22, 1, 0)
| eval is_weekend=if(strftime(_time,"%u")>5, 1, 0)
| stats count as total_pages,
        sum(is_after_hours) as after_hours_pages,
        sum(is_weekend) as weekend_pages,
        avg(is_after_hours)*100 as after_hours_pct
  by assigned_to_summary
| sort -total_pages
```

---

### Step 3: Calculate Actionable Rate
**Who**: Auditor

From the exported page data, identify incidents that were:
- Resolved without acknowledgment (auto-resolved): non-actionable
- Acknowledged and resolved within 2 minutes with no follow-up work logged: likely non-actionable
- Acknowledged, with work logged or resolved after >5 minutes: actionable

Calculate: `actionable_pages / total_pages * 100 = actionable rate %`

Note: If your tooling does not distinguish actionable vs. non-actionable, conduct a sample review — pull 20 random incidents and review the timeline. The sample rate will be directionally accurate.

---

### Step 4: Identify Top-10 Noisiest Alerts
**Who**: Auditor with PagerDuty/Opsgenie Admin

Sort all alerts by fire count over the 90-day window. Take the top 10. For each:
- Record fire count
- Record actionable rate (if available)
- Record which team/service owns it
- Record whether a runbook exists (check Confluence/GitHub/Notion)

---

### Step 5: Review Runbook Coverage
**Who**: Team Leads

For each P0 and P1 alert in the catalog (not just the top 10 — all of them):
- Does a runbook exist? (Y/N)
- When was the runbook last updated?
- Is the runbook linked in the PagerDuty alert body? (Y/N)

Calculate: `alerts with runbooks / total P0+P1 alerts * 100 = runbook coverage %`

---

### Step 6: Review Compensation Policy
**Who**: Auditor, with HR/People Partner if needed

Determine:
- Is there an explicit on-call stipend? If yes, amount and frequency.
- Is there a recovery time policy for after-hours incidents? If yes, is it written and enforced?
- Is on-call performance visible in performance reviews? Ask team leads directly.
- When was the compensation policy last reviewed?

---

### Step 7: Pull Attrition Data for On-Call Engineers
**Who**: Auditor + HR/People Partner

Request from HRBP:
- Engineers who left in the last 18 months who were active on-call rotation members
- Exit interview themes if available (specifically ask about on-call burden)
- Tenure at departure for on-call engineers vs. non-on-call engineers (if available)

This step may require a separate meeting with your HRBP. Schedule it in week 1.

---

### Step 8: Review Last Escalation Policy Change Date
**Who**: Auditor + PagerDuty/Opsgenie Admin

In PagerDuty: Settings → Escalation Policies → check "Last Modified" on each policy
In Opsgenie: Teams → Escalation Policies → audit log

Record: when was the escalation policy last reviewed? Is it written anywhere outside the tool?

**Decision gate**: If the escalation policy has not been reviewed in more than 12 months, flag this as a High finding regardless of other metrics.

---

## Phase 2 — Analysis

### Step 9: Calculate Health Scores

For each metric, assign a zone:

| Metric | Green | Yellow | Red |
|--------|-------|--------|-----|
| Pages/engineer/week | <5 | 5-10 | >10 |
| Actionable rate | >70% | 50-70% | <50% |
| After-hours page ratio | <20% | 20-40% | >40% |
| Runbook coverage (P0/P1) | >90% | 60-90% | <60% |
| Rotation size | ≥6 | 4-5 | ≤3 |
| Compensation policy | Written + enforced | Informal | None |

---

### Step 10: Identify Red/Yellow/Green Zones

Map each metric to its zone. Any Red finding requires a specific remediation recommendation in the report. Yellow findings require a monitoring plan.

---

### Step 11: Map Alert-to-Service Ownership

For the top-10 noisiest alerts, confirm:
- Is there a named service owner for each alert?
- Does the alert route to the correct team in PagerDuty/Opsgenie?
- Is the on-call rotation for that service up to date?

Unowned alerts are orphaned reliability risk. Flag any alert with no named owner.

---

### Step 12: Identify Engineers with >2x Average Page Load

From the Step 2 table, flag any engineer receiving more than 2x the team average. Investigate:
- Are they on-call more frequently due to rotation size issues?
- Do they own more services than others?
- Are they being escalated to disproportionately (secondary paged as if primary)?

---

### Step 13: Flag Missing Runbooks for Top-20 Alerts

Extend the runbook coverage check from Step 5. For the 20 most-fired alerts (regardless of severity), identify how many lack runbooks. These are the highest-priority runbook creation tasks because the alerts fire most frequently.

---

### Step 14: Identify Last Toil Reduction Effort

Ask team leads: when did the team last spend dedicated time reducing alert noise? Was there a sprint, a dedicated day, a campaign? If the answer is "never" or "I don't remember," that is a finding.

---

## Phase 3 — Findings Report

### Step 15: Format Findings

Use this structure for each finding:

```
Finding [N]: [Short title]
Zone: Red / Yellow / Green
Metric: [Specific metric and current value]
Benchmark: [Target value]
Impact: [What happens if this is not addressed]
Recommendation: [Specific action with owner and timeline]
```

---

### Step 16: Prioritize Findings by Severity

| Priority | Criteria | Action Timeline |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| **P1 — Immediate** | Red finding + active harm (engineer >15 pages/week, <30% actionable rate, rotation ≤2) | Within 2 weeks |
| **P2 — 30-Day** | Red finding without active acute harm (no compensation, missing runbooks, stale escalation policy) | Within 30 days |
| **P3 — Quarterly** | Yellow finding (rotation <6, MTTA above target, partial runbook coverage) | Within 90 days |
| **P4 — Monitor** | Green finding with minor gaps | Track at next quarterly review |

---

### Step 17: Prepare Executive Summary

The executive summary for your VP should be exactly one page. Include:
1. Program health summary (overall: Red/Yellow/Green)
2. The three VP metrics with current values and trend
3. Top 3 findings with priority and recommended action
4. Resource requirement (what you need to fix P1 and P2 findings: sprint capacity, headcount, budget)
5. Timeline to reach Green on the three primary metrics

**Decision gate**: Present executive summary to your direct manager before distributing broadly. Findings about engineer burden and attrition risk are sensitive and should be framed carefully before they reach HR or Finance.
