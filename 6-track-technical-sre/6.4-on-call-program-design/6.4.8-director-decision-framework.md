# Chapter 8 — Director Decision Framework: Diagnosing and Redesigning On-Call Programs

## The First 30-60 Days: Diagnosis Before Action

The most common mistake a new Director makes with an inherited on-call program is acting too fast. They see the symptoms — engineers complaining, high alert volume, missing runbooks — and start fixing things before they understand the system. Fixes made without a full diagnosis often address symptoms while leaving the underlying structure intact.

The goal of your first 30 days is diagnosis, not remediation. Resist the pull to show early progress by changing things. Change built on incomplete understanding creates new problems.

---

## Data to Collect in the First 30 Days

**Pull from PagerDuty/Opsgenie:**
- 90-day page history, per engineer, per service, per time-of-day
- Total pages, after-hours pages, weekend pages, consecutive-page incidents (3+ pages within a 4-hour window)
- Actionable rate: % of incidents that required human action to resolve
- MTTA by severity (P0/P1/P2)
- Top-10 noisiest alerts by fire count over 90 days

**Pull from Splunk (if PagerDuty data is in Splunk):**
```spl
index=pagerduty sourcetype=pagerduty_webhook
| eval hour=strftime(_time, "%H")
| eval is_after_hours=if(hour<7 OR hour>=22, 1, 0)
| eval is_weekend=if(strftime(_time,"%u")>5, 1, 0)
| stats count as total_pages,
        sum(is_after_hours) as after_hours_pages,
        sum(is_weekend) as weekend_pages
  by assigned_to_summary
| sort -total_pages
```

**Pull from HR/People data (request from your manager or HRBP):**
- Attrition rate for engineers who were on-call in the last 18 months
- Exit interview themes if available
- Tenure distribution for current on-call rotation members

**Document inventory:**
- How many P0/P1 alerts exist? How many have runbooks? (Coverage ratio)
- When was the escalation policy last updated?
- When was the on-call compensation policy last reviewed?
- Is there any historical data on on-call health metrics? Or is this the first time anyone has looked?

---

## Conversations to Have in the First 30 Days

**1:1 with every on-call engineer**: open with "Tell me about the last time you got paged between 10pm and 7am and what the experience was like." Do not ask about the alert or the system — ask about the experience. What happened, what did they do, how did they feel afterward, what would have made it better. This question reliably produces unfiltered honesty about the state of the program.

**Skip-level with engineers who recently left**: if any on-call engineers have left in the last 12 months and you can get a conversation, do it. Ask about on-call burden specifically. Departing engineers often have the clearest view of systemic problems that remaining engineers have normalized.

**With your manager or peers**: what does leadership know about on-call health? Are there prior escalations, prior proposals for improvement, prior budget requests that were declined? Understanding the organizational history prevents you from proposing solutions that were already rejected and not understanding why.

---

## Red Flags Requiring Immediate Action

Some findings cannot wait for the 30-day diagnosis to complete. These warrant immediate intervention:

- **Any engineer receiving >15 pages/week**: this is beyond the threshold where investigation quality is meaningful. Immediate alert suppression or rotation adjustment is warranted.
- **Actionable rate <30%**: more than 70% of pages are noise. Engineers have learned to dismiss alerts. Immediate alert audit campaign is warranted.
- **No runbooks for P0 alerts**: every P0 incident is a first-time event. This is a reliability risk that cannot wait for a quarterly review.
- **No on-call compensation with >10 pages/week burden**: attrition risk is active. Surface this to your manager immediately with the cost model.
- **Rotation of 2-3 engineers with no near-term headcount**: this is not an on-call program — it is a two-person burnout trajectory. Begin the headcount case immediately.

## The 90-Day Redesign Roadmap

**Days 1-30: Audit and Baseline**
- Complete data collection (above)
- Complete stakeholder conversations
- Publish baseline metrics: pages/engineer/week, actionable rate, after-hours ratio, runbook coverage %
- Do NOT make structural changes — only gather and baseline
- Deliverable: On-Call Program Audit Report (see audit playbook)

**Days 31-60: Quick Wins**
- Alert triage sprint: identify top-10 noisiest alerts, classify each (delete/downgrade/merge/automate), implement changes
- Runbook gap close: write runbooks for all P0 alerts that lack them; assign ownership, set review dates
- Add runbook links to all P0/P1 PagerDuty services
- Validate rotation size; if below 4 engineers on any rotation, initiate headcount case or rotation restructure
- Implement auto-suppression for known maintenance windows
- Deliverable: Updated alert catalog + runbook coverage report + headcount case (if needed)

**Days 61-90: Structural Changes**
- Redesign rotation if needed (see ch02)
- Rewrite escalation policy as a formal document; publish and review with team
- Draft or update on-call compensation policy; present to HR/Finance with cost model
- Implement on-call health dashboard (see dashboard spec template)
- Define quarterly alert review cadence; assign owner
- Deliverable: Published program design document + first health dashboard + compensation proposal

## The Three Numbers You Report to Your VP

When you report on-call program health to your VP, use exactly three metrics. More than three loses the audience. Fewer than three lacks sufficient signal.

**Metric 1: Pages per engineer per week (trending)**
Present as a 13-week trend line, not a point-in-time number. The trend is the signal. A current rate of 8 pages/week that was 15 pages/week two months ago is a success story. A current rate of 8 that was 5 two months ago is a warning.

**Metric 2: Actionable rate (trending)**
Same 13-week trend. Target: 70%+. An upward trend shows that alert quality work is having effect.

**Metric 3: After-hours page ratio**
What percentage of total pages fired outside business hours? This is the human cost metric that resonates with executives. "45% of our pages are firing between 10pm and 7am" lands differently than "we have some noise in our alerts."

## Interview Question Frameworks

**"How would you fix a team experiencing alert fatigue?"**

Frame your answer in three phases: Diagnose (pull 90 days of data, calculate actionable rate, identify the top-10 noisiest alerts), Intervene (alert reduction campaign — delete, downgrade, merge, automate), Sustain (quarterly alert review as a standing ceremony, alert SLOs with owner accountability). Emphasize that alert fatigue is a system failure, not an individual failure, and that the fix requires management commitment to protect sprint capacity for alert quality work.

**"How do you balance on-call burden with team health?"**

Lead with measurement: you cannot balance what you do not measure. Describe the specific metrics (pages/engineer/week, after-hours ratio, consecutive pages) and the thresholds that trigger action. Then describe the two levers: demand reduction (alert quality, auto-remediation) and supply increase (rotation size, compensation, recovery time). Acknowledge that there is a tension between moving fast and sustainable operations, and that your job as a Director is to make that tension visible to the business rather than absorbing it silently in the team.

**"What metrics do you use to evaluate on-call program health?"**

Lead with the three VP metrics above, then go deeper: MTTA by severity, runbook coverage %, rotation size adequacy, after-hours burden per engineer (not team average). The ability to name specific thresholds (actionable rate target: 70%; pages/engineer/week warning: 10, critical: 15) signals operational maturity. Generic answers about "tracking incidents and learning from them" do not distinguish a Director candidate.
