# Chapter 6 — On-Call Health and Sustainability: The Human Cost of Reliability

## A Direct Address

As a manager of a 15-person SRE team at T-Mobile — running a 25M message/day notification platform on EKS — you are very likely inheriting some version of a broken on-call program right now. Maybe the previous manager never tracked pager load per engineer. Maybe the rotation has been the same four people for two years. Maybe engineers mention being tired in 1:1s but nothing has changed. Maybe you are seeing attrition and you suspect on-call is part of it, but you do not have the data to say so definitively.

Here is how to diagnose it.

---

## Pager Load Metrics That Reveal Health

Do not start with the team average. Averages hide the distribution, and the distribution is where the problem lives.

**Pull these numbers per engineer for the last 90 days:**

1. **Total pages received** — raw count
2. **After-hours pages** — any page between 10pm and 7am in the engineer's local timezone
3. **Weekend pages** — Friday 10pm through Monday 7am
4. **Consecutive pages** — incidents where the same engineer was paged 3+ times within a single 4-hour window

Now sort by total pages. Who is at the top? Is it always the same engineer? Is one engineer receiving 3x the load of others? That is your first finding.

The 90-day window is important. A 30-day window can be unrepresentative (one bad week skews everything). A 90-day window gives you seasonal patterns, identifies chronic high-burden engineers, and is long enough to correlate with any attrition events.

**The metric that surprises most managers**: consecutive pages within a shift. An engineer who gets paged 12 times in a month — but never more than once per incident — is having a very different experience than an engineer who gets paged 3 times before 3am during a single cascade event. Consecutive pages trigger cognitive overload. After the third page in a row, the quality of investigation degrades significantly.

---

## The Burnout Cascade

The burnout cascade is a self-reinforcing system, and it moves faster than most managers expect.

Excessive paging forces engineers into a mode of rapid-triage-and-dismiss rather than genuine investigation. They stop looking for root causes because there is not enough recovery time between pages to do deep work. Alerts that should be investigated are acknowledged and monitored for auto-resolution. This means the underlying problems are never fixed, which means the same alerts fire again, which means the page rate stays high.

As the page rate stays high, engineers begin to route around the cognitive discomfort of alert fatigue by lowering their engagement threshold. They acknowledge faster, investigate less, and close faster. The actionable rate drops — not because the alerts are less real, but because the responses are less thorough. Now you have a team that is both burned out and operationally less effective.

The final stage is attrition. Engineers either formally request removal from the rotation, quietly disengage and begin job searching, or leave. When they leave, the rotation shrinks, which increases per-engineer burden, which accelerates the next departure. A Director who does not break this cycle will lose engineers.

Breaking the cycle requires both demand reduction (alert quality work, auto-remediation) and supply stabilization (rotation size, compensation, recognition). Doing only one will not hold.

---

## On-Call Compensation

Compensation for on-call burden exists on a spectrum, and where your organization sits on that spectrum has direct implications for attrition risk.

**Explicit compensation**:
- Cash stipend per on-call shift (industry range: $200-800/week at engineer levels; varies widely by company tier and geography)
- Overtime/TOIL pay for after-hours incidents that exceed some threshold (typically 1+ hour of active incident work outside business hours)

**Implicit compensation**:
- Protected recovery time: an engineer who worked a 2am incident for 3 hours gets compensatory time the next morning — not informally, but explicitly, in writing, with manager enforcement
- Career visibility: on-call engineers who lead incident responses and write strong postmortems should have that work visible during performance review, not invisible as "just doing ops"
- Recognition: monthly acknowledgment of on-call contributors in team channels or planning ceremonies; not empty recognition but specific callouts of problems solved

**The dangerous middle**: no explicit compensation, no implicit recognition, high burden. This is the configuration that produces the fastest attrition. Engineers are paying a real cost (sleep disruption, cognitive load, schedule disruption) and receiving nothing in return beyond their base salary. They eventually do the math and leave.

As a Director-level leader, your leverage point here is the explicit recognition and recovery time policies — you can implement these without organizational approval in most cases. The cash stipend requires a compensation framework change, which requires a business case. Build that business case using the attrition cost model: one senior SRE departure costs $200-400K in recruiting, ramp time, and lost institutional knowledge. A $400/week on-call stipend for a 4-person rotation costs $83K/year. The math usually favors the stipend.

## On-Call Toil Reduction Strategies

**Auto-remediation for known-pattern alerts**: if the same alert fires, the same diagnostic confirms a known cause, and the same action resolves it — every time, without exception — that is a candidate for automation. Kubernetes pod OOMKill restarts are the most common example: when a pod is OOMKilled and the service is stateless, an auto-restart is safe, idempotent, and faster than human response. The runbook for this alert becomes a validation runbook — the automation executes, and the engineer verifies that it worked.

**Alert suppression during maintenance windows**: the single most immediate noise reduction available. If your platform has a weekly deployment window that generates 15-20 alerts for expected pod restarts and traffic re-routing, and those alerts are paging engineers, that is 15-20 unnecessary pages per week that can be eliminated today with suppression configuration. Maintenance windows should be defined in PagerDuty/Opsgenie and linked to alert suppression rules.

**Dedicated toil reduction sprints**: one sprint per quarter where the primary deliverable is alert quality work. No feature work. The sprint backlog consists of: delete/downgrade noisy alerts, write missing runbooks, close stale postmortem action items, instrument auto-remediation for one high-frequency alert. This makes the investment visible in planning and gives engineers dedicated time for work that otherwise gets deprioritized against feature delivery.

## Engineer Attrition and On-Call Correlation

Industry research (Google SRE Book, DORA, PagerDuty State of Digital Operations) consistently shows average SRE tenure at organizations with high on-call burden ranges from 18-24 months. At organizations with healthy on-call programs, tenure extends to 3-5 years.

The 18-month pattern is significant: it is long enough that the engineer fully ramps, contributes meaningfully, becomes a senior operational resource — and then leaves because the burnout cost exceeds the compensation. The organization pays the ramp cost, receives peak contribution for 6-12 months, and then pays the departure cost. This is the most expensive possible way to staff an on-call rotation.

---

## The Director Conversation with Your VP

On-call health should not be presented as a morale issue. VPs hear "team health" and think "management problem." Present it as a reliability risk metric.

The frame: "Our current on-call program is producing [X] pages/engineer/week against an industry benchmark of [Y]. Our actionable rate is [Z]% against a target of 70%. At current burden levels, we are likely to see [N] attrition events in the next 12 months based on industry norms for this load level. Each attrition event costs approximately $[amount] in replacement and ramp costs. The investment required to bring on-call health to acceptable levels is [cost of stipend + tooling + sprint capacity]. The ROI case is straightforward."

This is a reliability and cost conversation. Keep it there.
