# Chapter 3 — Alert Quality Engineering: The War on Noise

## Why Alert Quality Is an Engineering Problem

Alert quality is not a configuration problem — it is an engineering discipline. The difference matters because configuration problems get fixed by adjusting a threshold. Engineering problems require sustained design work, ownership, and measurement.

A well-run organization treats its alert catalog the way it treats its codebase: it has owners, it has quality standards, it gets reviewed, and it gets deleted when it is no longer serving its purpose. A poorly-run organization treats alerts as append-only artifacts — something gets broken, someone writes an alert, nobody deletes it, and the catalog grows without bound while quality decays.

The result of an untended alert catalog is the same as the result of an untended codebase: it becomes increasingly difficult to work with, it generates friction for everyone who touches it, and the people who understand it least start making decisions about it.

## Alert Noise Taxonomy

Before you can fix alert quality, you need to classify what you have. Not all noise is the same kind of noise.

**Symptom alerts vs. cause alerts**: The gold standard alert fires on customer-observable impact — high error rate, elevated latency, failed transactions. A cause alert fires on the thing that *might* cause impact — high CPU on a node, elevated memory usage, a queue depth increasing. Cause alerts are useful for investigation dashboards; they are not appropriate as pages. A pod at 90% CPU that is not impacting service SLOs should not wake an engineer. The engineer cannot do anything useful about 90% CPU if the service is healthy.

**Flapping alerts**: An alert that fires, resolves, and fires again within a short window (under 10 minutes) without human intervention. Engineers rapidly learn to ignore flapping alerts because they resolve themselves. The problem is that the underlying condition causing the flap is never investigated — it is masked by the auto-resolution. Flapping alerts are also extremely difficult to acknowledge because by the time the engineer responds, the alert has resolved. The acknowledgment happens after the fact, producing misleading MTTA metrics.

**Noisy alerts**: A single underlying condition that generates multiple pages — for example, one degraded node triggering individual pod-restart alerts for every pod that was running on that node. The engineer gets 40 pages for one event. Noisy alerts should be merged into a single diagnostic alert with context about scope.

**Stale alerts**: Alerts written for a system that has changed. The threshold was appropriate for 2021 traffic volumes but the system now handles 10x the load. The service being monitored was deprecated six months ago and the alert still fires against dead metrics. Stale alerts accumulate silently and are only discovered during an audit or when they fire in an obviously wrong context.

**Watchdog alerts**: Informational signals that were miscategorized as pages. "The daily report generation completed successfully." "The batch job ran at 2am as expected." These belong in a dashboard or a ticket, not in PagerDuty. They inflate page counts, reduce actionable rates, and train engineers to dismiss pages.

## Alert Quality Metrics Every Director Should Know

**Pages per engineer per week**: The raw volume metric. Measure per individual, not as a team average. A team average of 5 pages/week can hide one engineer receiving 20 pages while others receive 2. The distribution matters more than the mean.

Target thresholds:
- Excellent: fewer than 5 actionable pages/engineer/week
- Acceptable: 5-10 actionable pages/engineer/week  
- Warning: 10-15 pages/engineer/week
- Critical: more than 15 pages/engineer/week

**Actionable rate**: The percentage of pages that required human action to resolve. An alert that fires and resolves without any human intervention was not actionable. Target: greater than 50% for an average program; greater than 80% for a high-performing program.

**False positive rate**: Pages that fired for no actual problem. Different from non-actionable — a page can be real but resolve before the engineer can act (flapping). A false positive is a page where no degradation existed.

**Mean Time to Acknowledge (MTTA)**: How quickly engineers acknowledge pages. MTTA is both a reliability metric and an alert quality signal. High MTTA can mean engineers are sleeping, or it can mean engineers are deprioritizing pages because they have learned that most pages are noise.

## Alert SLOs: Treating Alert Quality as a Product

A mature practice treats the alert catalog as a reliability product with its own SLOs. This framing changes behavior: instead of "we should reduce noise" (aspirational), you have "our actionable rate is 43% against a target of 70%" (measurable deficit with a clear owner).

Example alert quality SLO targets:
- Actionable rate: ≥70% per rotation, measured monthly
- Pages per engineer per week: ≤7, averaged over the month
- Alert review coverage: 100% of P0 and P1 alerts reviewed within the last 90 days
- Runbook link present: 100% of P0/P1 alerts include a direct runbook URL

## Director Framing: You Cannot Hire Your Way Out

If your engineers are receiving more than 10 pages per week, they are not on-call — they are drowning. The instinct is to add more engineers to distribute the load. This addresses the symptom, not the cause. Adding engineers to a noisy alert environment means more engineers are drowning, more slowly.

The only durable solution is alert quality work. The question a Director needs to answer: when did we last run an alert reduction campaign? If the answer is "never" or "I don't know," that is the work.

## Splunk-Specific Alert Tuning Patterns

When using Splunk SPL-based alerting for a 25M message/day notification platform, common quality improvements include:

- **Threshold normalization**: use `timechart` with `span=5m` to smooth burst traffic before alerting; avoid alerting on raw event counts without time-window smoothing
- **Baseline comparisons**: `| eval baseline=...` with a rolling 7-day average prevents alerts from firing on traffic patterns that are normal for the day-of-week
- **Deduplication at the source**: use `dedup` or `stats count by error_code` before alerting to prevent one error flood from generating multiple alerts
- **Alert suppression during known windows**: schedule Splunk searches to exclude maintenance windows by adding `earliest` and `latest` filters dynamically tied to a maintenance lookup table
