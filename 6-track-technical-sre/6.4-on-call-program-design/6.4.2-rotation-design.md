# Chapter 2 — Rotation Design: Building Schedules That Don't Break People

## The Primary/Secondary Model

The most durable on-call rotation structure is primary/secondary with a clear escalation contract between them.

**Primary on-call**: takes the first page for every alert. This is the engineer who is expected to be reachable, to acknowledge within 5 minutes, and to begin triage. The primary is fully engaged — not half-available.

**Secondary on-call**: the safety net. The secondary is never paged directly by the alerting system for normal incidents. The secondary exists for two scenarios: the primary does not acknowledge within the SLA (typically 10-15 minutes), or the primary determines they need backup (explicit escalation). If you configure the secondary to receive the same page simultaneously with the primary, you have not built a safety net — you have built a shared-responsibility trap where both engineers wait for the other to respond.

This model works because it preserves clear ownership. At any moment, there is exactly one engineer who is responsible. The secondary is the human override, not a co-owner.

## Follow-the-Sun vs Single-Timezone

**Follow-the-sun** rotations split on-call coverage by geography, with each region covering their business hours. The benefit is obvious: no engineer gets paged at 3am for their region if another region is awake and can handle it. The requirements are demanding: you need at least three regional teams (Americas, EMEA, APAC) with sufficient overlap to execute tight handoffs, engineers in each region with equivalent system knowledge, and a handoff discipline that reliably transfers context without dropping incident state.

For most mid-sized engineering organizations, follow-the-sun is aspirational. It requires organizational scale that takes years to build. The operational cost of poor handoffs — where the incoming region doesn't know what the outgoing region was monitoring — often exceeds the cost of a few after-hours pages.

**Single-timezone** rotations are simpler, cheaper to operate, and produce more consistent knowledge distribution. The trade-off is concentrated after-hours burden. This is manageable if the page rate is low (under 5 actionable pages/engineer/week) and the rotation is large enough. It becomes unacceptable when page rates are high and after-hours pages are frequent.

The Director's call: if your team is US-based with no immediate plans for international expansion, design a healthy single-timezone rotation first. Follow-the-sun is a hiring and organizational design problem, not a scheduling problem.

## Shadow Rotation for New Engineers

Before any engineer goes primary, they should spend 2-4 weeks in shadow rotation: receiving the same pages as the primary, attending the same triage sessions, and writing or updating runbooks based on what they observe — but not owning the response.

Shadow rotation serves three purposes. First, it transfers institutional knowledge in context — the engineer learns the system while seeing real incidents, not through documentation written in the abstract. Second, it validates runbook quality — if the shadow engineer cannot follow a runbook to a resolution, the runbook is not complete enough. Third, it establishes psychological safety — the engineer's first on-call shift is not their first encounter with the system.

Skipping shadow rotation to fill a rotation gap creates a false economy. You gain one more slot in the rotation and lose it again when that engineer has a traumatic first on-call experience and requests removal.

## Rotation Size and the Math

The relationship between rotation size and on-call burden is direct and unforgiving.

| Rotation Size | Weeks On-Call per Month | Weeks On-Call per Year |
|--------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| 8 engineers | 0.5 weeks | ~6.5 weeks |
| 6 engineers | 0.75 weeks | ~8.5 weeks |
| 4 engineers | 1 week | ~13 weeks |
| 3 engineers | 1.3 weeks | ~17 weeks |
| 2 engineers | 2 weeks | ~26 weeks |

Below 4 engineers, engineers are effectively living on-call. Vacation coverage, sick leave, or a single departure from the rotation leaves one person holding the pager for weeks at a time. This is not an edge case — it is the guaranteed outcome of a small rotation because life events are predictable at the aggregate level.

The minimum viable rotation is 4 engineers. The recommended floor is 6. Below 4, you do not have a rotation — you have an on-call burden distributed across too few people to absorb variance.

## Rotation Design for a 15-Person Team

With a 15-person SRE team, the instinct is to put everyone in one rotation. That is the wrong design. A 15-person rotation means each engineer is on-call twice a year. Infrequent on-call produces engineers who are unfamiliar with the system when their shift arrives — they have not been tracking incidents, have not updated runbooks, and approach on-call with anxiety rather than competence.

The better design: segment by domain.

**Platform SRE rotation (8 engineers)**: owns infrastructure-layer alerts — EKS cluster health, node pressure, control plane, network, storage. Rotation frequency: ~1 week/month. Engineers are engaged enough with platform operations to stay sharp.

**Application SRE rotation (7 engineers)**: owns service-layer alerts — notification pipeline, API latency, error rates, downstream dependencies. Rotation frequency: ~1 week/month.

Cross-training: rotate 1-2 engineers between domains per quarter to prevent siloed knowledge. Designate cross-domain escalation paths (application SRE escalates to platform SRE for infrastructure-root incidents, and vice versa).

The benefit of segmentation: each rotation stays small enough that engineers are engaged, and large enough that no single engineer carries disproportionate burden.
