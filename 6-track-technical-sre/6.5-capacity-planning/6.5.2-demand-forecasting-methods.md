# Chapter 2 — Demand Forecasting: Seeing Around the Corner

## Why Forecasting Is a Collaboration Problem, Not a Technical One

The most sophisticated forecasting model in the world is useless if it is fed bad business signal. This is the lesson most SRE teams learn the hard way. You build a beautiful trend extrapolation in Splunk or Grafana, it projects stable 8% month-over-month growth, and then marketing launches a campaign that triples traffic overnight — and your model predicted nothing.

Demand forecasting has two inputs: **historical data** (what your systems have done) and **business signal** (what your business intends to do). Technical teams are usually good at the first. They are usually bad at the second, because they do not own the relationships with the people who hold that signal.

A Director-level insight: your forecasting accuracy is directly proportional to how early you get into the room with product and marketing. If you find out about a major campaign the week it launches, your capacity model for that event is already wrong. If you find out eight weeks out, you have time to act.

## Historical Growth Modeling

Historical data gives you the baseline trend — what your system is doing when no one is deliberately intervening to change it.

### Linear Growth

Add a fixed amount per period. If your notification platform processed 18M msg/day six months ago and is at 25M msg/day now, that is approximately 1.2M messages/day added per month — a linear growth model.

Linear growth works well for **mature, stable services** where the growth driver is steady user adoption with no major acceleration. If your month-over-month additions are roughly consistent, linear extrapolation is appropriate and defensible.

Limitation: linear models underestimate growth that is accelerating. If the business is actively investing in growth, a linear model will systematically underforecast.

### Exponential Growth

Compound growth rate: a fixed percentage increase per period. A service growing at 12% per month doubles in approximately 6 months. Exponential models are appropriate for **early-stage services with network effects** — each new user brings more users, or each new feature drives disproportionate adoption.

The formula is: `Projected Value = Current × (1 + monthly_growth_rate)^months`

Warning: exponential models are dangerous for long-horizon forecasts. A 12% monthly growth rate that was accurate for 6 months rarely continues for 12 months. Validate against business targets — if the model projects 3× growth but the business plan only calls for 50% growth, something is wrong with either the model or the plan.

### Seasonal Modeling

Identify period-over-period patterns: day-of-week variation, time-of-day peaks, monthly cycles, annual events. For a notification platform, seasonality is critical:

- Morning news cycle: traffic peaks 7-9am as push notifications compete for attention
- Campaign send windows: marketing batch sends create sharp traffic spikes on send days
- Month-end: billing notifications, statement delivery
- Annual: holiday campaigns, open enrollment, Black Friday promotional pushes

Build a seasonal index: measure each hour-of-week and day-of-month as a multiplier against your baseline. A Monday 8am might be 1.8× your Tuesday 2pm baseline. Apply this multiplier to your growth trend when projecting peak capacity.

## Organic Growth vs Event-Driven Growth

This distinction is the most important conceptual divide in demand forecasting.

**Organic growth** is driven by user acquisition, feature adoption, and natural platform maturation. It follows trend lines. It is predictable with historical data. You can model it with linear or exponential extrapolation.

**Event-driven growth** is step-function. Marketing launches a campaign. A regulatory deadline forces user onboarding. The company announces a product. A news event creates a spike in engagement. None of these appear in your trend lines until after they happen — which is too late.

Event-driven growth requires a **capacity calendar** — a shared document, maintained jointly by SRE, product management, and marketing, that lists:

- Upcoming product launches with expected traffic multipliers
- Marketing campaign windows with expected send volumes
- Regulatory milestones with deadline-driven usage spikes
- External events that historically correlate with traffic (sports championships, election cycles, market volatility for financial notification services)

The capacity calendar is your early warning system for events that trend lines cannot predict. Maintaining it is not a technical activity — it requires you, as Director, to own the relationship with product and marketing leadership. If your SRE team is not in the room when campaign calendars are set, your capacity model will always be surprised.

## Bottom-Up vs Top-Down Forecasting

These are two different starting points for constructing a demand forecast. Both have their place.

**Bottom-up forecasting** starts from individual service metrics — requests per second, jobs per minute, messages per day — and aggregates upward to total resource requirements. You measure what workload your system actually receives, apply resource consumption ratios (CPU per request, memory per connection), and derive infrastructure needs.

Bottom-up is more accurate and more granular. It catches workloads that a top-down model would miss. It is also more labor-intensive — you need instrumented services with reliable metrics.

**Top-down forecasting** starts from business metrics — registered users, daily active users, transactions per day — and converts them to resource needs using empirically derived ratios. If you know that 1 million active users generates approximately 500K notifications per hour at peak, and you know that each 100K notifications requires 0.5 vCPU at peak, you can derive cluster CPU needs from user growth projections.

Top-down is faster and connects directly to business metrics that leadership understands. Its accuracy depends entirely on the validity of your conversion ratios. If user behavior shifts (users send more messages per session after a feature launch), top-down models go wrong silently.

**Director recommendation**: use top-down for quarterly forecasts and VP presentations (business metrics resonate with leadership), validate against bottom-up actuals monthly (operational accuracy check). When the two disagree, investigate the conversion ratio — something has changed in user behavior or system architecture.

## T-Mobile Notification Platform Example

Your 25M msg/day baseline gives you concrete numbers to model against.

**Baseline translation**:
- 25M msg/day ÷ 24 hours = ~1.04M msg/hour average
- Peak hour (assume 3× average for a campaign morning): ~3.1M msg/hour
- Peak minute: ~52K msg/minute
- Peak second: ~870 msg/sec

**Campaign event modeling**: a major marketing campaign that fires 5M messages in a 2-hour send window creates:
- 2.5M msg/hour during the send window — roughly 2.4× normal peak
- Your infrastructure needs to handle this without degradation

**Infrastructure translation**: if your current platform handles 1M msg/hour on X vCPUs at 65% utilization, then 3.1M msg/hour requires approximately 3.1× the CPU. Before a campaign that doubles your peak, you should have pre-provisioned 2× additional capacity.

This translation — from business metric (messages) to infrastructure metric (CPU) — is the core skill of demand forecasting. Building and validating these conversion ratios is a strategic SRE investment.

---

## Director Takeaway

Your forecast is a collaboration artifact, not a technical output. Its accuracy depends on the business relationships you maintain, the capacity calendar you keep current, and the conversion ratios you validate against actuals. Own the demand forecasting process — including the relationships with product and marketing — and you will stop being surprised by traffic events. Lose that ownership, and your capacity model will always be one campaign away from being wrong.
