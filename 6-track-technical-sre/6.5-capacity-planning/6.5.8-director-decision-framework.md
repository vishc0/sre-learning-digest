# Chapter 8 — Director Decision Framework: Presenting Capacity Investment to VP and CFO

## The Framing Shift That Gets Budgets Approved

Engineers present capacity requests in technical language: "We need to expand the Cassandra cluster by 4 nodes and add 3 EKS node pool instances." Finance hears: "We want to spend more money on servers."

Directors present capacity requests in financial language: "We are proposing an infrastructure investment with a defined cost, a defined risk reduction, and a quantifiable expected return based on incident cost avoidance." Finance hears: a business decision with numbers they can evaluate.

The shift from technical language to financial language is not spin. It is precision. The real question in every capacity investment decision is not "do we need more servers" — it is "what is the cost of having this capacity versus the expected cost of not having it?" When you frame the question that way, the answer often becomes obvious, and approval becomes a formality.

## The CFO-Level Expected Value Pitch

This is the core argument structure for any capacity investment proposal. Build it from the ground up for every major ask.

**Step 1 — Define the investment**:
- Monthly recurring cost: $X
- One-time provisioning cost: $Y
- 12-month total cost of investment: $(12X + Y)

**Step 2 — Define the risk being mitigated**:
- Probability of capacity-induced incident in next 12 months without this investment: P (expressed as a percentage)
- This probability is estimated from: current utilization trend, historical incident frequency for this utilization range, lead time to provision if reactive
- Be honest about uncertainty: "Based on current growth trajectory, we estimate 60-80% probability of crossing the 90% utilization threshold within 6 months without this investment."

**Step 3 — Define the P99 cost of the incident**:
- Incident duration: X hours (use historical averages from similar incidents)
- Engineering cost: Y engineers × Z hours × $150/hour loaded cost = $A
- SLA penalty: $B (from your carrier or enterprise agreements)
- Revenue at risk: $C (estimated revenue impact during outage window)
- Customer trust: hard to quantify — do not include in the base calculation, but acknowledge it exists
- Total incident cost: $A + $B + $C

**Step 4 — The expected value comparison**:

For 30% headroom on your DND platform (real numbers, Director-level calculation):

- Monthly headroom cost: approximately $2,200/month (4-5 extra EKS nodes + proportional database overhead)
- 12-month cost: $26,400
- Probability of capacity incident without headroom in 12 months: 70% (based on current growth trajectory)
- P99 cost of a notification platform capacity incident: 4 hours × 8 engineers × $150 + $25K SLA penalty + $50K revenue at risk = $79,800
- Expected cost of inaction: $79,800 × 0.70 = $55,860
- Expected cost of investment: $26,400 (certain)
- **Net benefit of investment: $55,860 - $26,400 = $29,460 in expected value**

"The cost of carrying 30% headroom is $26,400/year. The cost of a single capacity-induced outage is $79,800. We estimate 70% probability of that outage occurring without this investment. The expected value of the headroom investment is positive by approximately $29,000. We recommend approval."

That is a CFO-language argument. It works.

## On-Demand vs Reserved Capacity Economics

Every Director-level capacity decision has a pricing tier dimension that is separate from the sizing decision. Getting the pricing model wrong costs money on one of two sides.

**On-Demand pricing**: full list price, no commitment, maximum flexibility. The right choice for: unpredictable burst workloads, new services where demand is uncertain, instances that run less than 50% of the time.

**Reserved Instances (1-year or 3-year)**: 30-40% discount for 1-year commitment, 50-60% for 3-year. Requires commitment to a specific instance type in a specific region (Standard RIs) or flexibility within a family (Convertible RIs). The right choice for: stable baseline workloads that will run 24×7 for the foreseeable future.

**Savings Plans (AWS)**: commit to a dollar amount of compute spend per hour (Compute Savings Plans) or per EC2 family (EC2 Instance Savings Plans). More flexible than Standard RIs — your savings rate applies regardless of instance type, size, or region within the commitment. For Kubernetes workloads where you may resize node types over time, Savings Plans are often better than Standard RIs.

**Spot Instances**: 60-90% discount vs on-demand, with the caveat that AWS can reclaim Spot capacity with 2 minutes of warning. The right choice for: batch jobs, stateless processing, non-time-sensitive workloads that can be interrupted and retried. **Never use Spot for**: databases, stateful services, anything that cannot be interrupted without data loss or service degradation.

**Director decision framework — the baseline/burst split**:

| Workload Layer | Pricing Strategy | Rationale |
|---------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Steady-state baseline (P50 load) | Reserved or Savings Plans | Predictable, runs continuously, reserve discount justified |
| Normal variance (P50 → P90) | On-demand via autoscaling | Runs regularly but not continuously |
| Peak burst (P90 → P99) | On-demand or Spot (stateless only) | Infrequent, short-duration, not worth reserving |
| Batch, non-critical | Spot | Interruptible, significant savings |

A common mistake: reserving for peak capacity instead of baseline. If you buy reservations for 150 nodes because that is your Black Friday peak, you are paying reserved prices for 120 nodes that sit idle 350 days/year. Reserve for baseline, pay on-demand for variance and burst.

## Capacity Planning for New Products

New product capacity planning has a unique challenge: no historical data. You are forecasting demand for a workload that does not yet exist. Two methods address this.

**Analogy-based forecasting**: find the closest existing service in your portfolio, apply adjustment factors. "This new notification preference center will receive API calls proportional to user login activity. Our existing authentication service receives 2M API calls/day. We estimate the preference center will see 30% of that volume at launch. Baseline forecast: 600K calls/day, growing with user adoption."

The analogy gives you a starting point calibrated to your actual infrastructure. Adjust for known differences: the new service has more complex queries (+40% CPU per request), or simpler queries (-30%). Document your adjustment assumptions — they are what you revisit at the 30-day post-launch review.

**Load testing**: simulate target load before launch, measure actual resource consumption. No analogy is as accurate as measurement. Run load tests at 100% expected peak (baseline verification) and 200% expected peak (stress test, find the breaking point). Measure CPU, memory, disk I/O, and network at each load level. Use those measurements, not architecture estimates, as the basis for your pre-launch provisioning.

**Pre-launch capacity checklist** (minimum standards for any Tier 0 or Tier 1 service launch):
- Load test completed at 2× expected peak with resource measurements
- Autoscaling configured and tested (HPA triggered correctly at load test)
- Database connection limits verified at peak concurrent users
- CDN configured and cache warm (cold CDN = all traffic hits origin at launch)
- Carrier throughput agreements in place if notification delivery is involved
- On-call rotation briefed on new service behavior and runbooks available
- 30/60/90-day post-launch capacity reviews scheduled before launch date

**Post-launch reality check**: new product traffic forecasts are wrong. Almost always. The question is which direction and by how much. Pre-provision at 150% of expected peak, then adjust based on 30-day actuals. At 60 and 90 days, update the 12-month model with actual growth rate data.

## Interview Framing — Director-Level Questions on Capacity Planning

**"How do you balance cost efficiency with reliability margin?"**

"I classify services by customer and revenue impact, then apply tiered headroom standards — 40% headroom for Tier 0, 30% for Tier 1, 20% for Tier 2. This prevents both over-provisioning everything at Tier 0 standards (wasteful) and under-provisioning customer-facing services (risky). The tier classification is reviewed quarterly as the service portfolio evolves."

**"Tell me about a time you had to justify infrastructure investment to leadership."**

Use the expected value framework. "I framed the Cassandra ring expansion not as a capacity request but as a risk management investment. I calculated the probability of a capacity-induced incident given our growth trajectory, estimated the incident cost from our historical post-mortems, and showed that the expansion cost less than the expected incident cost over 12 months. The investment was approved in the quarterly review without escalation because the math was clear."

**"How would you build a capacity planning process from scratch?"**

"I would start with the three fundamentals: a capacity signals dashboard showing current headroom by resource, a demand forecast that combines trend data with business-sourced event calendar, and a monthly review cadence with defined escalation thresholds. In the first 90 days, I would focus on getting the dashboard built and the monthly cadence established. In the next 90 days, I would focus on building the relationships with product and marketing that supply the business signal for the capacity calendar. By six months, the process should be producing forward-looking action items instead of reactive incident responses."

---

## Director Takeaway

Every capacity decision you make as a Director is a financial decision with reliability consequences. The CFO-level framing — expected value of investment versus expected cost of inaction — is the language that gets budgets approved without firefighting. Reserve for baseline, pay on-demand for variance, never reserve for burst. Plan new product capacity with load test data rather than architecture estimates. And in interviews, demonstrate that you own the financial narrative of your infrastructure decisions, not just the technical execution. That is what distinguishes a Director-level candidate from a senior engineer who manages a team.
