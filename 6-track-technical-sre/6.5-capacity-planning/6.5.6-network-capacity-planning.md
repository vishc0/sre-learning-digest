# Chapter 6 — Network Capacity Planning: The Invisible Ceiling

## Why Network Is the Last Thing Planned and the First Thing to Surprise You

Network capacity has a unique failure signature: it often manifests as latency before it manifests as errors. When a server's CPU is exhausted, it starts dropping requests — errors appear in your dashboards. When a network interface is saturated, packets queue in buffers, then start dropping — but your application may interpret packet loss as retransmit-induced latency rather than errors. P99 latency climbs while error rates look clean. This makes network saturation hard to diagnose and easy to dismiss as "application slowness" or "database contention" when the real constraint is network bandwidth.

The second characteristic of network capacity: there is no gradual degradation curve to warn you. CPU throttling is proportional. Memory pressure increases GC frequency before causing OOMKills. Disk fills up incrementally. But network bandwidth saturation at the physical or carrier layer is a cliff edge — utilization at 95% looks almost identical to utilization at 75%, right up until the moment buffers fill and packets start dropping.

The third characteristic: network capacity is expensive and slow to increase. Cloud VPC bandwidth is tied to instance size (upgrading instance = downtime). Carrier agreements for SMS/notification delivery involve contracts and coordination windows. Physical network upgrades in on-premises environments require hardware procurement. You cannot emergency-provision network capacity the way you can spin up a new EC2 instance.

## Bandwidth Utilization Modeling

The foundation of network capacity planning is understanding your traffic profile in bytes per second, not just requests per second.

**Measurement methodology**:
1. Measure inbound and outbound bytes per second at each service's network interface, separated by traffic type (inter-service, external client, database)
2. Calculate P50 (steady state), P95 (high traffic), P99 (campaign peaks or traffic events)
3. For each interface, compute sustained utilization: `P95 bytes/sec / Interface capacity bytes/sec × 100`
4. Alert at 60% sustained P95 utilization on any interface; this gives you headroom for P99 spikes without entering the danger zone

**For your notification platform**: outbound traffic is the primary concern. Each notification message has a payload — push notification body (2-4KB typical), delivery metadata, headers. Model your outbound bandwidth:

- 25M msg/day ÷ 86,400 seconds = 289 msg/sec average
- Average message size including protocol overhead: 3KB
- Average outbound bandwidth: 289 × 3KB = ~867 KB/sec average (~7 Mbps)
- Peak at 3× average with campaign sends: ~21 Mbps
- Peak during campaign plus news event concurrence (stress scenario): ~50 Mbps

Most modern EKS node types have 10+ Gbps network interfaces — this looks trivially small. The constraint is not the node interface; it is the egress gateway, the NAT gateway, and the carrier-side throughput limits on your SMPP/HTTP API connections to SMS carriers.

## CDN Capacity Planning

For content-heavy services — anything delivering web assets, images, API responses that can be cached — CDN capacity planning is critical because the CDN changes your origin capacity requirements nonlinearly.

The key metric is **cache hit rate**: what percentage of requests are served from CDN cache vs. reaching your origin servers.

- At 90% hit rate: 10% of traffic hits origin. If total traffic is 100K req/sec, origin handles 10K req/sec.
- At 70% hit rate: 30% of traffic hits origin. Same 100K req/sec total, but origin handles 30K req/sec — 3× the load.

A drop in CDN hit rate does not require any increase in external traffic to triple your origin load. This is why CDN cache hit rate is a capacity signal, not just a performance metric.

**Events that spike origin traffic through CDN**:
- **Cache invalidation during deploy**: a deployment that invalidates CDN cache for your API response objects will temporarily send 100% of traffic to origin. For a large platform, this can be a 10× spike on origin during the invalidation window.
- **New content formats**: a new notification template or asset format that CDN has not seen yet will cold-start all at origin.
- **TTL expiry during high traffic events**: if your CDN objects expire during a campaign send window, the surge of re-validation requests hits origin at exactly the worst time.

**Director action**: ensure CDN cache hit rate is part of your capacity monitoring dashboard, not just your performance dashboard. Set an alert if cache hit rate drops below your P30 historical baseline — it means more traffic than expected is hitting origin.

## Egress Cost Forecasting

AWS egress pricing: $0.09 per GB to the internet (standard, varies by region and destination). For a notification platform that is primarily outbound, egress cost can be significant.

**Current state calculation for DND platform**:
- 25M msg/day × 3KB average = 75GB/day of outbound data
- 75GB/day × 30 days = 2,250 GB/month
- 2,250 GB × $0.09 = $202.50/month in egress costs (current baseline)

This seems modest. But model growth: at 15% monthly growth in message volume, you are at 50M msg/day in 5 months. That doubles your egress cost to $405/month. At 100M msg/day in 18 months, egress cost reaches $810/month. By itself still manageable. But for a richer notification format (10KB average), all numbers multiply by 3.3×.

**Egress cost optimization levers**:

| Lever | How It Works | Savings Potential |
|-------|-------------|------------------|
| CDN offload | Serve cacheable content from CDN, not origin | 60-90% of static content egress |
| PrivateLink / VPC peering | Inter-service traffic stays within VPC, no NAT gateway egress | Eliminates NAT gateway egress costs for service-to-service traffic |
| Data locality | Store data in the region where it is accessed | Eliminates cross-region egress ($0.02-0.09/GB depending on path) |
| S3 Transfer Acceleration | Not for cost savings — this increases cost. Avoid unless latency is critical. | — |
| Compressed payloads | Smaller messages = less egress | 30-70% depending on payload type |

Present egress cost as a line item in your quarterly capacity review. It is infrastructure cost that grows with traffic — finance leadership will ask about it when it becomes visible on the cloud bill.

## Carrier Capacity Agreements — T-Mobile Notification Platform Context

Your notification platform delivers through carrier gateways — SMPP sessions to SMS carriers or HTTP APIs to push notification providers (FCM, APNs). These delivery paths have throughput limits that are contractual, not merely technical.

**SMPP throughput limits**: SMPP sessions have a configured message-per-second rate negotiated with the carrier. A session configured for 100 TPS delivers 100 SMS/sec. Exceeding this rate causes message queuing at your SMPP client, which grows without bound during high-volume sends, degrading delivery latency and eventually causing delivery failures or SMPP session resets.

**Pre-coordinating campaign traffic**: for a major marketing campaign that will send 5M messages in 2 hours, you must pre-coordinate throughput with your carrier partners. The process:

1. Notify carrier capacity planning contacts 5-10 business days before major campaigns
2. Request a temporary throughput increase for the campaign window
3. Confirm the new TPS limit in writing and reflect it in your send throttle configuration
4. Monitor delivery latency during the campaign window — carrier-side queuing shows up as increased latency before it shows up as errors

If you skip carrier pre-coordination, your SMPP sessions will throttle at the negotiated limit, messages will queue, and your 2-hour campaign window becomes a 4-hour window — with late deliveries landing after the marketing moment has passed.

This is a capacity planning responsibility that lives at the SRE-to-product-to-carrier interface. It requires you as Director to ensure your SRE team has the carrier contacts and the process to engage them proactively before every major campaign event on the capacity calendar.

## Director Takeaway

Network capacity is the capacity dimension most likely to surprise you with a cliff-edge failure after appearing healthy. Build egress cost modeling into your quarterly capacity review — finance will start asking questions when it appears on the cloud bill and you want to have the answers ready. For your notification platform, carrier throughput agreements are a soft capacity ceiling that requires proactive management through the capacity calendar. CDN cache hit rate is a capacity signal: a drop in cache hit rate is functionally equivalent to a traffic surge on your origin. Own these dimensions explicitly in your capacity practice — they are not covered by compute headroom modeling.
