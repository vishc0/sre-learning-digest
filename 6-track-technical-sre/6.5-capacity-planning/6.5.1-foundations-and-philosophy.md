# Chapter 1 — Foundations: Capacity Planning as Reliability Finance

## The Framing That Changes Everything

Most engineers think of capacity planning as a technical activity: measure utilization, add servers when it gets high. That framing is wrong, and it explains why so many teams end up doing capacity reaction instead of capacity planning.

The correct framing is this: **capacity planning is a financial decision with reliability consequences**. You are deciding how much reserved insurance to carry against the risk of a capacity-induced failure. The cost of the insurance is infrastructure spend. The cost of not having insurance is an incident. A Director's job is to make that trade-off explicit, quantify both sides, and present it to leadership in a language they understand.

If you cannot attach a dollar figure to a capacity investment proposal, you are not doing capacity planning. You are doing capacity speculation.

---

## Autoscaling and Capacity Planning Are Not the Same Thing

One of the most common mistakes in organizations that have adopted Kubernetes is the belief that autoscaling eliminates the need for capacity planning. It does not. They solve fundamentally different problems.

Think of it like a warehouse. Autoscaling is the logistics system inside the warehouse — it moves boxes, optimizes shelf usage, reroutes forklifts when a row is congested. It operates efficiently within the space available to it. Capacity planning is the decision about how large to build the warehouse in the first place. A perfectly efficient logistics system in an undersized warehouse still runs out of space.

Autoscaling handles variance within a provisioned envelope. Capacity planning defines the envelope.

Cluster Autoscaler and Karpenter will add nodes when pods are pending — but they add nodes from a pool that has a configured maximum. If your max node count is too low, autoscaling cannot save you. If your node pool budget is correct but your pod resource requests are wrong, autoscaling triggers at the wrong thresholds. Autoscaling works only when the capacity model underneath it is right.

## The Three Questions Every Director Must Answer

Before any capacity review, before any VP presentation, before signing off on any infrastructure spend — you need to have confident answers to three questions.

**Question 1: What do I have?**

Current provisioned capacity by resource type. Not just total cluster nodes — CPU cores, allocatable memory, disk per node pool, network bandwidth per interface, database connections, Cassandra nodes × disk per node. You should be able to answer this from a dashboard in under two minutes. If you cannot, that is itself a capacity planning gap.

**Question 2: What will I need?**

Demand forecast at three horizons: 3-month (high confidence, operational planning), 6-month (medium confidence, budget planning), 12-month (directional, investment decisions). Each horizon requires a different method. Three-month forecasts use trend extrapolation. Twelve-month forecasts require business signal — growth targets, product roadmap, campaign calendars. Chapter 2 covers the methods in detail.

**Question 3: How much lead time do I need to add it?**

This is the question most teams skip, and it causes incidents. Adding capacity is not instantaneous:

- **Cloud compute (EKS node pools)**: 5-15 minutes for autoscaling, 1-2 days for manual capacity increase requests through procurement workflows
- **Managed databases (RDS scale-up)**: 15-30 minutes with a maintenance window, or hours if it requires a read replica promotion
- **Cassandra node expansion**: 2-8 hours per node for data streaming, plus validation — a full ring expansion can take days
- **On-premises hardware**: weeks to months (procurement, rack, cable, provision, validate)
- **Headcount** (SREs to manage the expanded infrastructure): 6-12 weeks minimum

Your action threshold must be set far enough in advance to accommodate the longest lead time in your critical path.

## Why Reactive Capacity Planning Creates Incidents

By the time a metric hits 90% utilization, you may already be inside an incident. Here is why.

At 70% utilization, a traffic spike of 50% over baseline will push you to 105% — over capacity. Traffic spikes of 50% are normal for a notification platform on a breaking news morning or a major campaign send. At 70% headroom, a single predictable event causes an outage.

At 80% utilization, a 25% spike — well within normal variance for most services — pushes you over the line. An on-call alert at 85% utilization is not a warning — it is an emergency with very little response window.

The reason teams end up reactive is that utilization growth is non-linear in its risk profile. Going from 40% to 60% feels fine. Going from 60% to 70% starts to feel concerning. Going from 70% to 80% is when the incidents start. But the time to act is when you cross 60%, not when you reach 80%.

The rule of thumb: **act at 70%, alert at 80%, incident at 90%**. Your provisioning decision must be made while you are still at 70%.

## The Buffer as Insurance — a Financial Mental Model

Carrying 30% headroom costs money. For a 15-node EKS cluster with m5.2xlarge instances at $0.384/hour each, 30% headroom means approximately 4-5 nodes of unused capacity — roughly $1,200-1,500/month in reserved but unused compute.

That is your insurance premium.

A capacity-induced outage on your 25M msg/day notification platform has a cost model:
- Incident duration: 2-4 hours (realistic for a complex platform)
- Engineers engaged: 6-8 (SREs, DBAs, networking, leadership)
- Cost of engineer time: 8 engineers × 3 hours × $150/hour loaded = $3,600
- SLA penalties: depends on contract, but often $10K-50K per breach
- Customer trust erosion: immeasurable in the short term, real in the medium term (churn, NPS impact)
- Revenue impact: for a notification platform, downstream revenue risk from missed delivery windows

A single serious outage costs more than 12 months of headroom insurance premiums.

When you present a capacity investment to a VP or CFO, this is the argument. Not "we need more servers." The argument is: "The headroom costs $X/month. The expected cost of not having it — probability of incident times cost of that incident — is $Y. The investment is justified."

That framing works at the VP level. It works at the CFO level. It is the difference between a Director who gets capacity budgets approved and one who is always fighting for resources after an incident.

---

## Director Takeaway

Capacity planning is not a technical afterthought. It is a standing financial commitment, reviewed quarterly, owned at the Director level, and presented to VPs with a cost model attached. The three-question framework (what do I have, what will I need, how long does it take to add) is the structure of every capacity conversation you will ever have in a leadership role.

Build the habit of carrying the insurance before you need it. The alternative — being the Director who explains a capacity-induced outage that was preventable with $1,500/month of headroom — is a far worse conversation.
