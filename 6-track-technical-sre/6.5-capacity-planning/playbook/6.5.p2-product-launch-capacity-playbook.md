# Playbook: Product Launch Capacity Planning

**Version**: 1.0  
**Purpose**: Ensure adequate capacity is in place before launch, preventing capacity-induced launch failures  
**Minimum lead time**: Start this playbook 8 weeks before launch date. For major launches (>2× current platform peak), start 12 weeks out.  
**Owner**: Capacity Lead (Platform SRE assigned to the launch)

---

## Roles

| Role | Who | Responsibilities |
|------|-----|-----------------|
| **Capacity Lead** | Platform SRE | Owns the capacity model, runs load tests, tracks all action items |
| **Product Owner** | Product Manager | Provides traffic estimates, owns the business traffic model |
| **Load Test Lead** | SRE with load testing expertise | Designs and executes load tests; Capacity Lead may also hold this role on small teams |
| **Director** | You | Approves pre-provisioning spend; makes pre-launch go/no-go capacity decision |
| **Database Lead** | DBA or Cassandra-expert SRE | Engaged for database sizing and connection limit validation |

## T-Minus 8 Weeks: Traffic Model and Initial Sizing

**Step 1 — Collect traffic model from Product Owner** *(Capacity Lead + Product Owner, Day 1)*

Product Owner provides (in non-technical terms, then Capacity Lead translates):
- Expected DAU (daily active users) at launch
- Expected DAU at 30 days, 90 days, 1 year
- Peak concurrent users estimate (what % of DAU is concurrent at peak hour?)
- Transactions per user per day (how many API calls, notifications, or events does a typical user generate?)
- For notification platform: expected notification volume per user per day

Example (fill in with actuals):
```
Launch DAU estimate: 500,000
30-day DAU estimate: 750,000
Peak concurrent: 8% of DAU = 40,000 concurrent users
Transactions per user: 5 API calls/session, 3 sessions/day = 15 API calls/user/day
Notification volume: 2 notifications/user/day = 1M notifications/day at launch
```

> If Product Owner cannot provide these estimates, they are not ready for a capacity-gated launch. Escalate to Director.

**Step 2 — Translate to infrastructure metrics** *(Capacity Lead)*

Convert business metrics to infrastructure metrics. Document the conversion ratios — they will be updated with load test actuals.

```
API requests/day = DAU × transactions/user = 500K × 15 = 7.5M req/day
API requests/sec at peak (assume 8hr peak window, 3× average during peak):
  Average: 7.5M / 86,400 = ~87 req/sec
  Peak: 87 × 3 = ~260 req/sec

Notifications/day = 1M notifications
Notification peak (assume 2hr send window, 4× average):
  Average: 11.6 msg/sec
  Peak: 46 msg/sec

Database reads: estimate based on analogous service or application team input
Database writes: estimate based on analogous service or application team input
Expected data volume at 30/90/365 days
```

**Step 3 — Identify analogous service for capacity benchmarking** *(Capacity Lead)*

Find the closest existing service in your portfolio with measured resource consumption. Document:
- Analogous service name
- Its traffic volume at comparison point
- Its resource consumption at that traffic volume (CPU, memory, database connections per N req/sec)
- Adjustment factors for the new service (more complex queries: +30%, lighter reads: -20%)

This gives you a starting capacity estimate calibrated to real system behavior before load testing.

**Step 4 — Draft initial capacity model** *(Capacity Lead)*

Complete `templates/capacity-model-template.md` with:
- Current inventory section: what resources already exist (shared platform) vs. what must be provisioned new
- Demand forecast: launch, 30-day, 90-day, 12-month projections
- Initial resource requirements derived from Steps 2 and 3
- Known gaps: what must be provisioned before launch?

File this as version 1.0 — it will be updated with load test data at T-minus 4 weeks.

**Step 5 — Identify required infrastructure changes** *(Capacity Lead + Director)*

Based on the initial capacity model:
- New Kubernetes namespace and node pool requirements
- New database instances or Cassandra keyspace capacity
- CDN configuration requirements
- New carrier throughput agreements (if notification volume requires additional SMPP throughput)
- Network egress estimate (is this within current egress capacity or does it require increases?)

Estimate total additional monthly cost. If above Director approval threshold, escalate to VP now — you need 4-6 weeks for budget approval and provisioning. Do not wait until T-minus 2 weeks to discover a spend approval problem.

> Decision gate: Director reviews initial capacity model and infrastructure change list. Approves or modifies scope. If budget approval is required from VP, initiate that process immediately.

## T-Minus 4 Weeks: Pre-Provisioning and Load Testing

**Step 6 — Pre-provision compute** *(Capacity Lead)*

Provision at 150% of expected peak capacity (not average — peak). The 50% buffer accounts for:
- Forecast error (new product traffic estimates are often wrong by ±30-50%)
- Launch traffic spike (launches often exceed forecasts in the first 48-72 hours)
- Autoscaling initialization delay (if autoscaling triggers, it takes 2-5 minutes — front-load capacity so you do not rely on reactive scaling at launch)

For the notification platform example:
- Peak capacity requirement: 46 msg/sec
- Pre-provision at 150%: provision for 69 msg/sec capacity
- Translate to pods and nodes based on load test data from Step 8 (or analogy from Step 3 if load test not yet run)

**Step 7 — Configure autoscaling for launch traffic patterns** *(Capacity Lead)*

Set HPA and Karpenter/Cluster Autoscaler for launch:
- minReplicas: at least 150% of expected average load (do not start at minimum and scale up)
- maxReplicas: at least 300% of expected average load (give autoscaling room to absorb a traffic surge)
- Scale-up threshold: CPU at 60% (lower than the standard 70% — you want autoscaling to be aggressive at launch when traffic patterns are uncertain)
- Scale-up cooldown: reduce to 60 seconds from default 5 minutes during launch window

**Step 8 — Configure monitoring and alerting** *(Capacity Lead)*

New services need monitoring configured before they receive production traffic:
- Create Splunk/Datadog dashboards for the new service
- Configure alerts at standard thresholds (CPU >80%, memory >85%, disk >80%)
- Set lower thresholds for launch period (CPU >70%) — you want early warning before the standard alert
- Confirm on-call routing: when this service alerts, who gets paged?

**Step 9 — Run synthetic load test at 100% expected peak** *(Load Test Lead)*

Design a load test that simulates:
- The expected peak traffic rate (260 API req/sec, 46 msg/sec notifications in the example)
- A realistic traffic mix (not just one API endpoint — the full request distribution)
- Sustained load (not a spike — run at peak for at least 30 minutes to let the system reach steady state)

Measure during the test:
- CPU utilization per service at peak
- Memory utilization per service at peak
- Database connection count at peak
- Database CPU at peak
- Response latency P50/P95/P99
- Error rate

Update the capacity model with these measured actuals. Adjust resource requests/limits based on measured usage (not the estimates from Step 2-3).

> Decision gate: if load test at 100% peak reveals any latency SLO violation, OOMKills, connection pool saturation, or error rate above 0.1% — the launch is NOT capacity-ready. Fix the identified issues before proceeding.

**Step 10 — Update capacity model with load test actuals** *(Capacity Lead)*

Replace analogy-based estimates with measured values from Step 9:
- Update CPU request/limit recommendations
- Update memory request/limit recommendations
- Update database connection pool sizing
- Recalculate 12-month projections using the measured resource consumption per unit of traffic

## T-Minus 1 Week: Stress Testing and Final Validation

**Step 11 — Run load test at 200% expected peak** *(Load Test Lead)*

This is a stress test — its purpose is to find the breaking point, not to simulate normal operations. Run at 200% of expected peak for 15-30 minutes.

Goal: understand what happens when traffic exceeds expectations. Specifically:
- Does autoscaling activate correctly and successfully add capacity within the acceptable time window?
- What is the first resource to saturate? (That is your leading indicator for launch monitoring)
- At what traffic level do latency SLOs break? (That is your launch abort threshold)
- Does the system recover gracefully when load is reduced after the stress test? (Recovery behavior is a reliability signal)

Document the breaking point traffic level. This defines your emergency capacity budget: if launch traffic approaches this level, you have a defined threshold at which to implement demand shedding.

**Step 12 — Verify autoscaling triggers and scale correctly** *(Capacity Lead)*

During the 200% stress test, verify:
- HPA triggered within expected threshold (CPU >60% for launch configuration)
- New pods scheduled successfully (no Pending pods — confirms cluster scheduling headroom is adequate)
- Pod readiness: new pods became ready within the expected startup time for this service
- Load balancer distributed traffic to new pods correctly
- Scale-in occurs correctly after load subsides (autoscaling is symmetric — bad scale-in can leave you over-provisioned long-term)

**Step 13 — Confirm database connection limits for peak concurrent users** *(Database Lead)*

At the load test peak (Step 11), verify:
- Connection pool active count at peak
- Connection pool waiting count at peak (ideally: zero waiting; any waiting indicates pool is undersized)
- Database CPU at peak
- Database query P99 latency at peak

If connection pool saturation was observed: increase pool size and rerun a targeted load test.

**Step 14 — Brief on-call rotation on new service** *(Capacity Lead + Director)*

Every on-call engineer must know before launch:
- What does the service do? (one sentence, in plain language)
- What are the top 3 failure modes? (from load testing: OOMKill, connection pool exhaustion, CPU saturation)
- What is the runbook for each failure mode? (link to the runbook, which must be written before this briefing)
- What is the escalation path for issues with this service? (who is the Service Lead on-call?)
- What are the expected traffic patterns? (so they can recognize "launch traffic spike" vs. "actual incident")

## Launch Day Operations

**Step 15 — Activate launch war room** *(Director)*

For any major launch, open a dedicated incident channel 2 hours before launch. Assign:
- Technical monitor: Capacity Lead watching resource utilization dashboards
- Product monitor: Product Owner watching business metrics (conversions, signups, user actions)
- Communications: who notifies stakeholders if an issue arises
- Decision maker: Director (you) with authority to call demand shedding or delay decisions

**Step 16 — Monitor traffic vs forecast in real time** *(Capacity Lead)*

First 4 hours post-launch: monitor every 15 minutes:
- Actual traffic (req/sec, msg/sec) vs forecast
- Resource utilization (CPU, memory, connections) vs pre-provisioned capacity
- Autoscaling events (are new pods/nodes being provisioned?)
- Error rate and latency P99

If actuals exceed forecast by >50%: activate pre-approved scaling actions (Step 17). If actuals are below forecast by >50%: do not scale down immediately — give it 24 hours. Under-forecast on day 1 is common (users take time to discover and adopt).

**Step 17 — Execute pre-approved scaling actions** *(Capacity Lead, Director authorization)*

Pre-approve before launch: if CPU exceeds 70% sustained for 10 minutes, Capacity Lead may:
- Increase node pool size by up to X nodes (Director pre-approves the specific action and spend level)
- Increase minReplicas to Y

Document these pre-approvals before launch day. The goal is to eliminate the "go find the Director to approve a scaling action at 2am during a launch" scenario.

**Step 18 — Escalation path if capacity is insufficient** *(Director)*

If traffic spike exceeds even pre-approved scaling actions:
- IC declared: Director becomes Incident Commander
- Activate capacity crisis response playbook (Phase 3 — Emergency Procurement)
- Consider demand shedding (rate limiting by region or user tier) as last resort
- If a delay was possible but capacity was not pre-validated — this is a post-launch learning, not a launch failure. Document it.

## Post-Launch Capacity Reviews

**30-Day Review** *(Capacity Lead + Director)*  
- Compare actual traffic to launch forecast: what was the error?
- Compare actual resource consumption to load test predictions: what was different?
- Update the capacity model with 30-day actuals
- Recalculate 12-month projections using actual growth rate from launch to 30 days
- Identify any capacity actions needed in the next 60 days

**60-Day Review** *(Capacity Lead)*  
- Is the growth rate following the expected trajectory?
- Any surprise workload characteristics not seen in load testing?
- Are resource requests/limits right-sized based on 60 days of actual data?
- Update capacity model

**90-Day Review** *(Capacity Lead + Director)*  
- 90-day growth rate is the most reliable early indicator of the 12-month trajectory
- Update the annual demand forecast
- Identify if the service requires its tier classification to be reassessed (did a Tier 1 launch become Tier 0 based on adoption?)
- Present updated capacity model at next monthly capacity review
