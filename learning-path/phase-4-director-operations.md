# Phase 4: Director of Operations

**Duration**: 16+ weeks structured, then ongoing  
**Entry point**: Principal SRE or Senior Manager with team leadership experience  
**Exit**: Can build and run an operations org, manage vendor and partner relationships, report to executives, build budgets, delegate domain authority, and develop people

---

## What Phase 4 Builds

Phase 4 is not a reading program — it is a management operating system. By this stage you have the technical depth of a Principal SRE. What Phase 4 adds is the organizational capability to multiply that depth across a team, a department, and a business.

The Director of Operations role is fundamentally different from every technical role that precedes it. The shift is this: in every previous phase, your leverage was personal — your skill, your judgment, your execution. In the Director role, your leverage is organizational — your team's skill, your team's judgment, your team's execution. The measure of a Director is not what they produce directly but what their organization produces consistently.

This phase teaches you to build and run that organization.

---

## Prerequisites

Before starting Phase 4, you should have:

- Phase 3 competency gates fully met, OR equivalent experience that covers all Phase 3 competencies
- Has managed a team of 5 or more engineers, directly or through senior leads
- Has prepared executive-level reports — operational scorecards, incident summaries, or equivalent
- Has managed at least one vendor or partner relationship, even informally
- Is actively in a Senior Manager, Staff Engineer, or Principal Engineer role — or is actively preparing for a Director interview within 6 months

If you are not yet in a people management role, you can read Phase 4 to build the mental model, but you cannot demonstrate the competency gates without management experience. Reading without doing is a foundation without a building.

---

## How Phase 4 Works Differently

Phase 4 content does not follow the "chapters then playbooks" structure of Phase 2–3 frameworks. It is organized as a Director's operating rhythm:

1. **Weeks 1–4**: Build your operating system — the weekly, monthly, and quarterly rhythms of Director work
2. **Weeks 5–8**: Build your people systems — hiring, development, performance, culture
3. **Weeks 9–12**: Build your external systems — vendors, partners, governance, stakeholders
4. **Weeks 13–16**: Integrate everything and apply it in a full operating cycle

Run the interview preparation track (`job-search/`) in parallel with this entire phase. The Director interview is a test of your operating model — you need to be able to describe, defend, and demonstrate it in conversation.

## Week-by-Week Curriculum

### Weeks 1–4: The Director's Operating System

The first month is about establishing the operating rhythm that makes a Director effective. Without a structured operating system, Directors either micromanage (doing their senior managers' jobs) or disappear (losing touch with what their org is actually doing). This block builds the structures that prevent both failure modes.

**Reading sequence**:

`4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/4.1.1-directors-role.md`  
Start here. This module defines the job: what a Director is responsible for, what they are not responsible for, and where the boundaries of the role sit. If you do not know the boundaries, you will either overstep into your reports' authority or under-deliver on your own accountability.

`4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/4.1.2-executive-communication.md`  
The Director communicates up (to VP and above), across (to peer Directors), and down (to their organization). Each direction requires a different approach. This module covers how to communicate risk, status, and asks to executives who have 15 minutes and dozens of competing priorities.

`4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/4.1.3-budgeting-and-financial-management.md`  
Budget ownership is one of the clearest markers of Director-level accountability. This module covers: how to read a cost center report, how to build an annual budget, how to write a headcount business case, and how to manage a team that is over or under plan.

`4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/4.1.4-managing-senior-managers.md`  
Managing managers is fundamentally different from managing individual contributors. The relationship is more peer-like, the feedback loops are longer, and the risks of under-managing are higher. This module covers how to set expectations, delegate authority, and hold senior managers accountable without micromanaging them.

`4-phase-director/4.2-director-operations-manual/4.2.1-the-directors-job.md`  
`4-phase-director/4.2-director-operations-manual/4.2.2-the-operating-rhythm.md`

The operations manual chapters give you the tactical, week-by-week operating cadence: what meetings to run, what reviews to own, what reporting to produce, and what decisions to hold versus delegate.

### Weeks 5–8: People and Organization

A Director's most important output is the quality and effectiveness of the people in their organization. Hiring, development, and performance management are the mechanisms by which that output is produced. This block builds the people systems.

**Reading sequence**:

`4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/4.1.5-org-design-and-people-strategy.md`  
Org design is not just drawing boxes on a chart. It is deciding where authority sits, how communication flows, where coordination costs are paid, and what organizational shape best serves the operational demands. This module covers team topologies, span of control, and how to design an org that can actually do its job.

`4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/4.1.9-people-leadership-and-culture.md`  
Culture is the collection of behaviors that happen when the Director is not in the room. This module covers how to build and maintain a culture of accountability, psychological safety, and operational excellence — and what to do when the culture drifts.

`4-phase-director/4.3-operations-team-roles/4.3.1-team-structure-and-org-design.md`  
`4-phase-director/4.3-operations-team-roles/4.3.2-role-definitions.md`  
`4-phase-director/4.3-operations-team-roles/4.3.4-hiring-framework.md`

These three chapters from the operations-team-roles framework give you the concrete operational layer: how to structure SRE and operations teams, what each role actually does, and how to hire for the roles you need. The hiring framework is particularly important — it includes interview design, evaluation rubrics, and the decision process.

These are not exercises — they are artifacts you will use in your current or next Director role.

### Weeks 9–12: Partners, Vendors, and Governance

Directors manage relationships outside their organization — vendors who provide tooling or services, partners who co-deliver programs, and governance bodies (risk, compliance, audit, legal) that have oversight over operational practices. This block builds those external relationship skills.

`4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/4.1.7-partnership-management.md`

Operations organizations are subject to audit, compliance requirements, and risk management frameworks. ## Director-Specific Mindset Shifts

These are the shifts that separate Directors who succeed from Directors who stall. You will not find them explicitly taught in any single module — they are the pattern that emerges from doing all of Phase 4.

**From problem-solver to problem-framer**: Directors do not solve problems — they ensure their organization has the capability and clarity to solve problems. When you get the urge to fix something yourself, pause and ask: "Is this my problem to solve, or is this a signal that someone on my team needs development?"

**From technical expert to organizational translator**: Technical credibility matters, but the Director's primary audience is not technical. You translate between the operational reality of your team and the business priorities of your stakeholders. That translation is your most important work.

**From reactive to anticipatory**: Senior SREs are great at responding. Directors must anticipate — workforce gaps, budget shortfalls, partner risks, architectural debt — 6–12 months before they become operational problems. Build the habit of looking that far forward.

**From individual accountability to distributed accountability**: You are accountable for everything your organization does. You are personally responsible for almost nothing your organization does. That distinction — being accountable without being personally responsible — is the Director's central operating challenge.

## Phase 4 Competency Gates

These are ongoing competencies, not one-time demonstrations. You are functioning as a Director when you can do all of the following consistently over a 90-day period.

| Gate | What "done" looks like |
|------|----------------------|
| Weekly Ops Review | Can run a weekly operations review that produces decisions and owners — not just status updates. Attendees leave knowing what changed, what is at risk, and who is accountable for what |
| Headcount Business Case | Can write a headcount business case with ROI framing that would survive CFO-level scrutiny — not just "we need more people" but "here is the cost, here is the value, here is the risk of not hiring" |
| Partner QBR | Can run a quarterly business review with a vendor or partner organization, extract commitments, document them, and track against them the following quarter |
| Annual Budget | Can build an annual operating budget — headcount, tools, vendor contracts, and contingency — with a fully-worked cut scenario ready if asked to reduce by 15% |
| Executive Scorecard | Can present an operational scorecard to a VP in 15 minutes: what is working, what is at risk, what you need, and what you are going to do about the risks |
| Authority Delegation | Can delegate domain authority explicitly to Senior Managers with clear escalation thresholds — they know what they own, they know what to escalate, and they know you will hold them accountable |
| Developmental 1:1 | Can conduct a 1:1 that moves a Senior Manager's professional development forward — not a status report, not a check-in, but a conversation that changes how they think about their role or their growth |
| Performance Action | Can identify a Senior Manager performance issue within 30 days of the first signal, have the direct conversation, set specific and measurable expectations, and follow through on consequences if expectations are not met |

## What Comes After Phase 4

Phase 4 does not end. The Director operating model is not mastered in 16 weeks — it is refined over the course of a career. What changes after the first 16 weeks is your feedback loop:

- You begin to see patterns in your org's behavior that indicate systemic issues
- You develop the peer network of other Directors and VPs that gives you external calibration
- You build the track record of decisions that gives your executive team confidence in your judgment
- You identify the next level of growth: VP of Operations, SVP, or equivalent

The frameworks in this path remain reference material for the rest of your career in operations leadership. The playbooks and templates in `4-phase-director/4.1-director-management-course/` are designed to be used, not just read.

Keep them close.
