# Director / VP Interview Question Bank

**Audience**: Director of SRE, VP of Platform Engineering, VP of DevSecOps — roles above IC/Staff, accountable for org outcomes, budgets, and strategy.

**How to use**: For each question, read the "What Interviewer Is Testing" column first — that reframes your answer instinct. Then use the Model Answer Framework as a skeleton. Always close with the T-Mobile Anchor to make it concrete and credible.

---

## Domain 1: Org Design & Team Building

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | "How do you structure an SRE org inside a large engineering organization — embedded vs. centralized vs. platform model?" | Whether you understand the tradeoffs, not just have an opinion. Can you map org model to company maturity? | • Describe the spectrum: centralized (economies of scale, slower feedback), embedded (fast feedback, tribal knowledge risk), platform (golden path + self-serve) • Name the forcing functions: team size, product velocity, incident volume, budget ownership • State when you'd change the model (post-merger, rapid headcount growth, microservices explosion) • Anchor to a principle: "Org structure is a lagging indicator of your incident model" | T-Mobile: Led 15-person SRE team on a centralized model serving notification platform. Identified embedded-SRE pressure as product teams scaled. Proposed hybrid: centralized on-call + embedded reliability consultants per squad. |
| 1.2 | "You inherited a team where 3 of 10 engineers are performing below bar. What do you do?" | People management maturity. Do you default to PIP theater or do you actually diagnose root cause? | • First: separate performance problem from system problem (unclear expectations, tooling gaps, poor onboarding) • Create individual development contracts with 30/60/90 checkpoints — not surprise PIPs • Two parallel tracks: raise the floor for recoverable gaps, manage out quickly for misaligned motivation • Protect the team from carrying the weight — visible fairness matters to the 7 performers | T-Mobile: Inherited a 3-year-old team with undocumented runbooks and no on-call rotation hygiene. "Below bar" was actually "below bar for undocumented expectations." Fixed 2 of 3 cases by creating explicit SLO ownership accountability. |
| 1.3 | "How do you hire for SRE roles at Staff and Principal level when you can't out-bid FAANG on comp?" | Whether you have a talent strategy, not just a wishlist. Can you attract on mission, growth, and scope? | • Articulate what you can offer that FAANG cannot: ownership, breadth, speed of decision, mission alignment (telecom infrastructure touches 100M+ customers) • Build a talent pipeline through conference talks, open-source contributions, internal promotion pathways • Use structured scorecards — reduce bias, increase offer acceptance by showing candidates you take hiring seriously • Create referral density: your best engineers know 5 more people like them | T-Mobile: Built a referral pipeline that sourced 3 of 4 last hires. Spoke at SREcon on MART framework — two candidates reached out directly citing that talk. |
| 1.4 | "How do you handle org design when you're merging two SRE teams after an acquisition?" | Executive stakeholder management, technical empathy, change management. | • Don't merge on day 1 — run parallel operating models for 90 days, catalog toolchains, incident models, and on-call structures • Identify cultural load-bearers in both teams — not the loudest voices, but the ones others go to for help • Create a shared "charter sprint" — both teams write the new team's operating principles together, not top-down • Flag risks: duplicate tooling cost, on-call coverage gaps, talent flight from "acquired" side feeling second-tier | T-Mobile: Post-T-Mobile/Sprint merger context — observed two distinct monitoring paradigms (AppDynamics-heavy vs. Splunk-heavy) requiring a unified observability strategy, not a tool mandate. |
| 1.5 | "How do you develop the next generation of SRE managers from your IC pool?" | Whether you think about talent multiplication, not just talent extraction. | • Identify candidates by signal: who runs the postmortem room when you're not there? Who documents things nobody asked them to? • Create deliberate management rotations: shadow on-call escalation, own one vendor relationship, lead one headcount planning cycle • Pair with peers at neighboring orgs for cross-company mentorship — your org is too small to be the only learning environment • Name your failures: "I promoted two ICs who turned out to prefer staying technical — I now have an explicit conversation about the management tax before any transition" | T-Mobile: Developed two TLs from within the 15-person team. One now owns the ML anomaly detection workstream end-to-end as a functional lead. |

## Domain 2: Budget & Business Case

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2.1 | "How do you build a business case for SRE tooling that costs $2M/year?" | Financial fluency, ROI articulation, ability to speak CFO language not just engineer language. | • Frame in avoided cost, not tool features: what does 1 hour of P1 downtime cost in revenue, SLA credits, customer churn, and engineer time? • Build three scenarios: status quo (baseline risk), partial investment (diminishing returns), full investment (expected NPV) • Quantify engineer toil reduction in FTE-hours: 20% toil = X FTE equivalent = $Y fully-loaded cost • Include a sunset plan for the tools it replaces — CFOs love to see you're not just adding cost | T-Mobile: 25M msg/day notification platform. Calculated P1 downtime cost at ~$150K/hour factoring in SLA credits, customer support surge, and executive escalation cost. MART framework investment paid back in 6 months by reducing MTTR 40%. |
| 2.2 | "Your platform budget was cut 30% mid-year. How do you decide what to cut?" | Prioritization discipline under constraint. Do you protect the mission-critical or just cut the loudest complaints? | • First: categorize all spend by reliability impact tier (Tier 1: cuts risk P1 severity; Tier 2: degrades velocity; Tier 3: nice-to-have) • Never cut from Tier 1 without explicit sign-off from a VP+ stakeholder — make the risk visible, not invisible • Negotiate scope reduction before headcount reduction — reduce roadmap ambition, not team capability • Propose a "fast follow" commitment: items deferred get written into the next planning cycle with a sponsor named | T-Mobile: Managed through belt-tightening cycles — prioritized on-call tooling and alerting infrastructure over roadmap feature work. Documented deferred risk formally in quarterly SRE health report shared with VP Engineering. |
| 2.3 | "How do you calculate the ROI of reducing toil by 20%?" | Quantitative thinking applied to engineering productivity, not just vibes. | • Baseline: if team is 10 engineers at $200K fully-loaded, 20% toil = 2 FTE-equivalents = $400K/year recovered • Recovered capacity converts to roadmap velocity: 2 FTE on reliability automation = N reliability improvements/quarter • Secondary benefit: toil reduction → improved retention → avoided recruiting cost ($30-50K per backfill) • Caveats: time to recover is not instant — automation investment has a J-curve payback period of 3-6 months | T-Mobile: Quantified toil reduction after MART framework deployment. Alarm noise dropped 60%, reducing per-engineer on-call interrupt rate. Estimated 15% engineer time recovered across the team = 2.25 FTE-equivalent redirected to platform work. |
| 2.4 | "A product VP is asking you to cut your on-call rotation from 24x7 to business hours to save budget. How do you respond?" | Whether you can hold technical ground without being defensive, and whether you understand business risk management. | • Don't say no — say "here's what no means in business terms": quantify expected P1 frequency outside business hours and average revenue impact per undetected incident • Propose alternatives: tiered on-call (reduced rotation for low-severity), vendor NOC augmentation, reduced on-call stipend • Escalate the risk formally: "I can implement this decision, but I need your sign-off that you accept the identified risk" • Frame as a risk registry item — not your fight, their decision, your documentation | T-Mobile: Had this exact conversation in 2023 cost optimization cycle. Used 12-month incident data to show 34% of P1 incidents occurred outside business hours. VP retracted the request after seeing the data. |
| 2.5 | "How do you justify headcount growth for an SRE team when the org is in a hiring freeze?" | Creative resourcing, internal advocacy, and the ability to reframe the ask. | • Don't ask for headcount — ask for outcomes: "I need 2,000 engineer-hours of capacity to achieve X reliability target" • Propose internal rotation programs: product engineers doing 3-month SRE rotations (transfers knowledge both ways) • Show the risk of not hiring: plot defect escape rate, MTTR trend, and on-call burnout indicators over 6 months • Offer to sunset a legacy system in exchange for headcount — show net-zero budget impact | T-Mobile: During 2024 freeze, secured contractor approval (not headcount) for Kubernetes migration work by framing it as risk mitigation for the notification platform SLA. |

## Domain 3: Strategy & Technical Roadmap

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3.1 | "How do you build a 3-year technical roadmap when technology changes every 6 months?" | Horizon planning discipline. Do you know how to plan at multiple timescales simultaneously? | • Three-horizon model: H1 (0-12 months, committed, funded, outcome-defined), H2 (12-24 months, directional, prototype-validated), H3 (24-36 months, thesis-level, assumption-tracked) • Separate the roadmap from the architecture: the roadmap answers "what outcomes by when," the architecture answers "how" • Build explicit assumption logs for H2/H3 — when an assumption breaks, you update the roadmap without a crisis • Review H2/H3 quarterly with a small group; never let them ossify into a Gantt chart | T-Mobile: Ran 18-month reliability roadmap for notification platform. H1: EKS migration. H2: OpenTelemetry standardization. H3: AI-assisted incident triage. H2 assumption (Kafka retained) broke when architecture shifted — updated roadmap without replanning from scratch. |
| 3.2 | "How do you decide when to build vs. buy vs. open-source for platform components?" | Strategic judgment, vendor management maturity, and long-term thinking over short-term convenience. | • Build: only when it's a true competitive differentiator AND you have the talent to maintain it long-term • Buy: when the problem is commodity and vendor lock-in risk is acceptable; always include exit clauses • Open-source: when community velocity exceeds your internal capacity AND you have engineering bandwidth to track upstream • Common trap to name: "We chose open-source to avoid vendor lock-in but created a maintenance burden that cost more than the SaaS alternative" | T-Mobile: Chose open-source Prometheus/Grafana for K8s observability over commercial alternative. Saved $300K/year. Accepted the tradeoff: 0.5 FTE ongoing maintenance overhead. Made the cost explicit in the business case. |
| 3.3 | "A new VP joins and wants to replatform everything on their preferred technology stack. How do you handle it?" | Political navigation, technical integrity, and change management without being a blocker. | • First: listen without defending — new leadership often sees real problems, even if the solution is wrong • Second: write down what you heard as a problem statement, separate from the proposed solution • Third: present a structured options analysis that includes their preference as one option, scored against shared criteria • Fourth: propose a bounded pilot — "Let's prove or disprove the hypothesis on one team before committing" | T-Mobile: Navigated a push to standardize on a single observability vendor after leadership change. Ran a 90-day bake-off between incumbent and challenger, with defined success criteria agreed upfront. Incumbent won on integration depth — decision was data-backed, not political. |
| 3.4 | "How do you manage technical debt at the org level — not just in one team's backlog?" | Whether you can operationalize technical debt governance across multiple teams. | • Create a cross-team technical debt register with three columns: debt item, risk if unaddressed, business owner • Translate debt into risk language for non-technical stakeholders: "This 10-year-old auth library is our highest CVE exposure" • Build debt retirement into planning as a first-class budget line: 15-20% of every sprint is the minimum, not a reward for good behavior • Report debt metrics quarterly: count, severity distribution, age trend — same cadence as reliability metrics | T-Mobile: Instituted quarterly "reliability health" report that included a technical debt heat map across notification platform services. VP Engineering used it to justify a 2-quarter "reliability quarter" that froze feature work and cleared top 10 debt items. |
| 3.5 | "How do you align SRE roadmap with product roadmap when product always wins prioritization fights?" | Stakeholder influence, organizational dynamics, and the ability to make reliability a shared metric. | • Change the framing: SRE roadmap IS the product roadmap's risk management plan — they're not competing, they're coupled • Create shared OKRs: product VP and SRE Director co-own error budget burn rate — when it hits 50%, product must fund reliability work • Use postmortem data as a roadmap input: every P1 that touches a product's component is a refactoring ticket with a business case attached • Escalate strategically: never fight in the sprint, but never accept chronic reliability debt without documented VP-level acknowledgment | T-Mobile: Built error budget policy with product leadership. When notification platform hit 70% error budget consumption in Q3, product PM proactively moved reliability sprint into next quarter planning — no SRE lobbying required. |

## Domain 4: AI Strategy for SRE

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4.1 | "What is your AI strategy for SRE operations over the next 2 years?" | Whether you have a real strategy vs. AI hype fluency. Can you sequence the investment? | • Phase 1 (6 months): AI-assisted, human-in-the-loop — LLM-generated runbook drafts, anomaly detection surfacing, postmortem summarization • Phase 2 (12 months): AI-augmented — auto-remediation for known failure classes, intelligent alert grouping, drift detection • Phase 3 (18-24 months): evaluate autonomous response for bounded blast-radius scenarios with human override always present • Governance thread: model observability, prompt versioning, false positive tracking, rollback protocol | T-Mobile: Already running Phase 1: Python + Splunk MLTK anomaly detection on 25M msg/day platform. Reduced false positive alert volume 60%. Presented to SRE leadership as a "learning system" — models retrain on postmortem-labeled data. |
| 4.2 | "How do you prevent AI-assisted remediation from making your on-call engineers deskilled?" | This is a people strategy question disguised as an AI question. | • Design the human-in-the-loop deliberately: AI suggests, human approves — never remove the human review step for novel failure classes • Run regular "manual mode" drills: one day per quarter, operate with AI assistance disabled — GameDays without the crutch • Pair AI recommendations with explanations: engineers must read the why, not just click approve — builds intuition over time • Track skill indicators independently of AI uptime: can your team diagnose a P1 without AI assistance? | T-Mobile: On-call team uses AI-assisted alert triage but owns the postmortem root cause analysis without AI assist. Postmortems are the learning surface — AI removes toil, humans retain reasoning. |
| 4.3 | "How do you evaluate an AI observability vendor's claims?" | Vendor evaluation rigor and BS detection. | • Ask for their false positive rate on YOUR data class, not their demo data — run a 30-day POC on production traffic • Ask who owns the model: is it a black box or can you inspect feature weights? Explainability matters for incident triage • Ask about failure modes: what does the system do when it's uncertain? Does it fail safe (alert) or fail silent? • Total cost of ownership: model training cost, labeling cost, inference latency impact on your alerting pipeline | T-Mobile: Evaluated 3 AI observability vendors for notification platform. Required POC on live traffic. Two vendors' anomaly detection models had high false positive rates on telecom-specific traffic patterns (burst messaging windows). Disqualified on POC data, not demo data. |
| 4.4 | "How do you use AI to improve mean time to resolution without creating a reliability liability?" | Risk management and governance maturity around AI in production. | • Define blast radius for every auto-remediation action: scale-out actions (low risk, automate), data mutations (high risk, human gate) • Build an "AI confidence floor": auto-remediation only executes above 95% confidence on a known failure class with historical success rate • Create an AI incident register: every time AI takes an autonomous action, log it — audit trail for postmortems and model drift detection • Run shadow mode before live mode: AI recommends but doesn't act for 30 days, measure recommendation accuracy against human decisions | T-Mobile: Deployed auto-scaling remediation for queue depth spikes on notification platform. Shadow mode for 45 days first. Measured 89% agreement between AI recommendation and human action. Went live with human confirmation gate; removed gate after 60 days of clean operation. |
| 4.5 | "How do you build an AI literacy culture in an SRE team that's skeptical of hype?" | Change management and culture-building, not just AI knowledge. | • Start with a pain point they already have: toil, alarm fatigue, slow postmortem drafting — AI solves a known problem first • Show, don't tell: deploy one AI tool that demonstrably saves 2 hours/week for every on-call engineer — adoption follows outcome • Create a "model explainability" norm: engineers who use AI tools must be able to explain what the model is doing, not just what it output • Build failure stories into training: share examples where AI was wrong and the human caught it — this builds healthy skepticism | T-Mobile: Introduced ML anomaly detection by framing it as "Splunk MLTK, which you already know, now applied to our alert stream." Skeptics became advocates after first month of reduced 3am pages. |

## Domain 5: Cross-functional Influence

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5.1 | "How do you get a product VP to care about reliability when they're only measured on feature velocity?" | Whether you can align incentives rather than fight them. | • Don't argue reliability vs. velocity — they're the same metric at sufficient scale: reliability failures destroy velocity via P1 response tax • Propose a shared OKR: "Product VP and SRE Director co-own customer-impacting error rate below X" — this makes reliability a product metric • Use incident data as a product roadmap input: every reliability incident is a customer experience failure — frame it in NPS/churn language • Find a champion inside product: product managers who've lived through a P1 caused by technical debt become natural allies | T-Mobile: Partnered with notification platform product manager to add "zero P1 incidents attributed to technical debt" as a shared team OKR. Product team voluntarily allocated 20% of sprint to reliability work because their own OKR depended on it. |
| 5.2 | "How do you influence a Chief Architect who disagrees with your platform direction?" | Executive navigation, technical humility, and knowing when to escalate vs. when to iterate. | • First: assume they have information you don't — ask clarifying questions before defending your position • Second: separate the architectural principle from the implementation: "I agree with the principle of zero-trust network segmentation; I disagree that this vendor is the right implementation" • Third: find external validation — reference architectures, analyst reports, peer company case studies carry weight with Architects • Fourth: propose a structured decision record (ADR) — put both positions in writing with criteria; forces clarity and creates an audit trail | T-Mobile: Navigated disagreement with enterprise architect over Kubernetes network policy approach. Wrote a formal ADR with three options, scoring each on security posture, operational complexity, and migration risk. Architect chose Option 2 (hybrid approach) — neither of us got 100%, both got something defensible. |
| 5.3 | "How do you manage the relationship with a CISO who sees SRE as a security risk, not an ally?" | Cross-functional relationship building in a politically charged context. | • Start by listening: what specific SRE behaviors create security concern? (Broad IAM roles? Informal change processes? Shadow infra?) • Create a shared language: SLOs and security control objectives are both reliability constructs — frame them together • Volunteer for joint ownership: "SRE will own the security monitoring runbooks for the top 5 CVE categories in our stack" • Build trust incrementally: bring security into postmortem reviews — CISOs who see blameless postmortems often become allies | T-Mobile: After a vulnerability disclosure required emergency patching on the notification platform, partnered with CISO org to build a "patch SLA" into the reliability framework — P0 CVEs patched within 24 hours, tracked the same way as P1 incidents. CISO team started attending SRE quarterly reviews. |
| 5.4 | "How do you communicate a major outage to the board or C-suite without being defensive?" | Executive communication under pressure. Can you hold the room with credibility when things went wrong? | • Lead with impact and current state, not cause: "As of [time], service is restored. X customers were affected for Y minutes. Revenue impact is estimated at $Z." • Avoid technical jargon: "database replication lag" becomes "a backup system fell behind and the handoff failed" • Show the fix and the prevention: "We've applied an immediate fix. Here are the three systemic changes that prevent recurrence." • Own the organizational learning: "This incident revealed a gap in our on-call escalation process, which I'm fixing personally by [date]" | T-Mobile: Presented P1 post-incident review to VP-level leadership after notification platform outage affecting 2M subscribers. Used a 3-slide format: Timeline (what happened), Impact (business effect), Prevention (what changes). No technical slides. |
| 5.5 | "How do you build relationships with peers in security, networking, and data platform when everyone is territorial?" | Organizational influence without authority. | • Start with giving, not asking: offer to own something they don't want to own (on-call runbook for network-related SRE incidents, for example) • Create joint working groups for shared pain: security + SRE + networking all hate false positive alerts — form a "signal quality" working group • Make their wins visible: when a network team change prevents a reliability incident, credit them publicly in the postmortem • Build bilateral SLAs between teams — "We commit to X; you commit to Y" — creates accountability without politics | T-Mobile: Built a "shared postmortem" model with the networking team after three P1 incidents had overlapping root causes. Joint postmortems led to a shared runbook for BGP route flap detection — both teams reduced MTTR by 30% on that failure class. |

## Domain 6: Incident Command at Scale

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 6.1 | "How do you design an incident command structure for an org running 500+ microservices?" | Systems thinking about process design, not just personal incident command skill. | • Tiered severity model: P0 (CEO-level awareness, all-hands bridge), P1 (VP-level, cross-team IC), P2 (team-level, single IC), P3 (async) • Federated IC model: each service domain has trained ICs; a Global IC coordinates cross-domain incidents without owning all the technical detail • Define the IC role explicitly: IC does not debug — IC coordinates, communicates, makes escalation decisions, keeps the room from rabbit-holing • Train ICs through GameDays, not just documentation — 20% of your ICs will freeze without practice | T-Mobile: Ran federated IC model across notification platform and its dependencies (RabbitMQ, Cassandra, EKS control plane). When P1 affected 3 domains simultaneously, each domain IC owned their blast radius while Global IC managed customer communication and rollback decisions. |
| 6.2 | "How do you reduce MTTR organizationally — not just technically?" | Process design and culture, not just tooling. | • Measure MTTR at every stage: time-to-detect, time-to-acknowledge, time-to-mitigate, time-to-resolve, time-to-prevent-recurrence — fix the longest stage first • Create explicit "war room" norms: who can speak, when to call a rollback, when to escalate — ambiguity in the room costs minutes • Pre-bake rollback decisions: define rollback criteria in advance ("if queue depth exceeds X for Y minutes, we rollback") — removes decision fatigue during incidents • Postmortem-driven MTTR reduction: every P1 postmortem must identify the one change that would have reduced MTTR the most | T-Mobile: Reduced MTTR on notification platform P1s from avg 47 min to 28 min over 18 months by fixing time-to-acknowledge (automated paging) and time-to-mitigate (pre-approved rollback runbooks). Detection time was already optimized via MART framework. |
| 6.3 | "How do you prevent on-call burnout at organizational scale?" | People sustainability in operational roles. This is a retention question disguised as a process question. | • Measure on-call load as a first-class metric: interrupt rate, sleep-time pages, pages per week per engineer — report it to leadership quarterly • Set explicit alert quality SLOs: every alert must be actionable, have a runbook, and have been reviewed in the last quarter — stale alerts are toil tax • Build recovery time into the rota: post-P1-shift recovery day is non-negotiable policy, not manager discretion • Create a sustainable on-call size: below 5 engineers in rotation, the rota is a retention risk — name it to leadership | T-Mobile: Team grew from 8 to 15 engineers partly by demonstrating on-call load metrics to VP. Showed that interrupt rate per engineer had exceeded sustainable threshold. Got approval for 3 additional headcount framed as "on-call sustainability investment." |
| 6.4 | "How do you run blameless postmortems at scale when teams are distributed and culturally diverse?" | Culture-building and psychological safety, with practical execution. | • Separate the postmortem from performance management — make it explicit policy that postmortem content cannot be used in performance reviews • Train facilitators independently of technical expertise: the best postmortem facilitator is often not the senior engineer — it's the person who asks "why" five times • Publish postmortems internally with a "lessons applicable to your service" tag — create a culture where postmortems are read, not filed • Track postmortem action item completion as a reliability metric — unfinished action items are future incidents | T-Mobile: Implemented a postmortem template with explicit "contributing factors" (not "root cause" — there is no single root) and required 5-Why analysis. Published all P1 postmortems to an internal wiki. Monthly "postmortem retrospective" reviewed action item completion. |
| 6.5 | "How do you know your incident command training is actually working?" | Whether you evaluate outcomes, not just process compliance. | • Measure it: track IC rotation coverage, number of trained ICs per severity tier, GameDay pass/fail metrics • Run live-fire exercises: once per quarter, inject a real-ish failure (not a drill they know about) into a non-production environment and observe • Assess IC behavior in real incidents: did the IC freeze? Did they rabbit-hole? Did they communicate proactively? Build a structured debrief rubric • Look at lagging indicators: MTTR trend, escalation rate, "time until first correct hypothesis" in incident timelines | T-Mobile: Ran quarterly GameDays simulating notification platform failure scenarios (queue saturation, Cassandra node loss, EKS node failure). Measured time-to-correct-diagnosis and time-to-mitigation. Improved both metrics by 25% over 4 quarters of regular GameDays. |

## Domain 7: Platform Engineering Philosophy

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 7.1 | "What is a golden path and when does it stop being golden?" | Conceptual depth on platform engineering, not just vocabulary. | • Golden path: the opinionated, paved road for how product teams build, deploy, and operate — reduces cognitive load, centralizes security and reliability defaults • It stops being golden when: it's too rigid (teams bypass it via shadow IT), too slow to evolve (teams are blocked waiting for platform updates), or insufficiently documented (teams don't know it exists) • Measure golden path adoption: what % of production services use the standard deployment pipeline? • The platform team's job is to make the golden path the path of least resistance, not the mandatory path | T-Mobile: Notification platform uses a standardized EKS deployment template owned by platform team. When 2 product teams bypassed it for a faster release, investigated: template had a 45-minute cert rotation step that could be async. Fixed the bottleneck, teams returned to golden path. |
| 7.2 | "How do you measure the success of an internal developer platform?" | Outcome orientation vs. output orientation. | • Wrong metrics: "number of features shipped," "uptime of the platform itself" • Right metrics: developer experience score (quarterly survey), time-to-first-deployment for new engineers, deployment frequency of product teams using vs. not using the platform • Track adoption rate and drop-off points: where do teams abandon the golden path? That's your product backlog • Net Promoter Score for internal platforms is underused and deeply informative | T-Mobile: Instrumented internal deployment pipeline usage. Discovered that new engineers took 3 days to first deployment — too long. Reduced to 4 hours by adding self-service bootstrapping. Developer satisfaction score (internal survey) improved from 3.2/5 to 4.1/5. |
| 7.3 | "How do you prevent a platform team from becoming a bottleneck?" | This is an org design and product thinking question. | • Platform team must have a product manager, not just engineering leads — someone who talks to customers (product engineers) weekly • Self-service first: if a product team needs to open a ticket to use a platform capability, that capability is not done yet • Define SLAs for platform team responsiveness to feature requests — treat internal customers like external customers • Regular "office hours" and embedded rotation: platform engineers rotate through product teams quarterly to stay connected to real pain | T-Mobile: Platform team (4 engineers) served 15+ product squads. Created a self-service terraform module library. Teams could provision infrastructure without a ticket. Platform team meetings shifted from "intake triage" to "capability roadmap" — fundamentally different kind of work. |
| 7.4 | "What's your philosophy on standardization vs. flexibility in platform choices?" | Strategic judgment on the standardization-autonomy tension. | • Standardize the contract (interface), not the implementation: "All services must emit OTEL traces" is a contract. "All services must use Jaeger" is an over-constraint. • Flexibility has a hidden cost: every non-standard choice creates an on-call burden for someone at 3am • Rule of thumb: if you have to support it in production, you have the right to have an opinion on how it's built • Create a "paved road, dirt road, off-road" framework: paved (supported, default), dirt (tolerated, no support SLA), off-road (explicitly discouraged, engineer owns all incidents) | T-Mobile: Applied paved/dirt/off-road framework to observability tooling. Splunk: paved. Prometheus/Grafana: paved. Custom metrics agents: dirt road (tolerated). Proprietary APM agents from vendors not on approved list: off-road. Reduced on-call confusion about who debugs what observability component. |
| 7.5 | "How do you handle the transition from ops team to platform team — the cultural shift is harder than the technical shift?" | Change management, culture, and the human side of platform transformation. | • The cultural shift: ops teams are reactive heroes; platform teams are proactive product builders — this is a fundamentally different identity • Name the grief: some engineers genuinely loved being heroes. Platform engineering asks them to be invisible. Have an explicit conversation. • Create early wins: port one high-value capability to self-service and celebrate it publicly — gives the team a taste of the platform identity • Protect the team from being pulled back into ops work during the transition — this requires management air cover, not just culture talks | T-Mobile: SRE team started as traditional ops. Gradually shifted by: first automating toil (5 runbooks-as-code), then making automation self-service (product teams could trigger runbooks), then building forward (new platform capabilities that product teams requested). Transition took 18 months — not a flip. |

## Domain 8: Career Development & Culture

| # | Question | What Interviewer Is Testing | Model Answer Framework | T-Mobile Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 8.1 | "How do you retain senior engineers in an environment where they're constantly recruited by FAANG?" | Retention strategy and what actually motivates senior engineers (it's not just comp). | • Senior engineers stay for: scope/ownership, learning velocity, mission alignment, team quality, and manager quality — in roughly that order • Create retention signals: are your senior engineers presenting at conferences? Publishing postmortems? Mentoring juniors? If not, they're coasting and will leave • Build succession visibility: senior engineers stay when they see a path to Staff or Principal with real scope, not just a title bump • Exit interview data is lagging — use stay interviews quarterly: "What would make you leave? What's keeping you here?" | T-Mobile: Retained a Staff SRE through a FAANG offer by: expanding scope (gave ownership of entire ML reliability workstream), creating a public speaking opportunity (SREcon talk on MART framework), and approving a 15% comp adjustment. All three were necessary — scope alone wasn't enough. |
| 8.2 | "How do you build psychological safety in a team that's been through a lot of painful incidents and leadership changes?" | Culture repair and trust rebuilding, not just culture maintenance. | • Psychological safety is built through consistent behavior, not speeches: if you say "blame-free" but then mention names negatively in post-incident reviews, trust is gone • Start with a team retrospective on "what's not working" — let the team name the problems before you do • Make your own mistakes visible: share a postmortem of a decision you got wrong as a leader — this models the behavior you're asking for • Celebrate appropriate risk-taking, not just success: "This experiment didn't work, and here's what we learned" must be acknowledged publicly | T-Mobile: Joined a team that had three VP-level leadership changes in 18 months. First 90 days: listened, documented complaints without judgment, ran a team retrospective. Stabilized on-call burden first (the most visible daily pain). Trust rebuilt over 6 months, not 6 days. |
| 8.3 | "How do you handle a top performer who is technically excellent but culturally toxic?" | Whether you can hold both performance and culture simultaneously — the hardest management call. | • Name it early: "brilliant jerks" compound in damage the longer they stay — every month you tolerate it, you lose goodwill from the rest of the team • Separate the feedback clearly: "Your technical work is excellent. The way you give code review feedback is creating a hostile environment for junior engineers." • Create a specific behavioral contract: not "be nicer" but "in code reviews, you must offer one constructive suggestion for every critical comment" • If behavior doesn't change: the culture cost exceeds the technical contribution — this is a difficult but correct calculation | T-Mobile: Managed one engineer who was the team's best technical contributor but regularly dismissed junior engineers' questions publicly. Private coaching first. Behavioral contract second. Behavior changed 60% — enough to retain without ongoing cultural damage. |
| 8.4 | "How do you build a diversity and inclusion strategy for a technical org, not just HR-driven checkbox compliance?" | Whether D&I is a real value or a performance for the interviewer. | • Start with pipeline: where do your candidates come from? If it's the same 3 CS schools, your diversity problem starts in sourcing, not bias • Structured interviews reduce bias more reliably than bias training: same questions, same scoring rubric, calibration before scoring, no "culture fit" as a criterion • Create inclusion indicators that are operational: who is presenting in tech reviews? Who is leading postmortems? Who is being nominated for Staff promotions? • Sponsor, not just mentor: as a Director, name underrepresented engineers for high-visibility opportunities — sponsorship creates career trajectory, mentorship gives advice | T-Mobile: Two of three promotions to TL in the last cycle went to engineers from underrepresented backgrounds — not by lowering the bar, but by ensuring they had equal access to the high-visibility incidents and architecture reviews that build the track record for promotion. |
| 8.5 | "Where do you want to be in 5 years, and how does this role get you there?" | Whether you have self-awareness, ambition calibration, and genuine interest in this specific role. | • Be honest about the direction: VP of Engineering, or Chief Reliability Officer, or a path toward building your own company — pick one and own it • Connect it to this role specifically: "This role builds [specific capability] that I don't yet have at sufficient scale" • Show you've thought about the organization's trajectory: "I see this company moving toward [X] — that aligns with where I want to develop expertise" • Avoid generic answers: "I want to grow and be challenged" is a non-answer at Director level | T-Mobile: Frame answer around: "I've built deep SRE and reliability expertise at scale. This role extends that into [platform engineering / DevSecOps / org-wide reliability culture]. In 5 years, I want to be the kind of VP who's built a platform that other companies learn from — and I think [company] is the right place to build that." |

## Section 9: Questions That SEEM Technical But Are Actually Leadership Assessments

These questions sound like they want a technical answer. They don't. The interviewer is evaluating your judgment, communication, and leadership pattern — not your Kubernetes YAML.

| # | The Question | What They're Actually Testing | What They Want to Hear Instead |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1 | "Walk me through your Kubernetes architecture." | Do you select the right level of abstraction for the audience, or do you dump technical detail? | Start with the problem it solves and the business outcome. Say "I can go as deep as useful — do you want the architecture decisions or the operational model?" |
| T2 | "How do you handle a database that's running out of storage?" | Do you firefight alone or coordinate? Do you communicate during an incident? | "The technical action is X. But the first thing I do is notify [stakeholders], set a status update cadence, and confirm our rollback options. Here's how I'd coordinate..." |
| T3 | "What's your approach to SLO definition?" | Are you customer-outcome-focused or metric-focused? Do you involve stakeholders? | Lead with "I start by asking what the user experiences when the system is degraded, not what the system metric is." Show that SLOs are negotiated with the business, not handed down by engineering. |
| T4 | "How would you debug a latency spike in production?" | Can you stay calm, communicate, and delegate? Do you know when to escalate vs. dig? | Structure the answer: "First, I'd confirm customer impact. Second, I'd pull in the right engineers. Third, I'd set a communication cadence with stakeholders. Here's the diagnostic approach the team would follow..." |
| T5 | "What CI/CD pipeline would you build from scratch?" | Do you understand that pipeline design is a developer experience and security problem, not just a toolchain selection? | "I'd start with the developer experience requirement: what does a good deploy feel like for a product engineer? Then I'd layer in security gates. Tool selection comes last." |
| T6 | "How do you implement zero-trust networking?" | Do you understand the organizational change required, or do you think it's just a firewall config? | "Zero-trust is a cultural and process change that happens to involve technology. The hardest part is getting legacy service owners to accept mutual TLS requirements on their service-to-service calls." |
| T7 | "How would you reduce cloud costs by 30%?" | Do you rush to cut or do you instrument first? Do you involve finance and product? | "I wouldn't start by cutting. I'd start by tagging and attributing. Most orgs don't know who owns what cost. Once you have attribution, cost ownership follows." |
| T8 | "How do you handle a situation where Terraform state gets corrupted?" | Do you panic and improvise, or do you follow a defined process? Do you learn from it? | "I'd follow the recovery procedure we have documented. If we don't have one, that's the real problem this incident revealed. Here's how I'd use this event to create one..." |
| T9 | "What's your opinion on GitOps vs. push-based deployments?" | Do you have dogmatic opinions or can you evaluate contextually? | "Neither is universally correct. GitOps wins on auditability and drift detection. Push wins on simplicity for small orgs. I'd choose based on [org maturity, team size, compliance requirements]." |
| T10 | "How do you ensure your monitoring catches everything?" | Do you understand the limits of monitoring, or do you oversell your system's completeness? | "You can't. The honest answer is that monitoring is a sampling of known failure modes. The investment is in making unknown failures surface faster, which is an organizational learning problem, not a dashboarding problem." |

## Section 10: Questions You Must NEVER Answer With Just Technical Detail

These questions look safe. If you answer them with a technical monologue, you have failed the interview at Director/VP level. Here's what to say instead.

| # | The Dangerous Question | Why Technical Answers Fail | What to Say Instead |
|---|---|---|---|
| N1 | "Tell me about a major outage you managed." | A technical answer sounds like you were an IC, not a leader. Interviewers at Director/VP level are hiring for someone who manages the organization through an outage, not someone who debugs it. | Lead with: "My role in major outages is coordination and communication, not technical diagnosis. Here's how I ran the room, what I communicated to leadership, and what organizational change came out of it." Close with the postmortem and what changed in the team's process. |
| N2 | "What technologies are you evaluating right now?" | A tool list sounds like a resume recitation. The interviewer is testing whether you evaluate technology strategically or chase trends. | Frame your answer around the business problem first: "We're evaluating [X] because we have a specific gap in [Y]. Here's the criteria we're using to evaluate it and who's involved in the decision." Never start with a tool name. |
| N3 | "How do you keep your team's skills current?" | Listing training budgets and conference attendance sounds like HR policy. They want to know if you create a learning culture, not a training program. | Answer with: "I build learning into the work itself — postmortems, GameDays, architecture reviews with external speakers. The formal training budget matters less than whether the team is learning from production every week." |
| N4 | "What would you do in your first 90 days?" | A project list sounds like you've already decided what's wrong before you've listened. This is the single most common Director-level interview failure mode. | "My first 30 days are entirely listening: postmortems, 1:1s with every team member, shadowing on-call. I don't make decisions in the first 30 days except for emergencies. My 60-day plan is shaped by what I heard in the first 30." |
| N5 | "Why are you leaving your current role?" | Any answer that sounds like criticism of your current employer, or that focuses on comp, signals immaturity at Director level. Even if true, technical or organizational frustration answers sound like whining. | "I've accomplished [X specific thing] at T-Mobile and built a team I'm proud of. I'm looking for [specific scope this role offers] that I can't build at my current organization at this stage. This role specifically offers [Y], which is exactly where I want to grow next." |
