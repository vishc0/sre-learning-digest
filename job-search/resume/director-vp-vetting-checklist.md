## PART 3: QUESTIONS TO ASK — BY STAGE

### Stage 1: Recruiter Screen (20–30 min)

Filter out H1B non-starters, title inflation, and unstable companies in the first 20 minutes.

| Question | What It Reveals | Red Flag Answer |
|---|---|---|
| "Is this role open to H1B transfer candidates? The company would need to file an I-129 — is that something the company has done for engineering leadership hires before?" | Whether they sponsor, whether they've done it at Director level, whether recruiter knows the difference between H1B new vs. transfer | "We'll figure it out later" / "We don't sponsor" / blank silence on I-129 terminology |
| "What's the company's current ARR or funding stage, and when was the last raise?" | Financial runway, whether role will exist in 12 months, whether they're in growth vs. survival mode | Vague answer ("we're well-funded"), won't say when the last round was, or it was 3+ years ago with no IPO/acquisition path |
| "How long has this role been open, and have you made and rescinded an offer on this position?" | Whether the org knows what it wants, whether there's internal dysfunction around the hire | Open 6+ months, or yes to rescinded offer without a clear explanation |
| "Is this a net-new Director role or backfill? If backfill — why did the previous person leave?" | Whether this is a growth investment or a patch job. Attrition reason reveals org health | "We're not able to share that" / "they just weren't the right fit" with no specifics |
| "How many direct reports does this role have today on Day 1, and what's the expected 12-month team size?" | Whether Director authority is real vs. a title with IC responsibilities | Fewer than 4 directs with no growth plan, OR "you'd be building the team from scratch" with no budget clarity |
| "Does this Director role have budget ownership — headcount and tooling? Who approves that budget?" | Tests whether Director has real authority or is a senior IC with a title | "Budget goes through the CTO/VP Eng for everything" — that is a Staff Principal job with a Director label |
| "What's the comp band for this role, and does it include equity with a standard 4-year vest?" | Wastes no one's time. Signals you know your market value | Band is 30%+ below market for Director SRE in the company's metro/remote tier, or equity cliff is non-standard |

### Stage 2: Hiring Manager Screen (45–60 min)

Understand the real problem they need solved and whether you want to solve it.

| Question | What It Reveals | Red Flag Answer |
|---|---|---|
| "What's the one problem that, if I solve nothing else in year one, makes this hire a success?" | Forces them to articulate real business pain vs. generic JD language. Reveals what actually broke | "We just need someone to run the team" — no specific pain signal means no clear mandate |
| "Walk me through your last major production incident — how was it declared, who commanded it, and what changed after the postmortem?" | Tests maturity of incident command, whether SRE has authority or is support, whether postmortems drive change | "We just fix it and move on" / no dedicated incident commander / postmortem action items that never close |
| "What is the current on-call rotation structure — how many people, what's the mean number of pages per week, and what's the P95 time-to-acknowledge?" | Reveals whether on-call is sustainable or a burnout factory. These are numbers a healthy org knows | Can't answer the numbers / "we page whoever is available" / more than 5 pages/week per engineer steady state |
| "What does your SLO governance look like today — are SLOs defined in code, who owns error budget, and has an error budget ever stopped a release?" | Tests whether SRE has teeth or is just a monitoring team. Error budgets stopping releases = SRE has authority | "We have uptime targets in a spreadsheet" / error budgets have never stopped anything / product owns the numbers |
| "What's the relationship between SRE and Platform Engineering here — are they the same team, adjacent, or competing?" | Reveals org design clarity. Competing = turf wars. No platform team = SRE is doing undifferentiated toil | "SRE does everything" with a 6-person team covering prod + platform + security |
| "Where is the team today on the toil reduction curve — what percentage of SRE sprint capacity goes to reactive work vs. engineering work?" | Reveals whether this is a true SRE org or ops with a rebrand. Healthy target: under 50% toil | "Most of the work is reactive" / no measurement / sprint capacity tracking doesn't exist |
| "What does the current CI/CD pipeline look like — what's the deployment frequency for the core platform, and what gates exist between commit and production?" | Tests pipeline maturity. Deployment frequency is the one DORA metric that doesn't lie | Manual deploy steps in production, no automated rollback, deploy frequency monthly or less |
| "Has the company gone through a significant RIF or reorg in the last 18 months? How did engineering leadership communicate through it?" | Reveals company stability, psychological safety, and whether leadership has integrity under pressure | Defensive answer / "we've had some restructuring" with no follow-through / "headcount is always fluid here" |
| "What does success look like for this Director at the 30/90/180-day mark — and who defines those milestones?" | Tests whether onboarding is structured vs. "figure it out yourself." Reveals how much support you'll have | No defined milestones / "you'd set your own goals" with no exec alignment / milestone is just "get up to speed" |
| "Why are you the hiring manager for this role — do you see this Director as a peer or a report?" | Tests org design. Director reporting to a VP Eng vs. a peer CTO changes authority entirely | Vague org chart explanation / "it's still being figured out" / Director would report to someone who doesn't understand SRE |

### Stage 3: Executive / Skip-Level (30–45 min)

Understand how engineering is valued and where SRE fits in company strategy.

| Question | What It Reveals | Red Flag Answer |
|---|---|---|
| "How does reliability factor into the company's product strategy — is SRE in the room when roadmap is set, or does it react to what ships?" | Tests whether exec views SRE as strategic or custodial. This is the single biggest predictor of Director authority | "SRE is involved post-launch" / "product sets the roadmap, SRE supports it" — Director will be a firefighter |
| "What's your personal theory of the SRE function — where does it create leverage that pure DevOps or Platform Engineering can't?" | Reveals how deeply the exec has thought about the SRE model. Shallow answer = they hired a title, not a function | "SRE is basically DevOps but more senior" / can't articulate the error budget model or production ownership concept |
| "What's the 3-year engineering org structure vision — does SRE stay a function, or does it get embedded into product teams?" | Reveals whether this Director role has a shelf life | "We'll figure it out as we scale" / they've already decided to embed without telling the candidate |
| "What's the current relationship between security and SRE — who owns runtime security posture, and has there been friction there?" | Tests whether DevSecOps is real or aspirational. Security vs. SRE turf conflict is very common in fast-scaling companies | "Security is a separate org and SRE doesn't touch it" / recent security incident that SRE wasn't looped into |
| "What engineering investment has the company made in observability in the last 12 months — tooling, headcount, and exec attention?" | Reveals whether observability is valued or treated as a cost center | "We have Datadog" with no follow-on investment thinking / observability budget was cut in last planning cycle |
| "If I join and the right call is to delay a major product launch to protect reliability — will I have your cover to make that call?" | Tests whether exec will back Director authority in a political moment. The answer to this question is the job | Hesitation / "we'd need to evaluate that case by case" / "product roadmap is set by the CEO" |
| "How does this company think about technical debt as a balance sheet item — is it tracked, and does it factor into planning?" | Reveals engineering culture maturity. Companies that don't track tech debt don't fund SRE properly | "Engineering takes care of it" with no exec visibility / tech debt has never been presented to the board |
| "What does the path to VP look like from this Director role — is there a defined scope expansion, or is this a terminal level here?" | Tests growth ceiling. Director with no VP path in 3 years = career plateau | "We don't really have VP titles" in a 500-person company / "it depends on company growth" with no concrete answer |

### Stage 4: Loop Interviews (Peer engineers, product, security)

Read the culture, technical state, and cross-functional friction — from people who will tell the truth.

| Question | What It Reveals | Red Flag Answer |
|---|---|---|
| "What's the one thing about working here that doesn't show up in the job description — good or bad?" | Opens psychological safety. Engineers tell truth here that managers won't | Nervous pause followed by only positives / "I can't really say" = culture of fear |
| "When was the last time SRE pushed back on a product release and won? Walk me through it." | Tests whether SRE has authority or is performative. Specific story required | "We don't really block releases" / can't cite a single example / "product has final call always" |
| "How does the team currently handle knowledge transfer — runbooks, architecture docs, on-call guides? What's missing?" | Reveals documentation culture, bus factor risk, and whether the team is set up to scale | "It's mostly tribal knowledge" / "we're too busy to document" / no runbooks in source control |
| "What does a normal week look like for someone on this team — what's the split between planned work, interrupt-driven work, and meetings?" | Best proxy for toil load and meeting overhead without asking directly | More than 40% of time in meetings / "it depends, it can be chaotic" / on-call bleeds into daytime work consistently |
| "What's the biggest technical decision the SRE team has made in the last 6 months, and how was it made?" | Tests team agency and decision-making process. Good teams make architectural calls | Decision was made by the CTO without SRE input / "we don't really make architecture decisions" |
| "How do product and SRE handle disagreements about launch readiness — who has the last word and how is it resolved?" | Tests cross-functional friction and escalation path. This surfaces in your first month | "It escalates to the CTO every time" / "product usually wins" / no defined process |
| "What's your relationship with the security team — do you get looped in early on compliance or infrastructure changes?" | Tests DevSecOps integration maturity from the practitioner level | "Security sends us requirements at the end" / "they audit us after the fact" / no shared tooling or pipelines |
| "If you could change one thing about how the SRE function operates today, what would it be?" | Surfaces the actual pain the new Director will inherit. This is the real job description | Silence / "everything's fine" from multiple engineers = team is coached or disconnected from reality |

### Stage 5: Offer Stage

Verify Director authority is real before signing. Negotiate everything.

| Item | Why It Matters | If Answer Is No |
|---|---|---|
| Confirm in writing: company will file I-129 transfer petition within 30 days of start date and will cover premium processing ($2,805) | H1B transfer portability requires I-129 filing — until filed, the prior employer's petition controls status. Premium processing ensures 15-business-day approval vs. 3–6 months uncertainty | Walk. Non-negotiable. A company that won't commit to this timeline is a visa status risk. |
| Org chart in writing — direct reports, reporting line, and dotted-line authority — included in offer letter or signed addendum | Title inflation is rampant at startups. Without written org structure, Director authority can be revised on Day 30 | Counter with: "I'm happy to accept verbally contingent on the written org structure being provided before I sign." Do not sign without it. |
| Equity grant documented: total shares, strike price, cliff date, vesting schedule, and last 409A valuation date | Options without a recent 409A are worth unknown amounts. Strike price relative to 409A reveals whether options are in-the-money | Request the 409A report directly. If they won't share it: that equity is speculative — discount it to zero in your mental model. |
| Headcount budget for this role in the current fiscal year — how many open reqs owned, and is that budget approved or pending board approval | Director with a team of 2 and no approved headcount is a Staff IC with a title | Ask for the current approved headcount floor in writing before signing. |
| 6-month compensation review clause tied to defined milestones, not just the annual performance review cycle | Startup comp adjusts slowly. A clause creates a forcing function for compensation alignment before the annual cycle | Counter: "Can we agree on a written 6-month check-in with the CTO/CPO tied to the milestones we've already discussed?" |
| Confirmation: no down round, no revenue miss >20%, no significant RIF in the last 24 months | A Director joining a company 6 months before a RIF or acqui-hire is the first cut | Pull Crunchbase, LinkedIn headcount trends, and Glassdoor layoff dates yourself before signing. |
| 90-day trial clause OR severance of at least 3 months base salary in event of role elimination within 12 months of hire | Startup Director roles are high-elimination-risk during reorgs. 3-month severance is standard at Series B+. Also protects visa status bridge time | Negotiate to 60 days minimum. Frame as: "Given the H1B transfer timeline, I need continuity protection in the event of restructuring." |

## PART 4: THE 3 POWER QUESTIONS

These demonstrate Director-level thinking AND extract critical information. Ask one per interview stage.

| Question | Why It Signals Seniority | What the Answer Tells You |
|---|---|---|
| "How does the board currently think about reliability as a competitive differentiator versus a cost of goods sold — and has that framing ever changed how a strategic decision was made?" (Ask in Stage 3 — CTO/Skip-Level) | Forces the exec to translate reliability into board-level business language. Only a Director who has presented to a board or worked alongside one thinks in these terms. A Staff IC asks about architecture. A Director asks about board framing. | Whether SRE has ever influenced strategy above VP level. A company where reliability is pure COGS will cap SRE authority at "keeping the lights on." |
| "If I look at your last three postmortems, what pattern do they share — and what has the organization *not been willing to change* that keeps appearing in those contributing factors?" (Ask in Stage 2 — Hiring Manager) | The second half of this question is the tell. Every org has chronic contributing factors they won't fix — usually cultural, political, or financial. A hiring manager who answers only the first half is managing perception. One who answers both is trustworthy. | The actual systemic risks you will inherit. Whether leadership owns failure or deflects it. Whether you are being hired to solve a known problem or to absorb blame for an unsolvable one. |
| "When the last Director or SRE lead left — or was moved out — what did the team lose that you're still trying to recover from?" (Ask in Stage 4 — Peer Loop, directed at a senior SRE) | Surfaces institutional knowledge gaps, trust deficits, and morale hangovers from the previous leadership transition. A Principal IC would ask "what are the biggest technical challenges." A Director asks what the human cost of the last transition was, because that is the actual state they're inheriting on Day 1. | Whether the previous leader was respected or resented. Whether the team has recovered or is still fragmented. Whether there is a toxic legacy pattern in the role that no one disclosed in earlier stages. |

## PART 5: WALK-AWAY TRIGGERS

If any of these appear at any stage — exit the process. No exceptions.

1. **Company has never filed an H1B** (h1bdata.info shows zero filings) — they have no immigration infrastructure and cannot execute a transfer reliably.
2. **Last funding round 30+ months ago with no revenue signals** — runway math does not work; this role will not exist in 12 months.
3. **Role reports 2+ levels below CTO/CEO** — confirmed by LinkedIn org or JD; this is a Staff IC job with a Director title unless scope is explicitly confirmed by the hiring manager.
4. **Base comp range confirmed below $190K** — below market for the level; title inflation is in play, authority will match the comp, not the title.
5. **Recruiter cannot confirm H1B sponsorship on the first call** — do not proceed until confirmed in writing; verbal assurances from recruiters who "need to check" are not binding.
6. **Two or more layoff rounds in the past 18 months** (Layoffs.fyi / Glassdoor) — the runway math does not work and the cultural damage is already done.
7. **CTO or CEO tenure under 12 months** — executive instability at this level invalidates every commitment made during the interview process.
8. **Equity cliff greater than 1 year** — hostile to employees; negotiate down or walk.
9. **No direct reports on Day 1 and team headcount listed as "TBD"** — this is an IC role with a Director title; decide accordingly before investing further time.
10. **Glassdoor score below 3.0 with recent reviews citing "chaos," "no direction," or "constant pivots"** — cultural dysfunction at this severity takes 2+ years to remediate and a new Director will not have the runway or political capital to do it.
